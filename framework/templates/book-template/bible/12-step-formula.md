# [BOOK TITLE] — 12-Step Mystery Formula
*[Genre / one-line descriptor] · Story Bible Entry · Revision 1*

Three acts, four steps each. Step 6 sits at the structural midpoint, where reader and protagonist take the gut-punch together — the reveal model the book is built around. Any planted details from the character bible are distributed across Act 1, with at most two per step and never two in the same scene.

> _For your book: define your Act-2 reveal in premise-and-twist.md._

---

# Act 1 — The Obvious Case

**1. Hook.** Open on the protagonist in motion, established as a distinct, slightly insufferable, or otherwise specific presence before the plot machinery starts. Introduce the supporting cast and the love interest (if any) with their own independent reasons for being here — reasons that exist before they have any narrative function in the mystery. The eventual "obvious victim" makes an entrance and starts collecting enemies on sight.
> _For your book: write the hook — who is the protagonist, where are they, and what first impression do they make? Seed your first planted detail here as texture, not investigation._

**2. Inciting Incident — The Body Is Found.** The crime is discovered. The protagonist's investigative role is triggered (a quest, a commission, a duty). The suspect pool snaps into shape: the obvious rivals, the jilted party, the debtor, the loud antagonist. Classic whodunit mode, fully engaged.
> _For your book: how is the crime discovered, and who are the obvious suspects? Plant one odd personal effect or anomaly here that the protagonist logs and moves past._

**3. Investigative Pairing — Protagonist Meets the Partner Properly.** The protagonist works the case in their trained mode; a supporting character's complementary skill is what mechanically justifies their catching what the protagonist misses. Someone opens up to the partner, not the protagonist — read it as a skill signal. The dynamic sets: one sees structure, the other sees people.
> _For your book: what does the protagonist see, and what does the partner see that they can't? Plant a detail here that the partner notices and the protagonist doesn't._

**4. False Trail — Building Toward the Wrong Solve.** Genre-appropriate set pieces and the protagonist showing off. They narrow in on the cleanest red herring on the suspect list. The reader thinks the book is about to solve.
> _For your book: who is the red herring, and what makes them look guilty? Drop the line that costs the protagonist later — an offhand remark by the partner the protagonist files under small talk and forgets. This is the sentence that comes back in step 6._

---

# Act 2 — The Fake-Out

**5. The Floor Drops.** The shared reveal. The protagonist and partner discover the first major reversal together; the reader learns at the same beat. The thing everyone assumed turns out to be false.
> _For your book: state the floor-drop reversal — the assumption that breaks here, discovered by reader and protagonist simultaneously._

**6. Reframe — The Corpse Has a Name.** The reframe that recontextualizes everything. The partner pushes the protagonist to confront the real human cost. The real victim is named on the page, and the protagonist uses that name from this point on. Any planted details retroactively belong to the truth — they rearrange into a person. The partner leads the recall passage: they name what they noticed, and the protagonist listens because they didn't see it and now they have to.
> _For your book: what is the reframe, and who is the real victim? Define the single offhand line (from step 4) that breaks the protagonist here — not a planted clue, but the moment they realize they weren't curious enough._
>
> _Image that closes the scene: choose one concrete object or gesture that carries the loss._

**7. The Humbling.** A character everyone misjudged squirms and turns useful — handing over the exposition about how the deception was arranged and the network behind it. Comic relief and exposition in one move. The book lets them be ridiculous and survive it.
> _For your book: who gets humbled and recruited, and what do they reveal?_

**8. Inversion — Suspect Pool Flips.** Everyone the protagonist cleared walks back in; everyone they dismissed becomes relevant. Former suspects become sources. The case restarts with less time on the clock and a harder problem than before.
> _For your book: how does the suspect pool invert, and what promise or stake does the partner lock in here?_

---

# Act 3 — The Real Case

**9. The Real Suspect Surfaces.** The true culprit comes into focus — someone quiet and methodical, visible only in the background of earlier scenes. They don't fit any of the patterns the protagonist was trained to spot, which is exactly why they were missed.
> _For your book: who is the real culprit, and why did the protagonist's training blind them to this person?_

**10. The Method Clicks.** The protagonist remembers a piece of theory or backstory they'd dismissed as irrelevant — useless in general, except for committing this exact crime. The setup planted early pays off mechanically. Their backstory was load-bearing all along.
> _For your book: what is the method, and what earlier-planted skill or knowledge lets the protagonist finally see it?_

**11. The Drawing Room — Reasoned Confrontation.** The protagonist lays it out in front of the assembled suspects, partner at their shoulder. The culprit doesn't deny it. They ask one question whose answer matters to them — and the answer ties back to the moral pivot of step 6. They go quietly.
> _For your book: stage the confrontation. What single question does the culprit ask, and how does its answer connect to the step-6 reframe?_

**12. Resolution & Denouement.** The case formally resolves; if there's an investigative-status mechanic, its framing flips to reveal what the real case always was. The partner registers the protagonist's reaction rather than the resolution itself. End on a final personal click for a supporting character, plus an optional book-two thread — noticed, set aside, unresolved.
> _For your book: how does the case resolve, what does the denouement reveal, and what thread do you leave open for the sequel?_

---

# Pacing Notes

Steps 1–4 should run roughly the first quarter of the book, 5–8 the second quarter through the midpoint, 9–11 the third quarter, 12 the final stretch including denouement.

**Step 6 is the structural and emotional hinge.** Protect the page count around it so the reframe beat and the recall passage have room to land. **Step 10 is the second-biggest beat** — don't bury the protagonist's recall in a single paragraph; let the theory click on the page so the setup pays off visibly.

---

# Notes on Plant Distribution

**Planted details aren't equal beats.** Some are discrete one-time plants; recurring ones are small moments meant to read as texture; the most loaded one may be a single sentence. Count textural beats, not "plants" — a handful of plants can spread across Act 1 as eight or nine small touches.

**The "don't cluster" rule operates at the scene level, not the step level.** Two plants in the same step are fine if they're in different scenes; two plants in the same scene is what hurts — the reader notices the oddities piling up in one sitting and the surprise softens. The formula assigns each plant to a step; the chapter breakdown handles per-scene pacing.

**Save the most emotionally loaded plant for last.** It should land closest to the reveal so it's freshest in memory when step 6 hits. Spread recurring textural plants evenly across the early steps to keep the texture present without pointing at it.

> _For your book: list your planted details and assign each to a step. Mark which is the most loaded (save for last) and which are recurring texture._
