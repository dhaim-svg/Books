## 2. Primary Anchor — [Your Positive Model Text]

> _Name the load-bearing positive reference for your prose voice. List the specific passages to re-read when returning to source. Then fill in the sub-sections below with what that model does well and how it ports to your book._

### 2.1 What to emulate

**POV distance.** Describe the distance your model holds between narrator and protagonist, and how it ports to your chosen POV. The lesson is usually that thought is rendered *in the moment*, *as character*, and *as data about the situation* simultaneously — never as a lecture.

**Sentence rhythm.** Describe your model's sentence-length pattern (e.g. medium sentences with deliberate short-sentence punctuation: think long, then snap short — the short sentence is the *recoil*). Action paragraphs run short with at least one very short sentence per beat; reflection paragraphs may run longer but never two long sentences back-to-back without a shorter one breaking the line.

**Interiority style.** The protagonist is allowed to be smart on the page, but intelligence is conveyed by **the speed and angle of the thinking**, not by stating what they know. **Show the protagonist deducing in scene, in dialogue, under social cost.** Never let the narrator step out and brief us on what the protagonist has worked out.

**Banter mechanics.** Three mechanics worth stealing from a strong banter model:
1. **The deflected compliment.** A character is praised; they immediately redirect to the praiser. This is the warmth engine — affection ricocheting.
2. **The straight-faced absurdity.** A serious line followed by a deadpan punchline of *internal* commentary. The narrator is the audience for the protagonist's own absurdity.
3. **The reciprocal undercut.** A nickname tolerated, later lampshaded, never explained. **Banter carries information that the characters won't say in plain speech.** Bake this into the protagonist and partner: every nickname, every formality dropped or kept, is plot.

**Warmth without saccharine.** The warmest moments should be the most concrete. Warmth in this voice = unsentimental concrete commitment + immediate self-deprecation. Never the inverse.

**Cozy-but-stakes balance.** High-stakes scenes can carry running wit; the jokes are how the character survives the stakes, not a way of undercutting them. The voice stays itself. Moral weight is carried by what the voice *notices*, not by the voice going solemn.

**Romance pacing (if applicable).** If there's a romance, pace it slow. The romantic scenes should not announce themselves as romance scenes — they are scenes about the case or the world, in which two people are quietly recognizing each other.

**Exposition.** Handle worldbuilding load through the protagonist **thinking about the implications of what just happened** rather than through narrator briefing. **Never explain a mechanic before it matters; explain it inside the moment the protagonist uses it.**

**Humor.** Humor lands when (a) the protagonist is the butt of some of it, (b) the supporting cast is allowed to be funny independently of the protagonist, and (c) **the humor never punches down at the intimacy of the scene.**

## 3. Contrast Anchor — [Your Negative Model Text]

> _Name the negative anchor: a text whose prose habits you refuse. List the moves to refuse, ideally as a two-column table mapping the bad move to your replacement move. Generic versions are below — keep the right column, replace the left with your specific anti-model._

### 3.1 The moves to refuse, and what we do instead

| Anti-model move | Our move |
|---|---|
| Summarizes entire conversations and maneuvers in 3–4-sentence narrator paragraphs. | Render conversations in real-time dialogue with beats of interiority, character-specific speech, and small physical business. Let the reader *be there.* |
| Introduces characters via inline epithet-dump ("It was [Name], the [Role], who spoke"). | Introduce characters through behavior and voice first; name and role land second, by inference. |
| Names emotions outright ("[Character] felt relieved," "[Character] was confused"). | Render emotion through specific physical sensation, displaced action, or texture of thought. |
| Adverb-and-verb-substitution dialogue tags ("said unwaveringly," "stomped angrily as he exclaimed"). | Predominantly invisible "said/asked." Tag color only for *unusual* beats, earned and rare. |
| Exposition by lecture: minor characters deliver paragraph-long worldbuilding monologues. | Drip-feed worldbuilding through observed detail and ambient overhearing. In a mystery, the clues *are* the worldbuilding. |
| Interrupts action with mid-scene calculation or technical aside. | Keep action sensory and present-tense in feel. Analysis happens after, in the still moment. |
| Frames romance transactionally from the first scene. | Let the love interest be a person before they are a resource. |
| Humor monopolized by the protagonist's modern-snark interior. | Distribute humor across the cast. The protagonist is sometimes the target of the joke. Comedy is observational, not anachronistic. |
| POV jumps from tagged interior thought to detached narrator summary inside one paragraph. | Maintain a consistent close third. Move into reflection or memory by free-indirect transition, never by a tag-and-step-back. |
| Sentences average long with monotone rhythm and conjunction-stacking ("and thus," "and hence," "and therefore"). | Vary sentence length deliberately. Use fragments. Let rhythm carry emotional tempo. |
| Recycles stock similes verbatim across description and recap. | Every image is fresh, specific, and *tied to the observer's particular angle of vision*. The image reveals the seer. |
| Supporting cast is foil or info-source; protagonist is the only thinker. | Side characters are competent in their own domains and capable of surprising the protagonist. The mystery is solvable in principle by the partner alone. |

## 4. Prose Mechanics Codification

### 4.1 Sentence-level rules

- **Action paragraphs:** average 12–18 words per sentence. Include at least one 4–7-word sentence per paragraph. No sentence over 28 words in active conflict.
- **Reflection paragraphs:** may average 18–24 words. Cap any one sentence at 35 words. No two 25+-word sentences in a row.
- **Discovery/reveal sentences** (the moment the protagonist sees the clue, the moment the partner corrects them): preferentially 5–12 words, often a fragment. The reveal is rhythmically distinct from its surround.
- **Conjunction discipline:** "and thus," "and hence," "and therefore," "moreover," "furthermore" are banned at sentence starts. Use "Then." Use "So." Use a period and a new sentence.
- **Em-dash quota:** maximum one em-dash per 250 words of prose. Em-dashes are a flavor, not a sauce.
- **Tricolon discipline:** maximum one rule-of-three construction per chapter, and only when all three elements are genuinely concrete and parallel. Never "the cold, the quiet, and the dawning realization" — that's the abstraction tic.
- **No "It was not just X, it was Y" sentence forms.** Ever. This is the single most identifiable AI-prose tic of 2024–2026.
- **Short-sentence chains (the register-clash rule):** Outside a deliberate staccato beat — a reveal, a hard stop, a moment of processing under pressure — do not run more than **two consecutive sentences of ≤6 words**. A clipped sequence must be resolved by a longer connector before the next clip. One-liner paragraphs (a single sentence of ≤6 words standing alone) are a precision tool; three or more in a row in a stage-direction context cost the *recoil effect* they should produce. _Exception:_ discovery/reaction beats and intentional rhetorical anaphora (see §5.4) may exceed this limit when the chain is carrying psychological weight, not procedural narration.
- **Sentence-opener variety:** No more than two consecutive sentences — or two consecutive one-sentence paragraphs — may begin with the same subject word: He, She, [protagonist name], [partner name], The, His, Her, They, It. Avoid runs of "He did X. He did Y. He did Z." or "She did not X. She did not Y. She did not Z." Vary with: object-first ("The notebook fell open."), adverbial lead ("Without waiting, he…"), subordinate clause ("When she looked up,…"), or by merging two beats into one compound sentence. This rule applies at both the action and the dialogue-tag level.

### 4.2 POV distance

Close-third on the protagonist, past tense. The narrator can hear the protagonist's thoughts as clearly as the protagonist can; the narrator never knows more than the protagonist does **except in deliberate, signposted POV-break interludes** (see §4.6).

- **Free indirect style is the default for thought.** No "he thought to himself" tags. The thought is allowed to sit in the same paragraph as the action, in the same voice.
- **Filtering verbs are minimized.** "He saw the body" → "The body lay where the lamp's light didn't quite reach." "He felt cold" → physical sensation, displaced object, or specific behavior.
- **One exception:** when the protagonist is being *deliberately stupid* — when the narrator wants us to see them misread a moment — we use a filter ("[CHARACTER] decided the silence was awkward") so the reader knows we're inside their interpretation, not the truth.

### 4.3 Dialogue tag economy

- Default: "said." Then: "asked." Both are invisible.
- Tag substitutions ("muttered," "snapped," "offered") are allowed only when the alternative would be *less* clear than the dialogue itself. Once per scene, not once per exchange.
- **No adverbial tags.** Not "said quietly." If quiet matters, render the quiet as an *action beat*: a half-smile, a glance at the door, a hand on the railing.
- **Action beats replace tags ~30% of the time.** They do double duty: identify the speaker, give us a body in the room, advance the scene's physical reality.

### 4.4 Scene openings and closings

- **Openings:** no weather. No "morning came." No scene-setting prologue. Start in motion — a small, specific, present action by the POV character (the protagonist handling a tool of their trade, a supporting character testing a door, a minor figure arguing about something domestic). The setting accretes around the action.
- **Closings:** no thematic summation, no "and then he knew everything would be different." End on either (a) a specific physical detail that troubles or settles the protagonist, (b) a line of dialogue with a question latent in it, or (c) a System note (used sparingly — see §5.5, if your book has a game layer).

### 4.5 Time compression

Compress between scenes via a **white line and a concrete temporal anchor** in the next scene's first sentence: "By second watch the body had been moved." Avoid "Hours passed." Avoid "Time crawled." Avoid montage paragraphs that summarize a sequence of activities — pick the one moment that mattered and write only that.

### 4.6 Setting

The setting is a world rendered through **texture and use** rather than description-dump. The reader should know a location only because a character walks across it, feels its cold, drops something on it. **Setting is a function of someone interacting with it.** A description that no character is using is dead description.

Any large genre element (a creature, a city, a machine) should appear in glimpses, sounds, the sway of a room, never in a paragraph that explains what it is or how it works. The reader can wait.

### 4.7 POV-break interludes

Brief third-person passages from another character's POV at structural hinge points. Use **one POV-break per act** at most, set off by a `* * *` divider, written in the same close-third voice but anchored to a different consciousness (the partner; a secondary figure; once, the culprit). These should reveal *interpretation*, never plot information the protagonist will later "discover." If the break tells the reader the answer, you've broken the mystery's fair-play contract.

## 5. Tonal Register Codification

> _Set your book's register here (this template's example was cozy LitRPG). The sub-sections below keep the STRUCTURE of a register definition; replace the cozy-LitRPG content with your own genre's equivalents._

### 5.1 Openings

An opening should have **small specific pleasures** as its sensory ground — concrete, domestic textures the POV character registers. Stakes can be present, even high, but the textures are specific and grounding. Choose a recurring small comfort that is "doing work" in your opening.

### 5.2 Banter cadence

A banter exchange between the protagonist and partner should follow this rhythm at least half the time:
1. The protagonist over-explains.
2. The partner responds with one short observation that is *more* accurate than the explanation.
3. The protagonist pauses (action beat, not a tag).
4. The protagonist either concedes or doubles down. Either is funny.

Avoid: tennis-match witticisms with no asymmetry. The humor isn't equal-weighted. **One character is verbose and clever; the other is brief and right.** That asymmetry is the engine.

### 5.3 Warmth without saccharine

Test for every warm moment: *does the warm beat contain something concrete and slightly inconvenient?* Warmth that *agrees* with the protagonist is sentimental. Warmth that *insists on the protagonist* — practical, sometimes unflattering, sometimes corrective — is real.

### 5.4 The moral-pivot scene (structural midpoint)

This is the load-bearing scene of the book — the step-6 reframe where the protagonist stops treating the victim as a puzzle piece and confronts them as a person.

**Voice rules for this passage specifically:**
- **No purple sensory cataloguing.** No "the air felt heavier." No "time seemed to slow." These are AI tics and grief clichés both.
- **No portentous fragments.** No "A man. A name. A life." Cut.
- **The pivot is carried by ONE specific, concrete recovery.** The protagonist learns *one* thing about the victim that is not relevant to the case, and the narrative voice slows by *exactly one beat* to let it land.
- **The partner triggers the pivot.** They name the victim first — not in a speech, in passing — and the protagonist's interior reaction is a single sentence. Possibly a fragment. Not three.
- **No therapy language.** No "humanize." No "honor the victim." No "see him as a person." These words must not appear in the prose. The work is done by the protagonist *using the victim's name without flinching* for the rest of the book.
- **Don't earn it twice.** This pivot is paid for in one scene. Returning to underline it would cheapen it. Subsequent chapters carry the change in vocabulary — the protagonist says the name where they used to say "the body" — and that's the whole signal.

### 5.5 Game / System screens (if your book has a game layer)

> _If your book has a game/System layer:_ The System is **diegetic**, present, sometimes funny, never a dashboard. If your book has no game layer, skip this section.

- **Frequency cap:** no more than four System interventions per chapter. In most chapters, two.
- **Form on the page:** indented block, italic, no decorative borders or ASCII boxes. Format:

> *Quest updated: A Death in the Cabin*
> *Status: One suspect cleared. Several remain.*

Or one-line in-line:

> *[Role — Insight +1. The way she held the knife was wrong.]*

- **Never the System narrating combat.** Never "+50 XP for successful deduction!" celebration text. The System is laconic, slightly dry, and observes the protagonist the way the protagonist observes others. It has the same voice register as the rest of the prose.
- **The System notices what the protagonist is not noticing.** Used this way it is *characterization* — what they overlook, what the System catches, the gap between them. The gap is the gag and sometimes the clue.
- **Level-ups are quiet.** A single italic line. No fanfare. No stat sheet inserted into the chapter end. The character reaction is the scene, not the numbers.
- **No status sheet recaps at chapter breaks.** Ever. This is the early-series dashboard tic to avoid.
- **The quest log is referenced, never reproduced.** The protagonist "checks the log"; we get one line of what's on it. Never a bulleted dump.

### 5.6 The slow-burn romance (if applicable)

A romance built on **shared moral instinct**, not attraction. The partner pushes the protagonist toward the right thing; this is the romance, structurally. Therefore:

- **No "their eyes met across the room."** No heart-pounding, no breath-catching.
- **Physical awareness, when it appears, is oblique and specific.** The protagonist notices that the partner's hands are steadier than theirs at a hard moment — and the observation tells us about *the protagonist*.
- **Mutual respect precedes mutual attraction by approximately one entire book.** First book: they become each other's necessary thinking partner. Anything more is a glance, a "should I" deflected, a brief held-look that breaks.
- **The almost-moment, if used, must be earned by a moral beat.** Not an interruption. A *decision*.

### 5.7 Serious beats in a light book

When a serious beat lands, the prose does NOT switch into "serious mode." It stays in voice. The seriousness is conveyed by **what the voice elects to look at** and **how long it stays**. Slow the camera. Don't change the lens.

## 6. Anti-Slop Constraints

### 6.1 Generic LLM tics — DO NOT / INSTEAD DO

| DO NOT | INSTEAD DO |
|---|---|
| Use "shimmer," "tapestry," "realm," "beacon," "cacophony," "delve," "underscore," "intricate," "multifaceted." | Use specific nouns. A "shimmer" is light on something — say what. A "tapestry" is a metaphor for variety — pick a concrete variety. |
| Open chapters with weather, time-of-day abstraction, or scene-setting prologue. | Open with a small, specific action by the protagonist. The world accretes from the action. |
| "It's not just X, it's Y." "Not merely X but Y." Any antithesis-of-elevation construction. | Make the point once, with the more accurate word. If both X and Y are needed, write two sentences. |
| Triplet escalation — "rule of three with escalating abstraction" ("a man, a name, a memory"). | At most one tricolon per chapter, all three terms concrete and parallel. |
| "A testament to," "in the heart of," "speaks volumes," "stands as a reminder," "begs the question." | Cut. These add ceremony, not meaning. |
| Body-language clichés: heart pounding, breath caught, blood ran cold, stomach dropped, knuckles white. | One physical sensation per scene, and it must be unusual or specific to the character (a thumb finding the worn place on a tool; a steady hand that forgets what to do with itself and reaches for a pencil). |
| Purple sensory cataloguing ("the air smelled of brass and ozone and the faint copper tang of fear"). | One sense, one detail, well-placed. The reader supplies the rest. |
| Falsely portentous fragments: "A life. A name. A choice." | Use a fragment sparingly, and only when a full sentence would over-explain. |
| Em-dash maximalism — two or more em-dashes per paragraph. | One em-dash per 250 words at most. |
| "In today's…", "In a world where…", "Throughout history…", "Time and time again…" | Begin in scene, in action, in a specific moment. |
| Hedging stock phrases: "arguably," "to some extent," "broadly speaking," "in many ways." | Commit to the claim or cut the claim. |
| Vague stakes language: "everything would change," "nothing would ever be the same." | Name the specific change. A concrete stake ("by breakfast he would have to choose between the partner and the truth") beats "everything would change." |
| Curly quotes that look "intentional" combined with conspicuous em-dashes (the 2025 AI-detection markers). | Use the punctuation conventions of the project's style sheet consistently; do not load punctuation with mood. |
| Closing chapters with a thematic gloss ("And in that moment, he understood that justice was not a verdict but a vigil"). | Close on a concrete detail, a held question, or one laconic System line. |
| The word "delve." | Don't. |

### 6.2 Genre-machinery slop — DO NOT / INSTEAD DO (if your book has a game/System layer)

> _If your book has a game/System layer, keep this table; otherwise delete it._

| DO NOT | INSTEAD DO |
|---|---|
| Status-screen spam (the early-series dashboard tic). | Hard cap of four System interventions per chapter; no recap sheets at chapter ends. |
| "A screen appeared before him." "The System pinged." "A notification flashed in his vision." | A single italic indented line in the System voice. No fanfare. |
| Tutorial-voice info dumps ("The [Class] allows the user to…"). | The class is revealed by use. The reader infers the rules. If a rule must be stated, the protagonist states it in dialogue, under pressure, because someone asked. |
| Mechanical level-up celebrations: stat increases listed, points to spend, prompts to allocate. | A level-up is one italic line. The reaction is half a sentence of free indirect. We do not stop to allocate. |
| Redundant stat descriptions ("His Strength was now 14, an increase from 12") in narration. | Narrate consequence, not number. "He could lift the cabinet now. Yesterday it would have taken both of them." |
| Power-fantasy floskeln: "the world would tremble," "his power was unmatched," "they would learn to fear him." | The protagonist is fallible and occasionally wrong. The voice never flatters them. |
| Translation patois: "and thus," "and hence," wooden honorifics, stiff exposition, repetitive epithets. | English-native rhythm. Honorifics dropped where natural. Epithets earned and varied. |
| "X said unwaveringly" / "Y exclaimed angrily" — adverbial-tag stacking. | "Said." "Asked." Action beats. |
| Quest logs reproduced in full at scene transitions. | A single line referencing what's on the log. The log is a tool, not content. |
| The protagonist commenting on game mechanics with knowing irony ("Of course it was a fetch quest"). | The world is real to the people in it. The protagonist does not wink at the System; the System winks at the protagonist. |
| Combat narrated by the System ("Critical hit! +50 XP!"). | Combat narrated by the body and the senses. The System notes the outcome afterward, briefly, dryly. |

## 8. Quick-Reference Checklist (one page, keep visible while writing)

Before drafting a chapter, set timer. After drafting a chapter, run this.

**Voice**
- [ ] Close third on the protagonist. No filtering verbs except where deliberately ironic.
- [ ] The protagonist is allowed to be smart. They are not allowed to lecture. If they're lecturing, the partner interrupts.
- [ ] At least one moment where the protagonist is wrong, and the prose doesn't protect them from it.

**Sentence-level**
- [ ] Action paragraphs: 12–18 word average, at least one short sentence per beat.
- [ ] No sentence opens with "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Consequently," "And thus," "And hence."
- [ ] No "It's not X, it's Y" constructions. Zero.
- [ ] Max one em-dash per 250 words.
- [ ] Max one tricolon per chapter, all terms concrete.
- [ ] Banned word check: shimmer, tapestry, realm, beacon, cacophony, delve, underscore, intricate, multifaceted, testament, in the heart of.
- [ ] **Short-sentence chain check:** No more than 2 consecutive sentences ≤6 words in a stage-direction or transition context. If 3+ short sentences in a row, at least one must be merged or given an adverbial/object-first opener. (Discovery beats and intentional rhetorical anaphora are exempt — see §4.1.)
- [ ] **Opener variety check:** No 3+ consecutive sentences/paragraphs opening with the same subject word (He / She / The / [protagonist] / [partner]). Vary opener or merge the beat.

**Dialogue**
- [ ] "Said" / "asked" dominate. No adverbial tags.
- [ ] Action beats replacing tags about 30% of the time.
- [ ] No tennis-match banter. One character over-explains; the other is briefer and more correct.

**Scene**
- [ ] Opens on small specific action, not weather or scene-setting.
- [ ] Closes on concrete detail, latent question, or one laconic System line — never thematic gloss.
- [ ] Setting rendered through someone using it, not described.

**Mystery / Fair Play**
- [ ] Every clue the protagonist sees, the reader sees, in the same chapter, in plain text.
- [ ] Every red herring is eventually explained.
- [ ] No "I had already deduced…" cheat in narration.

**Game / System layer (if applicable)**
- [ ] Max four System interventions in this chapter.
- [ ] No status sheet recap at chapter end.
- [ ] System voice: laconic, dry, sometimes observes the protagonist's blind spots.
- [ ] No tutorial paragraph explaining a class or skill.
- [ ] Level-ups: one italic line, half-sentence reaction, no allocation scene.

**Cozy-but-stakes (adjust to your register)**
- [ ] At least one warm, concrete sensory detail per scene (a cup of tea, a worn glove, the give of a seat).
- [ ] If this chapter touches the victim's death: their name appears at least once, in plain prose, without commentary.

**Romance (if applicable)**
- [ ] No "their eyes met." No heart-pounding. No breath-catching.
- [ ] If there's a romantic beat, it is built on a shared moral observation, not on physical proximity.
- [ ] The partner is competent in a way the protagonist isn't; that gap is visible in this chapter.

**Anti-AI check**
- [ ] Read three random sentences aloud. Do they sound like the writer, or like prose that would pass a content-mill quality bar?
- [ ] Is there at least one image in this chapter that nobody else would have written?
