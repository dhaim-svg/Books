# The Skybound Wyrm — Character Profiles
*Cozy LitRPG Murder Mystery · Story Bible Entry*

**Reveal model:** Reader and prodigy learn together. The cast is built around that — the love interest catches the *human* details, the prodigy catches the *technical* ones, and they hit the reveal as one shared gut-punch. Names below are placeholders, swap freely.

---

# Theron Mavik — The Protagonist

**Class:** Investigator (freshly assigned at graduation) · **Age:** 21  
**Origin:** Middle-class — son of a town apothecary, got into the Royal Conservatory of Magic on a merit scholarship

Brilliant, slightly insufferable, all theory and no practice. He sees structure where others see chaos, but he sees roles before he sees people. The book is partly his lesson in the second.

Clipped, precise, prone to citing textbooks. Drops his guard around Sable in small ways neither of them quite notice.

His blindspot — investigating "the lord" without questioning whether the corpse was the lord — is the engine of the twist. His academy training is the lockpick on the case.

**Carry-through detail:** a graduation pin he keeps tugging at. He hasn't earned the right to be relaxed about it yet.

**The moment that costs him later:** when Sable mentions in passing that "Vannic" wasn't as bad as she expected, he doesn't register it. Filed under *small talk* and lost. In the recall passage after the reveal, that's the sentence that comes back and breaks something in him — not because he was cruel to ignore it, but because he wasn't curious enough to ask why she'd said it.

---

# Sable Verrin — The Love Interest

**Class:** Witness (rare social-cognition class — reads people, not minds) · **Age:** 23  
**Origin:** Former courier for a magistrate's office. Self-taught observer. No academy.

Sees people clearly. Everyone — including the staff, including the corpse. She's on the gondola for her own reason (a small inheritance she's traveling to claim) — establish that early so she's not narratively reduced to "romantic foil."

Dry, warm, a little teasing. Lets him be brilliant without flinching, but does not let him be careless.

**What she carries before the reveal:** Sable is the one who clocks the human details Theron misses. She catches the accent slip during a meal and lets it go because correcting a lord at dinner is rude. She catches the kindnesses — the steward thanked by name, the ginger sweet sent to an airsick child — and tucks them away because she's been trained, like most women are, to read the gap between what men present and what they actually do. She catches the tone shift when "Vannic" speaks about someone with unexpected softness. None of these land for her as suspicion at the time. They land as *this man is not quite who he's pretending to be*, filed without urgency.

**What she carries after the reveal:** she was the only person on the gondola who saw Joren — even a little — while he was alive. That matters to her. The recall passage in the cabin is hers to lead: she breaks the silence, names what she noticed, and Theron listens, because he didn't see it, and now he has to. The romance moves forward in that scene more than it does in any banter.

**The promise she makes before the gondola lands:** someone has to tell Joren's family properly. Not a magistrate's letter. A person. Her cottage is in the same coastal region Joren came from, so she's the natural one to do it — a fact neither she nor Theron realizes until after the case is closed and she sees the address on the inheritance papers again. This is the bridge into book two.

She's the one who names Joren out loud first. The romance pivot at the end isn't her seeing his System screen — she doesn't see it. She sees his face while he reads it.

---

# Joren Aldis — The Real Victim

**Class:** Illusionist (working mage, no formal credentials) · **Age:** 34  
**Origin:** Coastal fishing town. Self-taught, then apprenticed to a stage magician, then drifted into less savory contract work to keep his sister's children fed.

A man who knew exactly what he was, didn't lie to himself about it, and was trying — slowly, imperfectly — to get out. Took the Vannic impersonation job because it paid enough to retire on. Was going to use it to do that.

**His emotional center: his niece.** Sister's daughter, eight years old, lives with her mother in the same fishing town he came from. The work was always for her. The retirement plan was so he could go home and be present for the rest of her childhood. She's the person he speaks of with softness on the gondola, and she's the one who carved the fish he carries.

**Five details to plant before the reveal, all attributed to "Vannic":**

1. Calloused right hand — odd for a lord; obvious for someone who casts ten hours a day. Theron registers it at the boarding handshake and files it as a noble who dabbles.
2. A faint coastal accent that slips through twice when he's tired. Sable catches it during a meal, almost mentions it, lets it go because correcting a lord at dinner is rude.
3. A small carved wooden fish, found in his pocket when the body is examined — not in the luggage. Theron logs it as an odd personal effect and moves on. In the recall passage, the fact that he had it on him — reached for it before he died, or never let go of it at all — is the image that closes the scene. This is the detail that breaks Theron a little when he understands.
4. The kindnesses that didn't fit. "Vannic" thanks the steward by name. Notices a child is airsick and quietly sends over a ginger sweet from his own tray. Steps aside for an elderly passenger in a corridor without making it a performance. Sable clocks each one immediately — that's not how that kind of man moves through the world. Post-reveal: those weren't a lord's lapses in character. They were the illusionist forgetting to stay in character, being himself briefly, because kindness was his default and the disguise was the effort.
5. The softness when he speaks of his niece. One sentence at a meal, easily missed. Sable catches the tone shift; Theron catches the words. Post-reveal: that child is real, and her mother is somewhere right now not knowing he's dead. Sable is the one who insists, before the gondola lands, that someone has to tell the family in person.

After the reveal these five details retroactively belong to a real person, and the reader feels the loss because they already met him without knowing.

**Deployment note:** don't cluster the details. One per chapter in the early section, woven into other business — a meal scene, a corridor encounter, the search of the cabin after the body is found. The reader should pass each one without stopping. They come back together only after the Act 2 reveal, in a quiet recall passage where Sable and Theron sit in the cabin and the details rearrange themselves into a person. That's where the *he had a name* line is earned. Don't rush it.

# Lord Petric Vannic — The Fake-Out

**Age:** 28 · **Running from:** A forced betrothal to a duke's daughter twice his age

Recommended over the gambling-debt option — keeps him sympathetic and low-stakes; the comic relief lands cleaner.

Loud, brittle, secretly terrified. Hired Joren because he genuinely couldn't think of anything else. Hiding in third class under the name "Petor Vann" — he is not a creative man.

Pre-reveal: all bluster. Post-reveal: deflated and oddly likeable, like a peacock in the rain. In Act 3 he gives Theron and Sable the contract details that help trace the killer. He doesn't get redeemed; he gets humanized. The book lets him be ridiculous and survive it.

---

# Halsa Renn — The Killer

**Class:** Illusionist (former apprentice of Joren's, now working alone) · **Age:** 29  
**Booked under:** A forged identity — using papers from the same network Joren once introduced her to

Joren took a contract eight years ago that left her younger brother dead. Collateral, not target. Joren never knew, or said he didn't. She spent the years since becoming exactly what he was, working her way close to his network until a chance came where his death wouldn't simply be picked up and finished by another contractor. The impersonation gig was that chance: a closed cabin, no witnesses, no contract chain.

**Method:** A sustained scryproof glamour exploit — niche illusion theory from Theron's final year, dismissed as exam fodder because it has no practical application. Except this one.

She is grieving, methodical, not cruel. When Theron names her, she doesn't deny it. She asks whether anyone used Joren's real name during the investigation. The answer is yes — Sable, halfway through Act 2. That answer matters to her. Then she goes quietly.

# Supporting Cast

### Madam Esherel
A merchant who loudly feuded with "Vannic" over a contract. Cleanest red herring in Act 1, fully cleared by the reveal. In Act 3 she becomes a surprisingly good source on Joren — she actually noticed him as a person, because that's how she does business.

### Calden, the Steward
Cabin staff. Knows everything that happens on the gondola but is bound by service discretion. Opens up to Sable, not Theron — read it as a class signal. Not a suspect; a key informant.

### Dr. Vellaine
A scholar on her way to a symposium. Plausible expert in early acts. Not the killer, but she remembers Theron's thesis and watches him with academic interest. Optional setup for a book-two mentor.

### Wisp, the Dragon Handler
Not a suspect. Comic relief and atmosphere. Has names for each of the three drakes pulling the gondola and treats them like difficult colleagues. Carries a lot of the book's texture.

# Locked Decisions

## Sable's Reason for Traveling

She's on her way to claim a small cottage left to her by a great-aunt she barely knew. Sign papers, decide to keep or sell, take a holiday on the coast while she's there. That's all the book-one reader needs.

**The hidden geography:** the cottage is in Joren's home region — the same coastal fishing-town stretch he came from. Sable doesn't know that. The reader doesn't either. When Joren's coastal accent slips through in Act 2, the reader doesn't connect it; geography is just geography.

**The book-two hum:** after the gondola lands, Sable goes to claim the cottage and walks into the same region Joren ran from. His sister still lives there. His niece — the one who carved the fish — still lives there. Halsa's old contracts trace back through the same network. Theron either goes with her or chooses not to, and that choice *is* the romance plot of book two.

**The funny layer:** Sable is grumpy about the trip. Inheritances are paperwork and guilt and aunts you didn't visit. From her perspective, the magical murder mystery may have been less inconvenient than the errand waiting at the other end.

**The mysterious layer (optional, can stack):** the great-aunt left a sealed letter to be opened *only with a witness present*. Sable assumed it was a notary formality. It wasn't. The aunt knew about Sable's class — and knew she'd grow into it. Hook for later, no payoff required in book one.

---

## Halsa's Brother — The Contract

Joren took the work willingly, with a specific shape to his complicity: he took it knowing it would be ugly; he just didn't know how ugly, and he chose not to find out afterward.

**The contract:** a "scare job" eight years ago. Frighten a debtor into paying. Magical intimidation, no killing intended. Joren took it knowing scare jobs sometimes spilled — he'd seen it before — and accepted the risk because his sister's children needed feeding.

**What spilled:** in the spellwork's confusion, Halsa's younger brother — a teenager, not the debtor, just trying to protect his father — was caught and killed. Joren wasn't on-site for it. He heard later that the job had "gone messy." He took the rest of his fee anyway. He didn't ask whose blood was in it.

**What this earns Halsa:** Joren chose the work. He chose not to look. He carried general guilt without ever facing the specific cost. Real grievance, the reader feels it. She spent eight years doing what he refused to do — looking at the outcome.

**What it withholds:** he didn't target her brother. He didn't know him. He was actively trying to leave the work; the Vannic gig was meant to be his last. She isn't avenging a man who deserved death. She's avenging one who deserved to *know what he'd done* — and chose, every day for eight years, not to.

**Why her final question lands:** she asks whether anyone used Joren's real name during the investigation because she wants to know whether anyone in his last hours saw him as a person. He never did that for her brother. The answer being yes — Sable, in Act 2 — is what lets her go quietly. Not forgiveness. Acknowledgment.

Sympathy without exoneration. She's tragic; she's also a murderer. The book honors both.
