# The Skybound Wyrm — 12-Step Mystery Formula
*Cozy LitRPG Murder Mystery · Story Bible Entry · Revision 2*

Three acts, four steps each. Step 6 sits at the structural midpoint, where reader and Theron take the gut-punch together — the reveal model the book is built around. The five planted details from the character bible are distributed across Act 1, with at most two per step and never two in the same scene.

---

# Act 1 — The Obvious Murder

**1. Hook — Boarding the Wyrm.** Theron boards alone, slightly insufferable, tugging at his graduation pin. Wisp and the three drakes carry the texture. Sable boards with her own reason for traveling — a small inheritance, a cottage on the coast she's grumpy about going to claim — established before she has any narrative function in the mystery. "Vannic" makes a loud entrance and starts collecting enemies on sight. **Plant: calluses.** Theron registers a working mage's ridge on the side of "Vannic's" index finger at the boarding handshake and files it as a noble who dabbles. **Plant: first kindness.** "Vannic" thanks the steward by name as he's shown to his cabin. Sable clocks it without comment.

**2. Inciting Incident — Locked Cabin, Dead Lord.** The body is found. Theron's Investigator class pings with the displayed quest title *Solve the Death Aboard the Skybound Wyrm.* The suspect pool snaps into shape: rival nobles, jilted mistress, debt collector, Madam Esherel. Christie mode, fully engaged. **Plant: carved fish.** During the body examination, Theron logs a small wooden fish in the corpse's pocket — not in the luggage — as *odd personal effect* and moves on.

**3. Investigative Pairing — Theron Meets Sable Properly.** Theron works the case in academy mode; Sable's Witness class — rare social-cognition, reads people not minds — is what mechanically justifies her catching what he misses about the staff and cheaper passengers. Calden the steward opens up to her, not him; read it as a class signal. The dynamic sets: he sees structure, she sees people. **Plant: accent slip.** During a shared meal, Sable catches a faint coastal lilt in "Vannic's" speech, almost mentions it, lets it go because correcting a lord at dinner is rude. **Plant: second kindness.** "Vannic" notices a child two tables over is airsick and quietly sends a ginger sweet from his own tray. Sable files it under *not how that kind of man moves through the world.*

**4. False Trail — Building Toward the Wrong Solve.** Cozy set pieces: dragon-flight banter, magical clues, Esherel's loud feud, Theron showing off. He narrows in on Esherel as a likely culprit — the cleanest red herring on the manifest. Reader thinks the book is about to solve. **Plant: the niece-softness.** One sentence at a meal, "Vannic" mentions someone with unexpected softness in his voice. Sable catches the tone shift; Theron catches the words. **Plant: third kindness.** "Vannic" steps aside for an elderly passenger in a corridor without making a performance of it. **The line that costs Theron later:** Sable mentions in a passing aside that "Vannic wasn't as bad as I expected." Theron files it under small talk and forgets it. This is the sentence that will come back in step 6.

---

# Act 2 — The Fake-Out

**5. The Floor Drops — Vannic Is Alive.** The shared reveal. Theron and Sable find "Petor Vann" together in third class; the reader learns at the same beat. He is Lord Petric Vannic, alive, hiding from a forced betrothal to a duke's daughter twice his age. The corpse is not his.

**6. Reframe — The Corpse Has a Name.** The corpse was a hired illusionist. Sable pushes: *he had a name. Use it.* Joren Aldis is named on the page, and Theron uses the name from this point on. The five planted details retroactively belong to him — the calluses are a working mage's, the accent is from a coastal fishing town, the fish was carved by his eight-year-old niece, the kindnesses were the illusionist forgetting to stay in character, the soft sentence was about the niece. Sable leads the recall passage: she names what she noticed, including the kindnesses, and Theron listens because he didn't see it and now he has to. **The line that breaks Theron** isn't any of the planted details — it's Sable's offhand "he wasn't as bad as I expected" coming back to him, because he wasn't curious enough to ask why she'd said it. The reader, having met Joren without knowing, takes the loss with him. **Image that closes the scene:** the carved fish in his hand or pocket — he reached for it before he died, or never let go of it at all.

**7. Vannic Humbled, Recruited.** Peacock-in-the-rain beat. Petric squirms, he's useful — hands over the contract details about how the swap was arranged and the network Joren came from. Comic relief and exposition in one move. The book lets him be ridiculous and survive it.

**8. Inversion — Suspect Pool Flips.** Everyone Theron cleared walks out. Everyone he dismissed walks back in. Esherel becomes a source — she actually noticed Joren as a person, because that's how she does business. Calden gives Sable the third-class manifest. **Sable's promise lands here:** someone has to tell Joren's family properly, in person, before any magistrate's letter reaches them. The case restarts with less time on the clock and a less experienced detective for the work that actually matters.

---

# Act 3 — The Real Case

**9. The Real Suspect Surfaces.** Halsa Renn comes into focus. Joren's former apprentice, now working alone, booked under a forged identity sourced from the same network Joren once introduced her to. Quiet, methodical, until now visible only in the background of earlier scenes. She doesn't fit any of the noble-rivalry patterns Theron was trained to spot — which is exactly why he didn't see her.

**10. Academy Theory Clicks — The Method.** Theron remembers the final-year theory he dismissed as exam fodder: the sustained scryproof glamour exploit. Useless in practice — except for killing someone in a sealed cabin without a trace, by an illusionist, against another illusionist who'd recognize any lesser approach. The graduation-with-flying-colors setup pays off mechanically. His backstory was load-bearing all along.

**11. The Drawing Room — Reasoned Confrontation.** Theron lays it out in front of the assembled passengers, Sable at his shoulder. Halsa doesn't deny it. She asks one question: did anyone use Joren's real name during the investigation? The answer is yes — Sable, in step 6. That answer matters to her. She goes quietly. The gondola lands; she's handed over at port.

**12. The System Knew — Quest Resolution.** The quest completes and the title flips on the screen: it was always *Solve the Death of Joren Aldis.* Investigator levels up with the flavor line *seeing the unseen.* Sable doesn't see the screen — she sees Theron's face while he reads it, and her feelings tip from interested to certain. **Denouement — the geographic click:** Sable looks at her inheritance papers again and recognizes the address. Her cottage is in Joren's home region, the same coastal stretch the accent slipped from. She is the one who'll tell the family. End on the optional book-two thread (a sigil or contract fragment among Joren's effects), noticed, set aside, unresolved. They go on holiday.

---

# Pacing Notes

Steps 1–4 should run roughly the first quarter of the book, 5–8 the second quarter through the midpoint, 9–11 the third quarter, 12 the final stretch including denouement.

**Step 6 is the structural and emotional hinge.** Protect the page count around it so the *he had a name* beat and the recall passage have room to land. **Step 10 is the second-biggest beat** — don't bury Theron's recall in a single paragraph; let the academy theory click on the page so the prodigy setup pays off visibly.

---

# Notes on Plant Distribution

**The five planted details aren't five equal beats.** Three are discrete one-time plants (calluses, accent slip, carved fish), the kindnesses are three small recurring moments meant to read as texture, and the niece-softness is one sentence. That's roughly eight to nine textural beats across Act 1, not five plants.

**The "don't cluster" rule operates at the scene level, not the step level.** Two plants in the same step are fine if they're in different scenes; two plants in the same scene is what hurts — the reader notices "Vannic" doing odd things in one sitting and the surprise softens. The formula assigns each plant to a step; the chapter breakdown handles per-scene pacing.

**Save the niece-softness for last.** It's the most emotionally loaded plant and it should land closest to the reveal so it's freshest in memory when step 6 hits. The kindnesses spread evenly across steps 1, 3, and 4 keep the texture present without pointing at it.
