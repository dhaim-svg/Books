# The Skybound Wyrm — Brand Voice & Brain-and-Voice

A persistent voice anchor for the cozy LitRPG murder-mystery novel *The Skybound Wyrm*. Read this before drafting any chapter. When a sentence feels wrong, the answer is usually in here.

---

## 1. Executive Summary

*The Skybound Wyrm* is written in **close-third past tense**, anchored to Theron Mavik, in the prose register of a translated Japanese light novel — specifically *Accomplishments of the Duke's Daughter Vol. 1* by Reia (Seven Seas Entertainment English edition, ISBN 978-1-64827-421-3, July 2021): a warm, slightly arch narrative voice that lets a clever protagonist think on the page without lecturing, and that lets warmth, banter, and moral weight share the same paragraph. The tone is **cozy-but-not-light**: beach-read pacing and texture, but one death that must be felt as a death, not as a puzzle piece. LitRPG mechanics (Investigator class, quest log, System screens) are real and visible but **always subordinated to character and scene** — they appear as moments of perception, never as dashboards. The anti-model is the prose habits of fan-translated Chinese web fiction such as *Release That Witch* by Er Mu: long, conjunction-stacked sentences; named emotions; narrator-summarized scenes; transactional romance; epithet-tag character introductions; humor monopolized by the protagonist's modern-snark interior. Where Duke's Daughter says *show me Iris noticing*, RTW says *Roland felt relieved*. We write the first kind.

## 2. Primary Anchor — *Accomplishments of the Duke's Daughter Vol. 1* by Reia (Seven Seas, 2021)

The user-supplied PDF (Drive ID `10dM-vyZ7vrClUtaHz8jKYnMsz6-KWCFw`) is the load-bearing reference. Read passages in this order when returning to source: Chapter 1 opening trial scene; Chapter 1 confrontation with Duke Louis; the Tanya scene ("I serve you, not House Armelia"); Chapter 2 carriage trip and the southern village; Chapter 2 inn-corridor scene with Dida.

### 2.1 What to emulate

**POV distance.** First-person in source, but the *distance* is the lesson, and it ports directly to close-third. Iris narrates her own thoughts as if half-amused by herself: she diagnoses her own emotional state in real time, often mid-action, but always with a beat of self-deprecation that prevents the interiority from feeling pompous. Port this to Theron: a close-third narrator who can think a paragraph but knows when the paragraph is becoming a lecture and cuts itself off. Compare Iris's mid-trial aside — *"If I weren't on trial, I'd have loved to study his beautiful face for hours"* (Reia, Vol. 1, Ch. 1, 13 words) — for the move. Thought is rendered *in the moment*, *as comedy*, *as character*, and *as data about the situation* simultaneously.

**Sentence rhythm.** Reia's translated prose runs in 12–22-word sentences with **deliberate short-sentence punctuation**. Iris thinks long, then snaps short. Example pattern: a 24-word self-justification followed by a 4-word self-rebuke ("Nailed it. Ah, that feels good."). The short sentence is the *recoil*. Use this. Action paragraphs should average 12–18 words per sentence with at least one 4–7-word sentence per beat. Reflection paragraphs may run 20–28 words per sentence but never two such sentences back-to-back without a shorter one breaking the line.

**Interiority style.** Iris is allowed to be smart on the page, but her intelligence is conveyed by **the speed and angle of her thinking**, not by stating what she knows. When she deduces her father's motives in Chapter 1, the deduction is laid out in dialogue *with him*, not in narrator summary — which means the reader watches her think under pressure, watches her be right, and watches her father react. This is the master move and the central anti-pattern to RTW. **Show Theron deducing in scene, in dialogue, under social cost.** Never let the narrator step out and brief us on what Theron has worked out.

**Banter mechanics.** Three Duke's Daughter mechanics to steal:
1. **The deflected compliment.** A character is praised; they immediately redirect to the praiser ("I'm the lucky one here"). Tanya does this; Dida does the inverse. This is the warmth engine — affection ricocheting.
2. **The straight-faced absurdity.** A serious line followed by a deadpan punchline of *internal* commentary ("Seriously, why did I love him, even for a second? Ugh."). The narrator is the audience for the protagonist's own absurdity.
3. **The reciprocal undercut.** Dida calls Iris "Princess"; she tolerates it; later she lampshades it; he refuses to explain it; Tanya later reveals he meant it sincerely. **Banter carries information that the characters won't say in plain speech.** Bake this into Theron & Sable: every nickname, every formality dropped or kept, is plot.

**Warmth without saccharine.** Reia's warmest moments are the most concrete: Tanya saying *"The day I leave your side will be the day I die…or the day you deem me unnecessary"* (Reia, Vol. 1, Ch. 1, 17 words) lands because it's an absolute — no qualifier, no softening — *and* because Iris immediately makes a small joke to deflate her own emotion. Warmth in this voice = unsentimental concrete commitment + immediate self-deprecation. Never the inverse.

**Cozy-but-stakes balance.** The Chapter 1 trial scene contains exile, public humiliation, the end of an engagement, and the destruction of a noble bloodline's reputation — and Iris narrates it with running internal jokes about dating-sim tropes. The stakes are NOT undercut by the jokes; the jokes are how this character survives the stakes. **Apply directly to the body of Joren Aldis.** Theron's wit does not retreat in the presence of the corpse. It changes register — sharper, drier, slower — but it does not vanish, because if it vanished the reader would feel the author trying for Significance. The voice stays itself. The moral weight is carried by what the voice *notices*, not by the voice going solemn.

**Romance pacing.** Reia paces Iris's eventual romance over multiple volumes; in Volume 1 there is essentially **no romance arc, only the establishment of mutual respect, mutual ribbing, and one quiet inn-corridor scene at night** (Iris and Dida, midpoint of Ch. 2). The scene is romantic in its texture but the characters are not romantic about it: they talk about danger, slums, regret. Steal the structure: **the romance scenes in Skybound Wyrm should not announce themselves as romance scenes.** They are scenes about the case, about a dead man, about a class system, in which two people are quietly recognizing each other.

**Exposition.** Reia handles a massive worldbuilding load — succession politics, dating-sim metafiction, noble hierarchies — almost entirely through Iris **thinking about the implications of what just happened** rather than through narrator briefing. The "scandal" that opens the book is explained inside Iris's reaction to it, not before it. Apply to Skybound: **never explain a System mechanic before it matters; explain it inside the moment Theron uses it.**

**Humor.** Reia's humor lands because (a) Iris is the butt of half of it ("Ugh, where had that resolve gone? My stomach was all in knots."), (b) the supporting cast is allowed to be funny independently of the protagonist (Dida's deadpan, Sebastian's dryness, even Louis's villain-laugh moment), and (c) **the humor never punches down at the cozy intimacy of the scene**. No sarcasm at the expense of warmth.

### 2.2 Two short attributed quotes (under 20 words each, fair-use minimum)

- *"Love is a fever. Once it cools, that's it."* — Iris, *Accomplishments of the Duke's Daughter Vol. 1*, Ch. 1 (Reia, Seven Seas Entertainment English edition, 2021). 10 words. Note the rhythm: aphorism, full stop, four-beat coda. This is the unit of the voice.
- *"Time flies when you're having fun, I suppose."* — Iris, same source, Ch. 1. 8 words. The qualifier "I suppose" is doing all the work — without it, this is a cliché; with it, it's a character.

## 3. Contrast Anchor — *Release That Witch* by Er Mu, Ch. 1 "Becoming a Prince" and surrounding early chapters

The translated English text accessed at webnovel.com (and corroborated across novelfull, wuxiaworld.eu, novelhi) is the negative anchor: a list of moves to refuse.

### 3.1 What RTW does, and what we do instead

| RTW move | Skybound move |
|---|---|
| Summarizes entire conversations and political maneuvers in 3–4-sentence narrator paragraphs (e.g., a fake execution staged in three sentences in Ch. 5). | Render conversations in real-time dialogue with beats of interiority, character-specific speech, and small physical business. Let the reader *be there.* |
| Introduces characters via inline epithet-dump ("It was Carter Lannis, the Chief Knight, who spoke"). | Introduce characters through behavior and voice first; name and role land second, by inference. |
| Names emotions outright ("Roland felt relieved," "Roland was confused"). | Render emotion through specific physical sensation, displaced action, or texture of thought — the held coffee cup, the third re-reading of a manifest, the way Sable does *not* look at the body. |
| Adverb-and-verb-substitution dialogue tags ("said unwaveringly," "stomped angrily as he exclaimed"). | Predominantly invisible "said/asked." Tag color only for *unusual* beats, earned and rare. |
| Exposition by lecture: minor characters deliver paragraph-long worldbuilding monologues. | Drip-feed worldbuilding through observed detail and ambient overhearing. In a murder mystery, the clues *are* the worldbuilding. |
| Interrupts action with mid-scene calculation (e.g., a 1,500 °C aside during a flame demonstration in Ch. 4). | Keep action sensory and present-tense in feel. Analysis happens after, in the still moment. |
| Frames romance transactionally from the first scene: *"Miss Anna, I want to hire you."* (Er Mu, *Release That Witch*, Ch. 4, English translation, 8 words). | Let the love interest be a person before she is a resource. Sable is competent in a way Theron isn't; she sees him as a curiosity before she sees him as a partner. |
| Humor monopolized by the protagonist's modern-snark interior (the "Gandalf" beat in Ch. 1; *"How could such a tall and handsome man be such a bugbear?"* — Er Mu, Ch. 3, 12 words). | Distribute humor across the cast. The protagonist is sometimes the target of the joke. Comedy is observational, not anachronistic. |
| POV jumps from tagged interior thought ("Roland thought to himself") to detached narrator summary inside one paragraph. | Maintain a consistent close third. Move into reflection or memory by free-indirect transition, never by a tag-and-step-back. |
| Sentences average ~22 words with monotone rhythm and conjunction-stacking ("and thus," "and hence," "and therefore"). | Vary sentence length deliberately. Use fragments. Let rhythm carry emotional tempo. |
| Recycles stock similes (the "pale blue eyes like a calm lake before heavy rains" line reappears almost verbatim across description and recap passages). | Every image is fresh, specific, and *tied to the observer's particular angle of vision*. The image reveals the seer. |
| Supporting cast is foil or info-source; protagonist is the only thinker. | Side characters are competent in their own domains and capable of surprising Theron. The mystery is solvable in principle by Sable alone. |

### 3.2 One short attributed quote (under 20 words)

*"Miss Anna, I want to hire you."* — Roland, *Release That Witch*, Ch. 4 by Er Mu (English translation, Webnovel). 8 words. This is the entire RTW romance pitch. It is the exact tone we will not write.

### 3.3 Caveat on RTW

Part of the wooden quality is fan-translation/MTL artifact. The *structural* problems — telling-not-showing emotion, narrator-summary of scenes, transactional framing, epithet introductions, humor as protagonist soliloquy — are architectural in the source, not in the translation. Treat the architectural problems as the contrast axis.

## 4. Prose Mechanics Codification

### 4.1 Sentence-level rules

- **Action paragraphs:** average 12–18 words per sentence. Include at least one 4–7-word sentence per paragraph. No sentence over 28 words in active conflict.
- **Reflection paragraphs:** may average 18–24 words. Cap any one sentence at 35 words. No two 25+-word sentences in a row.
- **Discovery/reveal sentences** (the moment Theron sees the clue, the moment Sable corrects him): preferentially 5–12 words, often a fragment. The reveal is rhythmically distinct from its surround.
- **Conjunction discipline:** "and thus," "and hence," "and therefore," "moreover," "furthermore" are banned at sentence starts. Use "Then." Use "So." Use a period and a new sentence.
- **Em-dash quota:** maximum one em-dash per 250 words of prose. Em-dashes are a flavor, not a sauce.
- **Tricolon discipline:** maximum one rule-of-three construction per chapter, and only when all three elements are genuinely concrete and parallel. Never "the cold, the quiet, and the dawning realization" — that's the abstraction tic.
- **No "It was not just X, it was Y" sentence forms.** Ever. This is the single most identifiable AI-prose tic of 2024–2026.

### 4.2 POV distance

Close-third on Theron, past tense. The narrator can hear Theron's thoughts as clearly as Theron can; the narrator never knows more than Theron does **except in deliberate, signposted POV-break interludes** (see §4.6).

- **Free indirect style is the default for thought.** No "he thought to himself" tags. The thought is allowed to sit in the same paragraph as the action, in the same voice.
- **Filtering verbs are minimized.** "He saw the body" → "The body lay where the lamp's light didn't quite reach." "He felt cold" → physical sensation, displaced object, or specific behavior.
- **One exception:** when Theron is being *deliberately stupid* — when the narrator wants us to see him misread a moment — we use a filter ("Theron decided the silence was awkward") so the reader knows we're inside his interpretation, not the truth.

### 4.3 Dialogue tag economy

- Default: "said." Then: "asked." Both are invisible.
- Tag substitutions ("muttered," "snapped," "offered") are allowed only when the alternative would be *less* clear than the dialogue itself. Once per scene, not once per exchange.
- **No adverbial tags.** Not "said quietly." If quiet matters, render the quiet as an *action beat*: a half-smile, a glance at the door, a hand on the railing.
- **Action beats replace tags ~30% of the time.** They do double duty: identify the speaker, give us a body in the room, advance the scene's physical reality.

### 4.4 Scene openings and closings

- **Openings:** no weather. No "morning came." No "it was a quiet day on the Wyrm." Start in motion — a small, specific, present action by the POV character (Theron polishing his case slate, Sable testing the give of the cabin door, the cook arguing with the wine steward). The setting accretes around the action.
- **Closings:** no thematic summation, no "and then he knew everything would be different." End on either (a) a specific physical detail that troubles or settles the protagonist, (b) a line of dialogue with a question latent in it, or (c) a System note (used sparingly — see §5.5).

### 4.5 Time compression

Compress between scenes via a **white line and a concrete temporal anchor** in the next scene's first sentence: "By second watch the body had been moved." Avoid "Hours passed." Avoid "Time crawled." Avoid montage paragraphs that summarize a sequence of activities — pick the one moment that mattered and write only that.

### 4.6 Setting

The gondola is a small, fixed, vertical world. Render it through **texture and use** rather than description-dump. The reader should know the third-class observation deck only because Theron walks across the brass rim of its hatch and feels the cold; should know the kitchen only because the cook drops a knife when the alarm sounds. **Setting is a function of someone interacting with it.** A description that no character is using is dead description.

The dragon itself should appear in glimpses, sounds, the sway of the cabin, the breathing in the bone of the deck — never in a paragraph that explains what dragons are or how the gondola is harnessed. The reader can wait.

### 4.7 POV-break interludes (used like Reia's "Louis de Armelia, her father, reflected" passages)

Reia inserts brief third-person passages from another character's POV at structural hinge points. Mirror this: use **one POV-break per act** at most, set off by a `* * *` divider, written in the same close-third voice but anchored to a different consciousness (Sable; the captain; once, the killer). These should reveal *interpretation*, never plot information Theron will later "discover." If the break tells the reader the answer, you've broken the mystery's fair-play contract.

## 5. Tonal Register Codification

### 5.1 Cozy openings

A cozy opening is **not** a quiet opening. It is an opening with **small specific pleasures** as its sensory ground: tea steam, the give of a leather seat, the smell of someone else's pipe, a steward's practiced courtesy. Stakes can be present, even high, but the textures the POV registers are domestic and consoling. Theron at minute one of the flight is irritated, brilliant, and underslept — but he is also drinking a very good cup of coffee. The coffee is doing work.

### 5.2 Banter cadence

A banter exchange between Theron and Sable should follow this rhythm at least half the time:
1. Theron over-explains.
2. Sable responds with one short observation that is *more* accurate than his explanation.
3. Theron pauses (action beat, not a tag).
4. Theron either concedes or doubles down. Either is funny.

Avoid: tennis-match witticisms with no asymmetry. Their humor isn't equal-weighted. **Theron is verbose and clever; Sable is brief and right.** That asymmetry is the engine.

### 5.3 Warmth without saccharine

Test for every warm moment: *does the warm beat contain something concrete and slightly inconvenient?* Tanya's loyalty is warm because it includes her refusal to be sent away "for her own good." Sable's care for Theron should be warm in the same way: practical, sometimes unflattering, sometimes corrective. Warmth that *agrees* with the protagonist is sentimental. Warmth that *insists on the protagonist* is real.

### 5.4 The "he had a name" moral pivot (structural midpoint)

This is the load-bearing scene of the book. The pompous noble everyone assumed was the victim turns out to be alive; the corpse is Joren Aldis, the illusionist he hired. Theron has spent the first half of the book regarding the body as a costume. The pivot is the scene where he stops.

**Voice rules for this passage specifically:**
- **No purple sensory cataloguing.** No "the air felt heavier." No "time seemed to slow." These are AI tics and grief clichés both.
- **No portentous fragments.** No "A man. A name. A life." Cut.
- **The pivot is carried by ONE specific, concrete recovery.** Joren had a sister, or owed money to a baker, or wrote left-handed, or always took his tea with two sugars and pretended it was one. Theron learns *one* thing about Joren that is not relevant to the case, and the narrative voice slows by *exactly one beat* to let it land.
- **Sable triggers the pivot.** She names Joren first — not in a speech, in passing — and Theron's interior reaction is a single sentence. Possibly a fragment. Not three.
- **No therapy language.** No "humanize." No "honor the victim." No "see him as a person." These words must not appear in the prose. The work is done by Theron *using Joren's name without flinching* for the rest of the book.
- **Don't earn it twice.** This pivot is paid for in one scene. Returning to underline it would cheapen it. Subsequent chapters carry the change in vocabulary — Theron says "Joren" where he used to say "the body" — and that's the whole signal.

### 5.5 System screens and LitRPG moments

The System is **diegetic**, present, sometimes funny, never a dashboard.

- **Frequency cap:** no more than four System interventions per chapter. In most chapters, two.
- **Form on the page:** indented block, italic, no decorative borders or ASCII boxes. Format:

> *Quest updated: A Death in the Cabin*
> *Status: One suspect cleared. Seven remain.*

Or one-line in-line:

> *[Investigator — Insight +1. The way she held the knife was wrong.]*

- **Never the System narrating combat.** Never "+50 XP for successful deduction!" celebration text. The System is laconic, slightly dry, and observes Theron the way Theron observes others. It has the same voice register as the rest of the prose.
- **The System notices what Theron is not noticing.** Used this way it is *characterization for Theron* — what he overlooks, what the System catches, the gap between them. The gap is the gag and sometimes the clue.
- **Level-ups are quiet.** A single italic line. No fanfare. No stat sheet inserted into the chapter end. The character reaction (Theron's quiet pleasure, then immediate intellectualization of why he shouldn't be pleased) is the scene, not the numbers.
- **No status sheet recaps at chapter breaks.** Ever. This is the early-series dashboard tic the user explicitly named in *The Scarlet Wolf: A Blood Magic Lycanthrope LitRPG* (Wolf of the Blood Moon, Book 1) by Shane Purdy, self-published February 2024 — a serial that grew from a Royal Road run of over ten million views and that visibly tightened its dashboard frequency across later volumes.
- **The quest log is referenced, never reproduced.** Theron "checks the log"; we get one line of what's on it. Never a bulleted dump.

### 5.6 The slow-burn romance

The romance is built on **shared moral instinct**, not attraction. Sable pushes Theron to honor Joren by name; this is the romance, structurally. Therefore:

- **No "their eyes met across the cabin."** No heart-pounding, no breath-catching.
- **Physical awareness, when it appears, is oblique and specific.** Theron notices that Sable's hands are steadier than his when she handles the dead man's effects. He notices it because his are not. The observation tells us about *him*.
- **Mutual respect precedes mutual attraction by approximately one entire book.** First book: they become each other's necessary thinking partner. Anything more is a glance, a "should I" deflected, a brief held-look that breaks.
- **The almost-moment, if used, must be earned by a moral beat.** Not an interruption. A *decision*.

### 5.7 Serious beats in a light book

When a serious beat lands — the corpse, the lie, Sable's class background, the moment Theron realizes the noble's smugness is grief — the prose does NOT switch into "serious mode." It stays in voice. The seriousness is conveyed by **what the voice elects to look at** and **how long it stays**. Slow the camera. Don't change the lens.

## 6. Anti-Slop Constraints

### 6.1 Generic LLM tics — DO NOT / INSTEAD DO

| DO NOT | INSTEAD DO |
|---|---|
| Use "shimmer," "tapestry," "realm," "beacon," "cacophony," "delve," "underscore," "intricate," "multifaceted." | Use specific nouns. A "shimmer" is light on something — say what. A "tapestry" is a metaphor for variety — pick a concrete variety. |
| Open chapters with weather, time-of-day abstraction, or scene-setting prologue ("Morning came to the Wyrm…"). | Open with a small, specific action by Theron. The world accretes from the action. |
| "It's not just X, it's Y." "Not merely X but Y." Any antithesis-of-elevation construction. | Make the point once, with the more accurate word. If both X and Y are needed, write two sentences. |
| Triplet escalation — "rule of three with escalating abstraction" ("a man, a name, a memory"). | At most one tricolon per chapter, all three terms concrete and parallel. |
| "A testament to," "in the heart of," "speaks volumes," "stands as a reminder," "begs the question." | Cut. These add ceremony, not meaning. |
| Body-language clichés: heart pounding, breath caught, blood ran cold, stomach dropped, knuckles white. | One physical sensation per scene, and it must be unusual or specific to the character (Theron's left thumb finds the worn place on his case slate; Sable's hands forget what to do with themselves and reach for a pencil). |
| Purple sensory cataloguing ("the air smelled of brass and ozone and the faint copper tang of fear"). | One sense, one detail, well-placed. The reader supplies the rest. |
| Falsely portentous fragments: "A life. A name. A choice." | Use a fragment sparingly, and only when a full sentence would over-explain. |
| Em-dash maximalism — two or more em-dashes per paragraph. | One em-dash per 250 words at most. |
| "In today's…", "In a world where…", "Throughout history…", "Time and time again…" | Begin in scene, in action, in a specific moment. |
| Hedging stock phrases: "arguably," "to some extent," "broadly speaking," "in many ways." | Commit to the claim or cut the claim. |
| Vague stakes language: "everything would change," "nothing would ever be the same." | Name the specific change. "By breakfast he would have to choose between Sable and the truth" is concrete; "everything would change" is filler. |
| Curly quotes that look "intentional" combined with conspicuous em-dashes (the 2025 Wikipedia AI-detection markers). | Use the punctuation conventions of the project's style sheet consistently; do not load punctuation with mood. |
| Closing chapters with a thematic gloss ("And in that moment, Theron understood that justice was not a verdict but a vigil"). | Close on a concrete detail, a held question, or one laconic System line. |
| The word "delve." | Don't. |

### 6.2 LitRPG-specific slop — DO NOT / INSTEAD DO

| DO NOT | INSTEAD DO |
|---|---|
| Status-screen spam (the *Wolf of the Blood Moon* / early-series dashboard tic explicitly named by the user). | Hard cap of four System interventions per chapter; no recap sheets at chapter ends. |
| "A screen appeared before him." "The System pinged." "A notification flashed in his vision." | A single italic indented line in the System voice. No fanfare. |
| Tutorial-voice info dumps ("The Investigator class allows the user to…"). | The class is revealed by use. The reader infers the rules. If a rule must be stated, Theron states it in dialogue, under pressure, to Sable, because she asked. |
| Mechanical level-up celebrations: stat increases listed, points to spend, prompts to allocate. | A level-up is one italic line. Theron's reaction is half a sentence of free indirect. We do not stop to allocate. |
| Redundant stat descriptions ("His Strength was now 14, an increase from 12 of two points") in narration. | Narrate consequence, not number. "He could lift the cabinet now. Yesterday it would have taken both of them." |
| Power-fantasy floskeln: "the world would tremble," "his power was unmatched," "they would learn to fear him." | Theron is a 21-year-old prodigy who is occasionally wrong and frequently insufferable. He is not a power-fantasy. The voice never flatters him. |
| Translation patois (the RTW house style): "and thus," "and hence," wooden honorifics, stiff exposition, repetitive epithets ("the tall and handsome Chief Knight"). | English-native rhythm. Honorifics dropped where natural. Epithets earned and varied. |
| "X said unwaveringly" / "Y exclaimed angrily" — adverbial-tag stacking common in MTL'd web novels. | "Said." "Asked." Action beats. |
| Quest logs reproduced in full at scene transitions. | A single line referencing what's on the log. The log is a tool, not content. |
| The protagonist commenting on game mechanics with knowing irony ("Of course it was a fetch quest"). | The world is real to the people in it. Theron does not wink at the System; the System winks at Theron. |
| Combat narrated by the System ("Critical hit! +50 XP!"). | Combat narrated by Theron's body and senses. The System notes the outcome afterward, briefly, dryly. |

## 7. Comparable Authors & What to Borrow

- **Travis Baldree (*Legends & Lattes*, Cryptid Press self-publication February 22, 2022; Tor trade paperback November 2022; *Bookshops & Bonedust*, Tor, November 2023; *Legends & Lattes* finalist for both the 2023 Hugo Award for Best Novel and the 2023 Nebula Award for Best Novel).** Borrow: the **lived-in workplace texture** — small ergonomic details of a working environment (the gondola galley, the steward's station, the captain's chartroom) rendered with the loving specificity Baldree uses for a coffee shop. Borrow: **post-violence protagonist energy** — Viv is competent and tired; Theron should be competent and *young*, but his class assignment has just made him newly responsible. Borrow: the **side characters who are surprising experts** — Thimble the rattkin baker is the model for, e.g., a third-class engineer who turns out to be the most morally clear-eyed person on board. Don't borrow: Baldree's near-total avoidance of mortal stakes; we have a death, and it counts.
- **T. Kingfisher / Ursula Vernon (*Nettle & Bone*, *Paladin's Grace*, *A House with Good Bones*).** Borrow: **humor coexisting with real horror without undercutting it**. Kingfisher's "I can't *not* crack jokes" voice (per her *Orion Magazine* interview, December 2023) is the closest English-language analogue to Reia's narrator. Borrow: **practical heroines who respond to supernatural and social threats with common sense and ill-timed jokes**. Borrow: **the relationship-as-the-plot structure** — the romance and the case develop in the same scenes, not in parallel. Don't borrow: Kingfisher's slightly more arch, more adult narrator-voice in the horror books; Theron is 21 and still has things to learn about wonder.
- **Becky Chambers (*The Long Way to a Small, Angry Planet*, *A Psalm for the Wild-Built*; Wayfarers won the 2019 Hugo Award for Best Series).** Borrow: **the deliberate, character-first pacing**; the willingness to **let a conversation be the scene**. Borrow: **gentle but not naive** — Chambers can write a bigot inside a kind society and never lose the kindness. Don't borrow: Chambers's near-plotlessness; we have a murder, a culprit, and a clock.
- **Agatha Christie.** Borrow: the **fair-play contract** as codified by Ronald Knox (1929) and S. S. Van Dine — every clue Theron sees, the reader sees, in the same chapter, in plain text — and the **drawing-room reveal staging** at the end of the book — the locked cabin, the assembled passengers, Theron walking through the deduction. Lucy Worsley's 2023 documentary identifies the "drawing-room denouement" as Christie's signature move; we are stealing it wholesale, transposed to a gondola cabin. Borrow: the **psychological misdirection** Christie repeatedly used in *The Mysterious Affair at Styles* and others — characters hide their true selves behind a veneer that is the *opposite* of their secret. The pompous noble's smugness is grief; Joren's bravado was professional confidence; Sable's silence is competence, not deference. Don't borrow: the omniscient narrator-with-attitude; we are in close-third on Theron.
- **John Dickson Carr** (for locked-room mechanics only; see *The Three Coffins* / *The Hollow Man*, 1935, and its famous "Locked Room Lecture" chapter). Borrow: the **explicit recognition that the room must be *seen* by the reader before the impossibility is asserted** — the cabin's geometry, the lock's mechanism, the dragon-harness chain that runs through the wall, all established naturally before the body is found.
- **Matt Dinniman (*Dungeon Crawler Carl*) — selectively.** Borrow only: the **dry, sardonic System voice** as a model for Theron's System notifications — laconic, slightly mean, occasionally observant about the protagonist in ways the protagonist won't be. Do *not* borrow: the dashboard frequency, the loot-box pacing, the audience-as-character apocalypse spectacle. Wrong genre.
- **Casualfarmer (*Beware of Chicken*; pseudonymous author from Ontario, Canada; first serialized on Royal Road in 2021 to over 22 million views per themerchguild.com; published in print by Aethon Books with audiobooks narrated by Travis Baldree) — selectively.** Borrow: **the way slice-of-life beats can carry genre weight** — domestic action that turns out to matter. Do not borrow: the cultivation-prose register or the multi-POV animal-cam structure.
- **NOT to borrow from:** **Shirtaloon** (pen name of Travis Deverell, Tasmania, Australia; *He Who Fights with Monsters*, Royal Road serial since July 27, 2019, novel form from 2021) — long social-commentary monologues from the protagonist; insufferable-narrator energy without the self-deprecation correction. **Shane Purdy** (*The Scarlet Wolf: A Blood Magic Lycanthrope LitRPG* / Wolf of the Blood Moon, Book 1, Amazon/Kindle self-publication February 7, 2024) — the explicit example of status-screen frequency the user wants to avoid in the early books, and the example of an author who improved on this across the series.

## 8. Quick-Reference Checklist (one page, keep visible while writing)

Before drafting a chapter, set timer. After drafting a chapter, run this.

**Voice**
- [ ] Close third on Theron. No filtering verbs except where deliberately ironic.
- [ ] Theron is allowed to be smart. He is not allowed to lecture. If he's lecturing, Sable interrupts.
- [ ] At least one moment where Theron is wrong, and the prose doesn't protect him from it.

**Sentence-level**
- [ ] Action paragraphs: 12–18 word average, at least one short sentence per beat.
- [ ] No sentence opens with "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Consequently," "And thus," "And hence."
- [ ] No "It's not X, it's Y" constructions. Zero.
- [ ] Max one em-dash per 250 words.
- [ ] Max one tricolon per chapter, all terms concrete.
- [ ] Banned word check: shimmer, tapestry, realm, beacon, cacophony, delve, underscore, intricate, multifaceted, testament, in the heart of.

**Dialogue**
- [ ] "Said" / "asked" dominate. No adverbial tags.
- [ ] Action beats replacing tags about 30% of the time.
- [ ] No tennis-match banter. Theron over-explains; Sable is briefer and more correct.

**Scene**
- [ ] Opens on small specific action, not weather or scene-setting.
- [ ] Closes on concrete detail, latent question, or one laconic System line — never thematic gloss.
- [ ] Setting rendered through someone using it, not described.

**Mystery / Fair Play**
- [ ] Every clue Theron sees, the reader sees, in the same chapter, in plain text.
- [ ] Every red herring is eventually explained.
- [ ] No "I had already deduced…" cheat in narration.

**LitRPG**
- [ ] Max four System interventions in this chapter.
- [ ] No status sheet recap at chapter end.
- [ ] System voice: laconic, dry, sometimes observes Theron's blind spots.
- [ ] No tutorial paragraph explaining a class or skill.
- [ ] Level-ups: one italic line, half-sentence reaction, no allocation scene.

**Cozy-but-stakes**
- [ ] At least one warm, concrete sensory detail per scene (a cup of tea, a worn glove, the give of the cabin seat).
- [ ] If this chapter touches Joren's death: his name appears at least once, in plain prose, without commentary.

**Romance**
- [ ] No "their eyes met." No heart-pounding. No breath-catching.
- [ ] If there's a romantic beat, it is built on a shared moral observation, not on physical proximity.
- [ ] Sable is competent in a way Theron isn't; that gap is visible in this chapter.

**Anti-AI check**
- [ ] Read three random sentences aloud. Do they sound like the writer, or like prose that would pass a content-mill quality bar?
- [ ] Is there at least one image in this chapter that nobody else would have written?
