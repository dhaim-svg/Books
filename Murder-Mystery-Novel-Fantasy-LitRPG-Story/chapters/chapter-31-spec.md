## Scenes to hit

1. **Morning at the Arms — the seating-phase finesse.** Theron and Sable resume at the corner table after breakfast. The night's certainty has weathered. Theron raises the question that the theory itself has surfaced: not everyone who trained next to an illusionist would know the seating phase as a *trigger architecture* (vs. as a method of laying glamour). It is a finesse Joren himself would have had to teach. The conversation moves from the structural exploit to the human transmission of the technique. Sable asks the load-bearing question: did Joren show his apprentice things he wouldn't show another professional? The answer they triangulate from Pessel's interview (Ch 27) is yes — Joren's relationship with the coastal woman was not student-and-master but a more specific kind of pairing. The conversation names the prodigy without naming it: only a particular apprentice would have come out of those eleven years with this in her toolkit.

2. **The Pessel re-read.** Sable opens her notebook to her own pages from the Pessel interview — not Theron's notes, hers. She reads two sentences aloud that did not land at the time. Pessel said *she could do the work after about three years, but Joren kept her seven more.* Pessel said *he sent her south alone for the work that needed her hand more than his.* These were filed under *training arrangement* and *trust*. Re-read against the method, they become: the apprenticeship lasted longer than competence required, because Joren was teaching her something else; and she was sent south alone because the work she could do was work he could no longer do himself. Theron does not seize the conclusion. Sable does not push it. They let the sentences sit on the page beside the abstract.

3. **The requirements list — working backward.** Theron writes a new page in the case book: *Person who could have performed the method.* Five lines, each a constraint derived from the exploit: (a) sustained-contact apprenticeship with the victim, of seating-phase duration, with a master who taught the trigger architecture and not only the wider craft; (b) signature compatibility with the victim, which apprenticeship produced; (c) post-apprenticeship retention of the trigger, which required that the apprentice had carried it out of the working relationship and not surrendered it back; (d) access to the victim, voluntary on his part — he had to seat himself near her, repeatedly, late in the apprenticeship, without knowing the seating was happening; (e) a working life after the apprenticeship that did not register on the same scry-detection rigs the trade would normally check. Each line is the method described from the perpetrator's side. Each line is a thing the case file already supports for one named person. Theron does not write *Halsa Renn* at the bottom. He doesn't have to. The shape on the page is the shape of a person and the shape has one fit.

4. **The exclusivity remainder.** Theron turns to the back-of-book page where last night's *exclusivity to Renn — not yet established* still sits. He reads it. He does not strike it out. He writes, beside it, in the smallest hand he uses for shadow notes: *Requirements list converges to one person on the manifest. Exclusivity not proven; structural fit is.* He sits with the difference between *proven* and *fit*. The chapter ends with him quietly sure — not yet ready for the magistrate but ready for the last step, which is to confirm that no other person on the Wyrm could have stood in the same shape. The Prodigy-backstory has done its work. He does not phrase it that way. Sable, watching him at the page, says one short sentence about her own day and stands to leave the table. He stays a little longer.
