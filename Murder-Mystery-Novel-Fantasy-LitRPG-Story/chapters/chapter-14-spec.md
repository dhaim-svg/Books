## Formula coverage

**Step 5 — The Floor Drops (continued).** Ch. 14 remains in Step 5: the immediate aftermath of the reveal plays out. Two-manifest shock settles; Theron works the reorientation. The active question is no longer who killed "Lord Vannic" — it is who killed the man performing him, and why someone who knew the swap would bother. The gondola is still in flight. Halverstow approaches.

## Scenes to hit

1. **Theron delivers the news to Esherel.** He arrives at her cabin with "I have new information that I think you'll find significant." She hears it — the dead man was not Vannic, a hired illusionist performed the role — and her reaction is sharp, almost vindicated (she was right that something was off about him). The conversation pivots her from cleared suspect to potential source: she watched "Vannic" across three days with a grievance sharpening her attention. What did she notice that she hadn't thought to mention? Cozy register: no grief, no horror, no melodrama. She is quick-minded and right.

2. **Mapping the third-class manifest.** Theron and Sable work the third-class passenger list together — steward's alcove or the captain's gallery. The question: who on this list could have known Joren was performing, not the real lord? This is reorientation, not resolution. Sable drives the categorization; Theron provides the geometry. The third-class suspects are new people — no prior character development. The chapter begins populating them as shapes.

3. **First letter from the oilcloth packet.** Theron opens the Berric Toll correspondence. He reads enough to establish the arrangement's timeline and logistics, and finds one detail — a date, a reference to Joren's prior work, a second name — that points toward the next question without answering the present one. Concrete, specific, functional; not a dramatic reveal.

## Continuity

Sable turned up a third-class register entry for "Petor Vann" that Calden had been holding; she and Theron descended to cabin three-T together and found Lord Petric Vannic alive, hiding from a forced betrothal to Duke Esseven's daughter. Vannic confessed he hired an illusionist named Joren through a factor (Berric Toll, Mirren west harbour) to perform the role of Lord Vannic; the arrangement was invisible to every other passenger. Theron took the correspondence packet and reoriented the case: the dead man is Joren, not Vannic, and whoever killed him knew it. Theron instructed Vannic to stay in cabin three-T until Halverstow; Sable told Theron he owes Esherel an apology, and he disagreed with the word "apology" while conceding the point. Tone: cozy register intact — Vannic is ridiculous and caught, Theron is reoriented and moving to the next problem, Sable is pragmatic.

## What NOT to do this chapter

- Do not have any character recall the five planted details as clues. They are texture, not evidence, until Ch. 16.
- Do not name Joren's surname. Theron and Sable know him only as "Joren" at this point.
- Do not let Esherel learn Joren's name. She learns only that the dead man was an illusionist performing Vannic; the name is Theron and Sable's for now.
- Do not have any character "almost figure out" the planted details or come close to the Ch. 16 recall passage.
- Do not resolve who killed Joren or move toward a named suspect. This chapter is mapping and reorientation only.
- No Sable interiority. She is a working partner — questions, observations, action only.
- Do not soften the cozy register into grief or horror. Esherel is sharp and nearly pleased to be right; Theron is problem-solving.

Closes on a concrete detail from the Toll correspondence or a name on the third-class list that catches the eye — a latent question, not a thematic gloss.
