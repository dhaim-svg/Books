That work is Ch 30. The chapter must end with the theory on the table — uncomfortable, plausible, unverified — and the reader understanding that this is the first time in seventy-two hours the method page is not blank.

## Scenes to hit

1. **Halverstow, Tuesday morning, the Arms — Sable's road-letter from Sannholt arrives ahead of schedule** — The morning post-coach brings the letter Sable promised in Monday's note, slightly earlier than expected (she gave it to the morning coach instead of the late one). She has done her *one further afternoon errand* in Sannholt: she walked the market quarter and located the Copper Finch, the boarding-house the Whitepost landlady had hinted at. The proprietor of the Copper Finch was guarded but answered three direct questions: yes a young coastal woman had stayed in the back room three or four times a year, yes she had stopped coming about two years ago, and yes she had always travelled south from Sannholt afterward, never north. The proprietor noted, unprompted, that the woman *did work that needed quiet rooms.* Sable wrote it down in those words. The letter also confirms Sable will reach Halverstow by Tuesday's last bell — she is, in fact, on the road now, the letter having reached the Arms three hours ahead of her. Theron reads it. He logs *did work that needed quiet rooms* into the column. The letter lands a fact he was not asking for and did not expect: Halsa's working pattern required isolation, the kind illusion casting requires. The chapter's first nudge toward method.

2. **Halverstow, Tuesday mid-morning, the Arms — Theron rereads his thesis-archive abstract** — Theron has carried a small slate-bound notebook from his graduation packet through this whole trip: the abstracts of his final-year work, kept for licensing reference. He has not opened it since boarding the Wyrm three weeks ago. The Sable letter has shifted something — *quiet rooms* against *sealed cabin* — and he pulls the notebook out because he wants to check something he has not yet articulated. He reads through the abstracts until he finds his own: *Scryproof Glamour Exploits: Theoretical Vulnerability in Long-Contact Illusion Chains.* He reads it. He reads it again. The abstract describes a niche structural exploit in illusion casting — a sustained glamour that, once seated, persists after the caster's withdrawal and registers as ambient, not as cast magic, to standard scry-detection. The exploit's known limitation was practical: it required sustained contact between caster and target during the seating phase and a target whose own magical signature was compatible with the glamour's structure. The examiner who had liked the thesis had called it *original, if limited in practical application.* The examiner who hadn't had called it *somewhat overstated.* Theron had received the pin and never thought about the exploit again. He does, now, on a Tuesday morning at the Arms, with a method page that has been blank since Monday.

3. **Halverstow, Tuesday early afternoon, the Arms — the theory clicks into the negative shape of the *how*** — Theron sits with the abstract open on the table, the case book open beside it, the partial Mirren transcript folded under the case book. He does not yet write *this is the method.* He writes, slowly: the exploit's known limitation (sustained contact during the seating phase) is satisfied if the seating phase is during the apprenticeship — eleven years ago, in a working relationship that involved casting alongside the master for years at a time. The exploit's second limitation (a compatible magical signature) is satisfied if both parties are illusionists. The exploit, once seated during a contact period that ran for years, would not require the caster to be physically near the target at the moment of activation. It would require a trigger, something the seated glamour was set to listen for, and a passenger in cabin nine-T at thirty feet of corridor distance who knew the trigger. He writes none of that down in plain ink. He writes it across the page where he had earlier written *negative shape of the how* and the writing is in the careful unindented hand he reserves for *not yet earned.* The chapter does not let him be confident. It lets him be uncomfortable.

4. **Halverstow, Tuesday afternoon, the Arms — the cost of the recall** — Theron sits with the theory on the table and does what a young investigator at the start of a career should never want to do: he reads back through the exam scripts in the slate-bound notebook to see whether he had been right to dismiss it. He had. By the examination's lights, the theory was an extreme-case theoretical curiosity — no recorded practical instance, no contract-trade application, no historical case for the examiners to reference. He had dismissed it because *no historical case* was the right professional reading at the time. The chapter sits with the small ugly knowledge that *no historical case* is what an extreme exploit looks like before its first historical case. He closes the notebook. He writes one line in the case book: *Method — working theory only. Sustained scryproof glamour exploit (Mavik thesis, Conservatory, final year). To be tested against cabin twelve geometry on Sable's return.* He underlines none of it. He does not name it as the method. He names it as the working theory. The chapter ends with the theory on the table and Sable's coach due at last bell.

## What NOT to do this chapter

- Do not have Theron confirm the theory is the method. He names it as a working theory only. Ch 30 does the test against cabin twelve geometry.
- Do not have Theron be triumphant about the recall. He is uncomfortable. He had dismissed this material as exam fodder, and the dismissal was professionally correct at the time. The recall costs him a small piece of his confidence about the right kind of professional reading. Let it cost.
- Do not lecture the magical mechanics of the exploit. The thesis abstract is two-three sentences in Theron's voice as he reads it. The exploit is described in working-professional terms, not in tutorial-voice. The reader infers the shape of it from Theron's reading and from his subsequent notes. *No tutorial paragraph explaining a class or skill* — apply this to the exploit too.
- Do not give Sable interiority. Her Sannholt errand is narrated only through the letter she has sent ahead. The letter is read by Theron, and only what is on the page is on the page.
- Do not name Halsa as the killer. *Working suspect* still. The theory is a method that the working suspect would have access to; the chapter does not collapse the *who* and the *how* into a verdict.
- Do not foreshadow the eight-years-ago brother contract. Halsa's specific motive remains spoiler-wall until Ch 33–35. The theory in Ch 29 is about *how*, not *why*.
- Do not have the System produce the theory for him. The recall is Theron's. The System may, at most, acknowledge the working theory has been logged — laconic, dry, one indented italic line. The System does not solve the case.
- Do not stack System pings — max 4, target 2.
- Do not collapse the chapter into a single contemplative scene. The four-beat structure carries the chapter's weight: letter, recall, theory-as-negative-shape, the cost of having dismissed it.
- Do not let the chapter close on triumph. Close on the theory on the table, the notebook closed, Sable's coach due at last bell. The reader should feel the recall has happened and that the test is still ahead.
- Do not write *eureka* prose. No *and then it hit him.* No *suddenly.* The recall is a slow professional movement of a mind that already had the material and was now reading it under a different lamp.
