# Chapter 21 — The Fell Interview

The clerk's ink-brush was making a noise.

Theron had been sitting at the side table in the magistrate's outer office for five minutes waiting for Fell to be brought through, and for four of those minutes the clerk had been copying something into a register, and the brush was dragging on the paper in the particular way of a brush that had been re-inked too recently. It set his teeth on edge. He moved his case slate an inch to the right and stopped noticing it.

The TL-9 notation was already on the table. Not his summary. The clerk-made copy from the factors' board, exact to the original, with Fell's own self-submitted hand visible in the margin column. Sable had suggested the copy; he had taken it. He was thinking now, as he looked at it, that she had been right about where the conversation would start when Fell recognized her own annotation.

The anteroom door opened. Fell came in ahead of the constable, and if she was surprised to find two people waiting she didn't show it.

She wore the same coat as the factors' board day, or one like it — dark wool, well-kept, buttoning to the collar. She looked at the table, at the slate, at Sable's notebook, and at Theron, in that order, with the calm professional inventory of someone accustomed to walking into rooms where not everyone was glad to see her. She sat when the constable indicated the chair.

"Maret Fell," Theron said. "Thank you for coming."

"I was asked," she said. Not *summoned*, not *required*. Asked. The word doing its small work.

---

The first ten minutes established the facts both parties already knew they would establish: her name, her professional history, her listing in the Gray Quay factors' board. Clean autumn and winter work in the east Mirren assessment yards. Two spring customs-assist jobs. The kind of interview she had apparently done before, if not this specific one, and she took each question in its correct shape and answered it in the same shape, not more.

Theron let her finish each answer before he moved.

"The two spring customs-assist jobs," he said, and pushed the copy of the factors' board notation across the table.

Fell looked at it. She took the same length of time to read it that a person with no reason to be concerned about it would take. Then she looked at Theron.

"TL-9," he said. "Not a standard dock-authority code. Not in the Gray Quay key. Your notation. In your hand."

"The first job used a customs broker who ran his own internal reference codes," Fell said. "I picked up the notation from him and applied it to the second job for cross-reference. A sloppy habit." She moved the paper an inch back toward him. "If it's created a filing difficulty —"

"The broker's name."

A pause. Brief, calibrated. Not the pause of someone caught, but of someone deciding something.

"Berric Toll." Her hands, which had been flat on the table, moved to stack themselves, one over the other, in her lap. "West harbour, Mirren."

"How did you come to work customs-assist for Berric Toll?"

"A referral through the yard. East Mirren assessment, as I said. There was a job in spring — a cargo manifest discrepancy, customs-side — that came through two offices. I was the faster turnaround. Toll's office contracted for a second job directly after."

Theron looked at her. She met it without difficulty.

"You listed yourself as Toll's subcontractor on both jobs."

"I used his reference code," she said. "As a broker's shorthand. I wouldn't describe myself as a subcontractor."

*[Investigator — Insight +1. She's distinguishing the administrative fact from the professional relationship. The distinction may be genuine or may be load-bearing.]*

"Did you know a man named Joren Aldis?"

The way she went still was not the way innocent people went still.

It lasted perhaps two seconds, and then she said, "I worked through Toll's office on two jobs. I didn't know his other contacts."

"That isn't the question I asked."

Fell looked at the copy of the notation. Then she looked at Theron, and there was something behind the professional guard now that he hadn't seen in the cabin-seven interview on the Wyrm — something that was deciding, the same way she'd decided before she named Toll, but taking longer. "I knew the name," she said at last. "By reputation. Through the yard circuit. A working illusionist who did the kind of contract work that took him off the coast occasionally."

"What kind of contract work?"

"The kind that didn't stay in the yards." Her tone was flat. Not hostile. Flat. The flatness of a person choosing neutral ground and standing on it.

"Did Toll tell you who else he had working the passage?"

"Toll didn't brief me on the passage. I did customs-assist in spring. The gondola run was separate."

"Did you see Joren Aldis on the Skybound Wyrm?"

A second pause. Different from the first: not deciding, more like landing somewhere she'd been expecting to land. "I saw a man in first class. I was in third. I didn't know who the first-class passengers were."

"But you knew you were on the same passage as someone from Toll's network."

Fell's jaw moved slightly. The first departure from the controlled register. "No one told me that."

"Did you work out it was likely?"

The room was quiet for a moment. Through the office wall came the sound of the outer register: the clerk's brush, the scratch and drag of it.

"I had the TL-9 reference from the spring jobs," she said, choosing her words with the care of someone navigating a credible story through a narrow channel. "I knew the passage had gone through Toll's office. Not in what capacity. I didn't ask."

*She knew.* Theron kept the thought off his face. What she'd said was almost true, and the almost was everything.

He set his pencil down.

"Roven," he said.

She went still.

Not the calibrated stillness of the Joren question. That had been a controlled response. This was the stillness of someone who had just heard a word in a place it should not have been, and was processing the fact of its presence before the rest of the thinking could proceed. Her hands, in her lap, stopped. Her gaze, which had been moving between him and the table, stopped.

Then she looked at Sable.

Sable had not moved, had said nothing since the interview began, and met the look with the same steady attention she'd given the room since they sat down.

Fell looked back at Theron.

"I don't know that name," she said.

"That's interesting," he said, "because you've just given me a very different answer to this question than you gave to every other one."

The silence that followed was not empty. Fell was in it, doing something, measuring something, and Theron let it run. There was a technique his instructor at the Conservatory had called *giving the space to the other person*, and it had felt artificial in training and felt entirely natural now. He waited.

"Roven," Fell said at last, and her voice had changed register. Not warmer. Careful in a different way. "Is a name in the network. Senior, I was given to understand, to most of the contacts working that side of the coast. I have never dealt with Roven directly." She paused. "I don't know where Roven is, if that's the next question."

"It was going to be."

"Then I've saved you it." She looked at the table. "What I know about Roven is what you learn in the yards when you work near a network without working inside it. The name attached to a category of job. Serious commission work, not one-off assessments. I was never offered that category."

"Why not?"

"I didn't want it offered." She met his eyes. "I was doing legitimate work, Mr. Mavik. The TL-9 notation was sloppy record-keeping from a spring contract. I have a cargo-verification job scheduled in three days. That is the whole of my business in this city."

Theron looked at her for a moment. The professional register was back. She'd gathered it around herself like the well-kept coat, and it fit. Everything she'd said was defensible. Most of it, he thought, was even true.

"One more question," he said. "The two customs-assist jobs in spring. The second one ended six days before the gondola run. Were the manifests for those jobs connected to the Wyrm's Mirren departure?"

A small pause. "One was adjacent. Not directly."

"Adjacent how?"

"The cargo line that ran through the same dock window." She said it steadily. "Not the passenger manifest. The freight side."

"I'll need the job reference numbers."

She gave them without hesitation. Whatever she was protecting, it wasn't the job numbers.

---

Outside, the magistrate's front step had a low stone wall running along its north side where the garden had once been and was now gravel. Sable was sitting on it. Theron came out and did not sit, because his legs had gone slightly wrong from forty minutes in the office chair and he preferred to stand. The harbor was three blocks south; you could smell it from here, salt and coal smoke and the particular sweetness of a bakery operating somewhere on the adjacent street.

"She knew," Sable said.

"She knew Joren's name. She knew the passage had a Toll connection. She almost certainly knew the shape of what she was on the same gondola as." He turned to look at the door he'd just come through, then at the gravel, then at his notes. "She didn't know Joren was the first-class passenger."

"You believe that."

"Yes." He turned the question over. "She saw a first-class man she didn't know. Joren was using the full glamour — she'd have seen Vannic. Not someone she recognized from the yards." He looked at his notes. "She's protecting the cargo-side connection, not the passenger side. The spring job adjacent to the Wyrm's freight manifest. That's where she draws the line."

Sable was looking at the gravel. "Roven."

"She knows that name. She gave me more on Roven than she intended to. Senior, serious-commission work, never made direct contact." He stopped. "She's afraid of that name."

"Not afraid of you finding Roven. Afraid of Roven knowing she talked."

It was the same thought he'd been reaching for, and she'd gotten there with less friction.

"She gave me the cargo job numbers," he said. "Those will trace."

"Back to Toll. Back to the freight side of the Wyrm's departure." Sable stood, brushing gravel dust from her coat. "She's telling you something with those numbers. She gave them too readily."

He looked at the note again. "She wants the cargo thread pulled and not the Roven thread."

"She wants to be cooperative without giving you anything that brings Roven into a room."

The bakery smell was stronger now. Someone had opened a door somewhere. Theron looked at his notes, then at the harbor-facing street. Fell had given him one genuinely useful piece: the cargo-side connection and its job numbers, which would trace through Toll's operation and might open the freight manifest from the Wyrm's Mirren departure. And she had protected one thing, carefully, behind a solid wall of plausible deniability: whatever direct knowledge she had of Roven's involvement in the passage.

The thread was not resolved. He hadn't expected it to be.

A boy came around the corner of the magistrate's building at a half-run, which was the city speed between walking and actually running that messengers used when the message was time-sensitive but not an emergency. He slowed, looked at them, and held out a folded note.

"Mavik?" he said.

"Yes."

The note was from the clearing house at the south Halverstow station. The handwriting was a clerk's, neat and quick and impersonal. Theron read it once, then again.

*Pessel, care of the Copper Finch, Sannholt — confirmed as registered box-holder, this clearing house and both southern transit houses. Box is active, payments current. Your letter was accepted Friday run and should arrive in Sannholt by tomorrow noon at latest. Reply within four to five days of delivery.*

He passed the note to Sable.

She read it. "Active box. Real address."

"Real address," he said. "Pessel exists. Or someone has maintained that name long enough to pay for a registered box."

Sable folded the note and handed it back. The morning light was at the angle now where the harbourmaster's hall windows would be catching it, sending the brass-colored light across the floor that meant mid-morning had arrived and the work traffic of the port had shifted from loading to administering. Theron put the note in his coat pocket beside the Fell interview notes.

The Sannholt reply was coming. Friday if Pessel wrote immediately, Saturday if they waited a day. By the end of the week, the address would either reply or it wouldn't, and the case would have a third thread confirmed or a thread ended.

He looked at his notes again: Fell's cargo job numbers, the Roven description, and the clearing house confirmation sitting beside each other on the page.

"Sannholt by tomorrow noon," he said, more to himself than to her.

"Then we wait," Sable said, "and follow the cargo numbers while we do."

He tucked the notebook under his arm and went down the magistrate's steps into the street.
