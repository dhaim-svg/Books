# Chapter 25 — The Coach Departs

The magistrate's clerk came at half past fifth bell, before Theron had finished his first cup of coffee.

The man was apologetic about the hour, which was its own kind of information: clerks who came at half past fifth were either carrying good news or had been up since third watch putting a search result together. He set a folded document on the table and accepted the chair Theron offered him but sat on the edge of it, which meant he was on his way somewhere else after this.

"Vantrel Secured Storage," the clerk said. "West harbour Mirren. You asked for the full-year registered-agent records." He tapped the document. "The warrant came through this morning from the harbor registry. Full disclosure, first of the year through now."

Theron set his cup down and unfolded the pages.

The records were organized in the harbor registry's standard format: columns for agent name, term dates, capacity, the notary's mark. There were four entries. The first three were consistent with the kind of storage firm that ran cargo through legitimate channels — commercial-rate agents, short terms, clean entries. The fourth caught him.

Registered agent, third month through seventh month of the current year: *H. Marbeth, independent, west harbour Mirren, vouched by Vantrel Secured Storage's own registered operator, Fenn Kolach.*

No address for Marbeth beyond the harbor district. No further detail. But the term dates ran through the seventh month — which put Marbeth's active period overlapping exactly with Fell's two spring customs-assist jobs, the ones that had carried the TL-9 notation.

Not Toll's name. Not Roven's name. A name he hadn't seen anywhere in the case file until Fell's job numbers had pointed him here.

*[Investigator — Registered agent H. Marbeth, west harbour Mirren. Term: third through seventh month, current year. Active during Fell's TL-9 customs-assist period. Next step: locate Marbeth through Fenn Kolach, Vantrel's registered operator.]*

He read through the rest of the document twice. Nothing else new. The Kolach name was clean in the harbor registry — a long-standing commercial operator, nothing irregular. But Kolach had personally vouched for Marbeth, which meant there was a conversation to be had.

"The Kolach vouching entry," he said.

The clerk nodded. "Unusual. Normally the registered operator isn't the vouching party for his own firm's agents — you'd get a third party. The magistrate's office flagged it when they ran the disclosure check."

"Is Kolach in Mirren?"

"Per the registry, he holds a west harbour address. We haven't verified current residence."

A letter would take three days each way. An investigation warrant to the Mirren magistrate's office could run parallel.

"I'll need a letter of introduction from the Halverstow magistrate's office," Theron said. "For Mirren counterparts. Authorization to request a formal interview with Fenn Kolach."

The clerk wrote something in his own folder. "I'll have it drafted by afternoon."

Theron set the records at the top of his working stack and walked the clerk to the door.

It was twenty minutes past sixth bell by the time he came back to the table. The coach was at seventh.

---

He had the fish.

He had carried it since the fourth day of the investigation — lifted it from Joren's outer coat pocket during the cabin examination, logged it in the case notes as *odd personal effect, small carved wooden fish, return to estate* — and had not found an estate to return it to. It had moved from his evidence pouch to his coat pocket at some point in the second week, without deliberate decision. He had taken it out once, in the captain's day-cabin at the dock with Sable sitting across from him, and turned it over, and put it away. He had felt the shape of it through the coat lining on the docks, on the Gray Quay steps, walking to the Arms with a cold harbor wind doing its best work.

The niece had carved it at age six. She was eight now. He had not let himself do the arithmetic on what the family was waiting for.

He put it in his left coat pocket, where it would be easy to reach.

---

The south station clearing house smelled of horses and coal smoke and the particular compressed urgency of an early departure. Two coaches stood in the yard, one already loaded, one still being worked on: a driver checking the harness buckles while a boy handed up the last of the luggage from a cart. Three passengers were clustered near the booking window with their bags; a fourth was speaking to the clerk through the grille.

Theron found Sable in the yard, not at the window. She had her bag at her feet and was reading a page from her notebook, which he had seen her do before a departure — checking what she had, not what she'd forgotten. She looked up when she heard him.

"Warrant came through," he said.

"Marbeth?"

"H. Marbeth, registered agent, Vantrel Secured Storage, west harbour Mirren. Active through seventh month — overlaps Fell's TL-9 jobs. Vouched directly by Vantrel's own registered operator, Fenn Kolach. That's the irregular piece. I'm requesting a letter of introduction for the Mirren magistrate's office to interview Kolach."

She folded the notebook page and put it away. "How long to get Kolach in front of someone?"

"Ten days if the Mirren counterpart is cooperative. Longer if they aren't."

"Send me a line when you have his statement." She meant it as a fact, not a request. He would, and she knew it.

The driver finished with the harness and climbed to the box. The yard man called the boarding, and the three passengers at the window began gathering their bags. Theron registered Calden at the far edge of the yard, near the factor's shed — the gondola was still docked in the harbor, and a steward's departure paperwork ran long — but Calden was watching the coach, not the passengers, with the particular stillness of a man waiting to see something depart safely. He did not come forward.

Esherel was not here. He had not expected her to be.

The boy loaded the last case onto the boot and the yard man checked it off his list. The coach door opened.

Theron reached into his left coat pocket.

The fish sat in his palm. He looked at it for a moment — birch or ash, the fin at its imprecise angle, the wood worn to the texture of something that had been held a long time. Then he held it out to her.

Sable looked at it, then at him.

He didn't say anything. There was nothing particular to say.

She took it. She put it in the breast pocket of her traveling coat, the inside one, the same motion she used for the notebook. The coach door was open behind her, the driver settled, the other passengers already in.

She picked up her bag.

"Don't let the Marbeth letter sit," she said.

"I won't."

She went up the step and into the coach. The door latched.

The yard man stepped back, the driver released the brake, and the horses moved. Theron watched the coach clear the yard gate and take the south road, the iron wheels loud for a moment on the stone and then quieter as the road softened to packed earth past the factor's sheds.

Then it was gone behind the chandler's building.

---

He stood in the yard for a moment. Calden had gone. The second coach was still being loaded. A woman was arguing with the booking clerk about something, not loudly, in the tone of someone with a legitimate complaint and the patience to press it.

He took out his notebook.

The Marbeth entry was at the top of the current page: *H. Marbeth, Vantrel, west harbour Mirren. Kolach vouching. Letter of introduction: afternoon.* He added a second line below it — *Follow-up: Mirren magistrate, formal interview request, Fenn Kolach, Vantrel Secured Storage* — and below that, in the smaller hand he used for threads that were open but not yet moving: *Renn: wrong frame confirmed; Sannholt thread active; Sable in transit.*

That was the shape of it. Two threads. One moving south on the coach road. One waiting on a letter that would come back from Mirren in ten days or fourteen.

He closed the notebook and walked back through the yard gate onto the Halverstow street, where the harbor was going about its business in the middle-morning light and the Arms was three blocks north and the magistrate's clerk had a letter of introduction to draft by afternoon.

The case had the right names in it now. That was something. The rest was work.
