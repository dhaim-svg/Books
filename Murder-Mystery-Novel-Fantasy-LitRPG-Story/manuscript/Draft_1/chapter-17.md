# Chapter 17 — The Magistrate and the Debt

The magistrate's aide knocked at the second bell. Theron had the cloth bag in his left coat pocket and his notebook in his right, and he answered the door.

The magistrate herself came in behind the aide. Compact, somewhere in her fifties, grey at both temples, a port authority sash worn flat and undecorated. She looked at the room, at Theron, at the desk with its two chairs, and made a small precise nod.

"Mavik. Investigator class, Conservatory graduation — three weeks ago." She had already read the wire. "You're the one who found the body."

"Yes. Theron Mavik." He pulled the second chair out for her. "The steward found the cabin. I examined the room."

"Halved it." She sat. Her aide took up the near wall with a ledger board and a stub of chalk. "Give me the names, the facts, and the outstanding questions. Don't give me a narrative."

Theron did not give her a narrative. "The deceased is Joren Aldis. Working illusionist, no formal credentials on record, contracted by Lord Petric Vannic of estate-of-record under the name of his factor, one Berric Toll of Mirren west harbour. The contract was an identity performance: Aldis was to travel the Halverstow run as Lord Vannic; Vannic traveled separately under a false name in third class, cabin three-T, to evade an estate obligation. Aldis was found in the first-class cabin twelve at second watch, third day of the run. Time of death three to five hours prior. Cabin sealed from the interior; no wound present; cause of death not yet confirmed."

The aide's chalk moved. The magistrate's expression did not.

"Primary suspect pool," Theron continued. "Third class. One passenger — Maret Fell, cabin seven-T, east Mirren — shares a port with the factor. Her account is clean and specific in a manner consistent with preparation. Investigation on that thread is not yet complete. Sannholt connection present in the Toll correspondence; not yet actioned."

"Vannic." The magistrate said the name like a line item.

"Cooperative. He'll give you the full account; he's been expecting it. He hired Aldis for the performance and met him twice — once at the factor's office in Mirren, once at dock before boarding. He has no motive I've identified for Aldis's death, and his presence in third class under a false name is adequately explained by the estate obligation."

She looked at him for a moment. "Are you keeping anything back."

"No."

"You're sure."

"I'm an Investigator. I have one active suspect lead and one outstanding location thread. Both are documented. I'll give you the case notes when this is finished."

A flat, considering pause. Then she made a short mark on her own notebook, the kind of mark that meant *acceptable, proceed*. "I'll see Vannic now."

Theron went to the door and asked the aide posted outside to bring him. When Vannic came through the cabin door, he looked exactly as he had in third class the morning they had found him: overheated, apologetic, wearing yesterday's second-best waistcoat. He had his hat in both hands.

"Magistrate," he said. "I — this is entirely my —"

"Sit down," the magistrate said. "Tell me about Berric Toll."

Vannic sat. He told her about Berric Toll. He did it in the agonized, digressive fashion of a man who had been rehearsing a story and kept catching himself adding disclaimers, and the magistrate let him go until she had what she needed and then said "stop" and he stopped and she asked the three questions that mattered and he answered them. The aide's chalk went. The magistrate wrote two lines. Theron, standing near the porthole, watched and said nothing.

At the end she said: "You hired a man under false papers to perform your legal identity on a transit gondola and that man died under circumstances still under investigation. The estate obligation question is not my office. Failure to report an identity substitution is, technically, a harbor infraction, but I am going to note the circumstances and move on. You will remain in port and available for further questions. Yes?"

"Yes," Vannic said, in the voice of a man grateful to have a yes left in him.

"Go."

He went. The door closed. The magistrate looked at Theron.

"The Fell lead," she said. "If it breaks, you have my office at the Gray Quay. I want a written report by the evening bell, Aldis on the front page, the Sannholt thread on the second."

"You'll have it."

She rose, tucked her notebook under her arm, and looked at him one more time. Not assessing. Just noting. "You're three weeks out of the Conservatory."

"Yes."

"The body was sealed in a cabin with seventeen other passengers for two days and you held the wireless silence."

"The captain held the wireless silence."

"Yes, and you asked him to." She nodded the same small precise nod. "I'll read the report."

She left.

Theron stood with his notebook and looked at the porthole and the still harbor outside it for a moment. Then he wrote *magistrate interviewed — Vannic cleared, Fell thread noted, Sannholt on file* and closed the notebook.

---

Esherel was waiting in the first-class corridor when he came out, her luggage still stacked against the bulkhead. She had not disembarked.

She looked at him and then at the spot six inches past him and said, "I'll take my introduction now."

"He's still aboard," Theron said.

"I know. I've been waiting."

He brought her to the day-cabin. Vannic was standing near the far chair, having apparently come back when the magistrate left, because Theron had not told him to go far and he had not gone far. When he saw Esherel he straightened up and took a breath, which was not the worst sign.

Theron positioned himself at the writing desk and said nothing. He had told Esherel he would be present as a witness. He was present.

Esherel set her folio on the desk, opened it to the first page, and looked at Vannic. "Forty-one thousand and five hundred crowns," she said. "Two years of unpaid back third on the southern wine contract, which was extended at your estate's request on the understanding that the Customs-error loss was under review." She turned one page. "The review concluded eighteen months ago. I have the letter."

"I — yes," Vannic said. "I know about the letter."

"The interest has continued to accumulate." She turned another page. "This is the full accounting. I'm going to leave it with you. I'd like you to read it before the end of the week."

Vannic looked at the folio the way a man looked at a summons he had already been served and was hoping might dissolve. He didn't take it from her; she set it on the desk.

"You had him hire an illusionist," she said. "To fly on a gondola in your place. While you hid in third class."

"To be fair, I didn't — I was avoiding —"

"Duke Esseven's daughter. Yes, I know. Calden told me. He told me rather quickly when I asked, which tells me either he doesn't like you or he simply treats everyone equitably." She closed the outer folio cover. "I am not interested in the betrothal. I am interested in the forty-one thousand five hundred crowns and the additional nine months of interest, which you will find itemized on the third page."

Vannic opened his mouth, looked at the folio, and shut it again.

"You've had the accounting for two years," she said. "If there's a dispute, name the specific line."

He looked at Theron. Theron looked back at him in a manner that communicated clearly that he had been introduced as a witness, not a resource.

Vannic picked up the folio.

He read the first page slowly and turned to the second and read that. He was not a fast reader. Esherel waited with the particular patience of a woman who had been waiting eighteen months for a specific page to be opened. Theron found himself watching her hands: the folio case under her elbow, her fingers still, the kind of stillness that came from having run out of things to do with restlessness and finding it inefficient.

"The interest calculation," Vannic finally said, "uses the standard rate."

"It uses the contract rate," Esherel said. "Which is slightly lower. Page three, footnote."

He found the footnote. He read it. He set the folio on the desk.

"I can't pay it from here."

"I know that."

"I mean — the estate, the board has —"

"I am aware of how estate boards function," she said. "This is a letter of acknowledgment." She produced it from the outer folio pocket and set it beside the accounting sheets. "It says: debt acknowledged, amount agreed, schedule to follow by the first of next month. It doesn't commit you to a payment date. It commits you to a number."

Vannic looked at the letter of acknowledgment. It was one page, brief, with a line for his signature and a line for his title and a line for the date, and nothing else.

"That's all," Esherel said. "Today."

He picked up the pen from the writing desk.

He looked at it for a moment. Theron had the sense that this was the first time, in the entire run of things, that Vannic had a clear lane ahead of him — one action, specific, no betrothal, no dead illusionist, no identity documents to maintain. Just a pen and a number he knew was correct.

He signed it.

Esherel took it back, read the signature, folded it into her folio. Her expression did not change except in a very small way that might have been relief and might have been the particular satisfaction of a woman putting a two-year account in order.

"Thank you," she said.

Vannic made a sound that was not quite a word. He was still holding the pen.

---

Theron left them sorting through the schedule discussion. It would take as long as it took; Esherel had both the patience and the documentation to outlast any objection Vannic could field. He went down to the gangway.

The Halverstow waterfront was fifteen feet below the gondola's lower deck. He could see the quayside factors' offices from the rail, the cargo ledgers going in and out, the day-labourers on the rope gangs, a harbor warden checking manifests at the second berth. Somewhere past the grain stores, the Gray Quay stretched east along the port. The magistrate's office would be out there. Maret Fell was somewhere out there too, if she had already disembarked.

Sable was at the foot of the gangway.

She was talking to a man in a dock-pilot's coat, older, grey-headed, with the particular solidity of someone who had worked the same fifty feet of water for twenty years. She had a small slip of paper; he was reading it with the expression of someone who preferred information condensed. Then he said something and she nodded, and he said something else and she glanced up at Theron on the deck above.

He came down the gangway. The dock boards were salt-bleached, with a smell of tar and low tide and caulk.

"This is Master Olen," Sable said, without preamble. "He works the pilot's association. He knows the Mirren run."

Theron looked at the pilot. "Berric Toll."

The pilot made a face that was not quite a confirmation and not quite its opposite. The kind of face people made when they had an opinion about a name and had learned that opinions in public about factors were bad for repeat work. "I know *of* him."

"Maret Fell. East Mirren harbour, shipping assessment."

The pilot's face shifted slightly. Less ambivalence; more consideration. "I've seen the name on the load assessments. She's done three or four runs through the east yard in the last — eight months, nine. Independent work. No standing contract that I know of."

"She's on this gondola. Traveling from Mirren to Halverstow."

"Plenty do."

"What does shipping assessment in the east yard normally look like?" Theron asked. "As work."

The pilot shrugged, unhurried. "Small-cargo verification mostly. Spot checks, load tolerances, sometimes customs-assist when the yard's short. You know somebody's load, you can find something wrong with somebody else's if you need leverage. Or it's entirely clean. Hard to say without the specific jobs." He handed the slip of paper back to Sable. "What's she done."

"We don't know yet," Theron said.

The pilot nodded. This was also, evidently, how the east yard worked. "If she's coming through Halverstow on the regular, the Gray Quay factors' board will have her name. They log all the independent assessors coming off the Mirren line. Saves time when they need one in a hurry."

Theron wrote *Gray Quay factors' board — Fell name check* in his notebook.

"The east yard," he said. "Would it overlap with the west? Berric Toll is west harbour, Mirren."

"Not naturally." The pilot considered. "The yards don't share much. Independent assessors work where the money is. If she was getting work from the west side, she'd have a reason for it. Or someone made it worth crossing."

He left it at that, in the manner of a man who had given what was freely givable and was now returning to the day's work.

Theron watched him go back along the quay. The harbor light was flat and direct, the late morning clear enough to read the names on the grain-store walls from where he stood.

Sable said, "He came off the early-morning run. Calden arranged the introduction; Joriss has the contact for the dock association on file."

"I know." He looked at the note. "She's on the factors' board, she's been in the east yard nine months, she knows Toll's name but hasn't dealt with him personally." He turned the page. "That last one came from her directly."

"Yes."

"Which is either true, or it's the kind of thing you say when you'd rather have the question stop."

Sable said nothing, which was how she agreed with something she didn't want to agree with too quickly.

He put the notebook away and looked at the magistrate's office buildings, gray-fronted beyond the cargo sheds. "The Gray Quay board," he said. "That's the next step."

"This afternoon?"

"After the written report." He looked out at the harbor — fishing boats, flat water, a pair of gulls working the far ropes. Somewhere in the hold of the gondola there was still a sealed first-class cabin with a locked-room geometry he hadn't finished explaining, and a name that was in the case log now, correctly placed.

*[Investigator — Status: Fell thread active. One address in Sannholt, one board list to check. The case is not tidier than it was. It is more accurate.]*

He read it and folded his notebook.

"Gray Quay this afternoon," he said. "Bring what you have on the Sannholt contact."

Sable tucked the empty slip of paper into her coat. "The Pessel name."

"Yes."

She nodded, once, and turned back toward the gangway.

He stood a moment longer at the quayside rail, looking at the harbor. A boat was coming in to the near berth with the particular low-riding heaviness of a full catch, and the dockers were already moving. Someone on it called out a name and someone on the quay called back, and for a moment the sound of it carried clearly across the water.

Then it didn't.

He went up the gangway to write the report.
