# Chapter 12 — The Page Finished

He was in the captain's gallery at the open of the second-watch coffee because the page in his pocket had decided overnight that it wanted finishing, and he had decided over the same hours not to argue with it.

The bow window was already at the brass urn. Two of the second-watch passengers were standing at it the way people stood at a long forward window on the tenth morning of a flight — comfortably, without commenting on the view, the way a person stood at a kitchen window in a house he had lived in for a year. He took the bench at the end that was not the brass, and put the slate flat on his knee.

The page was the page he had built yesterday.

Esherel at the top. Tannin and Vellaine at the tiers below her. The two windows in series, drawn on a single line. The corridor in pencil. The lunch scene with its tick. The four-crate scene with its tick. The three small lines in the margin under cabin twelve that said *carry-frame*, sitting in their margin the way he had left them.

He had not written the underline on *opportunity*.

He worked the late window, which was the column he had not finished. Calden had given him the three-minute gap on the seventh day, the small clean window in the back end of the fourth watch when the steward had been on the deck-three corridor and the deck-four corridor had been unwatched. He drew the gap on the line and put a tick under it. He drew Esherel's table position at the dining-room arch, the corridor sightline from her table, the half-step kink he and Sable had walked on the seventh day.

He had built the page on the sightline from the table.

He had not, on inspection, built it on the sightline from her cabin door.

He capped the pencil and looked at the column for a count, then for another count. It was not a thing he needed. It was a thing he wanted. The page would close cleanly with the second sightline, or it would close cleanly without it; he was aware of which of those was the careful answer, and he was also aware, in the small honest part of his attention he had begun to require of himself before he made a decision, that he wanted the second sightline because the page deserved it.

He decided he would check at the open of the lunch service, when she was at her table.

He uncapped the pencil and drew a small *cabin* under the corridor in pencil. He capped the pencil again and laid it parallel to the slate's top edge. The page was patient. He left it on the bench beside him and watched the bow window for a while, where the drakes were on their long approach against a sky that had thinned to the pale clean blue of an afternoon that intended to behave.

---

He went down to deck three at the second-bell call to lunch.

The corridor was empty in the small clean way the deck-three corridor was empty at the open of the lunch service — Calden up at the dining-room arch, the second-watch passengers up at the urns, the cook one deck down sending the first plates up. He walked the corridor at the pace he had walked it twenty times now and stopped at the threshold of Esherel's cabin door.

He did not turn the handle.

He stood at the threshold and looked down the corridor at cabin twelve. The sightline carried the half-step kink. From the doorframe at exactly the angle a person stood at when she was closing her own door behind her, the cabin twelve door took itself out of the line at the count of a half-step. He filed it. He took the second step that put the cabin twelve door back into the line, the same step he and Sable had taken on the seventh day. He filed that.

A small clean sound came up through the deck plates from the galley two decks down, the small metal sound of the lemon plates being set on the cook's bench.

He went up the stair to the dining room with a tick to add to the column under *opportunity (late)*.

---

The dining room at the open of the lunch service was warm. Vellaine was at her window table with the book and the cold coffee, the same configuration she had been at every meal since the first morning. The Henners were at the far table. Their daughter had taken her egg today with less ceremony than yesterday and was working on a piece of toast with the concentration of a child who had decided toast was a more useful project.

Esherel was at her table.

The folio was closed. It had been closed at the third bell, he could see the way her hand was set on it. Her coffee was at her left. The bread on her plate had been broken cleanly in half.

He took the chair across from Vellaine, who did not look up.

"Lunch pot," he said.

"Less bright. More useful." Vellaine turned a page. "It is my favourite of the three. The Wyrm does the pots in a serious order."

"I had been wondering."

"I keep a small list, Investigator. I am here for the route, not for the symposium, as I have explained."

"You are here for the lunch pot."

"I am here for the lunch pot." She turned another page. "Are you finished with the page?"

He had not, he realized, told her he was working a page. The room had told her. The room had been telling her since the first morning, possibly. He noticed this without alarm and set it under one of the small categories he kept now for the way information moved on the gondola — categories he had not had at boarding and would not have written down at the academy, where information moved through forms and registries and not through the cook to the galley boy to Sable to a quarter-turn on a chair.

"One tick from finishing it," he said.

"Then you should speak to her with the page finished."

"I intend to. This evening, after the supper."

"Where."

"The saloon. The long porthole, if she will sit."

Vellaine closed the book on her thumb. She did not look up at him. She looked at the porthole at her elbow, where the pale clean blue had carried on with its careful business since the first morning, and after a count she said:

"I will be at the long porthole this evening."

"You do not have to."

"I do not have to." She opened the book again. "I would prefer to. A room with witnesses is a different room than one without, and you did well in the room yesterday, but a saloon at the close of the bronzed hour is a smaller room. I will be at the porthole. You will not have to see me. I will see you."

He sat with this for a count.

"Thank you, Dr. Vellaine."

"Mm. Have the bread."

He had the bread.

Esherel finished her own bread and her coffee in the careful order in which she had finished them at every meal of the run. She rose. She put the folio under her arm. She went out through the forward arch without turning the room. Vellaine turned a page. The Henners' daughter had reduced the toast to a useful crescent. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the bench. The coffee, on second pass, was very good.

---

He went back to the gallery after lunch and sat on the bench at the end that was not the brass.

The page was where he had left it. He picked it up and looked at it. He drew the second sightline from Esherel's cabin door under the corridor line, and put a tick under it. He looked at the column under *opportunity (late)*. The two ticks were the two ticks. The early window. The late window. The corridor sightline from the table. The corridor sightline from the door. Demeanor under stress, two ticks. The folio closed at the third bell, today, as a third small tick under the same heading if he chose to use it; he chose not to. He was not in the column for completeness. He was in it for what was carried.

He uncapped the pencil. He held it for a small clean breath. He wrote *opportunity (late)* under Esherel's column at the bottom of the page, in the careful hand he had begun to favour, and he drew a single line under it.

The underline took the slate seriously. The slate took the underline seriously back.

He capped the pencil. He laid it parallel to the slate's top edge at the angle Sable laid hers. He looked at the two underlines on the page, *demeanor* and *opportunity*, and they sat on the slate the way the two drakes on the outside of the formation sat against the rigging on the bow, drawn forward to a shared line.

The column had closed.

> *[Investigator — Insight +1. A column closes when the careful hand stops adding to it.]*

He read it twice. He thought about flinching, the small habitual flinch he had not yet broken himself of when the System said something dry at him, and chose, again, not to. The dry was, on examination, friendly. It had been friendly for some days now. He had not entirely decided what to do with that.

He did not write the decision about the saloon on the page. He filed it under his own attention, where he had been filing other things since the third morning and had begun to trust the filing.

Below the gallery window, the drakes were in their formation. Petal had not yet handed Gravel the slot on the return. The harness's note came up through the bench in its long constant. He listened for the secondary note he had named yesterday and was not, on inspection, surprised that it was still there, a fraction below the main. He left it in the cabin-twelve margin and did not connect it to anything. He was not at that page.

He was at this one.

He put the slate under his arm and went to find Sable.

He did not have to. She was at the gallery door at the open of the bronzed hour, standing at the frame the way she did when she had come to find him and was prepared to be found doing nothing.

"Saloon," she said.

"Saloon."

"There is the soup with the green oil on it tonight. The cook did the morning fish well and is showing off."

"I had heard."

"From whom."

"From the floor of the corridor on my way up. The lemon plates were going on the bench when I came up from deck three."

"Ah." She turned and walked. "Then we are both being informed by the gondola."

"It is restful."

"I had told you."

He went after her with the slate under his arm. She did not slow for him; he did not hurry. They arrived at the aft stair in the rhythm they had begun to walk in, slower than his step and slightly faster than hers, and went down it.

---

The saloon at the open of the second-watch service smelled of the bread and the new soup and the lemon. Calden was at the door. He nodded at Sable. He nodded at Theron. He did not nod again.

They took the corner table at the long porthole.

"Six," Sable said, when the small plate came.

"Six."

"He has corrected his correction."

"He has."

"The cook is a careful man. He is overcorrecting because the first correction was over the bread schedule and the bread schedule was, on examination, not in error. He is restoring the correct count and adding a count to me as an apology for last night."

"You are being paid in biscuits."

"It is the running rate." She moved two onto his side without comment.

The soup was the thicker kind with the small white pieces, and the green oil sat on top in the small clean way the cook did it on a good fishing day. They ate it the way two people ate a soup on the third day they had eaten in this saloon, which was unhurriedly. The bread came. The biscuits sat in their small plate to be earned.

"The bolt on the port runner," he said.

"Held."

"You asked Wisp."

"I asked the bolt. Wisp confirmed."

"The bolt's account."

"The bolt's account is that it did not loosen. Wisp has decided to believe the bolt for one more watch. They are in a small period of mutual professional fiction."

"All three drakes and a bolt."

"It is a productive gondola."

The biscuits did not move for a while. They were content to be the biscuits.

She turned her cup a quarter so the handle pointed away from him, the same small move she had made the night before. He had begun to notice he was tracking it. He chose not to mind that he was. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the bench, and the harness's note came up through the floor, and underneath the note when he listened for it the secondary note was still there.

He set the spoon down.

"After supper," he said.

She did not pretend not to understand. She held the look.

"After supper, then."

"The long porthole in the saloon. Vellaine will be at the next table. She offered."

"She did, did she."

"I had not asked."

"Of course you had not asked. Dr. Vellaine does not require asking for a thing she has decided to do." Sable turned the cup another quarter, then put the handle back where it had been. "And me."

"I had not asked you either."

"I had not waited to be asked."

"I am — grateful."

"Mm."

"I would have asked."

"You would have. I have been at the corner of the long porthole on the saloon's second day, on the fourth day, and on the seventh. I am at the corner of the long porthole the way Vellaine is at the window table in the dining room. It is a small place that I have decided is mine for the run. I will be there this evening as I have been there on three other evenings, with the small additional reason that you have decided to do the talking part of the work this evening at the table beside mine."

"You make it sound restful."

"It will not be restful." She broke a biscuit in half and ate one half. "Have the soup."

He had the soup. The talk did not move into the case again, because she did not move it there and he did not. They talked, after a stretch, briefly, about the cottage on the coast — the small inheritance, the great-aunt who had been a name on a card at Sable's mother's house and not much else, the paperwork at the other end that had its own slow texture. He did not press. He had not pressed on it at any meal in the run; he had begun to understand that the not-pressing was a thing she was, in the unfussy way of a person who did not say what she was grateful for, grateful for. She moved the biscuit plate a half-inch toward him.

"The view," she said, when the soup was gone and the bread was gone and the biscuits were down to two on the plate. "From the long porthole at the open of the bronzed hour."

"I had heard it was good."

"It is good." She did not look at him while she said it. "You should sit by it before the supper proper, so the view is in the room before the room becomes the talking part."

"Good advice."

"I have been at the table on three evenings, as I said."

"You have an investment."

"I have an investment."

He almost smiled. He did not, but the not-smiling was, again, the kind the table got the use of.

---

He took the slate up to his cabin at the close of the bronzed hour.

He set it on the writing desk parallel to the desk's far edge, in the angle he had set it on the past two nights. He looked at the page once. The column was closed. The two underlines were the two underlines. The carry-frame note in the cabin-twelve margin sat in its margin and was patient and unconnected. He did not return to it. He did not open the slate.

> *[Investigator — Quest active. Nine hours, twenty minutes to Halverstow Port.]*

He read it. He moved his thumb off the graduation pin. He had not noticed it arrive at the pin; he had begun, in the past two days, to notice this less often, which he had begun, in the past two days, to consider its own small competence.

The cabin smelled faintly of his father's dispensary oil.

He took his good shirt off the hook in the wardrobe and changed into it for the supper. He had not put it on for a supper at the academy more than twice in three years; he was, on inspection, willing to be a little careful tonight. He buttoned the collar in the small mirror over the writing desk and ran his thumb along the seam at the cuff where his mother had set the stitches the week he had left home, the careful uneven hand of a woman who had not been a tailor and had refused to let him buy a shirt for the journey.

He stood at the desk for a count.

The page had closed cleanly. The conversation was the conversation he had been working toward for ten days, and he was, in the small honest part of his attention, ready for it. He was not rehearsing the sentences. He had decided two days ago that he would let the room have the conversation, the way he had let the room have the answer at lunch yesterday, and he had not changed his mind about it since.

He moved the slate a quarter-inch on the desk to align it with the desk's edge.

He put the lamp at trim.

Below the cabin window the drakes were on the long approach against a sky that had already gone to bronze and was beginning, at the western edge of itself, to take the first warm note of evening. Petal handed Gravel the slot on the return. The slot was two breaths wider than it needed to be. Gravel took the air. He did not acknowledge.

Theron pulled the cabin door closed softly behind him.

The corridor was still warm with the bronzed light. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the floor. He went down to the supper.
