# Chapter 27 — Unpicking a Profile

The Arms was quieter on Sunday than on the working mornings, and Theron took the corner table by the window because the corner table by the window had been his on every morning since he had arrived and the dining room had decided, without consulting him, that it was now where he sat. The pot came without asking. The toast came without asking. The host nodded at his case book and went back to the kitchen.

He opened the case book to the page he had written the column on yesterday and read it through.

The column was four lines long. *Cabin nine-T. Booking credential through Kolach. Sannholt destination. Status: in front of us.* He had stopped there yesterday afternoon because he had wanted to stop there. He picked up the pencil now to keep going.

He wrote *Profile* across the top of the next clean spread and underlined it once.

Then he sat with the pencil over the page for a while.

The trouble was that he knew how to write a profile. He had been writing profiles since his second-year practicum, and the format on the page was a habit of the wrist. Background. Motive shape. Method affinity. Network. Behavioural signature. He could fill the boxes for any noble in the manifest's first-class column inside an hour, and had: six of them, in the first three days of the case, when he had still been working the Vannic frame.

The trouble was that the format was a frame too.

He set the pencil down and looked at the column from yesterday.

The noble-rivalry profile was a specific shape. It assumed a person who had stood to gain (money, position, marriage, suppression of a scandal) in a public economy where motive was visible because reputation was the currency. The profile filtered for that economy. Halsa Renn had moved through his case file for three weeks because nothing about her fit the filter, and he had read the absence of fit as absence of relevance.

That was not a small misreading. That was the entire training of his first three years.

He picked the pencil up.

*Profile — Renn, Halsa,* he wrote, slowly, because he did not want to drift back into the old shape while pretending to be writing a new one. Then he stopped and wrote, underneath: *Question one — what is she doing on the gondola.*

Sannholt destination, said cash, said family business. He had taken those answers at face value when he had taken her interview, because in the noble-rivalry frame a cash third-class through-passenger heading to a southern coastal town on family business was the lowest-tier reading there was. He had been right that the answers were not for him. He had been wrong that they were not answers.

The answers were the cover. Cash because the booking credential had been placed through Kolach, and the credential was the reference, and the cash was the part that made the reference unnecessary on the manifest's face. Family business because a stated reason for travel that nobody would ask about was the cleanest cover available. South coast because Sannholt was where her actual business was, and her actual business, whatever it was, was not the kind that named itself in a harbor registry.

He wrote: *Cover designed to pass a Wyrm registry, not to pass an investigator.*

He set the pencil down and read the line and picked the pencil up again.

That was the part. The cover had not been designed to pass an investigator because the cover had not anticipated one. A Wyrm departure registry-checked a third-class booking by reference, payment, and stated destination, and Halsa Renn's cover had answered the three questions a registry asked. The registry's questions were not his questions. His questions only mattered because the cabin twelve door had been bolted from the inside and a man had been found dead inside it, and the body of an illusionist named Joren Aldis had pulled an investigator onto a Wyrm route that had not been planning to carry one.

He wrote: *She did not expect me. She expected the manifest.*

That was not a flattering line about himself. It was also accurate. He left it.

He went on.

*Question two — what does she do for a living.* He wrote that and stopped. He did not know. The Wyrm interview had given him *travel-through, south coast, family,* and the booking credential through Kolach told him she was inside the same professional channel as Joren, which was a channel made for working mages whose contract paperwork did not generate the standard references. That was not a profession. That was a category of profession.

He wrote: *Working mage, contract-side, no formal credentials registered. Inside Joren's channel.*

It was thin. It was as much as he had. He went on.

*Question three — relationship to Joren Aldis.* Sable's letter from yesterday had given him the apprenticeship shape: Pessel had hosted a coastal young woman three or four times a year, formerly apprenticed to a man in the same magical trades. The landlady had said *apprenticed*. The landlady had not said to whom. He wrote: *Apprenticeship line — pending Pessel confirmation. Source: Sable / Whitepost landlady.*

Then he wrote, because it was the next thing on the page he had to be honest about: *Motive — unknown. Working assumption: personal, not professional. Basis: she boarded a flight Joren was on, under a placed credential, with cover designed for registries, and the death method (working assumption pending) was suited to a closed cabin and an illusionist target.*

He underlined *unknown* once, lightly, and set the pencil down.

He drank coffee.

The noble-rivalry profile would have given him a motive in line one. *Wants the dukedom. Wants the dowry. Wants the silence about the affair.* He could write those sentences in his sleep. He could not write a sentence here because he did not know what Joren had done to a working mage who had apprenticed to him, eight or ten or fifteen years ago, in a coastal fishing town Theron had never been to. The motive lived inside a life he had not had a category for.

He looked at the column.

He had missed her because his training pattern had no slot for her. That was the finding. He wrote it at the bottom of the page in a single line: *Profile he had been trained to write: noble-rivalry economy, public motive, reputational currency. Profile required: contract-side network, private motive, personal currency. The first profile excluded the entire shape of the second.*

He read the line.

He read it again.

He did not feel particularly clever about it. He felt the way he had felt after the recall passage in Sable's cabin. The feeling of having been carrying a wrong shape for a long time and having it set down. The setting-down was a relief. The carrying had cost him three weeks.

He turned the page and wrote the morning's date and the place and *Working profile, R. Renn, draft one* at the top, and copied the lines across in the order he wanted them in front of him for the magistrate tomorrow. He did not include the line about himself. That one stayed on the page in the case book, where he could look at it again later and not forget what it had cost.

The pot was empty. He signalled the host for a fresh one and went on.

---

By second bell the post-coach from Sannholt was due, and Theron had read his working profile through four times and stopped revising it because he was past the point where revision improved it.

The boy from the station found him in the dining room with the second letter, which had come up on the morning return run and which carried the Whitepost Inn's wax. Theron gave the boy the same small coin and broke the seal at the table.

The letter was four pages this time.

*Theron —*

*I am back at the inn. The meeting was an hour and a quarter. I am writing this while it is in front of me because the shape of what she said will compress by tomorrow and I want you to have the uncompressed version.*

*Pessel met me at the door of her front room at the named bell. The room is small and clean and faces north. There were two chairs by the window and a tea-set already on the side table, which she had set out before I arrived. She is in her early fifties, grey, very still in the shoulders, the kind of stillness that comes from a profession that requires you not to react to other people's documents. She made the tea. She handed me a cup. She sat down. Then she said:*

*"Tell me whether he died well."*

*That was the first thing she said, after the cup. I told her what I could. I told her that the death was inside a sealed cabin, that there was no visible wound, that you had said, when I asked, that whatever had been done to him had been done by someone who knew how to do it cleanly. She listened without interrupting. When I was finished she nodded once and drank her tea and said: "Thank you. It was the question I needed answered before I asked the others."*

*Then she told me about him.*

*She has known Joren, known him as Joren and not as the working name he used in Mirren, for eleven years. He came through Sannholt three or four times a year. He sat in this front room. He brought her papers to look at, the small contract-side paperwork his work generated, marriage settlements that were not exactly marriages, inheritance papers that were not exactly inheritances. She made them clean enough for the registries he had to file with. She did not ask him what the work was. She had a sense. She is not naive.*

She turned the page. The paragraph below was newer ink, written after a pause.

*She told me his Sannholt errand. I am putting this here exactly as she said it, because I do not want to interpret it for you. She said: "He had been putting money aside for the child for eleven years. The arrangement was that when he had enough to come home, he would come home. He told me, the spring before last, that he was close. The Wyrm route was the last large piece of work. He intended to come back south, settle the money on the child's mother, and stay. The child does not yet know he existed. The child's mother knows. I held the money."*

*She held the money, Theron. She has been holding it for two years. She had been waiting for him to come home and tell the mother what he had been doing for eleven years, and to be in the child's life as something other than the man the mother never named. She said this without any tone in it. She said it the way someone says the address of a house they have walked past many times.*

*I did not write any of this down in front of her. I am writing it now.*

He turned the page.

*Then she said, without my asking, that he had trained someone, once. A young woman, coastal, the same fishing-town stretch he came from. She used to come through Sannholt as well, three or four times a year, sometimes overlapping with him and sometimes not. Pessel said she stopped coming about two years ago. Pessel said she did not know whether the apprentice had known Joren was putting money aside. Pessel said the apprentice had known about the work, because she had done the same work, because that was what an apprentice meant in their trade.*

*Pessel did not say the apprentice's name.*

*I did not ask it.*

*She told me this with the same evenness she had told me about the money. She did not link the two. She told me the apprentice had stopped coming two years ago, and the money had been held for two years, and she did not say whether the two facts were connected in her mind. I think they may be, but I do not know, and I am writing what she said.*

*I asked her one question. I asked her whether Joren had been afraid, on his last visit, of anyone in particular. She thought about it. Then she said: "He was not afraid of a person. He was tired of the work, and he was running out of time on the arrangement, and he wanted very badly to come home. That is not the same as being afraid. He was a man who had taken work he should not have taken, more than once, and he had decided some time ago that he would carry that himself and not bring it into this room. If he had been afraid of someone specifically, he would not have told me. He had a courtesy about what he brought through this door."*

*That was the meeting. She thanked me for coming. She asked, at the door, whether I would come back if there was more to say. I said yes. She thanked me again. The whole of it took an hour and a quarter, and I do not think she lied to me once.*

*The fish is in my coat. She did not ask about Joren's effects. I did not offer.*

*— S.*

He sat with the letter for a long minute.

The Sunday afternoon was doing its quiet thing through the dining-room window. Two factors at the next table were debating the price of slate. Someone in the kitchen had begun on a bread that smelled of caraway. He let the letter sit in front of him until the smell had filled the room, and then he refolded it carefully along its existing creases and laid it inside the case book.

He turned to the working profile he had drafted that morning.

He picked up the pencil.

He wrote, under *Question three — relationship to Joren Aldis*: *Apprentice line confirmed. Pessel: Joren trained a young woman, coastal, same fishing-town region, came through Sannholt three or four times a year for an extended period. Stopped approximately two years ago. Name not given by Pessel; not requested.*

He stopped. He read the column.

He added a fourth line, under the others: *Question four — what changed two years ago.*

He did not answer it. He underlined the question once and set the pencil down.

The line about the money he did not put in the working column. That was not yet the column's business. He turned to a blank page at the back of the case book, the place he kept the things he had been told that he had not yet earned the right to use, and wrote: *Joren had been putting money aside for the child for eleven years. He intended the Wyrm route to be his last large job. The child's mother knows. The child does not. Pessel held the money.*

He read that. He read it again. He let it sit.

*[Investigator — Working column updated: Renn, Halsa. Apprentice line confirmed via Pessel (Sable). Question four open: what changed two years ago.]*

The System line had not asked his permission. He read it and let it be.

---

He drew a fresh sheet for the reply.

*Sable —*

*I have the letter. Read it twice. Do not compress; you wrote it the right way.*

*The apprentice line confirms the column. I have not added the name; Pessel did not give it and you were right not to ask. The wire transcript will give us the personal-referral source from Kolach's end in three or four more days, and that will close the line from the Halverstow side. We will have the name from one side or the other. Probably both.*

*The thing she told you about the money. I have put that in the back of the book, separately. It is not the column's business yet. It may not become the column's business. It is hers and the child's mother's, and we are not the people who carry it. I am writing it down because you wrote it down, and so that one of us has it on the right page if it ever needs to be on a page.*

*The two-year gap is now a question on the working column. I do not know what it means. I am not going to guess at it from this side. If Pessel offers more when you go back, take it on her terms again.*

*The morning here was a profile draft. I will tell you about it when we are in the same room. The short of it is that my training pattern was the wrong filter for the person we are looking at, and the wrong filter excluded her cleanly for three weeks. I had not understood, before this morning, how clean an exclusion that was. It is a finding about my work. It is also a finding about her.*

*The fish stays where it is.*

*— T.*

He sealed it, addressed it for the afternoon coach, and handed it to the host on his way past the kitchen.

Then he went back to the corner table, refilled the cup, and read the working profile through once more.

He did not make any changes to it.

The bread came out of the oven somewhere behind the kitchen door, and the smell moved through the dining room the way good smells do on Sundays, in no particular hurry. He let it.

Outside the window, the Halverstow harbour was doing its Sunday work at half speed. The tar smell from yesterday was gone. Somewhere down the quay, a child was being given instruction about a rope, in the patient tones of a parent who was going to give the same instruction four more times before the morning was out.

He turned the case book back to the column page and looked at the four questions in their order.

Three of them he could begin to answer.

The fourth was still open, and he was content to leave it open until something else arrived to begin the answer for him. The post-coach to Sannholt left at fourth bell. The wire transcript was due Wednesday or Thursday. The working profile was on a page, in the order he wanted it in front of him, and the morning's finding about himself was on a separate page where it would not be forgotten.

He drank the coffee while it was still hot.

It was, he reflected, the most honest morning's work he had done on the case to date. He was aware that this was a low bar, and he was aware that the awareness did not make the morning any less worth keeping.

He closed the case book.
