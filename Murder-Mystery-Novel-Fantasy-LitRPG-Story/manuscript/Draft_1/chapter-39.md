# Chapter 39 — The Address on the Page

The front room of the inn did not have very much to do at first bell of a Sunday in October. The basket-and-dog woman had finished her tea and left a small clean place at her table where the dog had set its chin on the wood for the duration. The carters in the lane outside had finished one load and were on the second. The barman had moved from glasses to spoons and was working a thin cloth over the bowl of one with the patience of a man who would have done the same thing at the same hour on any October morning anyone could think of.

Theron sat at the table he and Sable had eaten at and read the room without meaning to read it.

He had told himself, walking back up the lane, that he would not work the morning. The work was Sable's. He had walked her to the chandler's door because she had asked him to walk her to the chandler's door, and the door had closed behind her with the small definite sound a door closed with, and from that sound forward the morning was hers. The barman had brought him a cup of tea without making a thing of him being there alone, and a piece of bread beside it he had not asked for and did not feel he could now decline. The southern light came down the lane through the front window and put two slanted bars across the table.

He put his palm on the wood between the bars.

The wood was warm where the light had been on it for half an hour and cooler at the edges. He took his palm back.

He did, for a little while. The carters in the yard up the lane were a father and a son or near enough; the son was loading and the father was counting, the count being the part the father had kept for himself when he no longer wanted to lift the crates. The barman had a wedding ring on the hand that worked the cloth and not on the hand that held the spoon. He stopped himself there. The work was Sable's. He drank his tea.

---

She came back a little before second bell.

He heard the door before he saw her — the lane door, not the bar door this time — and then the small shift of the room's attention as the room registered an arrival and decided it was not for them. She came across the front room in her travelling coat, unbuttoned at the collar in the way she had unbuttoned it last night, with her notebook in her left hand and a folded piece of paper in her right and the second folded piece of paper between the first two fingers of the same right hand. Two papers in one hand, held loose enough that they were two and not one.

She sat down across from him. She set the notebook flat on the table. She put the two papers on the notebook, one on top of the other, without separating them yet.

The barman brought a cup without being asked. She inclined her half-inch to him and put her hand around the cup but did not drink.

"How is he," Theron said.

"He's well. He held the address since the twenty-eighth, like he said. He wanted to give it to me at his counter and not on the doorstep, which is the kind of thing he's particular about."

"All right."

"He gave me the address. And a paper of his own with two lines on it, which is what Joren had given him to hold for the family in case Joren didn't come south himself."

"All right."

She moved the top paper a half-inch to her left with the back of one finger so that the second paper showed at the edge.

"And he gave me this back."

The second paper was familiar to him in the way a piece of paper was familiar to a man who had watched another person read it across a passenger lounge on a long-ago morning in the air — the thick weight of it, the dark border, the particular fold of a property document carried in a coat pocket for more days than was good for the fold. The inheritance papers. Her own.

"He had it?"

"I'd brought it. I'd asked him last week whether his trade extended to a second opinion on a deed I had not yet read carefully, and he had said it did when it was wanted, and the more important thing was the address. I let the deed go to the side. He read it this morning while I waited for him to finish a sale of a length of cordage to a man who knew exactly the length he wanted and would not be hurried about it."

"All right."

She did not open either paper.

She turned the top one — Pessel's address — through ninety degrees with the same finger she had moved it with, and the writing on the outer face came up the right way to her, the inked name and place running in Pessel's small clerkly hand. She did not unfold it. She read the outer face. Theron read it across the table from her, which was the angle his class had been training him in for three years and the angle his eyes had now begun reading at without his asking them to.

The outer face had Joren's family's name at the top and a place beneath it.

The place was three lines: the parish, the road, the marker. The parish was *Tellan's Cross*. The road was *the inner coast road, by the chandler's stile*. The marker was *third house above the white stone*.

Sable did not say anything.

She moved her hand across the table and laid her flat palm on the second paper — her own deed — and looked at it without moving the paper at all. The deed was folded with its outer face down. The outer face would have her own destination on it, the place she had been going to claim since before she met him.

Theron did not turn it over.

He did not need to. Her hand was on it the way a hand was on a thing whose contents the hand already knew, and her stillness was the stillness of a person comparing two pieces of paper she did not need to lay side by side because the comparison was already finished. He had seen that stillness in her once before, three doors down a corridor on the first morning of the run, when a man had thanked a steward by name without performance, and she had stood with her hand on her own door latch and not moved for the time it took a corridor to be a corridor again.

He sat with his cup between his hands and waited.

After a little while she said, "I know that stretch."

"All right."

"The inner coast road runs north of where I'm going. Tellan's Cross is the next parish over. The white stone is at the fork above the chandler's stile, which the road takes left for Tellan's and right for the road my aunt's cottage sits on, two miles further. It's the same coast."

"All right."

"It's the same coast."

She said it twice, the second time the way a person said a thing twice when she was setting the second saying down in a different place than the first. Not as discovery. As placement. As the moving of a piece of paper from the desk to the file where the paper now belonged.

Theron did not say anything for a moment.

The morning had a great deal of work in it that was not theirs — the carters at their yard, the barman at his spoons, the basket-woman three doors up the lane. The work in the room they sat in was the quieter work of a piece of paper carried south on a coach and a piece of paper written out in a chandlery this morning, laid one on top of the other on the cover of a notebook.

"I didn't know," she said.

"I know."

"He didn't either. We sat in the back of the front room last week with the deed between us and he asked the parish twice because he wanted to hear it right, and I told him twice, and we talked about the road instead. He didn't put it together at his desk. He put it together when he looked at the deed again this morning with the address he had been holding for Joren beside it. He gave me the deed back with the address on the top, and he said the parish, and that was when he stood up."

"All right."

"He said he had thought about it overnight and would not say it for me. The address was the part that was mine to be told. The rest was the part I should be allowed to read off the page myself."

"He was right about that."

She took her hand off the deed.

She did not turn it over and she did not need to. The two papers sat one on top of the other on the cover of the notebook, and the outer face of the address was the only face of either paper that anyone in the room could read, and Theron could read it from across the table and was not reading it any more, and Sable was looking at her hand on the wood where her hand had been on the paper a moment ago.

The slanted bars of southern light had moved a finger's width along the table since the carters had finished the second load.

He read the line once and set it down inside his head where he set lines he did not need to act on, which was a shelf that had begun to fill up in the past week and was, he thought, the kind of shelf that would need its own page in a different book one day.

"Tellan's Cross," he said.

"Tellan's Cross."

"That's where you'll go from the cottage."

"That's where I'll go from the cottage. A day or two with the deed and the cottage first. Then up the inner road. The white stone, the third house. I'll know the house when I'm at it. The work that gets done at a door is done at the door, and I'm not writing it down before I'm there."

"You aren't writing it before you're there."

"I'm not. I won't have the right sentences until I'm in front of her."

"All right."

She picked up the two papers together.

She did not separate them. She opened her notebook to the back, where the cover-fold made a small pocket against the spine, and she slid the two papers into the pocket together, the address on top and the deed below, in the same fold. She closed the notebook. She put the pencil back through the spine. She set her right hand into her coat pocket, the inside pocket, the way she had set it last night before they had gone out into the lane, and her two fingers rested for a half-count against the lining where the carved fish had been since the clearing-house yard in Halverstow.

She took her hand back out.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

She drank her tea then, and it was cold, and she did not remark on it being cold. She set the cup down and inclined her half-inch to the barman without turning her head, and the barman inclined his back from the bar without looking up from the spoon he was working. Theron poured her a fresh cup from the pot, which was still warm at the base if not the rim, and she let him pour it and put her hand around the cup as one puts a hand around a cup that has been poured for one by a person whose right it is to pour it.

"You're going on holiday," she said.

"I'm going on holiday."

"You haven't said where."

"I haven't decided. Two days here. I'll write the report for Davren and put it in Tuesday's coach. After that I am meant to be on a holiday, and I have been told twice by a man who does not say things twice that I am to come back with my head on straight."

"That's a man who knows his work."

"He does."

She drank her tea. She ate a small piece of bread without preserve. The carters' second load went out of the yard up the lane with the slow wooden complaint of a wagon's weight settling on its springs, and the lane was quieter for a moment, and then the boy with the bucket came back to his step and there was the small definite sound of water on stone.

"I'll write you," she said.

"You'll write me."

"From the cottage first. The post-coach from Tellan's Cross goes through here Wednesdays and Saturdays. You'll get the Wednesday one if I post it Tuesday."

"All right."

"I'll tell you what the cottage is like. I won't tell you the rest before I've done it. I'll tell you about it after."

"After."

"After."

That was the whole of it.

She did not move for a moment. Then she stood, and the standing was the working standing of a person whose morning had a next thing in it, and she picked up the notebook with the two papers folded into the back of it and she set it under her arm.

"Two days," she said.

"Two days."

"Then the coach."

"Then the coach."

She inclined her half-inch and went to the stairs. The barman did not look up from the spoon. The basket-woman's dog, which had been at the other table and had now finished considering the barman, had laid its chin back on the wood and was watching the door for whatever the door had next. Theron sat at the table with his cup and the second slanted bar of light running across the wood where Sable's hand had been a minute ago.

He looked at the place where the bar of light fell and read it once, the way his class had taught him to read a thing it had been given a name for now, and then he set the reading down on the shelf with the other reading he had not been asked for, and he drank his tea.
