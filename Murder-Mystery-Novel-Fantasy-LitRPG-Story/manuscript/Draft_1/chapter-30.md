# Chapter 30 — The Theory Holds

Last bell came in over the harbour with its usual slight argument from the church on the western slope, which rang fractionally early on Tuesdays and had done so for as long as the host had been at the Arms. Theron heard the difference and the resolution from his corner table. He set the pencil down and closed the case book on the method page and turned the cup so the handle pointed away from the door.

He had been sitting at the table since the soup. The soup had been four hours ago.

The host came past with the lamp-stick on his way to the front room. "She'll be on this one if she's on any."

"Thank you."

"I held the bean soup. There's a half-loaf put by." Said without ceremony, the way the host said most things. He went on to light the lamps.

Theron waited.

The coach came in at the side door with the small commotion of a coach arriving — bag down, fare paid, the driver thanking somebody who had ridden up top in the cold. He heard her voice before he saw her, asking after a room and being answered before she had finished the question.

Sable came into the dining room with her bag in her left hand and the brown travelling coat over her arm, already half off her shoulders, her hair pulled back hard against the road. She saw him at the corner table. She did not smile. She nodded once, the small efficient nod of a person who had been on a coach since dawn and had got off it.

"You sat."

"I sat."

She put the bag down by the chair and dropped into it. The host was already coming over with the bread and the bowl and a clean cup, and Sable accepted all three in order without breaking stride. She ate two spoons of the soup, paused, ate a third, then set the spoon down for long enough to lift the cup.

"Tell me about the room first."

"Top of the stair. Next to mine. The host put a warming-pan in at fourth bell."

"Then I can stop thinking about it." She picked the spoon back up. "Don't open the book."

"I wasn't going to."

She kept eating. He watched her do it without watching her do it, which was a small recent skill he had been developing without comment. The host moved past with the pot. Somebody in the kitchen called to somebody else about the second oven going cold. A man two tables over was paying his bill in the slow voice of someone who had been at the Arms since before dark.

She finished the soup. She tore the half-loaf into two pieces and put one of them back on the plate.

"Now," she said.

---

He turned the case book around so it faced her and opened it to the method page.

He did not say the four lines aloud. He let her read them. She read them once at her own pace, and then again with the cup at her mouth, and then she set the cup down and put her finger on the second line.

"Sustained contact during a seating phase." She looked up. "Define seating phase."

"It's the period during which a glamour is laid into a host signature." He kept his voice low; the room was thin. "The technical word is *seating* because the spell isn't worn — it's set in. Once it's set, it stays without further casting on the caster's part. Until then it requires the caster and the target to be in continuous contact-range channelling. Not touching. Within working distance. For long enough that the two signatures register one another at a structural level."

"How long is long enough?"

"It varies by signature. Theoretical floor is hours of continuous work. Conservative practical estimate is — " he stopped, because he had been going to give her the laboratory number, which was eighty hours, "— I don't know what it is between a master and an apprentice. The thesis didn't speculate. The thesis assumed laboratory pair-tests."

"But an apprenticeship would do it."

"An apprenticeship of any length would do it many times over."

She read the second line again. She turned the page back to the abstract and read the abstract, slowly, her lips moving once on a phrase, the way she read documents when she was not going to take them in any other way. He drank coffee. He did not interrupt.

She finished the abstract.

"The thing you wrote on the third line." She put her finger on it. "Activation does not require the caster near the target."

"Once it's seated. The seating builds the structural pair. After that, the caster carries the trigger in her own signature. The target carries the seated glamour in his. The trigger reaches the glamour through the structural pair, not through proximity."

"Distance limit?"

"In theory none. In practice the field falls off over very long ranges, but thirty feet of corridor is not very long ranges."

She looked at him.

She did not say *thirty feet of corridor.* She did not need to. She had been the one who had logged the corridor measurement on the original Wyrm interview sheet, three weeks ago, in her own hand, in the column where the steward's pass-by notes lived. The number had been in the case book for a long time. It had not, until tonight, attached to anything.

She read the fourth line.

"Trigger known to caster, not to target." She set the finger down. "He didn't know it was there."

"No."

"He couldn't have noticed it. Couldn't have looked for it."

"No."

"Because it wasn't being cast on him in the moment. It had been seated years ago and it was waiting."

"That's the shape of it."

She closed the case book on her own finger, kept it there, and looked at the wall for a moment. The lamp on the corner sconce was doing its small fluttering thing against the draught from the front-room door. He watched her not see it.

"All right," she said. "Show me the cabin."

---

He did not have the original Wyrm cabin schematic at the Arms — that was in the magistrate's evidence file, two streets over. He had something almost as useful, which was his own intake-note sketch from the first morning of the case, with the door, the porthole, the bolt diagram, and the corridor pass marked in his hand. He pulled it out of the back of the case book and laid it flat between them. Sable produced her own notebook from the inside pocket of her coat and turned it to the corridor sheet without being asked.

The sketch was not pretty. It was a working sketch. The cabin was a rectangle. The door was a thicker line on the corridor side. The bolt was a small notch on the inside of the door. The porthole was a circle on the outer wall with a latch mark on the inside. The corridor was a single straight line with the cabin numbers along it.

He pointed to the door.

"Bolted from inside at the time of discovery. The bolt was the Wyrm fitting, original, not tampered. The frame is steel-cored. The bolt seats into the frame. There is no external override on a Wyrm first-class cabin bolt. The crew used the master-key on the latch to open the door for the discovery, and the bolt had to be drawn from inside before that could be done, which it wasn't, so they had to cut the bolt."

"And the porthole."

"Latched on the inside. The latch was not tampered. The frame would not pass an adult. We measured."

"And the corridor."

"Two pass-by entries from Calden during the third watch. One at one bell of the third watch, one at three. He noted no movement at the cabin twelve door either time. He noted the lamp in cabin nine-T as on at one bell — that's your sheet — and on or possibly off at three." He moved his finger along the corridor line on the sketch. "Cabin twelve. Cabin eleven, empty. Cabin ten, the Stallens. Cabin nine, also empty by then. Cabin nine-T, Renn."

Sable looked at her own corridor sheet. She did not move it next to the cabin sketch. She kept the two side by side and ran her finger along the corridor in her own notebook, naming the same cabins under her breath in the same order, the way a person checked a list against a list.

She stopped at nine-T.

"She did not pass cabin twelve."

"No."

"She did not need to."

"No."

"Because she had seated the glamour in him eleven years ago, and she was sitting in nine-T with the trigger in her hand."

She did not put a question mark on it. He did not answer it. The sentence was on the page in front of them and the page in front of them was a sketch of a cabin and a corridor, and the sentence belonged to the sketch in a way it had not belonged to any sentence on any page he had drawn since the bolted door had been cut on the third morning.

He breathed out, once, slowly.

"That's the geometry," he said.

"That's the geometry."

He let himself sit with the sentence for the length of one short pull from the cup. Then he turned the page in the case book and showed her the second thing.

---

The scry log was a single folded sheet with the Wyrm's standing-detection record for the third night. He had it because the Wyrm captain had given it to him on the first morning of the case as a matter of course, the way Wyrm captains gave standing-detection records to investigators boarding a death they had not been expecting to host. He had read the sheet at the time. He had filed it under *no cast magic in cabin twelve during the third night* and moved on.

He unfolded it now. He turned it toward Sable.

She read it. She knew how to read a scry log because Calden had walked her through one the previous week, in the slow patient way Calden walked her through things. She found the cabin twelve row. She found the third-watch column. She read the three letters in the cell.

"*N.R.*"

"No registration."

"Meaning no cast magic was detected in cabin twelve during the third watch."

"On the standing-detection rig the Wyrm carries, yes."

"Which is the rig that registers cast magic."

"That's its job."

She set the sheet down. She looked at him.

"The exploit." She picked her words carefully, because the next sentence was the one that mattered. "It doesn't register as cast magic."

"It registers as ambient field. If it registers at all."

"On standing-detection."

"On standing-detection." He turned the abstract over so the relevant paragraph was facing up between them and he put his finger on the sentence. "*A sustained glamour, once seated within a compatible magical signature, persists after the casting party's withdrawal and registers to standard scry-detection as ambient field rather than as cast magic.*"

"So the scry log saying *no registration* — "

"Is what the scry log would have said whether the exploit was being used or not."

"It doesn't clear cabin twelve."

"It doesn't speak to cabin twelve at all. It speaks to cast magic, in cabin twelve, on standing-detection. None of those things are the exploit."

She read the paragraph in the abstract. She read it twice. She turned to the page in his case book where he had been keeping the negative findings — *no wound, no poison vessel, no entry, no exit, no cast magic on scry* — and she put her finger on the last entry.

"You can move this one off the negative page."

"I know."

"It's not a negative finding any more. It's a positive consistency."

"I know."

She looked at him for a moment, the steady look that was not pity and was not satisfaction either. It was the look a person gave another person when the other person had just done a piece of work that mattered and was not going to be allowed to enjoy it because the work was not finished.

She picked up his pencil. She did not write in his case book — she never wrote in his case book — but she turned to her own notebook and wrote one sentence in it, in her small clear hand, and turned the notebook so he could read it.

*Only an illusionist, or someone trained alongside one, would know to look for a method that does not show up on a scry.*

He read it.

He read it again.

Then he picked up his pencil and turned to the method page and wrote, under the four working-theory lines, in the same unindented hand: *Only an illusionist, or someone trained alongside an illusionist for sustained periods, would know that the absence on the standing-detection record is consistent with the method, rather than exculpatory. The method assumes its own invisibility on the rig that was watching for it.*

He read the line back.

He did not underline anything.

Sable was eating the second piece of bread now. She had cut it into smaller pieces on her plate while he had been writing. She did not look at the page while he wrote. He had noticed, over the last three weeks, that she did not watch him write. She let him write. The line on her own notebook page she pushed across the table when he set the pencil down.

"For your column," she said. "You can copy it across in your own hand."

"It's your sentence."

"It's the working theory's sentence. I borrowed it from the page."

He did not argue. He copied her line across, in his own hand, under the fifth line, in plain ink. He left the version in her notebook where it was. Then he set the pencil down and turned the case book around so they could both look at the method page right-way-up.

Five lines. The cabin sketch. The scry log. The abstract turned face-up under all of it.

---

The Arms was almost down for the night. The kitchen door had stopped swinging some minutes ago. The man who had been paying his bill had gone. The host was putting the chairs up on the far tables, two at a time, with the particular economy of a man who had been putting up chairs for thirty years.

Theron read the five lines through.

The theory explained the cabin. The theory explained the scry. The theory required three things of the person who had used it: training next to an illusionist long enough to satisfy the seating phase; signature compatibility with that illusionist; access to the trigger after the apprenticeship had ended.

Halsa Renn satisfied the first. Pessel had given them eleven years of apprenticeship. The Mirren transcript had given them an original placement made under Joren's vouching. Sable's three answers from Hesh had given them a back room in Sannholt let three or four times a year for nine of those eleven years and *did work that needed quiet rooms.*

Halsa Renn satisfied the second, by inference. An apprentice in a working magical trade developed signature compatibility with her master as a matter of the training itself. You could not avoid it. The compatibility was the apprenticeship's working product. Theron had not known this when he had written the thesis. He knew it now because Sable had said *because that was what an apprentice meant in their trade,* and Pessel had said it through her.

Halsa Renn satisfied the third because the trigger was hers. The trigger was always the caster's. Joren had not known it existed. He had carried the seated glamour in his signature for years and not looked for it, because *trigger known to caster, not to target* was the defining property of the exploit. Halsa had carried the trigger out of the apprenticeship with her and used it when the cabin geometry let her use it.

The theory held.

The theory held against the cabin. The theory held against the scry. The theory held against the eleven-year apprentice line and the credential placement and the cash booking and the corridor measurement. Every page in the case book that he had filed as a negative or as an awkward shape or as a fact without a frame now sat inside the frame the theory provided.

He picked the pencil up.

He turned to the back-of-book page where the careful sentence about the original placement sat.

He wrote, under it, in plain ink: *Exclusivity to Renn — not yet established. Joren may have trained other apprentices over a thirty-four-year working life. The theory holds against the case as known; the theory does not yet specify that Renn is the only person on the Wyrm capable of having performed it.*

He underlined nothing. He set the pencil down.

Sable read the line over his shoulder. She did not move from her chair to read it — she had simply turned her head — and he registered without comment that she had been close enough to do that.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

"Tomorrow."

"Tomorrow."

She picked her cup up. The cup was nearly empty. She finished it.

"It's a theory," she said.

"It's a theory."

"Not a thing for Davren yet."

"No. It is a thing for Davren when it has *only Renn* on the end of it."

"Then that's tomorrow."

She stood up. The travelling coat went back over her arm. She took her bag with her own hand, did not let him take it, and started for the stair. At the foot of the stair she stopped and turned.

"Theron."

"Yes."

"The thesis." She thought about the rest of the sentence. She let it go without finishing it. "Tomorrow."

She went up. The host came past with the lamp-stick and gave him the small nod that meant *I'll close the front room when you're done.*

He sat at the table for a few more minutes with the case book open on the method page and the cabin sketch beside it and the scry log on top, and the abstract underneath all of them with his thumb in the place where the paragraph about ambient field was, and read the five lines through once more. The fifth was in his hand and in Sable's hand both. He liked that about it. He did not say so to anyone.

*[Investigator — Method page updated. Working theory consistent with cabin geometry and standing-detection record. Status: theory holds against case as known; exclusivity to working suspect not yet established.]*

He read the System line through once. He gave it the small dry nod the System ever wanted from him. He did not particularly enjoy that the System had said *holds.* He approved of the qualification on *exclusivity.* The System had read the back-of-book page with him, which it sometimes did, and was being scrupulous about what the night's work had earned.

He closed the case book on the method page with the five working-theory lines unindented in it.

The kitchen lamp went out behind the door.

He carried the cup to the bar, thanked the host, and went up the stair.
