# Chapter 6 — Esherel, in Full

Esherel's cabin was the mirror of Theron's two decks up — same length, same single porthole — but she had moved the writing desk away from the wall by a hand's breadth and turned the chair to face the door, so that the room read as an office that had been pretending to be a cabin and had given up pretending some hours ago.

She did not stand when Calden showed him in. She did not offer the chair. She gestured at it.

"Sit."

He sat. Calden closed the door behind him with the soft click of a man who had closed many doors in his career and had developed a preference for the inaudible ones.

"Madam Esherel."

"Mr. Mavik." The folio was open on her knees and she closed it without finding her place again, which was a thing he registered. "Ask."

He had a small notebook in his left hand and a pencil at the page he had reserved for her. He had reserved a full page. He suspected the page would not be enough.

"The dispute with Lord Vannic."

"Which one."

"There were several?"

"There was one, in eight separate movements." She set the folio on the desk. "I'll save us both. He contracted me for a shipment of southern table wines, two years ago. Three hundred and twelve crates. Bonded house, registered routing, the lot. He took delivery at his cellar in the third week of harvest and refused to pay the back third on grounds of damage during transit. The damage during transit was the loss of four crates to a customs error at Ferren which was the carrier's fault and which the carrier had already settled with my house at full ledger. He was informed of this in writing. He continued to refuse the back third on the same grounds. I have spent two years writing him letters that he has spent two years not replying to."

"What did the back third come to."

"Forty-one and a half thousand crowns."

He wrote it down without comment. It was not a small number. It was not a number that killed a man.

"Why did you board this gondola, specifically."

She looked at him. The look was patient in the way a tutor's was when a student had asked a question that had already been answered by the question before it.

"Because I had heard that he had booked it, Mr. Mavik. I had heard that he had booked it three weeks before I booked it, and I booked it, and I have spent the past six days drafting in my cabin the letter I intended to put under his cabin door once we were five hours from port and he could not avoid being on the gondola with the letter for those five hours. That letter is in my folio. It is not finished. I have rewritten the third paragraph four times. The fourth paragraph is the one that wants the work."

"May I see it."

She turned the folio around and opened it. The letter ran across three pages in a clear merchant's hand. The third paragraph was crossed out and rewritten in the margin twice. The fourth paragraph was a half-page and stopped mid-sentence.

He read it through. The letter was not a threat. It was a closing of business — a clean statement of the ledger, the dates of correspondence, the names of three competitors of Vannic's whom Esherel intended to brief on the matter at the next year's southern wine fair, and a single line near the end that he stopped at:

*I would like the back third paid because it is owed; I would like the letter answered because we are both grown people; I would like neither at this point and have written this for my own use, which is also the use of the trade.*

He looked up.

"You meant to leave this under his door."

"On the last evening. I meant for him to read it and disembark with it. I meant him to know that I had stopped writing." She closed the folio. "I am, Mr. Mavik, more sorry than I expected to be that he will not read it. I'd been looking forward to him not replying to it. I'd composed the not-replying. The not-replying was the point of the letter."

He filed her face at that. She was not performing. She was, plainly, in the room with him, and she was annoyed at the man and at the ending and at the way the ending now belonged to him rather than to her.

He turned a page in the notebook.

"Where were you between first watch and discovery."

"In this cabin. Asleep until the third round, on this bed." She gestured. "I do not sleep well at altitude. I read until first watch, I slept until the gondola did the thing it does at the second pull, I read again, I slept again. I was at the breakfast room at the open. You saw me."

"I did."

"I did not leave this cabin between first watch and the breakfast room."

"Can anyone confirm that."

"Calden saw me at the third round through the door open three inches. I called him in for a sleeping draught and declined it once he was in the room, because I had remembered that I dislike them. We had a short conversation about the gondola's previous spring run, which he had also worked. He was here for perhaps four minutes."

"What time was the third round."

"You'll have to ask him."

He wrote it. The line *Calden, third round, four minutes, sleeping draught declined* sat under her name with a small underline.

"Madam Esherel." He set the pencil down. "Thank you. I'll have one more conversation with you tomorrow, probably brief."

"Tomorrow we will be landing."

"Yes."

She nodded, once, the way she had nodded yesterday at the wine substitution.

"Mr. Mavik." She had picked up the folio again, and she stopped before opening it. "You are being very thorough. I want to say that I noticed."

"Thank you."

"It is not a compliment. It is an observation." She looked at him as a merchant looked at a ledger that had been correctly kept but on the wrong pages. "I have been on the receiving end of thorough men before. The thorough is good. It is sometimes the thorough's shape that is the problem."

He held her eye for a beat.

"I'll note it."

"Note what, Mr. Mavik."

"That you said it."

She made the small sound that was either a laugh or the acknowledgment that a laugh would have been the wrong shape, again, and then she opened the folio and went back to the fourth paragraph.

He let himself out.

---

In the corridor he stood for a moment with the notebook closed in his hand.

The page he had filled was, by his lights, an excellent page. Motive: forty-one and a half thousand crowns and two years of unanswered letters. Opportunity: a cabin two decks up and a corridor she had not, by her account, walked in the relevant window. Means: not yet established. Alibi: a four-minute conversation with the steward at the third round, which placed her in the cabin at one specific point in the night, and not at the others. Demeanor under questioning: candid, dry, unflattering to herself, which his Conservatory tutor had taught him to treat as honesty until contradicted.

He underlined the word *candid* in his head and felt the small pleasure of a category that had landed cleanly.

*[Investigator — Insight +1. The candid suspect is not the cleared suspect.]*

He read the slate. He looked at it for a second longer than he needed to. Then he closed it and went to find Calden, because the line was either his class being instructive or his class being dry, and either way the next thing on the list was the steward.

---

Calden's pantry was a narrow run of cabinetry tucked between the first-class galley and the corridor's far stair — a fold-out shelf at counter height, two stoppered carafes, a tray of cups inverted on a cloth, and the small ledger book in which the steward made the marks that organized the gondola's day. He stood at the shelf with one carafe in his hand when Theron came in and made the small inclination of head that meant *yes, sir, I have a minute, sir, and you may have it.*

"Calden."

"Mr. Mavik."

"The night before discovery." He had moved to the wall opposite the shelf to leave Calden the room he needed for the carafe. "Walk me through your rounds."

"First watch, at the bell. Corridor lamp check, the cabin board, three knocks at the cabin doors that had requested a wake. Cabin twelve had not requested a wake."

"Did you go past."

"I went past. The corridor lamp at twelve was at trim, as I had set it. I did not stop."

"Second watch."

"As first, three hours later. The lamp at twelve had been turned down two notches from its trim. That is consistent with a passenger preparing for sleep. I made a note in the book." He gestured at the book on the shelf without opening it. "I did not stop at the door. There had been no call from the cabin."

"Third watch."

"I was called to cabin sixteen at the third round, before I reached cabin twelve. Madam Esherel asked for a sleeping draught and then declined. I left her cabin at approximately two minutes past the third bell. I continued the round. The lamp at twelve had been turned to dark since the second round. That is also consistent with a passenger asleep. I did not stop. I returned to my pantry and made a note in the book."

"Fourth watch."

A small pause. "I did not make a fourth watch round, sir, because I had decided the corridor was settled and I had a small administrative matter to attend to in the second-class corridor at the fourth bell. I returned to the first-class corridor at the half. The lamp at twelve was still dark. I did not stop." The pause again. "I have asked myself whether I should have stopped. I do not, at this moment, have a way of answering that question that would be useful to you, sir."

Theron made a note.

"Calden. The administrative matter."

"Sir."

"Was it the M. Tannin amendment."

A longer pause, this one professional rather than personal.

"I would prefer, sir, to answer that question with the captain's leave. I can tell you that the matter took me approximately twelve minutes and that I was in the second-class corridor for that period. I can tell you that the matter was administrative and was unrelated to cabin twelve. I cannot, without the captain's leave, tell you more than that."

"You're saying it concerned cabin three."

"I am saying, sir, that I would prefer to answer that question with the captain's leave."

Theron noted the sentence twice in the same way and underlined the second one.

"I'll speak to the captain."

"Sir."

"Calden."

"Sir."

"The sealed amendment. Without telling me what it is. Was the passenger who paid the steward's discretion fee on the dock, or before the dock."

A beat.

"On the dock, sir."

"Thank you."

"Sir."

He let Calden return to the carafe. The steward poured without looking up. The pour was steady. The pantry smelled of the same coffee Calden had set in his cabin that morning and the faint floor-wax smell of a corridor that had been kept clean by a man who treated the cleanliness as part of his profession.

Theron stood for a moment longer than the conversation required. He had the answers he had come for, more or less. He had a sentence — *that question with the captain's leave* — that Calden had said twice without flinching and had said with the air of a man who could say it three more times if required. He had a steward who had walked the corridor four times that night and had not stopped at the cabin and had asked himself whether he ought to have. He had a sealed administrative note that had been paid on the dock, after Vannic had boarded but before the gondola had lifted.

He could feel his page accumulating. It was a satisfying feeling, slightly unwelcome.

"Thank you, Calden."

"Sir."

He went.

---

The first-class lounge at second-watch's end was no longer the quietest it had ever been; the Henner family had come back through, and one of the children had been told that yes, they could still have the small lemon biscuits if they were quiet, and the child was being conspicuously quiet in the way children were when they had been promised biscuits.

Sable was at her corner table. The property papers were spread again. The pencil was in her left hand and she had her chin in her right, and she was looking at the porthole.

He stopped at the lounge arch. He did not cross to her.

The angle of her looking was the same as it had been at noon. She was not reading the harness chain — it had moved, with the gondola's slow correction, into a different segment of the porthole's frame — but she was looking at the place where the chain had been, and where it now was, and at the brass rim of the porthole where the morning light had been catching at noon and was now catching at a flatter angle.

He had Esherel on the page. He had Calden's *with the captain's leave* on the page. He had the M. Tannin amendment for the captain at second-watch end, which was now, and which was where his afternoon went next.

He filed the lounge. He filed the chin in the right hand. He filed *Verrin, S., still at corner table, still in property papers* in the part of the notebook that was the verify column, and he turned to take the stair up to the captain's chartroom.

He had taken three steps when he registered that he had filed her without looking at her. He stopped on the stair for a half-second.

Then he went on up.

---

His cabin was the temperature of the corridor. The second pot of coffee was on the writing desk under its cloth, as it had been at noon. He had not poured the noon pot either. He poured neither now.

He opened the notebook to Esherel's page. He read the page through. He added a line at the bottom: *check the carrier ledger at Ferren, three-year window, both directions.* He had no way to check the Ferren carrier ledger from the gondola. He wrote *port* in the margin and underlined it.

He opened to a fresh page. He wrote *Calden — first-class corridor rounds — confirms quiet at twelve through third watch. Fourth-watch absence twelve minutes in second-class corridor, M. Tannin amendment, captain's leave required.* He underlined *captain's leave.* He drew a small bracketed note beside it: *amendment paid on the dock, after boarding, before lift.*

He opened to the M. Tannin page. He wrote, under the *2* he had put beside the name yesterday, the four words *paid on the dock,* and stopped.

He looked at the page. The shape of the day was on it. Esherel at the top with motive and a four-minute alibi window that did not cover the night. M. Tannin at the second tier with an amendment whose timing was now slightly more interesting than it had been yesterday. The captain's chartroom on his stair, second-watch end.

He felt the small steadying pleasure of a method working. He did not know that he was feeling it. He felt it the way he felt the harness chains through the deck — as a low constant that did not require him to think about it.

He moved his thumb off the graduation pin. He had not noticed his thumb arriving at the pin.

> *[Investigator — Quest active. Sixteen hours, twenty-two minutes to Halverstow Port.]*

He read it. He capped the pencil.

He went to find the captain.
