# Chapter 1 — Boarding the Wyrm

The boarding line for the Skybound Wyrm opened at second bell, and Theron Mavik arrived at first bell and stood in front of a rope with eleven other people who had also arrived at first bell, all of them looking at different things to avoid acknowledging this.

He moved his thumb off the graduation pin for the fourth time this morning.

The gondola was larger than he'd expected. He'd memorized the standard passenger-cargo specifications for his licensing exam — hull length, deck plan, lift capacity, harness chain loads — but specifications were numbers, and the actual object in front of him was the length of a city block, lacquered the color of old bone, and gave off a low hum he felt through his boot soles. Three rigging chains ran up and over the forward boom, each as thick as his thigh. At the far end of the chains, the three drakes were settled in their harness, wings folded, radiating enough heat that he could feel it from thirty feet away.

One of them was eating something with tremendous concentration.

"That's Mottled." A small woman with sun-freckled arms and a leather handler's apron was running a stiff-bristled brush behind the ear-ridge of one of the other drakes, unhurried. "Gets anxious before a long flight. Settles herself by eating. The one she's eating beside is Gravel — I wouldn't approach him until he's introduced to you specifically, all bite and no warning and a very long memory about faces. The small one's Petal."

Theron looked at the small one. It was somewhere between six and seven feet at the shoulder and was watching him with one amber eye, the vertical slit pupil the size of his forearm.

"Petal," he said.

"She earned it." The handler — Wisp, her name badge said — grinned at the drake in the way most people grinned at old dogs, with the comfortable authority of someone who knew exactly what the other party was thinking and had long since made peace with it. Then she looked at him. "First class?"

"Yes."

"Good route for us this run. Petal knows these thermals, and Mottled will have settled by altitude — she always does." She said it with the easy confidence of someone who'd delivered this reassurance two hundred times and found it to be consistently true. "Boarding in twenty. You can wait by the boom if you like. Good view of the harness from there."

He thanked her and moved a few feet to the right. The dock filled around him in increments: a family of four with matching luggage in an ambitious shade of blue. Two scholars in travelling coats, arguing over a shared briefcase with the focused intensity of people who had been arguing about the same briefcase since before he could remember. A courier in working leathers eating a meat pastry with the contentment of a man whose morning had gone correctly. At the far end of the dock, a merchant in silk and two assistants — he caught her name in an exchange with one of the waiting passengers: Madam Esherel, said with the slight care of someone naming someone they respected and were not entirely at ease with — was examining the boarding ledger as though it owed her something.

Theron checked his own boarding card. Cabin fourteen, deck four, first class. Three days between here and his first active case assignment. His supervisor had set the card on his desk and said: "You have earned a rest. Take the Wyrm route. Come back with your head on straight."

She had said it twice. He had not needed it twice.

---

The rope dropped at second bell.

He picked up his travel case and moved into the queue. Calden — the gondola steward, badge and pressed uniform, the deliberate unhurriedness of a man who had boarded a thousand nervous passengers — checked his name, confirmed his cabin, and handed him a key with his berth stamped in red wax.

"Deck Four, starboard, Mr. Mavik. The cabin board will show you the way. I'll be around shortly to settle you in."

"Thank you."

"Of course."

The boarding area past the gangway was the predictable bottleneck: a corridor built for two people and five people trying to use it simultaneously. He stepped sideways to let a family with a toddler navigate through first, then moved toward the staircase. The layout settled into him the way layouts did — stairways, outer deck hatches, window line of the first-class corridor above — filed and available, taking up no more space than it needed.

He very nearly stepped backward into a woman who was pulling her bag through the crowd by a handle that was losing its structural argument with the bag's weight.

"Sorry," he said.

She didn't answer, just angled herself and the bag around him with the efficiency of someone who had already spent too much of this morning navigating around people. Brown travelling coat, the practical kind, the sort that was chosen for its pockets rather than its impression. Her bag was too heavy for the handle. She had the expression of a person who had agreed to a trip, arrived at the departure point, and was now conducting a quiet assessment of whether that had been the correct call.

She caught Calden's eye over the crowd and held up one finger: *just the one bag, then.* Calden nodded. She moved on.

He filed it and went up the stairs.

---

His cabin was halfway along the first-class corridor — small, clean, honest about its dimensions. West-facing, which meant long afternoon light and no dawn. The lamp worked; he tested it. He pressed once on the mattress to confirm it was a mattress rather than a board with ambitions. He set his case on the writing desk, hung his coat on the hook by the door, and went back out to the boarding deck.

He was getting air. That was all it was.

The tail end of the queue was still working through. The scholars had managed the gangway with their briefcase intact, apparently — only one of them was talking rapidly at the other, which seemed like progress. Madam Esherel had found a gap and was moving through it with the authority of someone who expected gaps to appear.

And then the sound of the boarding area shifted.

Not loudly. It was the small recalibration of a crowd's attention — the way a room adjusts when something has entered that everyone is choosing to process in real time. Theron looked toward the dock entrance.

The man coming through was somewhere in his mid-twenties, with the jaw that came from a hundred years of portraits and a coat that communicated its price by not discussing it. Behind him, a personal steward pushed a luggage cart stacked three high. He walked at the front of the convoy with the settled certainty of someone who had never been required to hurry and had developed opinions about people who were.

His gaze moved across the dock once and efficiently. The scholars — dismissed. The family — beneath notation. Theron — assessed as young and not immediately consequential, set aside. Madam Esherel — and here his expression shifted very slightly, the way a man's does when he's encountered something he didn't expect and doesn't want to give ground on — acknowledged, not greeted.

"Lord Vannic," someone murmured, nearby. The name moved through the remaining queue in undertones, neighbor to neighbor.

He crossed toward the gangway without deviation. The courier — still finishing his pastry, not entirely out of the path — looked up and to the side and performed the quiet calculation of someone weighing inconvenience against principle, then moved.

Vannic barely registered the movement. He had already reached Calden.

At the gangway threshold, he stopped for an elderly passenger finishing the step down. Not with any performance — not the wide gesture or the produced smile — just a pause, nothing more, waiting the three seconds required and then taking the step up. He did it without looking at the man.

Madam Esherel, watching from six feet away, said something low to her assistant. The assistant nodded. Whatever had been said did not appear to require elaboration.

---

Theron went back upstairs.

He was considering his cabin key with academic patience — the lock required an angle that was not disclosed on the key — when the sound of footsteps on the upper staircase announced Lord Vannic and Calden arriving in the corridor, apparently continuing a conversation about window orientation.

It was a narrow corridor. There was a moment at the landing, natural and unremarkable, where one of them was required to acknowledge the other.

Vannic offered his hand first. Theron took it.

The grip was firm, brief, bare palm. And the side of his index finger — the raised ridge, built up by years of sustained casting hold, where prolonged contact with a spellwork frame wears the skin to something harder and more specific than ordinary callus. Theron's thesis advisor had one. Both of his practical illusion professors had them. You didn't get that from an occasional hobby. You got it from ten hours a day.

*Noble who dabbles*, he filed. Then, amending: *practices more seriously than that implies.*

"You look young," Vannic said. It came out as observation rather than insult, which was something.

"First posting." The pin made it obvious; there was no use in false modesty about it.

Vannic's eyes went briefly to the lens embossed in bronze. "Investigator."

"Yes."

Something shifted in his face — not warming. Adjusting. "Hm." He released the handshake. "Well." And moved on down the corridor toward the upper stair.

Calden followed. Theron stood with his key.

*First posting, and no active case.* He was aware of the pin the way he'd been aware of it all morning — the slight weight of a new thing on his jacket, still unfamiliar in the way that new things were before they became ordinary. He was also aware that it would take roughly six months to stop checking whether it was still there, and that everyone who had been through the licensing examination had this same six months, and that this awareness did not make him stop checking.

He got the lock open on the fourth angle.

---

He was inside, coat back on the hook, when Lord Vannic's voice came from farther down the corridor — lower than his boarding voice, without an audience to address.

"Calden."

The steward, somewhere ahead: "My lord?"

"Thank you. For the — all of it." A pause. "The boarding business."

"Of course, my lord."

Not warm, exactly. Not performed. A man who had thought about saying something, said it plainly, and was done. The tone was aimed at one person, and that was the point of it.

Theron was putting his case on the writing desk. He heard it, registered it, and was moving on to the question of which drawer would be best for the case slate when he noticed the corridor: the three-inch gap his door had left open.

And in the edge of that gap, three doors down, the woman in the brown coat. Standing with her hand on her own door latch, not yet inside. Not moving.

She wasn't looking at Vannic's cabin. She was looking at the middle of the corridor — the specific patch of air where a man had just thanked the steward by name, without ceremony, and said *all of it* as if that covered everything without needing to be itemized.

He didn't see her face change. She was just still for a moment, in a way that had a quality to it.

Then she pushed her door open and went inside.

Theron pulled his own door closed.

---

The Skybound Wyrm lifted thirty minutes late, on account of a disputed crate in the cargo hold. He felt the moment in his feet first — the floor's quality changing, the deep hum of the harness chains rising to something thinner and more continuous — and then through the porthole: the dock sliding. The slow, courteous separation from solid ground.

The cabin lamp was lit. It smelled faintly of the flame oil his father kept in the dispensary cabinet at the back of the shop — not intensely, just the ghost of it, enough to be there. He had not thought of the shop in six weeks. Probably the altitude did something to memory, or the lamp oil was a common formulation, or it was nothing. He noted it and moved on.

He sat on the padded bench beneath the porthole.

The examination record: six weeks of written assessments, three of practicals, one oral thesis defense. His thesis — *Scryproof Glamour Exploits: Theoretical Vulnerability in Long-Contact Illusion Chains* — had been called "original, if limited in practical application" by the examiner who had liked him. The one who hadn't had called it "somewhat overstated." Both verdicts appeared in the record and he had received the pin regardless, which he considered an empirical resolution.

His mother had cried. His father had said *good* in the voice that meant something longer than good — meant the scholarships and the years and all the rest of it had come down somewhere he'd hoped it would. Theron had spent three days at home pretending he wasn't enormously pleased.

He touched the pin now.

Small. A bronze oval, lens embossed at the center, the size of a coin he'd carry without thinking about. At fourteen, when an Investigator had come through his hometown to consult on a poisoning case and he'd seen one for the first time — on the woman's lapel, catching the afternoon light — he had thought: *that is the most consequential object I have ever seen.* The actual object was considerably more modest than that memory. It was also, exactly, what it was supposed to be.

*[Investigator — Field status: Unscheduled interval.]*

He couldn't tell if the System was being dry on purpose. He chose to read it as such.

The dock had gone. The city was going — rooftops, market towers, the long green stripe of the river — tilting slowly east and then dropping below the hull line, replaced by the pale flat blue of a midmorning sky that would get better once it had more time to work on itself. The harness chains were singing one clear note in the slipstream.

At the edge of the porthole, three shapes moved across the blue: wings outstretched, banking slightly starboard as they found the first thermal. Petal was on the right — recognizable by her size, which was not a navigational reference he'd expected to develop, and yet.

She banked through the air with the ease of something that had never had reason to question whether it belonged there.

Three days. No active case. Nothing to investigate, no inference chain to close. He settled his shoulder blades against the padded bench.

He moved his thumb off the pin, and left it there.
