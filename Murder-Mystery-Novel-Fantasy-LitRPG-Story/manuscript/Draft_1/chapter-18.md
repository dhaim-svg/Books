# Chapter 18 — The Factors' Board

The Gray Quay office kept its ledgers in a narrow room behind the main hall, stacked on oak shelves that ran floor to ceiling along two walls. The clerk who led Theron in was young enough that he'd grown up with the shelving and moved through it without looking, pulling a bound volume of the current year's assessor logs before Theron had finished explaining what he wanted.

"Independent assessors, Mirren line," the clerk said. "You want the full year or from a specific date?"

"Nine months back."

The volume landed on the reading table. The clerk left to deal with a cargo dispute in the outer hall.

Theron opened it.

The entries were alphabetical within month, columns for name, port of origin, job reference, and factor of record if any. Fell appeared first in the early autumn — two load assessments for a grain-charter company he didn't recognize, east Mirren origin, no factor of record. A third in the winter, same column. Then two in the spring, closer together, with a different job reference that was new: customs-assist on perishable cargo, and a notation in the margin — *refer: TL-9* — in a different hand, written smaller than the others, with a different pen.

He looked at TL-9 for a moment.

The T could be Toll.

He put his finger over the L-9 and thought about that. Then he turned to the back of the volume, where the key for factor reference codes was printed inside the back cover. TL-9 was not listed there. He looked at the columns again. There were references to TS-4, TM-11, TA-2. None of them were listed individually in the key; the key was for the standard dock-authority factors, of which there were twelve, all named. The TL-9 notation was not among the twelve.

Theron wrote *TL-9 — not in key, margin notation only, spring entries, customs-assist perishable cargo* and went back through the autumn and winter entries to check whether the same notation appeared anywhere earlier.

It did not.

*[Investigator — Insight +1. The notation is new. So is the relationship.]*

He looked at the two spring entries again. They were dated three weeks apart. Between them: nothing for Fell in the ledger. The second customs-assist job had a finish date stamped in, and the finish date was six days before the Halverstow run departed from Mirren harbor.

He wrote that down too.

The clerk came back, a tea tray now balancing on one arm, and set a cup at the edge of the reading table without being asked. Harbor clerks were efficient that way; they knew when someone was in the room for more than a quick check.

"The reference code TL-9," Theron said. "Is that a factor of record, or a job notation?"

The clerk looked at it. Something flickered in his expression — not recognition, exactly. More like the expression of someone who has seen a thing before and not asked, because the floor around it was not clearly his to walk. "That's not one of ours," he said. "We have twelve standard codes. That would have been added by whoever was verifying the job."

"Which would be?"

"The assessor, or the factor who arranged the work, if they used a secondary reference they wanted tracked."

"Can you look up who submitted this entry?"

The clerk reached for a different shelf. He found the submission ledger for the same month, ran his finger down the right column. "Both spring entries, Fell Maret. Self-submitted, east Mirren address, assessed by dock-authority standing appointee on arrival." He looked up. "The notation would have come in with the self-submission."

So she had written it herself.

Theron drank the tea. It was from the harbor-side blend that all the Gray Quay offices used, slightly over-steeped, with the faint mineral edge of water that came through old pipes. A woman with three months of east-yard work and a clean account had added a notation code that matched no standard reference, on two jobs in the spring, after a gap, and the second job ended six days before she boarded a gondola out of Mirren harbor.

"Are there other assessors with TL-9 in the logs?" he asked.

The clerk thought about that. "I can't do a full search in the time you're here. That's a month of turning pages."

"Would you be willing to flag the volume for the magistrate's office?"

A pause, the length of someone calculating which of their obligations outweighed the others. "If the magistrate's office asks formally," the clerk said, "yes."

Theron wrote *request formal flag — TL-9 reference, full year's log — magistrate's office* and underlined it. "Thank you," he said. "And the tea."

The clerk managed a brief, professional nod. He was already watching the outer hall again, where two men had arrived with a counter-claim form, which was worse than a cargo dispute.

---

Theron came out of the ledger room and stopped.

Sable was in the main hall's front window, standing at the edge of the broad counter where a harbor notary was logging in documents. She had not been there when he'd gone in. She was talking to a man in a cotton work coat, older, wearing the canvas cap that most of the long-serving dock laborers favored in Halverstow, with a band sewn at the brim from a gondola-line company. Not one of the pilots. A deckhand or a loader, by the look of the hands.

She saw Theron and gave the small chin-tilt that meant *one moment*.

He stood at the hall window and looked out at the quay. The afternoon was well into itself; the cargo traffic had thinned. Someone had chalked a berth number on the gray post-stone outside, and a third-year courier was arguing with a porter about who had the right to re-chalk it.

Sable came across the hall. "His name is Pell," she said. "He loads for the Halverstow-Sannholt post-coach line. Came over when he saw me looking at the route board."

"Looking for what?"

"The coach schedule. The Sannholt run."

He turned from the window. She had a folded slip in one hand, the same size as the one she'd given Master Olen that morning. "How do we contact Pessel at the Copper Finch?"

"Standard letter. The coach carries mail through to Sannholt twice a week — Tuesdays and Fridays. Pell says there's a factor's clearing house at the south Halverstow station that handles forwarding." She held out the slip. On it she had written a station address, a box number, and the name of the clearing-house register. "If we put a letter in with tomorrow's run, it reaches Sannholt in three to four days. Pessel would have it before the end of the week."

Theron looked at the address. "That's the Sannholt thread," he said. "First formal step."

"Pell says the clearing house also handles return. If Pessel writes back, it comes to the same box."

He folded the slip and put it in his notebook. "You'll draft the letter?"

"You'll draft the letter. I'll read it."

He could not argue with this, which was probably why she said it that way. He made a note under the TL-9 notation: *Sannholt letter — clearing house box, Tuesday run, draft tonight*.

*[Investigator — Status: Two open threads, two forwarding steps. The case is moving. You are writing a letter.]*

Theron looked at the System line for a moment, decided it had earned the observation, and closed the notebook.

---

Outside the Gray Quay office, the quayside opened up into the broader harbor walk. The light had shifted to the flat, clean angle of mid-afternoon, and the harbor smell was different now than it had been at the gangway that morning: less tide, more tar and rope-salt, with the faint sweetness of unloaded grain from the east stores. A pair of dockers were moving a hand-trolley with a stubborn wheel along the near edge of the stone.

"She wrote in the TL-9 reference herself," Theron said. "Self-submitted."

Sable had her hands in her coat pockets. "Which means she knew what it stood for."

"And chose to record it, or had to because the work couldn't be submitted clean without it."

"What's TL-9?"

"Not a standard code. It's not in the key." He turned the page to his notes. "The clerk couldn't cross-reference without a month of page-turning; I've asked him to flag it for the magistrate. But it only appears on the two spring entries — the customs-assist jobs. After a gap from the autumn and winter work."

"Which is clean."

"Which is clean and has no notation. The notation appears on the jobs that are closer to the departure date."

Sable walked a few paces without speaking. The dockers moved past them with the trolley, the stubborn wheel scraping in its set groove.

"So she does regular east-yard work," she said at last. "Clean for most of it. Then something changes in the spring — she picks up two jobs with a reference she writes in herself and doesn't explain, and the second one finishes six days before she boards out of Mirren."

"Yes."

"We don't know if TL-9 is Toll."

"We don't know." He put the notebook away. "But the T fits. And Toll has a west-yard presence, and she's been in the east yard, and Master Olen said crossing them would need a reason." He looked back at the magistrate's office buildings, gray-fronted in the afternoon. "The magistrate's flag will answer it, when it comes."

Sable came level with him. "That's a day. Possibly two."

"Yes." He looked at the harbor. A barge was coming around the breakwater arm, deep-loaded and patient, and the harbor master's launch was out to meet it. "The Fell thread is real. We just can't walk it further today."

The barge moved on its steady course. The launch paced it.

Theron was watching the water and not quite watching it, the way you watched something when you were thinking about something else. He had the TL-9 notation. He had the six-day gap. He had the factors' board flagged and the Sannholt letter drafted in his head. He had the correct name in the case log, and the case log was where it should be.

---

The day had gone long. By the time they came back along the harbor walk toward the gondola, the late afternoon light was settling in over the waterfront, and the Wyrm's mooring ropes cast long shadows across the dock boards. The overnight berth crew had come on; the day-labourers were packing up. At the cargo shed corner, someone had left an empty tea crate turned upside down with a lantern balanced on it, not yet lit.

Sable stopped at the bottom of the gangway.

She looked at the harbor, at the water moving under the quay boards, at the direction of the Halverstow postal district. Not at Theron.

"Someone has to tell his family properly," she said. "In person. Before any letter from the port office gets there."

He heard it. He stood at the gangway foot and did not say anything.

She turned and went up the gangway.

Theron waited one moment. The harbor water moved under the boards. The unlit lantern sat on its crate, patient.

Then he followed her up.
