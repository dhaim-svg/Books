# Chapter 25 — The Coach Departs

The magistrate's clerk came at half past fifth bell, before Theron had finished his first cup of coffee.

The man was apologetic about the hour, which was its own kind of information: clerks who came at half past fifth were either carrying good news or had been up since third watch putting a search result together. He set a folded document on the table and accepted the chair Theron offered him but sat on the edge of it, which meant he was on his way somewhere else after this.

"Vantrel Secured Storage," the clerk said. "West harbour Mirren. You asked for the full-year registered-agent records." He tapped the document. "The warrant came through this morning from the harbor registry. Full disclosure, first of the year through now."

Theron set his cup down and unfolded the pages.

The records were organized in the harbor registry's standard format: columns for agent name, term dates, capacity, the notary's mark. There were four entries. The first three were consistent with the kind of storage firm that ran cargo through legitimate channels — commercial-rate agents, short terms, clean entries, each one vouched by an independent party whose name appeared nowhere else in the document. Routine work, routine paper. The fourth entry was different.

Registered agent, third month through seventh month of the current year: *H. Marbeth, independent, west harbour Mirren, vouched by Vantrel Secured Storage's own registered operator, Fenn Kolach.*

No address for Marbeth beyond the harbor district. No further detail. The term dates ran through the seventh month — which put Marbeth's active period overlapping exactly with Fell's two spring customs-assist jobs, the ones that had carried the TL-9 notation.

A name he hadn't encountered anywhere in the case file until Fell's job numbers had pointed him here.

*[Investigator — Registered agent H. Marbeth, west harbour Mirren. Term: third through seventh month, current year. Active during Fell's TL-9 customs-assist period. Next step: locate Marbeth through Fenn Kolach, Vantrel's registered operator.]*

He read through the rest of the document twice. Nothing else new. The Kolach name was clean in the harbor registry — a long-standing commercial operator, nothing irregular in the standing record, the kind of entry that had been renewed so many times it had acquired the texture of furniture. But Kolach had personally vouched for Marbeth, which meant the two men were connected well enough for Kolach to put his operator's license behind the introduction. That was not nothing.

"The Kolach vouching entry," Theron said.

The clerk nodded. "Unusual. Normally the registered operator isn't the vouching party for his own firm's agents — you'd get a third party. The magistrate's office flagged it when they ran the disclosure check."

"Has Kolach vouched for any other agents this year?"

The clerk opened his own folder and ran his finger down a column. "Two. Both through independent parties. This is the only one he carried himself."

Which made it particular to Marbeth. Whether that was professional loyalty, social obligation, or a more calculated kind of arrangement was a question for Kolach's own account — the document said what it said, and the document's job was not to explain itself. Kolach would do that.

"Is Kolach in Mirren?"

"Per the registry, he holds a west harbour address. We haven't verified current residence."

A letter would take three days each way. An investigation warrant to the Mirren magistrate's office could run parallel — one thread on the official record, one on the informal, Kolach pulled from both directions at once before he had time to calculate what each request meant. That was the sequence.

"I'll need a letter of introduction from the Halverstow magistrate's office," Theron said. "For Mirren counterparts. Authorization to request a formal interview with Fenn Kolach."

The clerk wrote something in his own folder. "I'll have it drafted by afternoon."

Theron set the records at the top of his working stack and walked the clerk to the door. He stood in the doorway for a moment after the man had gone, the harbor smell of fish-brine and coal smoke lifting off the street at this hour before the day had quite heated up. It was not Kolach he wanted to speak to, eventually — it was Marbeth. But Kolach was the door to that room, and the door was the door.

He went back to the table and picked up his coffee. Cold now.

It was twenty minutes past sixth bell by the time he put the cup down. The coach was at seventh.

---

He had the fish.

He had carried it since the fourth day of the investigation — lifted it from Joren's outer coat pocket during the cabin examination, logged it in the case notes as *odd personal effect, small carved wooden fish, return to estate* — and had not found an estate to return it to. The address in the Toll correspondence had given him the Sannholt thread, the Pessel name, the Copper Finch contact. It had not given him a family direction. That had waited.

The fish itself had moved, at some point in the second week, from his evidence pouch to his coat pocket. He had not made a decision about it. He had put it somewhere he could reach it and not thought about it further, and somewhere in the sequence of days and harbor walks and magistrate's offices it had settled into the left pocket the way things settle when you stop asking where they belong.

He had taken it out once, in the captain's day-cabin at the dock with Sable sitting across from him, and turned it over while she named the details in order — the callus, the accent, the kindnesses, the niece. She had not named the fish. It had been in his palm while she spoke and he had put it away without saying anything about it, and neither had she, which was the right choice. The recall was about Joren Aldis as a person. The fish was about where the person was going afterward.

He had felt the shape of it through the coat lining on the docks, on the Gray Quay steps, walking to the Arms with a cold harbor wind doing its best work.

The niece had carved it at age six. She was eight now. He had not let himself do the arithmetic on what the family was waiting for.

He put it in his left coat pocket, where it would be easy to reach. Then he put on his jacket, picked up his notebook, and went out.

---

The south station clearing house smelled of horses and coal smoke and the particular compressed urgency of an early departure — something about a yard before seventh bell that had more in common with a dock than a street, the sense of a timetable that did not negotiate. Two coaches stood in the yard, one already loaded and standing, one still being worked on: a driver checking the harness buckles methodically, running his thumb along each strap in sequence, while a boy handed up luggage from a cart. A woman in a gray traveling coat stood near the booking window with two bags at her feet, watching the boy work with the look of someone calculating whether he would get to her trunk before the boarding call. Farther along, two men in merchants' coats were arguing about something in the comfortable way of people who had argued about it before and expected to argue about it again.

The yard man moved between the coaches with a list, checking things off.

Theron found Sable near the far end of the coach, not at the window. She had her bag at her feet and was reading a page from her notebook, which he had seen her do before a departure — checking what she had, not what she'd forgotten. Her traveling coat was the darker one, good wool, built for weather. She looked up when she heard him.

"Warrant came through," he said.

"Marbeth?"

"H. Marbeth, registered agent, Vantrel Secured Storage, west harbour Mirren. Active through seventh month — overlaps Fell's TL-9 jobs. Vouched directly by Vantrel's own registered operator, Fenn Kolach. That's the irregular piece." He kept it compact; she was boarding in ten minutes and the full document would follow by letter. "I'm requesting a letter of introduction for the Mirren magistrate's office to interview Kolach. The address is in the harbor registry."

She folded the notebook page and put it away. "How long to get Kolach in front of someone?"

"Ten days if the Mirren counterpart is cooperative. Longer if they aren't."

"Send me a line when you have his statement." She meant it as a fact, not a request. He would, and she knew it.

The driver finished with the harness and climbed to the box, settling in with the air of a man who had done this at this hour for long enough that he had no more opinions about it. The yard man called the boarding. The woman at the window picked up her bags. The two merchants concluded their argument and moved toward the door. Theron registered Calden at the far edge of the yard, near the factor's shed — the gondola was still docked in the harbor, and a steward's departure paperwork ran long — but Calden was watching the coach, not the passengers, with the particular stillness of a man waiting to see something depart safely. Not a professional stillness. Something else. He did not come forward, which was the right thing to do.

Esherel was not here. He had not expected her to be.

The boy loaded the last case onto the boot and stepped back. The yard man checked it off his list, made a mark, looked up at the driver. The coach door opened from inside — one of the merchants holding it for the woman with the bags, which she acknowledged with a brief nod and went through.

Theron reached into his left coat pocket.

The fish sat in his palm. He looked at it — birch or ash, the fin at its imprecise angle, the wood worn smooth to the texture of something that had been held a long time. The niece's work, the way a six-year-old's hands worked: not quite right, better than it had any reason to be, shaped with the confidence of someone who had not yet learned to doubt the shape. Eight years old now. The fish was meant to go somewhere. Not an evidence locker. Not a coat pocket on a Halverstow street while the harbor went about its business. He had been carrying it long enough. It had somewhere to go, and Sable was the right person to carry it there.

He held it out to her.

Sable looked at it, then at him.

He didn't say anything. There was nothing particular to say.

She took it. She put it in the breast pocket of her traveling coat, the inside one, the same motion she used for the notebook. The coach door was open behind her, the driver settled, the other passengers already in.

She picked up her bag.

"Don't let the Marbeth letter sit," she said.

"I won't."

She went up the step and into the coach. The door latched.

The yard man stepped back. The driver released the brake and spoke to the horses — the same short phrase they would have heard every morning from him, at this yard, at this hour — and the horses moved. The coach rolled across the yard stones toward the gate, iron wheels loud on the cobbles, the sound bouncing off the factor's sheds and the booking office wall, and then it went through the gate and out onto the south road. Theron watched it go. Past the gate, the road softened to packed earth, and the wheel sound dropped to something lower, the horses moving into their working stride, and then the coach went around the chandler's building and the horses and the sound and the particular line of it went with it.

Calden was gone. He had not heard the steward leave.

---

Theron stood in the yard for a moment. The second coach was still being loaded. The woman who had been arguing with the booking clerk was at the window again, carrying a different document this time, her tone unchanged — patient, specific, not going anywhere. The boy went past with an empty cart and disappeared into the stable end.

He took out his notebook.

The Marbeth entry was at the top of the current page: *H. Marbeth, Vantrel, west harbour Mirren. Kolach vouching. Letter of introduction: afternoon.* He added a second line below it — *Follow-up: Mirren magistrate, formal interview request, Fenn Kolach, Vantrel Secured Storage* — and below that, in the smaller hand he used for threads that were open but not yet moving: *Renn: wrong frame confirmed; Sannholt thread active; Sable in transit.*

He read the page. The shape of it was right. The Kolach thread was the active work; the Sannholt thread was Sable's to run until her first letter came back; the two would converge somewhere in the middle weeks, and where they converged would tell him which way to press. He had made similar diagrams before, in Conservatory, in the second-year case practice where they gave you partial records and asked you to name the next step. The diagrams from case practice were always cleaner than this. This one had a man's name in the header and a carved fish traveling south on the morning coach.

That was the shape of it. Two threads. One moving south on the coach road. One waiting on a letter that would come back from Mirren in ten days or fourteen.

He closed the notebook and walked back through the yard gate onto the Halverstow street, where the harbor was going about its business in the middle-morning light and the Arms was three blocks north and the magistrate's clerk had a letter of introduction to draft by afternoon.

The case had the right names in it now. That was something. The rest was work.
