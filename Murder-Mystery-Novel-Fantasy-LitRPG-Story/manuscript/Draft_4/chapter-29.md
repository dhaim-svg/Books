# Chapter 29 — The Exam-Fodder Theory

The morning post-coach came in at first bell instead of second, which the host mentioned in passing as he set the pot down. Tuesday's coach ran early when the harbour wind ran true, and the harbour wind had run true since before dawn. Theron thanked him for the pot and the information, in that order.

The letter arrived twenty minutes later, in the small brown wrapper Sable used for road letters, with the Whitepost Inn's stamp on the seal and a smudge of ink on the corner where she had refolded the sheet without waiting for it to dry.

He opened it. He set it flat against the table where he could read it without holding it up.

*Theron,* she wrote.

*One further errand done. The Whitepost landlady's hint about the boarding-house was good: Copper Finch, two streets north of Pessel, back room let by the week. The proprietor is a woman named Hesh and she does not give a stranger anything she has not decided to give. I gave her your name and Davren's and she gave me three answers, no more, and was careful with each one.*

*One. A young coastal woman did stay in the back room three or four times a year, for the last nine of the eleven Pessel has been here.*

*Two. She stopped coming about two years ago without quarrel — Hesh's word — and without notice.*

*Three. She always travelled south from Sannholt afterward, never north.*

*Hesh added one line without my asking. She said the woman did work that needed quiet rooms. I wrote it down in her words. She did not elaborate and I did not press.*

*I am on the road south. I will be in Halverstow by Tuesday's last bell, possibly earlier if the second coach holds its schedule. — S.*

He read it through once.

He read it through a second time on the line about the quiet rooms.

He picked up the pencil, turned to the column page, and wrote, under the apprentice-line entry: *Copper Finch, Sannholt — back room, three to four visits/year for nine years, stopped two years ago, southbound after each visit. Proprietor Hesh, unprompted: "did work that needed quiet rooms."*

He set the pencil down.

The dining room was working its morning. A factor at the second table was reading a manifest aloud to a clerk who was not writing anything down. The kitchen door swung. Somebody on the stair was carrying a child who did not want to be carried.

*Did work that needed quiet rooms.*

He drank coffee.

The sentence did not do its work fast. It sat in the column and got picked up later, when something else came near it and the two of them gave off a small heat together. He had a great many sentences like that in the case book at this point. He was not, as a rule, in a hurry to read them.

But this one was sitting next to a blank method page.

He read the line again.

Then he stood up.

His cabin slate was in his coat pocket, where it had been since he had unpacked at the Arms on Friday. The thing he wanted now was not the slate. The thing he wanted now was a small unlovely notebook he had not opened since he had left the Conservatory three weeks before he had boarded the Wyrm. He went upstairs to get it.

---

The notebook had been part of the graduation packet — every newly licensed Investigator was issued one, and most of them put it in a drawer and forgot it. It was a slate-bound thing, the size of a hand, containing the abstracts of the licensee's final-year thesis and any examined practicum work, copied in the Conservatory clerk's hand and bound for licensing reference. The point of the thing was that any working magistrate could, in principle, demand a young Investigator's working background on the spot, and the young Investigator could open the notebook and put it in front of the magistrate.

In practice no magistrate had ever asked for one. Theron's was at the bottom of his case-bag.

He brought it down to the table. He set it beside the case book and beside the partial Mirren transcript and beside Sable's letter, and the four of them made the shape his table had been making for three days now: a working surface, no decoration, the host moving the cups around them with the practised inattention of a man who knew when not to look at a guest's papers.

Theron opened the notebook.

The first page was the licensing summary, which he skipped. The second was the practicum log, which he skipped. The third was the thesis abstract.

*Scryproof Glamour Exploits: Theoretical Vulnerability in Long-Contact Illusion Chains. Th. Mavik, final year, Conservatory.*

He read his own abstract.

He had not read it in nine months. It was, in the cool way of clerk's transcription, almost not his — the sentences were the sentences he had written, but the rhythm in the clerk's hand was different from the rhythm he heard in his head, and the abstract sat on the page like a piece of someone else's work he had been asked to evaluate.

*The thesis examines a class of long-contact illusion-chain exploits in which a sustained glamour, once seated within a compatible magical signature, persists after the casting party's withdrawal and registers to standard scry-detection as ambient field rather than as cast magic. The exploit's defining requirement is sustained contact between caster and target during the seating phase. Its second requirement is structural compatibility between caster's and target's signatures. The exploit's practical applications are limited by the difficulty of arranging the seating phase under non-laboratory conditions; no historical practical instance is recorded. The thesis examines the theoretical vulnerability and recommends scry-detection refinements to the Conservatory's current field standards.*

He read it twice.

He read the line on sustained contact a third time.

The examiner who had liked the thesis had written, in the margin of his examined copy, *Original, if limited in practical application.* The examiner who had not liked it had written, in the margin of the same copy, *Somewhat overstated.* He remembered both of those marginal notes the way he remembered his mother's voice on the morning she had cried and his father's saying *good* over the kitchen table. The notes had become part of the pin, in his head. He had not, since the pin had been put on his lapel, gone back to read the thesis itself.

He sat with the notebook open in front of him.

The dining room was still working its morning. The child had stopped objecting to being carried. The Arms' pot was, as always, very good.

He went on reading. The abstract was a page long. He read the entire page. Then he turned to the back of the notebook, where the examiner's marginal notes had been copied separately, and he read those too.

The examiners had asked him, at his defence, why he had spent a year of his final work on a class of exploit for which no historical instance was on record. He remembered answering that the absence of recorded instance was itself the structural interest: a class of exploit that did not generate historical instance was, by definition, a class of exploit whose successful uses did not register as exploits at all. They had nodded at the answer. The examiner who had liked the thesis had liked it more after the answer. The examiner who had not liked it had not liked it less.

He closed the notebook on his thumb and looked at the case book.

The Mirren transcript lay folded next to it. The method page was the page after the column page, and it had been blank since Monday morning.

He opened the case book to the method page.

---

He did not write *the method is the scryproof glamour exploit.*

He had written that kind of sentence before, in his second-year practicum, and the practicum supervisor had read it and underlined the word *is* and written in the margin *too early.* He had carried the marginal note since. He carried it now.

He wrote, instead, on the method page, in the unindented hand he reserved for working theory not yet earned: *Working theory only.*

He wrote, under it: *Long-contact glamour-chain exploit (Mavik thesis, final year, Conservatory). Defining requirement: sustained contact between caster and target during the seating phase.*

He stopped.

He sat with the pencil over the page.

The seating phase. He had written, nine months ago, *non-laboratory conditions* in the dry voice the thesis had asked for. He had not written, anywhere in the thesis, what a non-laboratory seating phase would look like. He had not been writing a case. He had been writing a theoretical paper. He had assumed, with the comfortable assumption of a student under examination, that any real seating phase would have to be arranged deliberately, in private, by parties who had reason to arrange it.

He did not believe that any more.

He wrote, slowly: *Seating phase satisfied by a working apprenticeship. Eleven years. Casting alongside the master across a working relationship that required contact-range channelling.*

He read it back. The pencil hovered. He did not underline.

He wrote, under it: *Signature compatibility satisfied between two illusionists.*

He read that line back, and stopped, and read it back a second time, because the sentence on the page was not the sentence he had imagined he would ever write. *Signature compatibility between two illusionists* was a phrase he had used in his abstract to indicate the laboratory pair-test scenario, in which two licensed casters of the same school agreed to a controlled seating under supervisor observation. It had not occurred to him, while writing the thesis, that the same words described a master and an apprentice working in a coastal town for years at a time.

It had not occurred to him because he had not been writing about people. He had been writing about a structural exploit, in a theoretical paper, for an examination he had passed.

He wrote, in the same careful hand: *Once seated, the exploit does not require the caster to be near the target at the moment of activation. Activation requires only a trigger keyed to the seating signature. Trigger known to caster; not known to target.*

He set the pencil down.

He read the four lines together.

The four lines did not say that Halsa Renn had killed Joren Aldis in cabin twelve from cabin nine-T with a glamour seated during her apprenticeship.

The four lines said that, if Halsa Renn had killed Joren Aldis in cabin twelve from cabin nine-T, the long-contact glamour-chain exploit from the final year of his own thesis would have been a method she had access to, and that the access was structurally consistent with the apprentice-line confirmation from Pessel and with the *did work that needed quiet rooms* note from Hesh and with the eleven-year placement from the Mirren transcript. The four lines were a negative shape. They described what a method could be, if there were a method.

That was not the same as the method.

He let it be a negative shape. He did not let it be more. He had been at the table for three hours and the host had come past and refilled the pot twice and the four lines on the method page were the most he had been able to write in three weeks about how a man had died in a cabin nobody had entered, and he was not, on a Tuesday morning, going to let himself name them more than they were.

He did write, in plain ink, under the four lines: *To be tested against cabin twelve geometry and against the bolt and porthole on Sable's return.*

He underlined *tested* once, lightly. He underlined nothing else.

---

The host brought him soup at midday, on the assumption that a man at a working table since first bell would not have thought to order it himself. Theron looked up, noted the soup, clocked the spoon and the host's mild expression, and thanked him.

"It's the white-bean," the host said. "Tuesday's. The kitchen does it with the parsley early on, before the long flavour comes in. You catch it now or you catch it at four."

"Now," Theron said.

"Good answer." The host moved on.

He ate the soup. It was, in fact, a soup that did better caught early. He thought about the four lines on the method page while he ate it, and the soup did not lose by the thinking. The two did their work in parallel and were companionable about it.

He set the bowl aside and went back to the notebook.

The thing he had not yet done was the thing he should have done in the first hour, and he had not done it because he had not wanted to. He turned, in the slate-bound notebook, to the section of his examined defence script that the Conservatory clerk had copied verbatim for the licensing record. The script was the transcript of the oral defence — his words and the examiners', clean, no editorial. He had not opened the section since the day he had collected the pin.

He read the examiner's question about the absence of historical instance.

He read his own answer.

He read the examiner's follow-up, which was the question he had remembered better than the first one and the answer to which had won him the pin.

The follow-up had been: *Mr. Mavik, in the absence of any recorded case, on what evidentiary basis should the Conservatory recommend altering its field scry-detection standards?*

He had answered: *The absence of recorded case is the evidentiary basis. A class of exploit that does not register to current scry-detection will not generate cases under current scry-detection. The Conservatory is being asked to alter the detection because the detection itself is what is filtering out the evidence.*

He had been pleased with the answer at the time. He had thought, as he sat down after the defence, that he had said the thing he had come to say. The examiner who had liked the thesis had written, on the inside cover of the examined copy after the defence, *A good year's work. Well argued.*

He had not, on the day, considered the corollary.

He considered it now, at his table at the Arms, on a Tuesday afternoon, with the white-bean bowl pushed to one side.

The corollary was that *a class of exploit that does not register to current scry-detection will not generate cases under current scry-detection* meant, also, that any case it did generate would arrive looking like something else. A sealed cabin. A man dead inside it without a wound. No method on the page.

That was the corollary he had not stated at his defence, because at his defence he had been twenty-one years old and writing a theoretical paper and the corollary belonged to a working investigator, not to a candidate. He was a working investigator now. He had been one for three weeks.

He closed the notebook.

He sat with the case book open on the method page and the working-theory lines unindented on it.

He did not feel pleased.

He had been told, at the Conservatory, that there would be a moment in the first year of a working career when an examined piece of his own past work would arrive in the present and be useful. The Warden had said it, dryly, on the day of the licensing ceremony, as a thing to expect and not as a thing to wait for. *The pin is for what you have already learned. The work is for what you will be made to learn it for.*

He had taken the line for the kind of ceremony-day sentence the Warden was given to producing on ceremony days. He had been wrong. The Warden, as a rule, did not produce sentences for ceremony days. The Warden produced sentences for use, and the day had been incidental.

He read his four lines on the method page one more time.

He left them as they were. He did not add a fifth. The fifth line was *to be tested,* and *to be tested* was already on the page, and the testing was a thing he was not doing this afternoon because Sable was on a coach south and would be at the Arms by last bell and the testing wanted both of them at the table for it.

He picked up the pencil. He turned to the back-of-book page, where the Sunday note about the money for the child sat in its single line, and where the careful sentence about the original placement sat under it. He wrote, at the bottom of that page, in the smallest hand he had: *The thesis was not exam fodder.*

He read it.

He drew a single line through it and wrote, beside the struck-out line: *The thesis was exam fodder. It was also correct.*

That was the sentence he had needed to write. He did not need to write any more.

*[Investigator — Method page updated. Working theory logged. Status: theory only; pending field test against cabin geometry.]*

The System was being its laconic self. He read the line through once and gave it the small dry nod that was the only acknowledgement the System ever wanted from him. He did not particularly enjoy the System's neutrality this afternoon. He approved of it anyway.

He closed the case book on the method page with the four working-theory lines still unindented in it.

Outside, the Halverstow harbour was doing its Tuesday afternoon. The tide was on the turn, and the rope-master from yesterday had a new apprentice and the same patient instruction, and the brass on the slip fittings was throwing back the afternoon sun at a friendly angle. The Arms' kitchen smelled, now, of bread, which meant the host had started the second loaves of the day for the evening trade.

The coach to Halverstow from the north was due at last bell.

The case book sat on the table. The slate-bound notebook sat beside it, closed, the abstract three pages in and the defence script ten pages further. The method page was, for the first time since Friday, no longer blank.

It was not solved.

He let it be unsolved. He drank what was left of the coffee, which was cold, and waited for the bell.
