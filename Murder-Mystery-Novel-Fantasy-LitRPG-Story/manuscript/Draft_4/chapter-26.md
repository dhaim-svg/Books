# Chapter 26 — A Name on the Wrong Register

The wire from Mirren came in while Theron was still at the magistrate's office.

He had arrived at first bell for a follow-up on the warrant disclosure — a formality, mostly, to confirm the Vantrel records request was properly on the books and that Halverstow had sent its acknowledgment to Mirren in the right form. Davren's office was on the second floor of the courthouse building, a narrow room with two windows that looked out over the civic square, and Davren himself was behind the desk when Theron came in, making his way through a stack of case correspondence with the methodical quiet of someone who would make his way through the entire stack regardless of what else happened that morning.

They had been through the warrant paperwork in twenty minutes. Theron was still at the small clerk's table in the corner, adding a column to his working copy of the Vantrel request, when the clerk knocked and came in with the folded telegraph sheet.

"Wire from Mirren," the clerk said. He addressed it to Davren, as was proper. "Came through at first bell. The Mirren counterpart says the Kolach interview concluded yesterday afternoon."

Davren put his pen down and took the sheet. He read it once, in the way he read everything — without expression, without hurry. Then he looked up and held it across toward Theron.

"Relevant to yours, I expect."

Theron took it.

The summary was three paragraphs in the close hand of someone trained for it. He read the first paragraph standing, then sat down to read it again.

Kolach had not denied anything. The vouching irregularity for H. Marbeth had been on the Mirren books for some months as a flag awaiting cause; under the introduction letter and the threat of formal proceedings, Kolach had explained the arrangement in fifteen minutes. He ran a side service through Vantrel: discreet credential placement for clients who paid cash and could not, or chose not to, provide professional references. The clients were typically working mages whose contract work was not the kind that generated the references the harbor registries required. He charged a placement fee and a renewal fee. He had been doing it for eleven years. Marbeth's papers had been placed through this channel.

The third paragraph was the part Theron read twice.

Kolach had been required to produce the channel's placement log for the past two years. The Mirren magistrate's office had attached the relevant page from the current year. Twenty-three placements in the period under examination. The wire had transcribed all twenty-three.

He set the cup aside and read the list.

Names he didn't recognize, mostly. Two factor's names he knew from cargo correspondence around Toll's office. One name he half-knew from an apprentice register at the Conservatory, which was its own line of work for later. He went down the column with his finger.

The seventeenth entry stopped him.

*Renn, Halsa. Booking credential placement, third class, Skybound Wyrm, twenty-third departure of the current year.*

He read it. He read it again. He set the sheet down flat against the clerk's table.

Davren was watching him from behind the desk with the patient attention of a man who had recognized a result without yet knowing what kind. Theron schooled his face into nothing in particular and looked up.

"I'll need the original of this page sent through," he said. "Sealed copy, registry to registry. And I'll need the Mirren counterpart's full statement on Kolach's testimony — verbatim transcript, not the summary."

"Already requested," Davren said. "Ten days for the sealed copy, four to six for the transcript by post." He glanced at the sheet. "The seventeenth name."

"Yes."

Davren looked at it a moment longer, then set his pen back to the correspondence stack. He did not ask the name aloud. He did not offer an interpretation. He simply returned to his work, which was, Theron thought, exactly right.

"I'll be in this afternoon to add a column to the working file," Theron said.

The clerk was still at the door. He paused. "Mavik."

"Yes."

"The name on that list. I don't know who she is. But the Mirren counterpart noted at the end of the wire that Kolach had said the seventeenth placement was personally referred to him."

"Referred by whom?"

"It's in the transcript," the clerk said. "The wire wouldn't carry the full name through cleanly. I'd wait for the post."

He let himself out.

Theron sat with the sheet for a long minute, the civic square outside going about its Saturday business at half speed. Then he turned the page of his case book to a clean spread, dated it, and wrote the line at the top: *Renn, Halsa — booking credential placed through Kolach channel. Personal referral. Source pending.*

He underlined *personal referral* once, lightly, and went back to the table.

---

The third-class manifest was still where he'd left it, back at the Arms.

He had filed Halsa Renn at the time of the Wyrm interview as fourth-tier: travel-through, south coast, family business, cash, no professional reference. He had marked the cash booking as low-tier, because his frame was Vannic's world, and a cash passenger with no reference in that world was someone with nothing to hide and no leverage to use.

He had been wrong about the frame. He had written that down in the working column.

He had not, until now, understood what *no professional reference* had been doing in the cash booking process. He had read it as absence. It was substitution. A cash booking on a Wyrm route required a reference; a reference could be provided through the standard professional channels; or it could be provided through a placement service whose existence was not advertised because its clientele preferred it that way. Kolach had been running that placement service for eleven years. He had placed Halsa Renn's booking credential for the Wyrm's twenty-third departure of the current year.

The departure that had carried Joren Aldis under a forged name. The departure on which Joren Aldis had died.

He put the pencil down because he wanted to be sure he was not writing something he hadn't yet earned.

Then he picked it up and wrote, in the working column under the Renn line: *Booking credential channel — same channel through which Toll's contract paperwork moved (working assumption, to be verified against Vantrel records).* He added: *Halsa Renn's reference was not absent. It was placed.*

He sat back.

The Sannholt destination he had filed as innocuous geography. The cabin-nine-T late lamp Calden had flagged with uncertain provenance. The observation Sable had made about how Renn watched corridors. The detail in the Wyrm interview about hearing a creak from toward the second-class passage on the third night, the night of the death, which he had taken at face value as a witness's small contribution, because that was how he had categorized the witness.

Halsa Renn had walked through three weeks of his case file as background. A through-traveler, cash, family business, low-tier dismissal. She had moved through his frame the way a steward moves through a room: present, polite, unremarkable to anyone whose attention was elsewhere.

She had been in Joren's professional network. That was what the placement channel said. Not in the noble-rival pool. Not in Vannic's world. In Joren's.

*[Investigator — New column opened: Renn, Halsa. Booking credential through Kolach channel, personal referral. Joren's professional network. Status: working suspect, evidentiary basis incomplete.]*

He read the System line once. He didn't touch it. He turned the page and began drafting the cross-reference list he would take to the magistrate that afternoon.

---

Sable's first letter arrived at the Arms shortly after second bell.

The boy from the post-coach station carried it in the inside pouch he reserved for letters that had been paid extra to move on the morning's return run from Sannholt. He found Theron in the dining room and handed it over with the formal half-bow the post boys at this station all used, which Theron was now familiar enough with to nod through without comment.

He gave the boy a small coin for the carry and broke the seal at the table.

Sable's hand was the same on paper as it was in her notebook, small and economical and slightly leftward, and the letter was three folded pages.

*Theron —*

*The Sannholt coach was as advertised. Three other passengers, none of them my business. The road was good. I am at the Whitepost Inn on the south side of the market square, room six, until I find out whether I want to be somewhere else.*

*Pessel agreed to speak in the morning. I sent word from the coach office on arrival; she replied within the hour at the inn. The reply was three sentences. She named the hour, she named the place — her own front room, not the Copper Finch — and she asked one question, which was whether I had been on the Wyrm with him. I wrote back that I had been. That seems to have been the answer she needed.*

*I have not seen her yet. What I can tell you about her so far is that her hand on paper is steady, that she writes her own letters and does not use a clerk, and that she did not ask me to bring documents. She is not approaching this as an inquiry. She is approaching it as a conversation she has been waiting to have for two years.*

*I asked at the inn for her address and got it without difficulty. The landlady knew her by name and by what she did for a living, which is what she had said it was in the harbor registry: small-contract paperwork, marriage settlements and inheritance papers and the occasional probate consultation. She is what she said she was. That is worth knowing as a starting point.*

He turned the page.

*The detail I am writing this letter for: when I asked the landlady — casually, in the way you ask landladies things — whether Pessel had many friends in the town, she said not many but loyal ones, and then she said something I think you should have before tomorrow's meeting. She said Pessel used to host a young woman in the back room of the Copper Finch perhaps three or four times a year. Coastal, she said. Worked the same kind of small magical trades. Quiet. Apprenticed once to a man who did the same work, the landlady thought, though the apprenticeship was years ago by the time she had started noticing the visits.*

*I did not ask the woman's name. The landlady did not offer one. I am writing this because I think the connection — Pessel, an apprentice, coastal, small magical trades — is the kind of detail your column-work would want before I sit with Pessel and ask her about Joren tomorrow. If the name turns out to matter, it will come up. I will let it come up the way she wants to bring it, not the way I want to take it.*

*Still have the fish. I have not taken it out. I will not bring it to the meeting tomorrow. I do not know yet whether tomorrow is the day for it.*

*— S.*

He read the letter through once at the table, and then he read the middle page again, slowly, line by line, and then he folded it along its existing creases and put it inside his case book.

He sat with the cup for a moment.

The shape of it was visible now. Pessel was Joren's long-running connection in Sannholt; an apprentice had moved through that connection; the apprentice was coastal, female, in the same trade. Halsa Renn was in cabin nine-T on the Wyrm, traveling through to Sannholt under a booking credential placed through Joren's professional network, and had observed the corridor with the attentiveness of someone who watched how people moved through spaces. Sable would have a name from Pessel by tomorrow afternoon, or she wouldn't, and either way the Halverstow side and the Sannholt side were converging on the same suspect column.

He did not write *Halsa Renn was Joren's apprentice* in the case book. He had not earned that line. He wrote, in the new column: *Sannholt source confirms coastal female apprentice in Joren's professional circle, name not yet given, visits to Pessel three to four times yearly. Cross-reference pending Pessel statement.*

He underlined nothing.

---

The dining room had filled around him while he wasn't watching. A factor at the next table was arguing politely with his wife about whether they should leave for Mirren on Monday or wait for the wire from her brother. A traveling cleric was eating a slow breakfast at the far window. The light through the window had moved the angle it had been at when he'd arrived back from the courthouse, and the harbor smelled, faintly, of the tar they were applying to one of the slips a block over.

He drew a fresh sheet from his case book and set it square in front of him.

*Sable —*

*The Kolach interview came back from Mirren faster than the budget. The wire arrived at first bell, the original transcript follows by post in four to six days. Kolach broke at the introduction letter. He had been running a credential placement service through Vantrel for eleven years; the Marbeth papers came through that channel; so did twenty-two other names in the past year. One of those names is Halsa Renn — booking credential, third class, our Wyrm departure.*

*She was in his network. Not Vannic's. Joren's.*

*Your apprentice detail is the same name in a second column. I do not have the personal-referral source yet — that is in the transcript, not in the wire — but Pessel may give it to you in the morning before Mirren gives it to me by post. Take it on her terms. I am not asking you to test the connection. I am asking you to know it is there while you listen.*

*I have not added her to the working column as a suspect. I have added her as a name that should now be in front of both of us.*

*The fish is right where it is. Tomorrow may or may not be the day. You will know.*

*— T.*

He read it through, made one small correction (*I am not asking you to test the connection* was clearer than the longer phrasing he had begun with), and sealed it for the afternoon post-coach back to Sannholt.

Then he turned back to the case book and wrote the new column out properly, in the order he wanted it in front of him for the next morning's work.

*Renn, Halsa. Cabin nine-T, Wyrm 23rd departure. Booking credential through Kolach channel — personal referral, source pending. Sannholt destination. Late lamp, cabin nine-T, third night (Calden, uncertain). Corridor observation behavior (Sable). Sannholt source — coastal female apprentice in Joren's circle, Pessel connection, name pending. Status: in front of us.*

He set the pencil down.

*[Investigator — Working column: Renn, Halsa. Status updated: in front of us.]*

He read the System line and almost smiled, because the System occasionally did this. It quoted his own phrasing back at him as though it were holding the page steady for him. Then he closed the case book.

The Saturday harbor outside was busy in its ordinary way. The wire transcript would arrive in four to six days. Sable would sit with Pessel in the morning. The post-coach to Sannholt left at fourth bell.

He picked up his coat and went to find the magistrate's afternoon clerk.

The case had a name in it that had been there all along. That was something. The rest was work.
