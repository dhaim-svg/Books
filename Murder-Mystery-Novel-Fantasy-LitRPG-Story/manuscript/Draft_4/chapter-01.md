# Chapter 1 — Boarding the Wyrm

The boarding line for the Skybound Wyrm opened at second bell, and Theron Mavik arrived at first bell and stood in front of a rope with eleven other people who had also arrived at first bell, all of them looking at different things to avoid acknowledging this.

He moved his thumb off the graduation pin for the fourth time this morning.

The gondola was larger than he'd expected. He'd memorized the standard passenger-cargo specifications for his licensing exam — hull length, deck plan, lift capacity, harness chain loads — but specifications were numbers, and the actual object in front of him was the length of a city block, lacquered the color of old bone, and gave off a low hum he felt through his boot soles. Three rigging chains ran up and over the forward boom, each as thick as his thigh. At the far end of the chains, three drakes were settled in their harness, wings folded, radiating enough heat that he could feel it from thirty feet away.

One of them was eating something with tremendous concentration.

"That's Mottled." A small woman with sun-freckled arms and a leather handler's apron was running a stiff-bristled brush behind the ear-ridge of one of the other drakes, unhurried, the kind of unhurried that came from long practice. "Gets anxious before a long flight. Settles herself by eating. Works through about half a crate of river fish when we're docked in the bigger ports. By altitude she's fine."

Theron looked at the eating drake more carefully. She was the second-largest, her scales the color of dried clay where they weren't mottled with a rust-orange that gave her her name, and she was working through a fish the size of his forearm with the studied indifference of a creature too occupied to care about an audience.

"And that's Gravel?" He indicated the one the handler was brushing — broad-headed, darker, with the particular stillness of something that was choosing to hold still.

"Gravel, yes. I wouldn't approach him until he's been introduced to you specifically. All bite and no warning, and a very long memory for faces. He's fine once he knows you, but he does prefer to make the knowing his own idea." She said it without looking up from the brush. "The small one's Petal."

He looked at the small one. It was somewhere between six and seven feet at the shoulder and was watching him with one amber eye, the vertical slit pupil the size of his forearm. Pale gold scales along her underbelly shaded to something closer to cream at the jaw. She had the alert, unconcerned quality of a creature that knew exactly what it was.

"Petal," he said.

"She earned it." The handler — Wisp, her name badge said — grinned at the drake in the way most people grinned at old dogs, with the comfortable authority of someone who knew exactly what the other party was thinking and had long since made peace with it. She'd positioned herself between the brush strokes so Gravel could see every motion before it landed. "Petal knows these thermals by name, near enough. The Brindle Range approach, she'll shift her wing angle before I've told her to — she's been down this route seventeen times and she remembers them all."

"Do they communicate between themselves? While flying?"

Wisp considered this. "That's a question I've had for nine years." She moved the brush to the base of Gravel's neck. "They work together better than most mated pairs I've seen, but I couldn't tell you if that's language or just long familiarity. Mottled and Petal argue about altitude sometimes. You can tell by the way Mottled extends her right primary — she does it when she disagrees."

"And Gravel?"

"Gravel," Wisp said, with the gravity of long experience, "does as Gravel sees fit, and the other two have made their peace with that."

Theron looked at Mottled again. She had finished the fish and was regarding the dock with a half-lidded satisfaction that suggested she might be starting to feel better about the flight. Then she looked at him.

He looked away first.

"Boarding in twenty," Wisp said, still working at the brush. "First class?"

"Yes."

"Good route for us this run. Petal knows these thermals, and Mottled will have settled by altitude — she always does." She said it with the easy confidence of someone who'd delivered this reassurance two hundred times and found it to be consistently true. "Good view of the harness from the forward promenade, if you want it once we're up. Petal tends to fly slightly ahead and to the right on this approach."

He thanked her and moved a few feet to the right.

---

The dock filled around him in increments.

A family of four arrived with matching luggage in an ambitious shade of blue, the children immediately and separately making a case for a different vantage point while the parents held the line with practiced diplomacy. Two scholars in travelling coats arrived together, one of them carrying a shared briefcase and the other of them arguing about it in the specific, low-intensity way of people who had been having the same argument since before he could remember. A courier in working leathers was eating a meat pastry with the contentment of a man whose morning had gone correctly, taking his time, his spot in the queue settled and sufficient.

At the far end of the dock, a merchant in silk and two assistants had arranged themselves a careful distance from the queue. He caught her name in a low exchange between her and a waiting passenger: Madam Esherel, said with the slight deliberateness of someone naming someone they respected and were not entirely at ease with. She was examining the boarding ledger as though it owed her something. One of her assistants watched the drakes; the other watched Esherel.

Theron checked his own boarding card. Cabin fourteen, deck four, first class. Three days between here and his first active case assignment. His supervisor had set the card on his desk and said: "You have earned a rest. Take the Wyrm route. Come back with your head on straight."

She had said it twice. He had not needed it twice.

He was aware, standing there, that a man who had just passed his licensing examination and received a posting was not necessarily a man whose head was on straight. He was also aware that this awareness was itself slightly insufferable. He noted it. He looked at the drakes.

Mottled had started on a second fish.

---

The rope dropped at second bell.

He picked up his travel case and moved into the queue. Calden — the gondola steward, badge and pressed uniform, the deliberate unhurriedness of a man who had boarded a thousand nervous passengers — checked his name, confirmed his cabin, and handed him a key with his berth stamped in red wax.

"Deck Four, starboard, Mr. Mavik. The cabin board will show you the way." A small pause, the kind that was not really a pause. "I'll be around shortly to settle you in."

"Thank you, Calden."

"Of course, Mr. Mavik."

There was something in the way he said it — not rote, exactly, but well-worn, the phrase used so often it had become something different from automatic. Like a word that had been spoken into the right context enough times that it meant what it said. Theron filed it and moved on, which was what he did with most things: a clean, quiet receipt of the world as it presented itself, then the drawer closing, then the next thing.

He was aware this was a habit his supervisor had not described as a strength.

The boarding area past the gangway was the predictable bottleneck: a corridor built for two people, five people trying to use it simultaneously. He stepped sideways to let the family with the toddler navigate through first, then moved toward the staircase, filing the layout as it came: stairways, outer deck hatches, the window line of the first-class corridor above.

He very nearly stepped backward into a woman who was pulling her bag through the crowd by a handle that was losing its structural argument with the bag's weight.

"Sorry," he said.

She didn't answer, just angled herself and the bag around him with the brisk economy of a morning already too full of obstacles. Brown travelling coat, the practical kind, chosen for its pockets rather than its impression. She had the expression of a person who had agreed to a trip, arrived at the departure point, and was now conducting a quiet assessment of whether that had been the correct call.

She caught Calden's eye over the crowd and held up one finger: *just the one bag, then.* Calden nodded once, with the unhurried competence of a man who had already noted the bag and would see to it presently. She moved on.

Theron went up the stairs.

---

His cabin was halfway along the first-class corridor — small, clean, honest about its dimensions. West-facing, which meant long afternoon light and no dawn. He tested the lamp; it worked. He pressed once on the mattress to confirm it was a mattress rather than a board with ambitions. He set his case on the writing desk, hung his coat on the hook by the door, and went back out to the boarding deck.

He was getting air. That was all it was.

The tail end of the queue was still working through. The scholars had managed the gangway with their briefcase intact, apparently — only one of them was talking rapidly at the other now, which seemed like progress. Madam Esherel had found a gap and was moving through it with the cool assurance of a woman for whom gaps reliably appeared. In the boarding area below, the family of four had spread out enough to be navigating in three directions at once.

And then the sound of the boarding area shifted.

Not loudly. It was the small recalibration of a crowd's attention — the way a room adjusts when something has entered that everyone is choosing to process in real time. Theron looked toward the dock entrance.

The man coming through was somewhere in his mid-twenties, with the jaw that came from a hundred years of portraits and a coat that communicated its price by not discussing it. Behind him, a personal steward pushed a luggage cart stacked three high. He walked at the front of the convoy with the settled certainty of a man who had never been required to hurry and had built his opinions on people who were.

His gaze moved across the dock once and efficiently. The scholars — dismissed. The family — beneath notation. Theron — assessed as young and not immediately consequential, set aside. Madam Esherel — and here his expression shifted very slightly, the way a man's does when he's encountered something he didn't expect and doesn't want to give ground on — acknowledged, not greeted.

"Lord Vannic," someone murmured, nearby. The name moved through the remaining queue in undertones, neighbor to neighbor.

He crossed toward the gangway without deviation. The courier — still finishing his pastry, not entirely out of the path — looked up and to the side and performed the quiet calculation of someone weighing inconvenience against principle, then moved.

Vannic barely registered the movement. He had already reached Calden.

At the gangway threshold, he stopped for an elderly passenger finishing the step down. Not with any performance — not the wide gesture or the produced smile — just a pause, nothing more, waiting the three seconds required and then taking the step up. He did it without looking at the man.

Madam Esherel, watching from six feet away, said something low to her assistant. The assistant nodded. Whatever had been said did not appear to require elaboration.

Then Calden stepped forward and began showing him to his cabin — speaking quietly about the window orientation, the dining arrangements, the first-class steward's hours. Vannic was listening with the attentiveness of someone who expected to get his money's worth, which was different from not listening.

Halfway across the boarding area, without breaking stride or looking at Calden, he said his name. Not the family name — the personal one, the one on the badge.

"Calden." A brief pause. "Thank you. For — all of it. The boarding business."

Calden replied that it was of course no trouble, and kept moving. The exchange had lasted perhaps four seconds. It was aimed at one person, in a tone that had no audience in mind.

Theron was still at the upper-deck rail.

He had already moved on from it — the way you moved on from something that didn't require further thought. Then he noticed the dock again. The woman in the brown coat was at the far edge of the boarding area, one hand on the strap of her bag, not moving. She wasn't looking at Vannic's back. She was looking at the middle of the crowd — the specific patch of air where a man had just thanked the steward by name, without ceremony, and said *all of it* as if that covered everything without needing to be itemized.

He didn't see her face change. She was just still for a moment, in a way that had a quality to it.

Then she shouldered her bag and moved on toward the staircase.

Theron went back upstairs.

---

He was considering his cabin key with academic patience — the lock required an angle that was not disclosed on the key — when the sound of footsteps on the upper staircase announced Lord Vannic and Calden arriving in the corridor, apparently still on the subject of window orientation.

It was a narrow corridor. There was a moment at the landing, natural and unremarkable, where one of them was required to acknowledge the other.

Vannic offered his hand first. Theron took it.

The grip was firm, brief, bare palm. And the side of his index finger — the raised ridge, built up by years of sustained casting hold, where prolonged contact with a spellwork frame wears the skin to something harder and more specific than ordinary callus. Theron's thesis advisor had one. Both of his practical illusion professors had them. You didn't get that from an occasional hobby. You got it from ten hours a day, reliably, across years.

*Noble who dabbles*, he filed. Then, amending: *practices more seriously than that implies.*

"You look young," Vannic said. It came out as observation rather than insult, which was something.

"First posting." The pin made it obvious; there was no use in false modesty about it.

Vannic's eyes went briefly to the lens embossed in bronze. "Investigator."

"Yes."

Something shifted in his face — not warming. Adjusting. "Hm." He released the handshake. "Well." And moved on down the corridor toward the upper stair.

Calden followed. Theron stood with his key.

*First posting, and no active case.* He was aware of the pin the way he'd been aware of it all morning — the slight weight of a new thing on his jacket, still unfamiliar in the way that new things were before they became ordinary. He was also aware that it would take roughly six months to stop checking whether it was still there, and that everyone who had been through the licensing examination had this same six months, and that this awareness did not make him stop checking.

He got the lock open on the fourth angle.

The corridor was quiet again — just the distant low hum of the gondola's hull and, below it, the sound of the dock at work: calls between loaders, the mechanical knock of a winch settling a crate into the hold. He stood in his own doorway for a moment before going in. First-class corridor, deck four, starboard: six cabins to his left, a narrow window at the end admitting a pale bar of morning light, a runner carpet that had been replaced recently enough to still hold its pattern. Someone had put a small table with a water carafe opposite the third door. That was Calden's thinking, probably — the kind of considered detail that didn't announce itself.

He went inside and set the key on the writing desk.

The case slate. The notes folder. His reference volume, which he had carried for three years and had memorized sufficiently that it was now ballast rather than resource, but he had not been willing to leave it behind. He set them in the right order, checked that the desk lamp could reach the writing surface, confirmed that the writing surface was level enough to matter, and then stopped himself, because he was on holiday and there was nothing to prepare.

He put the reference volume in the drawer instead.

---

The Skybound Wyrm lifted thirty minutes late, on account of a disputed crate in the cargo hold that took three dockhands and some sustained disagreement to resolve. He felt the moment in his feet first — the floor's quality changing, the deep hum of the harness chains rising to something thinner and more continuous — and then through the porthole: the dock sliding. The slow, courteous separation from solid ground.

The cabin lamp was lit. It smelled faintly of the flame oil his father kept in the dispensary cabinet at the back of the shop — not intensely, just the ghost of it, enough to be noticed. He noted it and moved on.

He sat on the padded bench beneath the porthole.

The examination record: six weeks of written assessments, three of practicals, one oral thesis defense. His thesis — *Scryproof Glamour Exploits: Theoretical Vulnerability in Long-Contact Illusion Chains* — had been called "original, if limited in practical application" by the examiner who had liked him. The one who hadn't had called it "somewhat overstated." Both verdicts appeared in the record and he had received the pin regardless, which he considered an empirical resolution.

His mother had cried. His father had said *good* in the voice that meant something longer than good — meant the scholarships and the years and all the rest of it had come down somewhere he'd hoped it would. Theron had spent three days at home pretending he wasn't enormously pleased.

He touched the pin now.

Small. A bronze oval, lens embossed at the center, the size of a coin he'd carry without thinking about. At fourteen, when an Investigator had come through his hometown to consult on a poisoning case and he'd seen one for the first time — on the woman's lapel, catching the afternoon light — he had thought: *that is the most consequential object I have ever seen.* The actual object was considerably more modest than that memory. It was also, exactly, what it was supposed to be.

*[Investigator — Field status: Unscheduled interval.]*

He couldn't tell if the System was being dry on purpose. He chose to read it as such.

The dock had gone. The city was going — rooftops, market towers, the long green stripe of the river — tilting slowly east and then dropping below the hull line, replaced by the pale flat blue of a midmorning sky that would get better once it had more time to work on itself. The harness chains were singing one clear note in the slipstream.

At the edge of the porthole, three shapes moved across the blue: wings outstretched, banking slightly starboard as they found the first thermal. Petal was on the right — recognizable by her size, which was not a navigational reference he'd expected to develop, and yet.

She banked through the air with the ease of something that had never had reason to question whether it belonged there.

Three days. No active case. Nothing to investigate, no inference chain to close. He settled his shoulder blades against the padded bench.

Petal made a banking adjustment — a small cant of the right wing, just enough to use the thermal's edge rather than fight it. He hadn't expected to be able to read her movements from this distance. He could, though. She was the one who moved like she was enjoying it.

Below the porthole, the roof of the city's market quarter was still visible, its copper weather vanes bright in the late-morning light, turning south-southwest in a wind he couldn't feel from inside the cabin. He watched until the rooftops dropped below the hull line. Then there was only sky, and the chains' continuous singing, and the three shapes holding their formation out ahead.

He had made the right choice not to become a naturalist. He would have been insufferable about drakes.

He moved his thumb off the pin, and left it there.

He became aware, sometime in the next few minutes, that he didn't know what he was going to do with the rest of the day. Not in a way that required solving — there was a dining room, there was a promenade, there was presumably a procedure for both — but the awareness was new enough to be slightly odd. His whole adult life had organized itself around the next thing to prepare for. He looked at the porthole. The sky outside was a very plain, undemanding blue, and it had nothing for him to find.
