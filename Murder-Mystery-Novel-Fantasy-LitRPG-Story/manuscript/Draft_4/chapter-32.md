# Chapter 32 — The Name at the Foot of the Page

The Thursday post-coach came in at second bell with the small commotion the host's side door always made of an arriving coach. Theron heard the wheels on the cobble and the driver thanking somebody and the inn door opening twice in close succession. He kept his hand on the case book and did not turn around.

Sable was at the table opposite him. She had been there since first bell. The bread was on a board between them, half a loaf left, and the second pot had come and was three-quarters down.

"That's Sannholt," she said.

"Yes."

She did not get up. The host was at the side door already and would bring the letter through when it came through, and there was no point standing in his way. She turned a page in her notebook to a blank face and set the pencil beside it the way she set pencils beside blank notebook faces when she was about to write something and had not yet decided what.

The host came across the dining room with a single small envelope between his thumb and forefinger.

"From your Sannholt friend," he said. "Driver said second-fastest reply he's carried this season."

"Thank you."

"Pot?"

"Please."

The host went on. Sable broke the seal with her thumbnail and slid the folded sheet out. She set it on the table flat. She read the salutation upside-down to him first.

"Pessel's hand," she said.

"Yes."

She turned it the right way for herself and read it through. He drank coffee. He did not lean forward. Watching her read a letter did not make the letter arrive any sooner.

She read it a second time. Then she set her finger on a line in the middle of the page.

"Here."

She turned the sheet around for him. Pessel's hand was small and sloped slightly to the left and used a particular comma he had got used to looking for as a signature. He read the line her finger was on.

*The seven years past sufficiency were a deliberate teaching. He kept her because there was a thing he meant to show her that he showed nobody else. He called it the late-stage work, when he called it anything. Quiet method, mostly. He did not write it down.*

He read it.

He read it twice.

"Quiet method," he said.

"Mostly."

"That's the trigger architecture without the words for it."

"Pessel doesn't have the words for it. She has the shape and what he called it. The words are yours."

He set the page back down between them. Pessel's letter ran another six lines past that one, and he read those too. She wrote that she had thought about Sable's question on the walk back from the bakery and again on the way to bed and a third time over breakfast, and that she had nothing to add to the line she had set down at the top, except that he had taught no one else the late-stage work, she would have known, and that the apprentice had carried it out of the house with her when she went south, the way a journeyman carried out a master's last gift. *He did not get it back from her*, Pessel wrote, in the smaller hand she used for things she had thought about for longer than she liked to admit. *I never asked whether he wanted to.*

He read the closing sentence.

He pushed the page back so it lay between them on the open notebook face and weighed it down with the back of his pencil.

"That's the master side," he said.

"That's the master side."

"He taught it to her. He taught it to her only. The trigger left the house with her."

"And he didn't take it back."

"And he didn't take it back."

She did not put a question mark on it. He did not answer it. The sentence belonged to the letter and the letter belonged to the table, and the table had been getting longer sentences put on it for a week now.

The host arrived with the new pot. He set it down without ceremony and took the old one with him, and the smell of the new pot was a different smell from the smell of the old pot in the way the second loaves of the day smelled different from the first loaves of the day. Sable refilled her cup and did not refill his because his was still half.

She closed Pessel's letter along its old fold and set it on the corner of the table where they kept letters they were going to want again.

"All right," she said.

---

The Wyrm passenger manifest was a long folded sheet from the magistrate's office that Davren had let them take a fair copy of on Wednesday afternoon. It was three columns: cabin number, name, place of disembarkation. There were forty-one entries on the working manifest (distinct from the 72-passenger booked manifest). Theron had read it through twice already, on Wednesday evening and again before Sable had come down this morning, and he had not, until now, sat with it beside the requirements page.

He laid it flat between them.

He turned the case book to the requirements page and set it parallel.

"Read me the list," he said.

"All of it?"

"Cabin by cabin. I'll mark the page where each one fails."

She put her finger on the first row. Cabin one, the Mirren factor and his clerk, Halverstow-bound. She read it aloud in the slightly careful voice she used for documents, name and cabin and disembarkation in the order they were on the page, and waited.

He looked at the requirements page.

"(a). Fails."

"How?"

"He's a Mirren factor with eleven years in the same office. No coastal apprenticeship. No illusion training. The work he could do for Joren would have been credential-only and would not satisfy contact-time."

"Mark it."

He drew a small flat line through the cabin-one row on his copy of the manifest. He did not write *fails* against it. He had no use for the word on the page. The line was enough.

"Cabin two."

"The Stallens. Mother and adult son. Gladwater-bound."

"(a). Fails for her. She's a textile factor, sixty-one, no working magical practice. The son was at the Conservatory for one term and dropped to his father's trade. He could pass first-year glamour but not seating, and the time isn't there."

"Mark it."

A small flat line, through both Stallen entries.

"Cabin three."

"Dr. Vellaine."

He paused on Vellaine for a moment, because she was the only entry on the manifest that did not fail at line (a) in a way that did not need explanation. He had cleared her in his head twice already, but the work was to clear her on the page.

"(a) clears. She is a working scholar of illusion with a thirty-one-year career. She knew Joren by professional acquaintance; they shared a panel at the Erethon symposium in the year I was nine. (b), signature compatibility, fails. Vellaine and Joren never trained together. Compatibility is the apprenticeship's working product, not the panel's. They were colleagues. They were not paired. (c) fails for the same reason. She would have known the theory of the trigger architecture; nobody taught it to her in practice because she had no practice with him to teach it in."

"Mark it."

The flat line, on the cabin-three row. He had been a little reluctant to draw it. He drew it anyway, because the work was to draw it.

"Cabin four. The Rennison sisters."

"(a). Fails for both. They are road-coats, third trip together this season, both work in textile dye. There is no illusion in their hands at all. Calden said one of them asked him on the third night what the bell schedule meant by *first watch*, and Calden told the story to your steward friend, who told you. People who do not know what *first watch* means are not running long-form magical apprenticeships."

"Mark it."

She read on.

Cabin five. Cabin six. Cabin seven. He marked the line each time. Some failed at (a) before he had finished hearing the name; some required (b) or (c). The factor in cabin five was an iron merchant from a town on the upper river; he failed at (a). The widow in cabin six had been an apothecary's wife and had filed her own grief letters through the Halverstow office and had no contact-range magical practice on her record; (a). The captain's cousin in cabin seven had been a courier his whole adult life and could not lay glamour at all; (a).

"Cabin eight. Madam Esherel."

He sat with her for two breaths.

Esherel had been his cleanest red herring in the first week of the case. He had narrowed in on her in the Wyrm corridors and had been wrong about her and had let her become a source about Joren in the second week instead. He held her name in his head now without the feeling either time had put on it.

"(a) clears. Esherel did three years as a stage assistant for a touring illusionist in her twenties; she was open about it when I asked her last Sunday. Three years is laboratory-floor contact-time but probably below seating-phase under non-laboratory conditions. (b) fails. Her apprenticeship was not with Joren. Their relationship is mercantile and recent; their signatures are not paired. (c) fails for the same reason. Nobody taught her the trigger architecture during a three-year stage-assistant arrangement two decades before Joren's working method existed in usable form."

"Mark it."

He marked it.

She read the second-class rows. He worked through them. The road-coat couple in twelve-A failed at (a); the courier in twelve-B failed at (a); the second-class purser failed at (a); the academic couple in fourteen-A and fourteen-B failed at (b) and (c) respectively, by separate routes. The third-class rows were faster. Most of them failed at (a) on the first half of the line, and most were marked before Sable had finished reading the row.

She came to cabin nine-T.

She did not read the name. She read the cabin number and stopped. The pencil was in his hand. He did not move it to the page.

"Renn," she said.

"I know."

"(a)."

"Clears. Eleven-year apprenticeship with Joren, confirmed by Pessel on Sunday and reconfirmed by this morning's letter."

"(b)."

"Clears. Apprenticeship's working product. Signature compatibility between two illusionists who worked alongside each other for eleven years."

"(c)."

"Clears. Pessel this morning: *he taught her the late-stage work, the quiet method, only her, did not write it down*. The trigger architecture is the quiet method. He taught it deliberately and to her alone."

"(d)."

"Clears. Trigger retention. The trigger left the house with her, Pessel's letter says, and he did not take it back."

"(e)."

"Clears. Late-stage proximity from victim to caster. The seating phase completed during the back half of the apprenticeship. We have nine years of southbound visits from Hesh, and the Pessel apprentice-line, and Joren personally vouching her placement eleven years ago through Vantrel."

"(f)."

"Clears. Working life undetected on standing-detection rigs. Nine years of private rooms at the Copper Finch, Sannholt-bound trips, no professional reference on the Wyrm manifest, cash booking, the Mirren transcript's pattern of *picked up the work, never wrote it down*."

He stopped.

The page in front of him was a manifest with forty flat lines drawn through it and one row unmarked.

Sable was looking at the page. He was looking at the page.

She set the pencil she had been holding down beside the line of the unmarked row and did not draw any line through it.

"That's the subtraction," she said.

"That's the subtraction."

"It doesn't prove she did it."

"No. It proves no one else on the Wyrm could have."

"Different thing."

"Different thing. And the thing Davren needs."

He read the manifest through one more time, top to bottom, and his eye stopped at the unmarked row the way an eye stopped at a window where a lamp was burning when every other window on the street was dark. The lamp did not name the person inside the room. The lamp said the room was the one that was lit.

---

He turned the case book.

He opened the requirements page to its second face. He had left it blank yesterday morning at the foot of the six working lines, on the chance that he would need the space and on the second chance that he would not need it and the blank would mean something different. He had not, on Wednesday, known which it would be.

He knew now.

He set the case book square in front of him. The six lines stood at the top of the page in the unindented hand he used for working theory not yet earned. The page below them was as he had left it, no marks, no stray pencil-shadow at the margin. The blank ran the length of the page from the bottom of line (f) to the page's foot.

He picked up the pencil.

He held it for the length of one slow breath.

He had been thinking about this moment, in the back of his head, since Wednesday afternoon, since the line on the back-of-book page had read *converges to one person on the manifest* and he had not let himself write more than that. He had not, at the time, known what the moment would feel like. He had thought, in the way a person thought about a working sentence he had not yet written, that it would feel like the second examiner's follow-up question in his thesis defence: the moment of hearing the answer in his own head before he said it.

It did not feel like that.

It felt the way the lamp on the unmarked row had looked: simple, particular, no larger than itself.

He set the pencil to the page.

He wrote, in the unindented hand he used for working theory now earned, two words at the foot of the page.

*Halsa Renn.*

He read what he had written.

He did not underline it. He did not date it. He did not put any sentence under it explaining what the name meant or what it did not yet mean. The two words sat on the page below six requirement lines, and the requirement lines, read down to the name, made the name the only English the page was capable of producing. He had built the page so that this would be true. The pencil had done what the page had asked.

He set the pencil down beside the case book.

He had a strange small impulse to close the book. He did not close it. He let the page lie open between them.

Sable had not moved while he was writing. She had not looked away from the page. He registered the not-looking-away the way he registered most of what she did, in a part of his head that did not say it.

She said it.

"You wrote it," she said.

"I wrote it."

"It's true."

"It's true. Which is a different thing from proved, and the page knows the difference."

"The page knows the difference."

"And so do we."

She did not say anything for the length of a breath. She did not pick up her own pencil. She did not turn her notebook. She read the two words at the foot of the page with the steady look that was not pity and not satisfaction, and he had seen her give it now four times since Sunday, and he was beginning to suspect it was the look she gave a piece of work she was willing to stand beside without needing it to be hers.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

> *[Investigator — Working page closed. Method-of-perpetrator structural requirements: six lines, exclusivity established by exhaustive subtraction against the Wyrm passenger manifest. Named candidate logged: Renn, H. Status: structural and exclusive fit. Working theory now of a quality to be carried to a magistrate.]*

He read the System line through once.

It came in with the same dryness it had come in with on Wednesday, after *one named candidate, exclusivity check pending*. This time the qualification had moved. *Structural and exclusive fit.* He registered, without taking pleasure in it, that the System had used the word *carried*, which was the word he had been about to use himself and had not yet said out loud. The System had read the page with him again. He gave it the small dry nod that was the only answer it had ever wanted.

He looked at the name at the foot of the page.

He let himself look at it for a slow count he did not count.

He had not, on a Thursday morning in November, in a dining room two streets from a magistrate's office, in a town he had not heard of three weeks ago, expected to be the person who put that name on that page. He had expected, three weeks ago, to be on holiday. He thought briefly of the graduation pin under his coat, and of the Warden's sentence about a working investigator's first year, and of the small careful difference between *the theory holds* and *the theory holds against everyone else on the manifest*. One of those sentences had been Tuesday's. The other was today's.

He did not write any of that on the page.

The page already had what the page needed.

---

He closed the case book on the requirements page, with his thumb in for a moment to hold it, and then without his thumb in, so that the case book lay shut on the table between them with the name at the foot of a page nobody else could see.

Sable was drinking the last of her cup. The host was at the front-room door, putting the morning's chairs back the way a host put chairs back at the change of bell.

"Tomorrow is Davren," she said.

"Tomorrow is Davren."

"You'll bring him the case book."

"I'll bring him the case book. I'll bring him the requirements page and the manifest and the abstract and the scry log and the Mirren transcript and Pessel's two letters. He'll read the page that says her name. He'll read the lines above it. He'll ask what I'd like him to do with it."

"And what would you like him to do with it?"

He thought.

He had not, until she asked, thought of the answer in the form of a sentence. He had thought of it in the form of a shape: a room, two doors, the magistrate at one end of a table and Halsa Renn at the other, and Theron and Sable at neither end. He had not let himself dwell on the shape because the shape was Friday's, and Friday was the day after the day he was sitting in.

He thought now in plain words.

"I'd like him to bring her in," he said. "Not by force. By appointment. He sends a clerk to her boarding-house with a magistrate's request. She comes to his office at a time of his arranging. The case book is on his table. I am at his shoulder. You are at mine. He puts the page to her and asks her to read it. She reads it. What she does after that is her piece of the morning and not mine."

"You'll let her read it."

"I'll let her read it. The page is the page. She'll know what it says before she's read three lines of it. The reading is the courtesy."

"All right."

"All right."

Sable set the cup down. She did not stand up. She stayed at the table opposite him and looked at the closed case book, and at the manifest with its forty flat lines and its one unmarked row, and at Pessel's letter at the corner of the table where they kept letters they were going to want again.

"It's a quiet morning," she said.

"It is."

"Quieter than I'd have thought."

"I'd have thought louder."

"And it isn't."

"And it isn't."

He let himself sit with it for the length of one slow pull from the cup. The host went past with the lamp-stick to the front-room sconces, which he lit at the change of bell on weekdays in November when the harbour wind ran off the land. The brass of the sconces took the small flame and held it without commentary, which was about right for the room and for the morning and for the table between Theron and Sable and the closed case book on it.

He did not open the case book again.

He did not need to. The page was the page. The name was at the foot of it. The work for tomorrow was known.

He drank what was left of his cup, and Sable drank what was left of hers, and the second pot sat warm between them and was not, this morning, asked to do any more than that.
