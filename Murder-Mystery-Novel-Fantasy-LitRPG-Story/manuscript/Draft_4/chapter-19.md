# Chapter 19 — The Reply from Berric Toll

Theron was two pages into a fair copy of the magistrate's supplemental request when the messenger arrived.

He had requisitioned a small table in the harbourmaster's waiting room that morning — not an office, just a table with two chairs and a lamp that pulled sideways on its gooseneck — and had been there long enough that the staff no longer looked up when he crossed the main desk. The letter to Pessel at the Copper Finch had gone in Tuesday's post-coach run the evening before. The TL-9 flag was in at the magistrate's office. Both threads were now moving without him, which meant the morning belonged to paperwork, and Theron, despite everything the Academy had said about field work, was better at paperwork than almost anything else.

The messenger was twelve, the kind of twelve that had done this many times, and set the sealed packet on the table beside Theron's inkwell without ceremony. The wax impression on the seal was the magistrate's stamp, which meant it had come through their office rather than directly from the factors' board. Theron looked at the time of the gooseneck lamp — the oil reservoir had a calibrated scale on the side, and he had started it at second watch — and calculated that the flag had turned over in less than two days.

He thanked the boy, who left, and broke the seal.

*[Investigator — Insight +1. Faster than expected. Someone in the magistrate's office recognized the notation.]*

The packet held three sheets. The first was a cover from the clerk, formal and brief: the TL-9 flag had been cross-referenced at the magistrate's request through the harbor customs register, the bonded factor ledger for the current year, and the Mirren district trade correspondence file. The results were attached. The clerk had underlined the result in the second sheet himself, which was unusual enough that Theron looked at the underline first.

*TL-9 — private factor shorthand, registered informally to Berric Toll, west harbour, Mirren, as of the 14th of the third month of this year.*

He read it twice.

Then he set the second sheet beside the first and read the third. It was shorter: a supplemental note from the magistrate's own hand, not the clerk's. *The registration is informal — Toll submitted it to the bonded factor registry as a shorthand for his current network of sub-contracts, but it was never formally assigned. An informal code, used by Toll's subcontractors to self-identify work arranged through his network. The bonded registry accepted it as a notation of record, not a license. Advise whether the Fell matter requires a warrant inquiry.*

Theron put the three sheets together and aligned them by their top edge.

So Maret Fell had been doing work for Berric Toll's network. Had been, at minimum, self-identifying as a subcontractor of Toll's when she logged her spring jobs into the Gray Quay board — either because she was required to, or because she wanted the notation on record. Either way, the connection was no longer a guess about one letter in a code. It was confirmed, if not yet explained.

He wrote in his notebook: *TL-9 confirmed — Toll private shorthand, informally registered, third month of this year. Fell used it on two spring customs-assist jobs. Second job finished six days before departure from Mirren.* He added a box around that last sentence. Then he sat back and thought about the magistrate's question at the bottom of the third sheet.

A warrant inquiry meant naming Fell as a person of formal interest. That was a step he didn't have the standing to compel from here. The magistrate could, if Theron asked and the case held enough weight. But a warrant also meant Fell knew she was named, wherever she was, and the Fell thread was currently running on the assumption that she didn't.

He wrote: *Do not recommend warrant at this stage. Request: can the magistrate's office locate Fell in Halverstow — without formal contact? Determine whether she is still in the city.* He folded the note into the front of his notebook for later.

---

Lord Petric Vannic was still in port.

Theron knew this because the magistrate's hold-for-questions letter was still valid and the harbor master had confirmed that morning that no private passage had been booked under Vannic's name or any variant of Petor Vann. He knew, too, that Vannic had not used the first two days of his enforced stay to make himself useful, which was perhaps consistent with Vannic's overall character, and that the man was currently lodged at the Halverstow Arms on the harbor-facing side, which cost approximately three times what it should.

What Theron did not know was what Vannic had not told him on the gondola.

He found him at noon, in the Arms' street-facing parlour room, eating what might charitably be called a late breakfast. Vannic looked up when Theron came in, and the expression that crossed his face was a sequence — relief that it wasn't anyone official, wariness that it was Theron, and then a performance of ease that sat slightly wrong, like a collar adjusted in a mirror just before the mirror was taken away.

"Mavik," he said. "I wasn't expecting you."

"No," Theron agreed, and sat.

The parlour was mostly empty at this hour. A second table had a man reading a shipping gazette, boots off, feet resting on his own empty chair. The Arms' parlour was that kind of room — comfortable enough to stay in, public enough not to need explaining.

Vannic's plate held a soft-boiled egg, a small pot of something preserved, and a bread roll that he had been tearing apart rather than eating. His coffee was already cold; the steam had been gone from it for some time. He picked up the roll fragment nearest to him and put it down again.

"Theron," Vannic said, with the tone of someone about to say something he'd been practicing, "I want you to know that I understand I'm in a difficult position."

"You're in a straightforward position," Theron said. "You hired a man and he died on the arrangement you set up. The straightforward position is that anything useful you know, you tell me."

Vannic looked at the cold coffee. A muscle in his jaw moved. "I've told you everything about the arrangement."

"You've told me the arrangement's shape. Toll, the factor's office, two meetings, the correspondence packet." Theron set his notebook on the table, but didn't open it. "What you haven't told me is anything about Joren. About him specifically."

A pause. Theron counted to five in his head, which was the interval his practicum supervisor had once told him held more diagnostic weight than any question.

At four, Vannic said: "He was careful."

Theron waited.

"I don't mean careful like he was nervous. I mean careful like — a craftsman." Vannic picked up the coffee and looked at it and set it down. "When we met at the factor's office, he was already there when I arrived. He'd looked up the records. My manners, my typical port, the way Vannic —" he stopped, made a small, uncomfortable gesture that acknowledged the strangeness of the third person — "the way *I* dress, what I eat at public functions. Not just the face. He had a whole file."

"From whom?"

"I assumed Toll. He didn't say." Vannic broke off another piece of roll without eating it. "He asked me questions. What my posture was like, whether I had any habits that were specifically mine and not just class habits. I'd expected someone who would look at me and cast something. He actually took notes."

Theron wrote: *Joren — prepared notes on Vannic's specific mannerisms, distinct from class habits. Consulted records before the factor's office meeting.* "What kind of questions?"

"Small ones. Whether I gestured when I was bored. Whether I held my glass differently when I was tired. Whether there was a way I arranged my desk items when I was anxious." Vannic finally ate a piece of bread roll. "He said he'd spent two years doing a magistrate's council secretary. Said you had to hold the performance in the small things, because anyone could learn to walk like a lord but only a person knew how to sit like one."

"A magistrate's council secretary." Theron looked up from his notebook. "In which district?"

"He didn't specify. He mentioned it in passing — by way of explaining his method." Vannic rubbed the back of his neck. "Toll had said something, at the signing — that Roven would be aware of the passage. That it was reassurance, that the arrangement was being watched over from the Halverstow end."

Theron's pen stopped. If Roven was watching from the Halverstow end, that put him on the route — possibly aboard. "Was Roven a passenger on the Wyrm?"

Vannic shook his head. "No — Toll meant Roven knew of the run in advance and would be waiting at Halverstow when the gondola arrived. Not on board. At the end." He looked faintly puzzled by the question.

Theron let the inference go. A contact at the landing end, not a passenger. He made a note — *Roven: Toll said "aware of the passage"; waiting at Halverstow end, not a Wyrm passenger* — and moved on.

"He also said something else. At the dock, when we were doing the last check. He said —" Another pause, different in quality from the first. This one had something in it. "He said, *if anyone asks about Lord Vannic during the passage, tell them I'm difficult to find*." Vannic looked at the table. "I thought he meant me. If anyone in third class somehow connected me to him."

Theron was very still. "But he said it to you about himself."

"I think so. Now. I didn't think about it then." Vannic's voice had shifted — the performed ease was gone, and what was under it was not pleasant to look at, and not because Vannic was dramatic about it. It was the face of a man realizing that a joke he'd laughed at earlier had not been a joke. "He said it like — like it was a standard precaution. Like someone had already asked about him before, and it had not been academic."

*[Investigator — Insight +2. The warning was active intelligence, not professional habit. Someone was already in Toll's network when Joren took the contract.]*

Theron looked at the System line and thought about the six days. Fell's second customs-assist job ending six days before departure. The observation window question on the first morning of the flight. Twenty minutes at the shared window on the second evening.

He wrote: *Joren's warning re: Lord Vannic — suggests prior awareness of surveillance or contact. Possibly knew he was being tracked through the Toll network.* Then underneath, in a separate line: *Did Fell and Joren know each other? Or did Fell only know the contract?*

He did not write the next question out, because it was the one that would need Fell in a room to answer, and she was not currently in a room.

"Is there anything else?" he said. "About Joren directly. Any other conversation, any other thing you noticed about him."

Vannic considered. He looked, for a moment, like he was actually thinking rather than managing what he was saying, which Theron had not seen from him before and noted accordingly. "He was — kind," Vannic said at last. "Not to me specifically. I just noticed it. At the dock, when we were going over the last details, one of the handlers dropped a stack of crate-checks and they scattered everywhere. Joren helped him pick them up. In character — in the voice, doing the lord thing — but he helped."

Vannic picked up the cold coffee and drank some of it, then made a small, private expression about its temperature that he did not appear to notice he was making. "I thought it was a choice about maintaining the performance. But he did it before he'd put on the voice for anyone. It was just what he did."

Theron did not write that down. He already knew that about Joren. He let the pause run for a moment, then closed the notebook.

"Thank you," he said. He stood. "Don't leave Halverstow before the magistrate's office releases you."

"I know," Vannic said. He was looking at the cold coffee. He seemed, for the moment, no longer bothered by the instruction.

---

Sable was at the harbor agent's table in the harbourmaster's hall when Theron came back from the Arms, making notes from a route schedule the agent had spread out for her. She looked up, did the brief visual check that Theron had come to understand meant *anything urgent*, and returned to the schedule when he shook his head.

He waited.

She thanked the agent, rolled the schedule, handed it back, and came across to where he was standing by the hall window with the notebook open on his palm.

"Vannic gave me something," he said. "Joren warned him on the dock. If anyone asks about Lord Vannic during the passage, tell them I'm difficult to find."

Sable looked at the harbor through the window. A moment passed. "He knew someone was looking."

"He suspected. Or he'd been warned. Either way, the precaution was specific." He showed her the notebook. "The TL-9 result came this morning. Confirmed. Toll's network notation, informally registered this spring. Fell was self-identifying herself as a Toll subcontractor on those customs-assist jobs."

Sable read the relevant lines, turned the page, read the next entry. She did not read quickly; she read the way she looked at things, which was at a slight angle, as though the center of a page was less useful than its edges. When she was done she gave the notebook back. "So Fell was working for Toll, Joren was hired through Toll, and Joren knew someone was tracking him through Toll's network."

"It's a triangle," Theron said. "Three points. We have the shape of it."

"We don't have how they connect."

"We don't." He looked at the harbor, at the deep-water berths where the Wyrm was still moored. "The magistrate has the flag. I've asked them to locate Fell in the city — quietly — before we move further."

Sable looked at him. The look had a question in it that she didn't say, which was the version she used when the answer was already in the room.

He found it. "If she's still here, we talk to her. Directly, with what we know. The confirmed Toll connection gives me something to put on the table — she self-submitted that notation, she knew what it was. She can choose to tell us what TL-9 was for on those jobs, or she can say nothing and the magistrate files a warrant."

"And if she's left the city."

"Then the magistrate's warrant is the next step anyway." He put the notebook in his coat. "Either way, the Sannholt letter is in. If Pessel knows something about Joren's contacts in the last months, it comes back to us through the clearing house."

Sable nodded. She looked at the route schedule she'd been studying, still thinking about it, which meant she was still thinking about the thing next to it. "How long is Vannic in port?" she asked.

"Until the magistrate releases him."

"And he's at the Arms."

"Third floor, harbor-facing, which he presumably chose for the view."

The corner of her mouth moved. "Does he know Fell's name?"

Theron stopped. He had not asked that. It was a clean question and he had not asked it, which was the gap between his method and hers that had not closed over the course of the case. "I don't know," he said. "I'll go back."

"Don't," Sable said. "Write him a note. Ask one question. Don't sit with him again today — he's in the mode where he wants to confess things, and if you give him the room he'll confess things that haven't happened yet."

Theron looked at her.

"One note," she said. "Yes or no. Did Toll name any of the network to him when they arranged the contract."

He wrote it in the notebook and tore out the page. "You're better at this than I am," he said, which was something he had not said out loud before, in this register, without qualifying it.

Sable took the note and folded it. "You're better at knowing what the question is. I'm better at knowing when to ask it." She put the note in her pocket. "I'll have it sent to the Arms."

*[Investigator — Status: Fell/Toll thread confirmed. Joren's awareness noted. Next step: location of Fell. Thread is narrowing.]*

Theron read the System line. The case log had not been this ordered since the early morning runs on the gondola, when everything had been wrong in an organized way. Now the organization was in the right direction.

"The Sannholt thread," he said. "If the reply from Pessel comes back before the magistrate locates Fell."

"Then we have two threads arriving at once." Sable was already walking toward the side hall where the outgoing messages were handled. "Write the summary for the magistrate tonight. Clean copy, facts only. Give them Vannic's dock conversation as supplemental."

He was making a note of that when she stopped at the hall entrance and looked back. "Theron. Joren's name should be in the header."

He had already written *Case of Joren Aldis* at the top of the page. He showed her.

She turned and went through to the outgoing messages. He watched the doorway for a moment, then looked at the summary in his notebook, then at the harbor through the window glass where the deep-water berths held the familiar silhouette of the Wyrm against the late-afternoon quay.

The case had the right name in it. The thread had the right shape. Tomorrow or the day after, Fell would be found or would not be found, and either answer moved the work forward.

He pulled the notebook into his coat and went to write the clean copy.
