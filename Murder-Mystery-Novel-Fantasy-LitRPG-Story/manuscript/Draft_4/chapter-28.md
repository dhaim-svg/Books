# Chapter 28 — The Wrong Kind of Certainty

The magistrate's small office was at the back of the courthouse, on the side that took the morning light through one square window and gave it to a desk that had been the same desk for thirty years. Theron arrived at second bell because that was when the magistrate had said to arrive, and the clerk in the front room nodded him through without asking.

Magistrate Davren was already at the side table with two cups poured.

"Sit, Mavik."

Theron sat. He set the case book down between them, square to the edge of the table, and opened it to the column page. He had not made any changes since Sunday afternoon. He had read it through twice this morning to be sure.

"Walk me through it," Davren said.

Theron walked him through it. He kept the order he had practised: cabin nine-T, the Kolach placement, the Sannholt destination, Calden's uncertain lamp observation, the corridor-watching behaviour Sable had logged from the original Wyrm interview. He named the apprentice line from Pessel and was careful to say *confirmed via Sable* rather than *confirmed by Pessel directly,* because the distinction mattered for the magistrate's notes and would matter again if any of this went into a brief.

He did not raise his voice. He did not say *the suspect.* He said *the column,* and *the working line,* and once, *the person we are looking at.*

Davren listened. He did not write anything down. He was a man who had learned to listen first and write afterward, and the listening had a quality to it — not patient, exactly. Available.

When Theron had finished, Davren drank coffee.

Then he asked the first question.

"Did she enter cabin twelve on the third night?"

"No," Theron said. "Calden's corridor sheet has her in cabin nine-T from second watch to first bell. Two of the steward's pass-by notes have her cabin lit at first watch and dark at third. Nothing in the corridor record has her moving."

"Did anyone else enter cabin twelve on the third night?"

"No. The door was bolted from inside at the time the body was discovered. The bolt was the original Wyrm fitting, not tampered. The cabin's only other access is the porthole, which was latched, and the porthole would not pass an adult."

Davren nodded once. He set the cup down.

"Then I have a column I can keep in front of me, Mavik, and I do not yet have a case I can bring forward."

"I know," Theron said.

"The Kolach placement is good work. The Sannholt apprentice line is good work. The corridor behaviour is observation, not evidence. The lamp is steward's uncertainty. If you put this in front of a coastal magistrate at her destination, and she asked you on what grounds she should hold the woman for examination, you would have to tell her: on grounds that the woman's booking was placed through a channel the dead man also used, that she was in his old professional circle, and that she watched a corridor with attention. Those are not arrest grounds. They are interview grounds at best, and only if you can produce the channel-source link in court, which means the Mirren transcript, which is not yet in your possession."

"It's due Wednesday or Thursday."

"Good. Bring it to me when it lands." He paused. "What I do not see here, Mavik, is the method. You have a working suspect. You have a closed cabin and a man dead inside it without a wound. What I do not have on this page is any account of how the woman in cabin nine-T caused a death in cabin twelve without entering it."

Theron had been waiting for that one. He had hoped not to need to hear it said aloud, and he had known he would.

"I don't have the method," he said.

"No," Davren said. "You don't."

He let the silence be exactly long enough.

"You have done a good piece of work on the who," he said, after. "Take it the rest of the way. Find me how. When you find me how, we go to the coastal magistrate together."

"Yes."

"Wednesday or Thursday for the transcript. I'll keep the column open at this end." He closed the case book and slid it back. "Drink your coffee, Mavik. The morning's still young."

Theron drank his coffee. It was good coffee, the courthouse blend, brewed thicker than the Arms ran it. He left twenty minutes later with the case book under his arm and Davren's two questions clean in his head, which was the way Davren had wanted them to be.

---

The partial transcript came up on the late-morning post-coach.

He had not been expecting it before Wednesday. The boy from the station found him at the table with the cup half empty and handed over a single folded sheet sealed in the Mirren magistrate's office wax, which was the small green wax used for partial issues, not the heavy black used for full transcripts.

The note inside the wax was the magistrate's, not the clerk's. *Mavik — the personal-referral source from Kolach's testimony you asked us to expedite. Full transcript follows by post as agreed. — Velden, Mirren.*

The sheet itself was two sentences in the Mirren clerk's hand.

*Kolach, sworn statement, paragraph nine: the seventeenth placement of the current year, Renn, Halsa, was personally referred to my service by Joren Aldis at the time of her first placement, eleven years prior. The current-year credential is the renewal of the original placement made under his vouching.*

Theron read it.

He read it again.

He set the sheet down flat against the table and put his cup beside it.

The dining-room sounds did their ordinary thing for a moment. A factor at the next table laughed at something his clerk had said. Someone on the stairs was carrying a tray. The smell from the kitchen was the same caraway loaf Sunday's had been, or the host had baked another, or it was a different smell altogether and he was not noticing properly.

He picked the pencil up.

He did not write the thing he wanted to write, which was *Joren placed her in the trade himself.* That was the sentence the page wanted from him, and the page was wrong. The placement was true. The sentence carried freight he could not yet warrant. He knew that eleven years ago a young woman had needed a credential placement to work in a contract-side trade with no formal channel for new entrants, and that Joren had vouched for her with a man who ran a discreet placement service, and that the original vouching had been renewed in the current year for the Wyrm departure on which Joren had died.

That was what he wrote, in the working column, in the order he had practised: *Personal-referral source confirmed. Kolach paragraph nine, Mirren transcript partial issue. Source: Joren Aldis, eleven years prior. Current-year placement is a renewal of the original.*

He stopped.

He read the line.

He did not underline anything.

He sat back. The cup was lukewarm now. He drank it anyway, because the alternative was to ring for a fresh pot and have to say *thank you* to the host, and his face was doing whatever it was doing about Joren having put her into the trade, and he did not particularly want the host to see his face for the next few minutes.

He turned to the back of the case book, to the page where he kept the things he had been told that he had not yet earned the right to use, the same page where Sunday's note about the money for the child sat in its careful single-line summary. He wrote, under it: *Joren personally referred Halsa Renn into the Kolach placement channel eleven years prior. Current-year credential is the renewal of that original vouching. Awareness on Joren's part of any grievance against him at time of original placement: unknown; current working assumption is that no such grievance existed yet, or had not yet reached him.*

That sentence took him longer to write than he wanted to admit.

He read it. He read it again. He let it sit.

Then he turned back to the column and looked at the four questions in their order, and at the new line at the bottom of the page.

*Method — unknown.*

He drank the cold coffee.

---

The afternoon brought the second post.

There was no letter from Sable. The boy carried up a thin courier note from the Whitepost Inn, no seal, just the inn's stamp, and the note was four lines.

*Theron — leaving Sannholt on the evening coach. One more thing to look at this afternoon; will write properly tonight from the road, the letter to follow the morning coach. In Halverstow by Wednesday's last bell at the latest, perhaps Tuesday's. — S.*

He read it twice and put it in the case book.

He did not know what the *one more thing* was. She had not said, because she had not had time to write, and because she had a habit, by now, of not writing what she had not yet looked at. He would have done the same. He approved of it without making a particular point of approving of it; the approval was the kind of small habitual recognition he had stopped registering as a thing he did about her some weeks ago.

He drew the working profile out, and the partial transcript, and the column page, and laid them in front of him in their order.

He had the *who.*

He read the column down. Halsa Renn, cabin nine-T, Kolach placement personally referred by Joren eleven years prior, Sannholt destination, apprentice line confirmed via Pessel, corridor-observation behaviour, late lamp uncertain, Q4 (what changed two years ago) open. The column was not yet a brief. It was, however, no longer four lines of inference. It was a person.

He could see her in it. Not as the killer. As the person who had moved through three weeks of his case file as background and was now in front of him, in the column, with eleven years of context attached to the name. He could see what she had been doing on the gondola: travelling under a credential she had every administrative right to carry, on a route her destination explained, with cash because that was how her trade booked itself, and watching a corridor because that was the kind of attention her working life required of her. None of it was guilt. All of it was *being on the gondola for a reason that was not Sannholt.*

She had been three doors down the corridor from cabin twelve on the third night. The cabin had been bolted from inside. The man inside had died without a wound.

He did not know how.

He turned to a fresh page in the case book and dated it. He wrote *Method* across the top and underlined it once. Then he sat with the pencil over the page for some time.

He had not been ready to think about method since the assumption had collapsed when Joren's real name came clear — three weeks of working one interpretation, and then the dock, and then none of it. The assumption he had been carrying through those weeks had brought its own probable methods with it: poison, hired hand, a quarrel that escalated. When it had broken, the methods had broken with it, and he had been working the *who* ever since. He had not been wrong to do that. Davren had said the morning's work was good work. It was good work.

It was also, now, finished. The *who* was the column. The column was in front of him. The *how* was a blank page.

He wrote, slowly: *A passenger in cabin nine-T did not enter cabin twelve on the third night. The door was bolted from inside. There was no visible wound. The body was Joren Aldis. The working suspect is an illusionist trained by Joren Aldis, who knew his working methods better than anyone else on the gondola.*

He stopped.

He read the last line.

The last line was new. He had written it without planning to. It had come out at the end of the paragraph because it had been sitting at the back of the morning's thinking since Pessel's apprentice line had landed in Sunday's letter, and at the front of the afternoon's thinking since the Mirren partial had landed in the morning's post. *An illusionist trained by Joren Aldis.* Halsa Renn had been Joren's apprentice. Apprenticeship in a working magical trade was specific. An apprentice learned, over years, the working repertoire of the master. The master's habits. The master's specialities. The kinds of work the master refused, and the kinds of work the master took, and the small private exploits the master had figured out across a career that had not generated any formal scholarship to record them.

Whatever method had been used in cabin twelve was a method that could be used by a passenger in cabin nine-T at thirty feet of corridor distance, against a sealed door, without entering, against a target who was an illusionist himself and would have recognised any standard approach.

He stopped writing because he had reached the edge of what he could put on the page tonight without going past what he had yet earned. He had the shape of the *how* in the negative. He did not have the *how* in the positive.

He set the pencil down.

*[Investigator — Working column updated: Renn, Halsa. Personal-referral source confirmed (Joren Aldis, eleven years prior). Method: unknown.]*

He read the System line.

He did not particularly want the System to be helpful tonight. He wanted to be the one who got there. He read the line through twice anyway, because the System was not in the business of doing his work for him; it was in the business of agreeing, in its laconic way, that the work was now where it was. The line was an acknowledgement, not a hint. He took it as the acknowledgement it was.

He turned the case book back to the column page and looked at the four questions in their order, and then at *Method — unknown* under the column, and then at the new sheet behind it with the negative shape of the *how* on it.

He had been certain about the *who* since Davren's first question this morning. He had not articulated the certainty in those words because the column did not permit them yet, and would not until the method closed. The certainty was unprofessional in the technical sense (the column was not a case) and it was also true. He had not had this kind of certainty about any name on the interpretation he had been carrying since the dock, and looking back, he never would have. He had been working a frame that produced confidence, not certainty. The thing that had come into the case in the last seventy-two hours was different.

The trouble with certainty about the *who* was that it did not, by itself, produce the *how.* And without the *how,* the certainty was a private possession of his, and Davren's, and not a thing the world could be told.

He closed the case book on the method page.

He drank the rest of the coffee, which had been cold for an hour.

Outside, the Halverstow harbour was settling into its evening half-speed. The vessel in the near berth had finished its unloading. The rope-master was gone. The smell from the kitchen had moved on from caraway and was now something heartier, a stew or a pie, the kind of smell the Arms put out on a Monday because the Monday trade liked to eat at the bar and walk home before dark.

He would have the host bring him whatever it was. He would eat at his table. He would go to bed early, because the *how* was not going to come tonight, and tomorrow was a clean morning, and the wire transcript was due Wednesday, and Sable would be in Halverstow by Wednesday last bell at the latest.

The method was not coming tonight.

He was, however, certain. He let the certainty be private. He kept the case book closed.

The host came past to ask whether he wanted the pie or the stew. Theron asked which the kitchen was prouder of. The host said the stew, but the pie was the better bet on a Monday, because the stew had been simmering since lunch and the pie was an hour out of the oven. Theron asked for the pie.

It came on a thick plate with a single bay leaf laid on the crust for decoration, the way the Arms' kitchen had been doing it for as long as the Arms' kitchen had been baking pies, and the crust was very good indeed.

He ate it slowly.

The case book sat by his elbow, closed, with the column page underneath the method page underneath the back-of-book page where the careful sentence about the original placement sat in its single line. The whole of it was a quiet weight on the table. He was content to let it be a weight he did not open again this evening.

The *who* was settled.

The *how* was the next morning's problem, and the morning after that, and however many mornings after that it took.

He finished the pie. He paid for it. He went up the stair.
