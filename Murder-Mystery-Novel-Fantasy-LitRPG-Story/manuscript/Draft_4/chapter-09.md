# Chapter 9 — A Letter That Did Not Want Sending

He came up the aft stair with the slate in his coat pocket and the small clean feeling of a conversation that had gone where he'd asked it to go and a little further. The captain's note was still warm against his ribs. He hadn't needed to produce it. Calden had answered the second question first, in the order Sable had given him, and then the first one when the second one made it natural; and Theron had walked back out of the pantry with the start-minute of the twelve and the end-minute of the twelve and a small dry pleasure he was choosing not to examine.

He found her at the alcove on deck four, as agreed.

She was leaning her shoulder on the wall at the edge of the recess, not standing inside it, which would have read as the steward's place, but at its lip, where a passenger could plausibly be considering the view of the corridor without committing to it. The pencil was behind her ear. The slate was not visible. She turned her face when his step landed on the boards three doors out.

"Well?" she said.

"Forty-six on the fourth bell to fifty-eight on the same." He kept his voice down. The corridor was the late-afternoon quiet he had walked through to get to it. "Twelve minutes. Cabin three. The matter he won't discuss without the captain's leave is cabin three, which we knew."

"And the half-second past twelve?"

"Was on the third round. Not the fourth. He didn't get a fourth at twelve because he wasn't there." He stopped at the alcove's edge, where she was. "And he asked himself, on the way back up from cabin three, whether he should have. He has been asking himself that for two days."

"Yes."

"You knew."

"I thought so. He told me without telling me. You confirmed it." She came off the wall. "Where do you want to take it next?"

"Out of the corridor."

"Reading alcove?"

"Reading alcove."

They went down the corridor together at the pace of two people who had nowhere in particular to be and would not have agreed on the pace if they'd discussed it. He noticed, in the part of his attention that had recently learned to do this, that she walked half a step ahead of him on the inside of the corridor, closer to the cabin wall, which left him the broader pass on the porthole side. He had not been given the broader pass by a person before in his memory and could not work out, for several doors, whether he liked it or whether he was being managed. Then he decided it was probably both and stopped thinking about it.

The reading alcove at the stern was empty. The last of the day was coming through the long stern window in the flat, slightly bronzed colour the gondola's port-side glass took on an hour before the lamps were lit. He took the bench seat. She took the chair across from him, at the angle he had begun to recognize as her working angle, half toward the window, half toward him, the slate's eventual reading surface tipped slightly toward her own pencil hand.

He set the slate on the small reading table.

"Calden," he said. "First."

"Yes."

He walked it through. The captain's note had unlocked the class-of-errand framing first, the duration second; Calden had given him both ends of the twelve without ever naming cabin three out loud, and had let Theron name it, and had not corrected the naming. The amendment had been *the matter* and not *the cabin three matter*; the steward's discretion had held the line he had said it would hold. Theron had asked about the half-second slowing past cabin twelve without using the words *half-second* and Calden had said, "I went past at the usual pace, sir, on the third round. I did not go past on the fourth." Which was the same answer in a different shape, and Theron had taken it and not pressed.

Sable nodded once at the end of it.

"You opened with the general."

"I opened with the general."

"And the time."

"And the time. He gave me both numbers. He had them both. He had been carrying them around in his head and was, I think, relieved to put them down somewhere."

"Mm." She moved the pencil from behind her ear to her hand and back. "He's been carrying a lot."

"He has."

He drew the corridor on the slate with the start-minute and the end-minute marked in. He drew Esherel's third-round window across the line — the four minutes Calden had spent in her cabin declining a sleeping draught — and Vellaine's lamp record adjusted by Sable's three minutes from the day before, and then the new gap that the start-minute had opened on the early end, a free three minutes he had not known he had. He looked at it.

He had two windows now. Not one.

"That's the geometry," he said.

"Yes."

He looked at it a moment longer. The early window was the one that mattered. The late window — the one that closed on Calden's return to the first-class corridor at fifty-eight past the fourth bell — was the one he had been working with for two days, and it pinned Esherel against her own four-minute alibi cleanly enough that he had been preparing to write *opportunity confirmed* under her name and underline it. The early window was a different shape. The early window was three minutes before Calden had even left the corridor, on a watch in which Esherel had been, by her own account, asleep, and by Calden's account had not yet been seen at all.

"Two windows," he said. "I had one."

"You had the one the captain's leave gave you the easy way. The other one was always there."

"Yes."

He sat with that for the small honest beat it required. He had had it in him to ask the early question; he had not had it in him to ask the early question until she had moved one tick three minutes earlier the day before and given him the pencil back. The pencil was now in his hand and the tick was where she had put it, and he found that he had no particular wish to move it.

The galley boy passed the alcove arch with a tray of cups and a folded cloth, on his round to the cabins; Theron put his hand flat over the slate as a courtesy, although the boy did not look in. The Wyrm's hum came through the bench under him as a low constant. Mottled was settled, again, on the long approach.

"All right," he said. "Esherel."

Sable looked at him.

"I'd like to talk about Esherel," he said.

"All right."

He had the shape of his argument already in the back of his teeth — the motive on the page, the opportunity, the demeanor — and was opening his mouth when Sable said:

"I don't think she sent the letter."

He paused.

"She didn't, in fact. It's still in her folio."

"I know it's still in her folio. I mean I don't think she was going to. I don't think she ever was."

He set the pencil down on the slate the way he set things down when he wanted to make sure they were not in his hand for the next sentence.

"All right," he said. "Walk me."

She did not look at the slate. She looked at the bronze edge of the stern window where the long light was lying along it, which he had begun to understand was the place she put her eyes when she was assembling.

"You read the letter, you said."

"I read it."

"How many rewrites in the third paragraph."

"Four."

"And the fourth paragraph?"

"Half a page. Stopped mid-sentence."

"How long has she had the folio open in the dining room."

"Six days, by her account. Since boarding."

"Six days of writing the third paragraph four times and the fourth paragraph not at all." She turned her eyes back from the window. "That is a person who is not going to send the letter."

"It's a person who hasn't finished the letter."

"It's a person who is composing the not-finishing. There is a line in the letter — you told me the line."

"*I would like neither at this point and have written this for my own use, which is also the use of the trade.*"

"That line."

"Yes."

"That is a letter someone is writing to herself, Theron. The trade is the alibi for keeping the page open. She is sitting in her cabin and her dining room and she is rehearsing the conversation she will never have, and she is making the conversation good. She has spent six days making it good. She has not slid it under his door. She was never going to. The letter is the place she is keeping it. The man was the excuse for the letter."

He thought about this.

He thought about it the careful way he was learning to think about the things she said — which was to let the sentence land first and look for what it touched, rather than to look at the sentence and decide whether he agreed with it. The sentence touched Esherel at the breakfast table on the first morning, folio open before the dishes were cleared. It touched her at the corridor outside Vannic's cabin, where she had not, in fact, ever stood. It touched the four rewrites of the third paragraph. It touched the half-page stopped at the fourth.

It also touched, in a part of his attention he was less easily able to name, the small look she had given him in her cabin — the merchant looking at a ledger that had been correctly kept but on the wrong pages.

He picked up the pencil again.

"All right," he said. "I hear you."

"I know you hear me."

"I want to take it apart on the page, though. Not against you. With you."

"Take it apart."

He turned the slate so she could see it from where she was sitting and he wrote out, in the columns he had been writing in for two days, the three pieces of the case he had on Esherel.

"Motive," he said. "Forty-one and a half thousand crowns and two years of unanswered letters. The letters are a documented pattern. The folio is the evidence."

"Yes."

"Opportunity. She booked the gondola specifically because Vannic had booked it. She placed herself within four hours of him for the duration of the flight. She has, by her own account, no alibi for the early window on the fourth watch, which the captain's leave has just given me. She has an alibi for one point in the night — the four minutes Calden spent in her cabin at the third bell — and no alibi for the rest of it."

"Yes."

"Demeanor under questioning. Candid. Dry. Unflattering to herself when she could have flattered. My Conservatory tutor's instruction was to treat that as honesty until contradicted."

"Yes."

"And the letter," he said. "Which I read in her folio. Which I hold to be motive made visible — the long, careful, accumulated motive of a person who has spent two years being refused a thing she is owed."

"And which I hold," she said, evenly, "to be a person who has spent two years building the room she lives in alone."

He stopped.

He did not put the pencil down. He let it rest on the slate at the angle it was at.

"That's a different reading of the same letter," he said.

"It is."

"It's not on the page."

"It is on the page. It's the page you read. You filed it under *motive*. I'd file it under *what she does instead of doing the thing*."

He looked at the column on the slate where he had written, two days ago, the word *candid* and underlined it. He had drawn the underline twice. He could see the pencil bear-down through the chalk. He had been pleased with the underline at the time and was, on examination, still pleased with it.

"I think," he said, "I'd rather hold her in column one until something moves her out of it."

"All right."

"You've heard me, and I've heard you, and the case as I have it now still puts her in column one."

"All right."

"It's not that I'm dismissing what you've said." He set the pencil down properly this time, parallel to the slate's edge. "It's that the page is what I work from. The page has motive, opportunity, candor, and a letter that documents the motive at length. The shape of the letter is a thing I would have to weigh against three other things on the page that are pointing the other way. I'd be working against my own method to weigh it heavier than them."

"That's fair," she said.

"Is it."

"I think so. It's your method. I asked you to hear it. You heard it."

"You're being generous."

"I'm not."

He looked at her. She looked back at him. Her face was the face it had been three minutes ago: the working angle, the small patient set at the mouth she did when she was watching him do something he was about to do anyway and had decided to let him do it.

"You think I'm wrong," he said.

"I think you're holding her where you have her because you have her there cleanly. I'd want to know what would move her, if anything would."

"A second alibi for the early window."

"Or."

"Or material that contradicted the demeanor. Or a motive larger than hers somewhere else in the manifest."

"Or."

"Or," he said, slowly, "a reading of the letter that the rest of the page sat down to agree with."

"Yes."

"Which would be a reading the page didn't currently have."

"Yes."

"I'd want to see it on the page first."

"I know."

He sat with that. The slate sat between them. The columns on it sat the way columns sat — Esherel at the top, Tannin second, Vellaine third, the early window now drawn in beside the late one, the geometry he had walked into the alcove with intact. He had thought, on the way down from the captain's note, that the chapter of the day was Calden. The chapter had widened by one room. The page had not.

"Right," he said. "On the page."

She picked up her cup, which had been empty since before he had sat down, and turned it a quarter so the handle pointed away from him. It was the same quarter-turn she had done in the bay window the day before. He had filed it then under *consideration or habit*. He had not, since, decided which it was, and he found, in the part of his attention that was not occupied with the slate, that he was still not deciding.

The light at the stern window had thinned by another notch. The galley boy passed back the other way with the tray emptier and the cloth folded smaller. Somewhere two decks below, the cook was working on whatever the saloon was going to be told, with the small assurance of certainty, was the second-watch dinner.

He read it once. He did not flinch at it and did not enjoy it, which felt, on inspection, like effort. He set the slate face-down on the reading table.

"I'll keep working the page," he said.

"Do."

"And I'll think about the letter."

"You will think about it as a thing you've considered and set aside, which is a form of thinking about it. That's all right."

"It's all right."

"It is."

"Are you teasing me."

"A little." She set the cup down. "Not unkindly. Go and write up Calden. The two windows. The cabin-three confirmation by inference. Sleep on it. You'll bring better questions in the morning than you'd bring tonight."

"And you."

"I'm going to go and have a small conversation with the second-class cook, because I have an excuse to and because he has been letting Calden eat standing up for two days in a row and someone should mention it."

"Is that an investigation, that conversation."

"It's a kindness with my eyes open." She got up. "There's a difference. Most days."

"Noted."

She smiled at *noted*, briefly, the way she always did, and then she did the thing she had done at the alcove an hour ago, which was to lean for a half-second on the back of the chair before she walked away from it — a small still moment that was not a posture, only the pause a person made when they had decided to leave and were giving the room one count before they did. Then she went.

He sat with the slate face-down a moment longer.

He turned it over. He read the columns. Esherel at the top, with the underline twice on *candid*; Tannin second, with *paid on the dock* in the margin; Vellaine third, with the three-minute lamp adjustment in Sable's hand. The new window drawn beside the old one. The motive, the opportunity, the demeanor, the letter.

He did not move the underline.

He thought about the half-page stopped mid-sentence and decided that he had thought about it. He capped the pencil. He picked up the slate.

The corridor on the way back up to his cabin was lamp-lit now, the lamps coming on in their order as the last of the day pulled away from the port windows. He passed Calden's pantry on the way and did not look in. He passed cabin twelve and made the small registration he had been making for two days of the wax seal and the folded note in his own hand. He went up the stair. He let himself into his own cabin and put the slate on the writing desk.

He moved his thumb off the graduation pin. He had not noticed his thumb arriving at the pin.

He sat down to write up Calden.
