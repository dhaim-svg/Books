# Chapter 15 — The Third-Class Corridor

Calden was in the steward's alcove reshuffling a row of passenger files when Theron came down the aft stairs with the manifest, and Sable came down two steps behind with the second sheet and a pencil she hadn't stopped carrying since the alcove.

The steward looked up. His expression said he had been waiting for this conversation, had organized the relevant facts into something presentable, and would like credit for that without being asked for it directly.

"The four I haven't heard from," Theron said, setting the manifest on the table fold-down.

Calden straightened the passenger files by a quarter-inch. "Three of the four made contact at some point. That's what I've been sorting."

"Tell me."

Calden's briefing was methodical and careful, in the way of a man who had worked out in advance exactly how much he was permitted to say. Passenger one, in cabin two-T, was a woman returning to Halverstow from a trade visit. She had asked about baggage claim at disembarkation, mentioned her employer by name, a textile house in the upper market district that Calden could confirm existed. Passenger two, cabin five-T, had requested a sleeping draught on the second night, which was in Calden's records, and had told Calden at that point that he was traveling for a family funeral, gave the name of the deceased, which Calden had not verified but had no reason to doubt. Both, Calden felt, were soft alibis by profession and timing. Neither had left their cabins after the third watch.

"Soft but credible," Theron said.

"They're good witnesses to themselves," Sable said, from beside the folded-napkin shelf. "That's not the same thing, but it's not nothing."

Theron had noted it down before she finished the sentence.

"The third," Calden said, "is the Fell woman. Cabin seven-T. She spoke to me on the first morning, asked about the observation window in the shared passage. Whether it was accessible in the evening." He paused. "Which is a question I can't fairly mark up or down, since half the third-class passengers ask it."

"But you noted it," Sable said.

"I noted it because she was the only one on this run who asked specifically about the evening."

Theron looked at the manifest. Maret Fell, cabin seven-T, Mirren east harbour, no stated profession. He circled the name again, not because it needed circling, but because the motion helped him think. "What's the fourth blank?"

"Cabin nine-T." Calden's tone shifted slightly, or didn't shift, which was itself a shift. "Halsa Renn. She hasn't spoken to me or any member of staff beyond boarding acknowledgment. No requests, no complaints, no meals taken outside cabin on record, though she was present at the shared observation window on the second evening."

"Alone?"

"As far as I saw. Briefly." He paused again. "She's quiet. Not conspicuously quiet. I mention it only because you asked."

"Thank you," Theron said. He wrote *9-T, Renn, no contact* on the margin and left it there.

*[Investigator — four passengers inventoried. Two credible soft alibis. One positive ask about the window at evening. One absence of contact.]*

Sable glanced at the note beside the manifest. She did not add anything to it.

"We'll start at the far end and work back," Theron said. "Calden, thank you. If any of the four come to the steward's station before we've reached them, please don't mention we're working the corridor."

Calden looked very slightly pained, as if the suggestion that he might do this otherwise was itself an imposition. "Of course not, sir."

---

Third class ran narrower than the second-class passage above it. The carpet had been replaced somewhere in the gondola's service history by a scrubbed wood floor that flexed slightly on the dragon-harness tremors, and the wall-lamps were single-wick rather than double, which meant that late afternoon in the corridor had the quality of a room where someone had just left after occupying it: warm but not bright, full of small sounds.

Theron knocked on cabin two-T. The textile buyer was forthcoming and uneventful: a compact woman with a trade schedule in her coat pocket and a firm handshake — quick, dry, practiced — that said she had been introduced to investigators before and knew what they needed and would provide it efficiently. She had her receipts in order. She had heard nothing unusual on the third night. She had been awake at first watch reading contracts and asleep before second. She had not, to her knowledge, interacted with any other third-class passenger beyond nodding in the shared passage.

"The Fell woman," Theron said, toward the end. "Do you know her by sight?"

She thought about it. "Brown coat. Keeps to herself." She frowned slightly. "She doesn't look like she's on holiday."

"What does she look like?"

"Someone doing a job." She shrugged, without apology. "I don't mean that badly. It's just how she sits in the corridor. Occupied."

Occupied. Theron wrote it down.

Cabin five-T: a middle-aged man with the permanently fatigued look of someone in the middle of a long grief. He confirmed the funeral, gave the name of his brother, named the village outside Halverstow without prompting. He had taken his sleeping draught at second watch and had no memory of anything after. He showed Theron the empty vial still tucked in the pocket of his coat because he had meant to return it to Calden and kept forgetting. The small kindness of showing it unprompted, the wanting to do the right thing even in the right-of-mind administration of it, left no room for anything else.

"I'm sorry about your brother," Sable said, from the doorway.

The man looked at her. His expression was the kind that doesn't have a quick answer and doesn't try to find one. "Thank you," he said.

They stepped out.

---

The shared observation window in the third-class passage was a broad brass-framed port set just above waist height on the starboard side, angled slightly forward. It was the longest window in this section of the gondola, long enough that three people could stand at it comfortably or two could stand at it closely, and through it the sky in the late afternoon was a deep and layered blue, quiet above the cloud-line, offering nothing.

Theron stood at it for two seconds while the passage was empty.

Halveth. Still two hours out, or close enough.

He knocked on cabin seven-T.

Maret Fell opened the door with the particular manner of someone who had been expecting a knock and had decided in advance how to answer it. She was in her early forties, angular, with the kind of composed attention that would have read as polite in a shorter conversation and read as something more precise in a longer one.

"The investigation," she said. Not a question.

"Theron Mavik. I'm working the flight manifest. This is my working partner, Sable Verrin. May we have a few minutes?"

"Of course." She stepped back. The cabin was orderly: bag closed, no loose papers, the traveling coat on the hook rather than on the bed. A professional habit, or the habit of someone who expected to be looked at.

Sable took the narrow chair by the wall without being asked. Theron stood at the end of the bunk.

"Your address in the manifest is Mirren east harbour," he said. "Trade work?"

"Shipping assessment." She offered it without hesitation, the way you offer a word you've said many times. "I consult for a house in the inland districts. I've been in Mirren three months on a contract."

"What brings you to Halverstow?"

"The end of the contract. I'm taking a week before the next one." A slight pause. "I usually take a week."

"Do you know the Mirren docks well? The full harbour?"

She looked at him steadily. "Well enough for the assessment work. I know the main commercial stretch and the factor district." She said it evenly. "The west harbour less so."

"Have you had reason to deal with a factor named Berric Toll?"

Her expression stayed where it was. Not a flicker, not a breath held. The expression of someone genuinely thinking, not performing thinking. "I know the name. He's in the upper-factor tier. I have not dealt with him personally."

Theron watched her. She was watching him back, without challenge, with the specific attentiveness of someone who understood that an interview had a structure and was content to meet it at each step.

"Were you at the shared observation window on the second evening?" he said.

"Yes." She didn't ask how he knew. "I wanted air and the deck hatch was closed. I was there for perhaps twenty minutes."

"Did you see anyone in the corridor during that time?"

"One other passenger, briefly." She looked at the port window in her own cabin, a smaller one, and then back at him. "Quiet woman from the far end. She looked at the view and left before I did."

Theron noted it. "Was there any conversation aboard between you and any other passenger? Work, travel, the Mirren docks?"

"No." Flat, not unfriendly. "I tend not to socialize on transit."

He asked two more questions about her movements on the third night. She had been in her cabin after supper, had heard a gondola creak she couldn't place sometime past first watch, had not opened her door. Her answers were clean and specific and added up to a picture of someone with nothing to hide and no reason to offer more than was asked. She gave him the name of the inland house she worked for, the name of her contact there, unprompted.

It was also the profile of someone with the right background and the patience to know that the cleanest accounting tends to pass earliest.

He wrote it down without drawing a line.

"Thank you," he said. "If you think of anything from the third night that seemed unusual, please speak to Calden. Any sound, any light under a door, anything that didn't fit."

"I will." She held the door for them with the same composed attention she'd opened it with.

In the corridor, Sable fell into step beside him. She said nothing for a beat, which he had learned by this point was not absence of opinion.

"Well?" he said.

"Clean," she said. "Correct clean or practiced clean, I can't tell you."

"That's the question."

"Yes."

---

Cabin nine-T was at the end of the corridor. Theron knocked twice, brief and even.

The woman who opened the door was younger than he'd expected from the travel register. Late twenties, perhaps thirty. Dark-haired, plainly dressed in a way that was clean rather than worn, with the kind of direct stillness in her expression that read as either patience or deliberate calm.

"Halsa Renn?" Theron said.

"Yes."

"Theron Mavik. I'm working the manifest, speaking to all third-class passengers as part of the investigation. This won't take long."

She nodded. The door opened a little wider, but she didn't step back. Theron noted the habit: answering in the doorway, not retreating into the space.

"Where are you traveling to in Halverstow?"

"Through to Sannholt. I have family there." Her voice was level, unhurried.

"You'll take the coast road?"

"The morning post-coach. Yes."

He asked about her work. She said she did small-contract labour, seasonal, had spent the last three months on the inland plains, was taking the flight as the faster route home before the roads turned in autumn. She had paid cash, which she acknowledged plainly, because she always paid cash on transit. She had no professional reference he could check, which she also acknowledged plainly, as a fact rather than an apology.

"Third night," Theron said. "After supper. Where were you?"

"In my cabin." She met his eyes without extra effort. "I sleep early on transit. The hum helps."

"The dragon hum?"

"Yes." The faintest shape of something in her expression that he filed as recognition of an unremarkable thing, the way you look when someone names a preference you've had for years. "I was asleep by second watch."

"Did you hear anything unusual after the second?"

She considered it, genuinely. "A creak from toward the second-class passage. Could have been the frame. It's not unusual."

"Was that before or after second watch?"

"After. Not much."

He asked whether she'd spoken to any other passengers during the flight. She said no, except for an exchange at the observation window on the second evening. A woman in her forties, brown coat, brief.

Theron wrote it down. Two accounts of the same window, the same evening, neither inconsistent with the other.

"Is there anything you noticed during the flight that seemed out of place to you? Anything that didn't fit?" He asked it the same way he had asked Maret Fell, as a closing question rather than a pointed one.

She thought for a moment. "The steward was quieter on the third morning than the first two." She said it with mild curiosity, not suspicion. "He has a particular walk. Professional but easy. It changed."

"You noticed that."

"I notice how people move when something's changed for them." She said it the same way she said everything: simple, without theater. "I didn't know what it was."

Theron looked at the notation in his margin. Traveling through to Sannholt. Family. Cash. Early sleeper. Observant.

"Thank you," he said. "We're nearly at Halverstow. Safe travels to the coast."

"Thank you," she said. She had already started to close the door before he turned away, and she closed it quietly, without hurry.

Sable waited until they were past the observation window before she said anything.

"She notices how people move," she said. Neutral tone. Not an accusation.

"She does," Theron said. He was looking at the four names on his sheet. Fourth-tier. Traveling through, south coast, family, cash, no apparent connection to Mirren west harbour or Toll or any of the professional paths that linked to Joren's work. Nothing to push on. He folded the sheet.

The corridor creak of the gondola shifted under them, a long slow accommodation: the frame was working harder, the angle of the gondola's nose beginning to change.

Theron looked up at the port window at the far end of the passage.

---

They came out of the third-class corridor into the aft stairwell landing, and the landing's narrow window faced south-west, and through it the light had a different quality than it had had an hour ago. Amber-edged. Lower. The sky had shifted from afternoon blue to the first thickening of early evening, and where the cloud-line broke away toward the horizon, the land was there.

Halveth coast. He could see the dark run of the headland, and below it, the long pale strip of the shore, and below that — just at the edge, at the line where sky and sea stopped competing — the clustered lights of a harbor, beginning to show.

Halverstow Port.

He stood at the window for a moment. Behind him Sable stopped on the step.

"Fell," she said. "How do you want to play it at dock?"

"Carefully." He watched the harbor lights. "She gave clean answers and the right name and a professional background that holds at arm's length. I want to push the Toll connection with someone who has access to the west harbour registers before I move any further." He paused. "The captain can authorize a shore contact in advance of landing."

"I'll ask Calden to ask him," Sable said. "He'll move it faster than a formal request."

Theron looked at the manifest sheet in his hand. Joren had had a destination after Halverstow. Three weeks in Sannholt, a name at a postal house, a contact called Pessel. He did not know whether Pessel was family or colleague or something else, but the address was real and the timing was specific and whoever had arranged to meet Joren there would, by now, have been waiting past the expected arrival by two days.

He folded the manifest and put it in his coat.

The gondola shifted again, this time decisively — a long and conscious reduction in angle, the whole frame acknowledging the descent with a low harmonized note from the harness chain. Through the window the harbor grew by a fraction, the lights sharpening as the distance closed.

Halverstow was coming up.

The lights ran along the waterfront in a broken chain, amber and white and one red marker at the harbor mouth, and below the headland the dark water was beginning to show the first pale creases of the port approach.

He had Maret Fell on the leading thread and three weeks of Joren's plans sitting in a letter, and the gondola was going down.
