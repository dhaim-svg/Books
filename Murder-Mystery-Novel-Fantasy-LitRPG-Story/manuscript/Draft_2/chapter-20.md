# Chapter 20 — The Peacock in the Rain

Theron was sharpening a pencil when the reply came.

He had borrowed a knife from the harbourmaster's writing table and was working around the cedar barrel in slow even passes when the boy appeared at the door with a folded note and the flat look of someone who had been told not to wait for an answer. Theron thanked him, took the note, and went back to the pencil while he read.

The writing was Vannic's: heavily pressured, slightly tilted, the hand of someone who had been educated for signatures rather than correspondence. It said, in its entirety: *Yes. One name. Roven. Come if you need more — I don't want to put it in writing.*

He turned the pencil point against the lamp glass to check the tip, blew the shaving from it, and read the note again.

*Roven.*

A subcontractor's name. Someone inside the Toll network that Toll himself had seen fit to name to Vannic when the contract was arranged — which meant either Roven was notable enough to mention, or Roven was the one who would handle the arrangement if anything went wrong.

He put on his coat.

---

The Halverstow Arms was doing a steady noon business in the ground floor taproom — dock brokers, a pair of customs clerks at the far end of the bench, a woman at the corner table who was eating fried fish and reading a letter and ignoring everyone with the practiced efficiency of a person who ate alone by preference. Theron went up the stairs to the harbor-facing rooms without being stopped.

Vannic opened on the second knock. He was fully dressed, which was presumably an improvement over some previous condition, and had the careful stillness of a man who had been waiting and was now pretending not to have been.

"Mavik," he said, and stepped back from the door.

The room was large — harbor-facing, as reported, with a window looking out over the deep-water berths where the Wyrm's lines were still visible from the right angle. The bed was made. The writing desk had a stack of correspondence on it, half-sorted, with one letter set apart from the others under a paperweight shaped like a small brass anchor. A second chair had been pulled to the window at some point and never returned.

Vannic sat on the edge of the desk. He gestured at the window chair by way of invitation and apparently noticed as he did it that it was at the wrong angle, and made no move to correct this, and then appeared to notice that he'd noticed. He looked at his hands instead.

Theron sat in the window chair at the wrong angle.

"One name," he said. "Roven."

"Yes." Vannic looked at his own hands. "When Toll presented the contract — in the office, just us and Toll and the clerk who notarized it — he said the arrangement had been handled through someone he called Roven. That this was the person who had found Joren and vouched for him. He said it by way of reassurance. That the man working my passage had been verified by someone in the network."

"He gave you Roven's full name?"

"Just Roven. I assumed it was a surname. He didn't offer a first name and I didn't ask." Vannic's jaw tightened a fraction. "I know I should have. I know a great deal of things I should have asked, in hindsight."

Outside the window, a cargo barge was clearing the Wyrm's berth on the slow outgoing tide, its crew moving in the loose, unhurried way of men doing a routine they had done enough times to stop thinking about it. Theron watched it for a moment.

"Toll named Roven specifically," he said, thinking aloud — not to Vannic, but not hiding it from him either. "Not just 'we have people who verify.' He named this particular person."

"He said Roven had worked with Joren before. Several times. That the pairing was reliable."

Theron turned back from the window.

"He said that precisely? That Roven and Joren had worked together before?"

Vannic's expression shifted — the particular shift of someone recalling something more clearly than they'd wanted to. "He said — I remember because it was the kind of thing you hear at an auction preview, when a dealer is explaining provenance — he said, 'Roven and your man have run work together. The relationship is established.' Then he moved to the fee schedule." Vannic spread his hands slightly. "I thought it was professional reassurance. Like telling a client the surgeon and the anaesthetist have worked together before."

*[Investigator — Insight +1. Roven is not just a network contact. Roven knew Joren's working style. The relationship was already established.]*

Theron read the System line and thought about the Fell thread and the shape of the triangle and the one point of the triangle he couldn't yet see clearly. "Toll also named Joren to you. By name, or by description?"

"By name. Joren. Just the one name." Vannic rubbed the back of his neck. "I asked, when the contract was drawn up, whether I'd have a way to verify the arrangement was working. Whether anyone would be watching to ensure the performance held. Toll said Roven would be aware of the passage. Those were his words. 'Roven will be aware of the passage.'"

The room was quiet for a moment. From below, the taproom sounds drifted up — the clatter of a tray, a brief burst of laughter from the dock brokers' end.

*Roven will be aware of the passage.*

"Was it stated as oversight?" Theron said. "Or as protection?"

Vannic blinked. The question had apparently arrived from a direction he hadn't anticipated. "I — I assumed oversight. Of Joren. Of the performance."

"But Toll didn't say which direction the awareness ran."

Another pause. Vannic looked at the floor, at the worn center stripe of the carpet where someone had walked back and forth between door and window long enough to thin it. "No," he said at last. "He didn't." He looked up. "You think Roven was there to watch the passage for something other than — quality control."

"I think," Theron said carefully, "that Joren warned you on the dock that if anyone asked about Lord Vannic, you should say he was difficult to find. And Toll told you Roven would be aware of the passage. And Roven had worked with Joren before." He stood. "Those three facts are in the same shape. I don't know what shape that is yet."

Vannic was quiet. He had the look now of a man rearranging furniture in a room and discovering that every configuration left something blocking the door.

"What happens to the Vannic estate," he said, not quite a question, "when this resolves. When you find whoever — when this ends."

"That's outside my class assignment." Theron picked up his hat. "The magistrate handles the estate implications. You should have your factor write to them."

"My factor," Vannic said, "is Berric Toll."

Theron stopped.

Vannic made the gesture again — the small, uncomfortable spread of hands that acknowledged the shape of his own situation with minimal drama. "He's been my factor for three years. The gondola arrangement was a separate thing — I went to him precisely because the arrangement was separate. But yes. He manages the Vannic accounts."

"Get a different factor," Theron said. "Today, if you can. Ask the magistrate's office for a referral — they maintain a list of bonded agents outside active case connections. Do not send correspondence to Toll while this investigation is running."

Vannic nodded slowly. He was not performing anything now. He just looked tired, and somewhat chastened, and also like a man who had eaten half a bread roll this morning and not replaced it.

"The Sannholt matter," Theron said, pausing at the door. "Joren's contact in Sannholt. If you had any dealings with anyone on the south coast through Toll — any factor's referrals, any names that came through that account — I need those."

"I don't — I haven't done business in Sannholt." Vannic frowned. "Is that where it connects?"

"Possibly. I don't know yet." Theron opened the door, then stopped with it half open. Something was still sitting at the edge of the afternoon's accounting, and he turned back. "Vannic. When Toll said 'Roven and your man have run work together' — did he say it as though Roven was senior to Joren in the network? Or as equals?"

Vannic thought about it. He was doing this more carefully now than he had been two days ago, and Theron noted it without mentioning it. "Senior," Vannic said at last. "Definitely. The way Toll described it — Roven was the one who made the evaluation. Not the other way."

Theron filed that. "Thank you," he said, and went.

---

Sable was where he'd left her, at the far end of the harbourmaster's main hall, having a conversation with the same harbor agent as the day before that was apparently still going. She was looking at a chart this time rather than a route schedule. When Theron came in she did not look up, but she angled the chart slightly so he could see it from where he'd stopped — a reflex, he had come to understand, that meant *I'm not done but you can start.*

He waited. The agent finished the point he was making, tapped a distance marking on the chart, and Sable thanked him and took her notes and folded them.

"Roven," Theron said when the agent had gone. "A name in Toll's network. Senior to Joren. Toll told Vannic when the contract was arranged that Roven had worked with Joren before and would be 'aware of the passage.'"

Sable looked at the folded notes in her hand. "Aware. Not managing."

"Precisely."

"So Toll told his own client that a third party would be watching the arrangement." She set the notes in her satchel. "Why would Toll do that?"

"I've been asking that." He sat in one of the hall chairs. "If it was quality control — reassurance that the performance would hold — you don't name the watcher. You say we have protocols. You name someone when you want the client to know the name."

"Or when you want the client to feel safe. To feel like the network is watching in the direction of Joren, not the client."

Theron looked at her. "Vannic could have bolted. At any point. If he panicked about the contract, canceled the passage —"

"The arrangement collapses, the job fails, Roven's relationship with Joren becomes a liability." Sable had the considering expression she wore when she was reading something no one else had written. "But if Vannic believes Roven is watching Joren, he stays calm. The passage runs."

"And if Roven was actually watching the passage from inside it —"

"Then Roven knew exactly where Joren was," she said. "For the duration."

The hall had the particular late-afternoon quiet of a working building where the morning's urgency had passed and the evening's hadn't arrived yet. A clerk at the front desk was sorting incoming packages by size with the focused satisfaction of someone who preferred tasks with clear endpoints.

"Fell," Theron said. "The magistrate's office hasn't placed her in the city yet. If she's already gone —"

"She's still here." Sable said it without inflection, which meant she had information she hadn't presented yet. She reached into her satchel and produced a folded note, smaller than the one the morning messenger had brought.

He took it. It was from the magistrate's constable — four lines, plain hand, very clear: *Fell, M., observed yesterday noon at the harbor-agent registry on Dockwater Lane. Registered as a transit assessor for a cargo verification scheduled three days from now. She is booked at the Compass and Beam inn, south harbor quarter.*

He read it twice, the way he always read things that changed the shape of what he was doing. "She's still working."

"She's still here and she's taken a new job." Sable's tone was not quite dry but it was close. "Either she doesn't know how close the thread is, or she does know and she's decided staying visible is safer than moving."

"The second," Theron said. "If she knew the thread was loose, she'd be gone. She's stayed because she thinks she's clean on the gondola record."

"Which she is, by the factors' board." Sable paused. "She's not clean on TL-9."

"No." He stood. "I should write to the magistrate. Request a formal introduction — not a warrant, an interview. With the TL-9 notation on the table." He was already working through the shape of it: what to lead with, how much to show Fell before asking the thing he actually needed, which was how Roven connected to a dead man in a sealed cabin and a six-day gap before departure from Mirren. "Tomorrow morning, at the magistrate's office. Neutral ground."

"Take the full TL-9 documentation," Sable said. "Not a summary. The actual notation in the factors' board ledger — ask the clerk to make a copy. If Fell sees her own handwriting identified as Toll's shorthand, the conversation starts in a different place."

*[Investigator — Insight +1. Evidence first, theory second. The notation is indisputable. Start there.]*

He made a note. The System had caught up to Sable three seconds after she'd said it, which was about its usual interval.

"The name Roven," Sable said. "You're going to put it to Fell."

"Not first." He was writing. "First we establish the Toll connection and let her choose her answer. If she chooses to be cooperative, I ask about Roven then." He looked up. "If she knows that name — if she goes still when she hears it — that's a different conversation."

Sable was looking at the harbor chart she'd left on the hall table, not reading it now, just looking at the shape of it. "Roven," she said quietly, by way of thinking the word rather than asking a question. "Senior to Joren in the network. Toll names him to Vannic as a guarantor. He's 'aware of the passage.'"

"Three points," Theron said.

"Four." She turned from the chart. "You, Joren, Toll, and the person Toll pointed at as the one holding the arrangement together. Roven is in the middle of this. Not on the edge of it."

He looked at what he'd written. The triangle he'd drawn in yesterday's notebook wasn't a triangle anymore; it was a shape with a fourth point, and the fourth point had a line running from Toll to Joren to the sealed cabin on the Skybound Wyrm.

"Fell tomorrow," he said. "And the Sannholt reply."

"The coach clears in three to four days. If Pessel writes back immediately —" She did the arithmetic. "Possibly Friday. Possibly Saturday."

"Possibly Friday." He closed the notebook. The case had the shape now of something that was almost ready to hold its own weight — not resolved, but coherent, the way a building under construction reaches the point where you can see what it's going to be, even through the scaffolding. "We need Fell's account and Roven's name in the same room."

"Start with Fell," Sable said. "The rest follows, or it doesn't." She gathered her satchel. "Write the magistrate's note now. If you send it before fifth watch they'll have it before morning."

He was already writing.

The harbor sounds came in through the high windows — the long pull of the tide against the quayside, the chain-ring of a mooring line, somewhere a drake calling over the water in its low carrying tone, nothing urgent, just the noise of a working port at the end of a working day. Theron finished the note, sanded it, folded it, and pushed back his chair.

He had Roven's name. He had Fell's location. He had the shape of the fourth point and a meeting in the morning.

It was enough for today.
