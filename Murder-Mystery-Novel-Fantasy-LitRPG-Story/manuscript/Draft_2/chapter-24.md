# Chapter 24 — Everyone Walks Back In

Theron spread the third-class passenger list across the table in his room at the Arms and weighted the top corner with his case book.

Fourteen passengers, minus Vannic. Twelve of those accounted for, at least in outline: the two with soft alibis from the steward's briefing, the textile buyer, the woman with the funeral record, the six who had cleared on profession and reference, and Fell, who had not cleared so much as complicated. That left two columns that, until yesterday, he had considered closed. He turned to the first.

*Maret Fell.* Mirren east harbour, shipping assessment. On the gondola for cargo-side reasons that overlapped with the Wyrm's freight manifest in ways she had been willing to give him, cheerfully, without being asked to give more. He had his read; Sable had her counter-read. The warrant was in the magistrate's hand. He left the column where it was and moved on.

The second column stopped him.

He had written it out at the time with the unhurried confidence of someone filing a complete entry: *Halsa Renn. Cabin nine-T. Traveling through to Sannholt, family business. Cash. Seasonal work. No apparent connection to Mirren west harbour, to Berric Toll, to Joren Aldis's professional network.* He had closed the column and turned the page.

The question had been: who had motive against Lord Vannic?

He knew what the question should have been.

He picked up the pencil and wrote it in the margin. *Who had motive to stop Joren Aldis? Who had prior knowledge of the swap? Who, among the people I filed as no motive, had a connection to the network I hadn't found yet?*

He looked at what he had written on Renn.

*Traveling through to Sannholt.* He had filed this as confirmation of no connection — the through-traveler, south coast, local errand. Now he read it differently. The Sannholt address in Joren's correspondence. Pessel at the Copper Finch, waiting two years. A contact whose business with Joren was close enough that she had declined to put it in writing. Sannholt was not a large town. Someone traveling through to Sannholt on personal business was — he stopped himself. That was not evidence. That was geography.

*No apparent connection to Mirren west harbour, Toll, or Joren's network.* Apparent. He had written *apparent*, which was honest. He had also filed it as functional closure, which was not.

*Seasonal small-contract labour. Cash. No professional reference.* He had read this as low-tier at the time because his frame was Vannic's world: contracts, nobles, factors, cargo accounts. A seasonal worker on cash terms had no obvious entry point into that hierarchy. But Joren's world was not Vannic's world. Joren's world was illusionists and network brokers and a ten-year career taking off-coast contract work to feed someone else's children. The entry point into Joren's world was different.

*Halsa Renn observed that Calden's walk was quieter on the third morning. She noticed it but did not know what it meant.* He had written this in his notes and filed it under *third-class corridor, incidental observations* without following the thread. He read it again now.

Calden's walk was quieter because a man who had thanked him by name was dead. It was the kind of specific, attended observation that came from someone who watched how people moved through spaces. He had written down that Sable had said the same thing about Renn herself, the observation delivered in the corridor after the interview: *she notices how people move*. He had folded the page and walked on.

He put the pencil down.

The re-examination didn't give him an answer. What it gave him was a correction: he had asked whether Renn had a connection to a network he'd understood at the time as Vannic's world, and she hadn't. But that wasn't the right network. He had been filing suspects against the wrong map.

He wrote across the bottom of the Renn entry, in the small hand he used for things that were neither conclusions nor dismissals: *Revisit. Wrong frame at time of interview. Sannholt thread may have contact.*

Then he turned the page.

*[Investigator — Case file annotation: four fourth-tier dismissals reclassified as wrong-frame, not cleared. Method correct; input set incomplete. No new leads. Process continues.]*

He looked at that for a moment, then turned back to Fell's column and began the follow-up notes for the warrant.

---

He ate breakfast in the Arms dining room at the table by the window, which he had occupied long enough by now that the young woman who laid the cloth put the coffee down first and the bread second without asking. The harbor was starting its day in the street below: a dray horse objecting to something at the corner, two factors with their cases walking quickly in opposite directions, a boy carrying a stack of slates almost certainly heavier than he was allowed.

Sable's bag was in the corridor when he came down. He registered the brown traveling coat over the chair back when he crossed to his table, which was how he knew she'd been up earlier and had not slept through the coach window.

She came in twenty minutes later with a folder tucked under one arm. She got her own coffee from the sideboard without asking and sat at the far end of his table, which had happened every morning for a week, the table being large enough that it was easier than finding another.

"Calden found me last night," she said.

He looked at her over his cup.

She set the folder on the table between them and opened it. Inside was a handwritten register, several pages in a close, practiced hand, not the Arms's stationery: smaller, the kind of notebook a man uses for work rather than for correspondence. "He made notes during the passage," she said. "Third-class corridor. Which passengers spoke to staff, which ones he read as unusual, what he couldn't place at the time."

Theron set his coffee down. "He gave you his working notes."

"He said he'd been waiting for someone doing the follow-through to come past." She turned to the first page. "He left them with me because I was the one leaving in the morning. Figured they'd be useful in Sannholt."

The register was Calden's. The notation was the same economical hand from the corridor passes Theron had studied in the first week. Third-class passengers organized by cabin number, with marginal annotations in a different pencil: timing observations, behavioral notes, a row of small marks Theron didn't immediately read.

"What are the marks?" he said.

"Ones who made no contact with staff beyond boarding acknowledgment," she said. "Which he codes separately from the ones who kept to their cabins but thanked him for the sheets." She pointed to a row. "There are three in that category. One of them is Maret Fell."

He looked at the page. Fell was there, annotated in Calden's hand: *professional, polite, asked once about observation-window hours, no further contact, no cabin conversation.* The observation-window question was already in his notes. The "no further contact" had a small additional mark beside it in pencil, different from the notation key. He looked at Sable.

"He put that there last night," she said. "When he remembered something he hadn't put in the original. He said one of the quiet-cabin ones, the no-contact passengers, had a lamp on past third watch on the third night. He'd noted it at the time as insomnia or reading and hadn't flagged it because it wasn't unusual enough. He wasn't certain which cabin."

Theron went still for a moment. "He doesn't know?"

"He said he thought it was nine. He wasn't certain."

Cabin nine-T. He kept his face where it was.

"He filed what he remembered," Sable said, in the even tone she used for facts that did not require emphasis. "He wasn't certain. It's in there."

He looked at the annotation. Calden had written it in the uncertain hand of a man putting down a memory he wasn't fully confident in: *third night, late lamp, nine possibly, not certain.* Then he turned back to the first page and began reading through the full set of cabin observations, methodically, from the front.

*[Investigator — New material: steward's working notes, third-class corridor. Third night, cabin nine-T, lamp past third watch — uncertain provenance. Flag for verification.]*

"That's useful," he said.

"I thought so." She picked up her coffee and watched the harbor through the window.

---

The coach departure was confirmed for seventh bell. He had asked at the clearing house counter the previous afternoon, and the clerk had confirmed it with the flat certainty of a woman who had given the same answer several hundred times. He relayed this to Sable now, across the remains of breakfast, more for the record than because she didn't know.

She nodded and reached for the notebook she kept in her inside coat pocket, the small one, not the one she used for correspondence. She opened it on the table and read through what she had.

"Pessel," she said. "The letter accepted Friday. Reply within four to five days, which is already in and waiting. Copper Finch, Sannholt." She turned a page. "Two-year wait. Joren wrote in spring that the time was nearly right. Won't explain by post."

"Approach her on her terms," he said. "Not as the investigation — as the person who's been waiting."

Sable looked at him briefly. "Yes."

He turned to his own notes. "I have the cargo trace in the magistrate's hands: Vantrel, Marbeth, the warrant pending. That's the thread I'm running here." He tapped the third-class list. "And the re-examination."

"Any movement on the re-examination?"

"Corrections," he said. "I was working four of the dismissals against the wrong map. I've got a revisit flag on Renn."

She looked at the page he was on. He watched her read the notation, the marginal note he'd written that morning: *wrong frame at time of interview, Sannholt thread may have contact.* She didn't comment on it. She turned back to her own notebook.

"If Pessel knew Joren well enough to wait two years," she said, "she may know who else he was in contact with in that final period. Before the Vannic commission."

"That's what I'm hoping you'll find out."

"I know." She closed the notebook. "And if she's protective —"

"She will be."

"Then I'll be slow about it." She said this the way she said most things: as a statement of method rather than a reassurance. He nodded.

She knew the shape of the case as well as he did. Listing the open threads would be recapping for no audience.

"The carved fish," he said instead. "If there's a conversation about Joren's family — anything about where they are, whether they've been told —"

"I'll find out." She closed the folder with Calden's notes and tucked it under her arm. "And if there's a family address, I'll send it on before you need to ask."

He didn't say anything to that. There was nothing particular to say.

She stood, and he stood, and they went out into the hall where her bag was packed and sitting against the wall with the particular tidiness of someone who traveled often enough to have a system for it. He walked her to the Arms door and held it for her against the harbor wind.

On the step she paused and adjusted the folder under her arm. She looked at him — not the way she looked at the case, but the way she looked at things she was about to leave and wouldn't see for a while.

"Keep the Marbeth thread moving," she said. "Don't let the warrant sit."

"I won't."

"And eat something that isn't coffee and case notes."

He looked at her. "I had bread."

"Half a piece," she said.

He couldn't immediately contradict this, because it was true. She went down the steps, and he stood in the doorway until the harbor road turned and she was out of sight behind the factor's building.

Then he went inside and back up to his room, where the third-class passenger list was still spread across the table and Calden's notes were already making him want to pull the timing slate.

---

He worked until mid-afternoon, when the light through the window started its angle shift and the Arms dining room below produced the sound of the early supper setup. He had built a new working column: the re-examination, the four wrong-frame dismissals, the Renn flag and what he still needed to verify. He had written to the harbor registry about Vantrel Secured Storage's annual records, a second letter reinforcing the warrant request. He had noted Calden's lamp observation in its proper place, uncertain provenance marked.

The case had the shape of something waiting for its next two pieces: Pessel's account from Sable, and Marbeth's records from the magistrate. Both were moving. Neither had arrived.

He made a clean copy of the afternoon's notes into the case book and closed it. Then he checked the coach schedule on the folded slip he'd tucked into the inside cover three days ago and confirmed what he already knew: seventh bell, Friday, Halverstow south station. Sable would be on it. The Sannholt road was four hours in good weather, and the weather had been good all week.

He set the case book at the desk's far edge, where he could see it without looking at it, and poured the last of the afternoon's cold coffee into his cup and drank it standing.

He had fourteen passengers, minus one. Twelve of those with some kind of accounting. Two columns still working. One of them waiting on a warrant. The other flagged for revisit with an uncertain steward's note and a wrong-frame correction and a journey to Sannholt that was already in progress.

He wrote one more line in the margin of the Renn column, under the revisit flag: *Sannholt thread active as of today.*

Then he put the pencil down, picked up his coat, and went to find the harbor registry before end of business.

The coach was confirmed for seventh bell.
