# Chapter 7 — Working a Scene Together

Theron came down to the steward's station to ask the captain's leave and found the leave already in progress without him.

The pantry passage on deck three was narrower than its diagram suggested. Service brass on one side, the row of bell-cords on the other, a folded chair propped under the cord-rope and a small slate with the morning's substitutions chalked on it in Calden's neat hand. Theron had meant to step in, ask the steward whether he'd had time to consider, and proceed up to the chartroom from there. Instead he stopped at the corner of the frame.

Sable was inside the doorway. Calden was facing her, holding a folded cloth that had something heavy in it, mid-sentence. Theron caught the end of the sentence.

"—Second-class corridor, yes, ma'am, but only the run from cabin nine to the steward's station and back. I'd not have crossed past nine."

"And the door to nine?"

"Closed when I went, closed when I came back."

She nodded, once, with the small finished nod of a person who had been given a fact and was setting it down where she meant to put it. Calden adjusted the cloth in his hand. Theron had the distinct sense, standing in the corner, that he had not been registered as present.

He cleared his throat.

Calden's chin lifted. "Mr. Mavik. Sir." The tone was different. Not cold — a step back. A man closing a drawer that had been open. "I was just answering Miss Verrin's question about the fourth-watch round."

"I'd like to hear it, if it's the same one I asked yesterday."

"It is, sir. Substantially." A pause. "I'd ask leave from the captain before going into the matter of it. As I said yesterday."

"Of course."

Calden's eyes flicked once to Sable. "Ma'am," he said, and stepped past Theron with the cloth, who turned to make room. The pantry passage was barely wide enough for the courtesy.

When he was gone, Sable looked at Theron and said, "Sorry. I should have asked you first."

"You didn't ask him anything I didn't ask him."

"He answered me, though."

"Yes."

She waited. Theron registered that he was meant to say the next thing, and that he did not yet have it. The brass on the bell-cords was warm-looking in the lamp-light; the slate's chalk smelled faintly of the morning. He shifted his weight off his back foot.

"I'd like to compare," he said. "If you've an hour."

"I've an hour."

---

The dining room had emptied for the captain's noon address, except for the small window table where Vellaine had left her book face-down on the cloth and gone forward with the rest. The galley boy was clearing pastries onto a tray with the careful slowness of someone whose only instruction had been *don't drop anything.* Theron took the small table. Sable took the chair across from him, set her hands flat on the linen, and said: "What do you have?"

"The four rounds," he said. "The geometry of the cabin. Esherel's grievance, in full. The amendment under M. Tannin, paid dockside before lift. Vellaine asleep through the third bell." He thought about it. "And the steward absent twelve minutes on a matter he'll discuss only with the captain's leave."

"That's what I have, too." She angled her chin slightly. "Mostly. I have the corridor."

"The corridor."

"Where Calden was in it. Not the times — I can't help you with the times. Where his body was."

He had not heard a person say a steward's *body* in a sentence that did not mean *corpse*, and he realized in the same beat that this was not of hers. He set his slate on the cloth and turned it ninety degrees so they could both read it.

"Walk me through it."

"After we eat."

The galley boy arrived with the second-watch leftovers, which on this gondola meant a pot of something soft and brown the cook had decided was now soup, half a loaf, and the dish of small lemon biscuits that had been migrating around the first-class cabins all day. Theron poured for them both. The coffee was the last of the morning's pot and had the slight burnt edge of coffee that had stood; he registered it without minding.

She picked up her spoon. He watched her tear the bread in half and offer him the larger piece without making the offer a thing. He took it.

"Tell me what you have on him," he said.

"On Vannic."

"On Vannic."

She considered her bread. "He was very tired the first night. I don't know that you'd have caught it from where you were sitting — he was facing the wall by then. He wasn't tired the second night, by which I mean he'd slept since." She took a bite, swallowed, went on. "He said *forty-one* when he meant *forty-one and a half* about Esherel's invoice. Esherel corrected him; he didn't argue. He likes — liked — the small lemon biscuits, the ones with the seed in. He took two. He doesn't eat in front of people if he can help it. The chair-facing-the-wall thing is real."

He found he was writing. He had not consciously decided to write. He set the pen down.

"Anything that didn't fit?"

She looked at her spoon. "Nothing investigatively. Small things. He reached for a word at dinner the first night — a perfectly ordinary word, *harbor* — and the vowel came out flat. Coastal flat. He said it again the right way two words later and went on. You wouldn't correct a lord at dinner. You wouldn't even register it, really. I registered it because I was sitting on his blind side and looking at the back of his head, which is where I do my registering."

"Coastal flat."

"Sea-town vowel. Pulled forward in the mouth. Two or three regions it could be." She took another bite. "He didn't do it again. I think he was tired."

He wrote *Vannic — country house, where?* in the slate's margin and underlined it once and then read what he had written and crossed it out, because the lord had been at the academy on his uncle's grant for two terms in his teens, which his slate had already noted and which Theron had already filed, and people picked up vowels from where they lived. *Small talk*, he wrote instead, in the same place. He moved on.

"What about Calden?"

She considered. "Calden likes him."

"Liked."

"Yes."

He paused. He had been about to ask why she thought so and had heard, in his own head, the sound of the question landing wrong. He took a swallow of coffee instead. The slight burnt edge was useful; it gave him a beat.

"Tell me," he said.

"Calden thanks people who thank him by name. Not formally — internally. You can see it because he goes a half-second slower the next time he comes back. He went a half-second slower on every round past Vannic's door. He's been going a half-second slower today, too, in the corridors near the cabin. He doesn't know he's doing it."

"That's something I wouldn't have caught."

"No."

He picked up the pen and set it down again. The small concrete fact she'd put on the table — the steward's half-second — was not a piece of evidence as he was trained to assemble evidence. It was not a piece on the board. It was a piece *about* the board, and he had spent the morning moving pieces.

"You said you have the corridor," he said.

"After we eat."

She had a piece of bread halfway to her mouth and the other hand around her cup, and she was eating without performing the eating, which he noticed because he was performing his eating slightly, the way he always did with new people. He stopped performing.

"What did Calden tell you in the pantry," he said, "that he wouldn't tell me yesterday?"

"He didn't, actually." She set the cup down. "He told me what door he didn't go through, in the second-class corridor. He didn't say what the errand was. He said he'd need the captain's leave for that, same as he told you. He just answered me before he said it." She lifted one shoulder, set it down. "I think he's been waiting all morning for someone he could give the part-answer to."

"Why you, and not me."

She looked at him. The look was kind without being soft.

"Because you're going to write down what he says, sir, and I'm not."

He thought about the slate on the cloth, his hand on the pen, the small precise way he was holding both. He moved his hand to his cup. The pen stayed where it was.

"Theron," he said.

"Theron."

"Not sir."

"Noted." She picked up her spoon again. "Eat your soup before it cools, *Theron*. We have a corridor to walk."

The first-class corridor on deck four was the kind of quiet that announced itself. The captain's address had ended, the passengers had returned, and the corridor was empty in the deliberate way a corridor was empty when everyone behind every door was choosing not to come out. The cabin twelve door was sealed with Calden's wax and a small folded note in Theron's own hand. The lamp at the corridor's mid-point was lit; the lamp at the end nearer the chain trunk had been dimmed.

Theron stopped at the cabin twelve door, took the geometry in once for the discipline of taking it in, and turned to her. "Show me."

She did not stop at the door. She walked past it.

"Calden's station is here." She came to a stop at the recessed alcove between cabins fourteen and fifteen, four doors down on the same side, where the small wall-mounted bell-board lived and a folded chair was set into the wall. "He stands here when he's between rounds. He can see the full length of the corridor from this point, both directions. Light to his left, dark to his right." She looked toward the dim lamp at the chain trunk end. "He's not seeing into the dim end. He's *registering* it. There's a difference."

"He told you this."

"No. He stood here while we were talking yesterday afternoon and shifted twice. He shifts when he's been here a while. He didn't think about it. Stewards are bodies in spaces. The space tells you where his body is."

He moved past her to the alcove. The wall came up at his shoulder; the bell-board hung at the height of his hand. From here, the line of doors ran clean to the chain trunk hatch — twelve was the third door from the trunk, on the left. He could see the wax seal from where he stood. He could see, too, that the alcove put a slight kink in the sightline: the doors closer to the trunk required a half-step out of the alcove to read.

"You'd come out of the alcove to look at twelve," he said.

"Once a round, yes."

"And if you didn't?"

"Then twelve isn't in your sightline at the rest position." She moved past him, walked four steps down the corridor toward the trunk, and stopped at the cabin twelve door. "But Calden would. He'd come out for every round. He told you that, didn't he? Quiet through every pass."

"Yes."

"He meant it."

He looked at the alcove from where she was standing now, and at her, and at the line between them. Calden's body in the alcove. Calden's body taking the four steps. Calden's body, in some twelve-minute window in the fourth watch, *not* taking the four steps because he wasn't in the corridor at all — he was somewhere on deck three, in a second-class run that ended at cabin nine and turned around. Theron had had the times. He had not had the body.

"He was here," Theron said. "Except for the twelve minutes."

"Yes."

"And the twelve minutes is the thing the captain has to authorize him to discuss."

"Yes."

He looked at the dim lamp at the chain trunk end. The corridor was very quiet. Somewhere, two decks down, the drakes' rhythm came through the hull — three-part pull, then held, then release — the slight irregularity that Mottled had not yet been talked out of.

"I'm going to ask the captain about the twelve minutes," he said. "Today."

"Good."

"And the M. Tannin door."

"That's yours, yes." She came back up the corridor toward him. "I'll see what Calden says next. He's not going to say anything more without the captain. But he might tell me a thing he doesn't know he's telling me. He's been doing that already, today."

"Yes."

She had stopped at the edge of the alcove. He was still inside it. The recess put them at roughly the same height — closer than they had been in the dining room, less close than they had been in the pantry. He moved out of the alcove and into the corridor proper, which gave them both back a foot of distance and gave him back the door to cabin twelve at the corner of his eye.

"Sable," he said.

"Yes."

"Why did you walk past the steward's station yesterday to bring me the manifest yourself."

A pause. Not a long one. "Because you'd filed me in your verify column," she said, "and I wanted you to know I was a person you could ask."

He felt the small mortified heat at the back of his neck that he had learned, in the academy, was generally the correct heat to feel when he had filed something wrong.

"Noted," he said.

She smiled — quick, dry, not making a thing of it. "Good. Go and find the captain."

She turned and walked the corridor back toward the staircase. He stayed in front of the alcove for a half-beat after she had gone, looking at the line her body had made across the boards — alcove, four steps, door — and then at the dim lamp, and then at the wax seal.

He left to find the captain.
