# Chapter 16 — He Had a Name

The Halverstow harbormaster's tug came out to meet them at the half-mile mark, and Theron stood at the aft rail and watched the ropes go across.

He had the small cloth bag in his coat pocket. Three things in it: one handkerchief, used, folded because he had folded it; one steel pen-nib, bent slightly at the tip; one carved wooden fish, which he had not touched since logging it in the cabin and logging it again at the stern alcove, and which he was now aware of with the constant low awareness of something occupying a pocket that had nothing else in it.

The drakes dropped out of harness forty feet above the docking mast. He watched them bank south — Petal first, Gravel close, Mottled lagging by the beat she had already apologized for, or Wisp had apologized on her behalf. Three shapes going small against the harbor sky. The gondola settled on the mooring lines with the slow deliberate sigh of a large object agreeing to rest.

He stayed at the rail.

Below, the cargo bay opened. The first-class passengers were already in the corridor — he had passed Esherel on the way aft, her luggage neatly stacked, the folio in its case for the first time in three days. Vellaine had stopped to hand him a card: *Larent Institute, if the case asks for theoretical consultation.* He had put it in his breast pocket without looking away from her face, and she had nodded the small nod that meant she found him worth the card and had decided not to say so. The Henners were bringing their daughter down the gangway now. The scholar pair. The second-class passengers after.

One man was still in third class, in cabin three-T, waiting to be told when he could disembark as himself.

Captain Joriss had the wireless open to the port magistrate. Theron could hear the click of it through the chartroom window, three decks up. He had delivered his brief report at the first-watch bell: facts only, full name of the deceased unknown pending further inquiry, matter of identity-illusion involved, passenger in third class to be held aboard pending formal interview. Joriss had listened with the expression of a man who had heard stranger things from Halverstow approaches and had decided not to say so either. Good man.

The cloth bag sat in his pocket.

He went inside to find Sable.

---

She was in the captain's day-cabin, which Joriss had opened to them as the magistrate's arrival was expected at the second bell. Two chairs and the narrow writing desk and the smell of beeswax and old charts. The porthole looked out onto the harbor — still water, the long line of fishing boats, a coal barge working the far slip. She had taken one chair; the other was waiting.

He came in and shut the door.

She looked at him steadily across the desk. He had the sense she had been waiting long enough to decide how this was going to begin.

"Joren Aldis," she said.

He heard it. The full name landed on the desk between them, unhurried.

"He had a surname," Theron said.

"He had a surname. It's in the letters. Second page, the Berric Toll correspondence — Toll uses the full name when he signs off the contract particulars. I read it at the alcove when you were reading the schedule addendum."

He had not read that far. He sat down. He put the cloth bag on the desk without deciding to.

"Joren Aldis," he said.

"Yes."

The name was different now that he had said it. That was the specific mechanics of it. Theron Mavik. Sable Verrin. Joren Aldis. There was an order to these things.

She leaned forward, elbows on the desk, hands loose. "Can I walk through it."

"Walk through it."

"All of it."

"Yes."

She looked at her hands for a beat. Then she looked at him, which was how she set herself, and began.

"The kindnesses," she said. "There were three I logged. The steward on the boarding morning — you would have heard it from the corridor; I saw it from the doorway. *Thank you. For the — all of it. The boarding business.* Not a performance. Aimed at one person. Calden went a half-second slower past cabin twelve every round after that because he had been thanked by name and it mattered."

He nodded. He had heard it.

"The second one was the second night at supper. The airsick child — there was a family in the wall-facing section, their daughter had been sick on the approach, she was in the seat nearest the galley passage. He took a ginger sweet from the condiment dish on his tray — the one the cook sets out for the wine courses, you don't usually eat them plain — and slid it onto the steward's tray without looking up, and Calden knew what to do with it, and the child's mother didn't see where it came from. He went on eating."

He had not seen it. He had been facing the opposite direction.

"The third was the gangway threshold," she said. "The elderly passenger on the step. He waited. No gesture, no production. Just the three seconds."

"I saw it."

"You told me you'd refiled it under idle courtesy."

"I know what I told you."

A beat. The harbor was quiet outside; a rope somewhere creaked in the gentle rock of the mooring.

"Those weren't lapses," Sable said. "He wasn't forgetting to be Vannic. He was forgetting to try." She was not telling him he was wrong to have missed it. She was not doing the thing that looked like that. She was setting down a weight, and the weight was simply heavier than it had looked. "Vannic didn't have a default toward kindness. Joren Aldis did. Every time the disguise held, he'd put the effort in. Every time he let go of the effort, he was himself. Three times. That's the shape of it."

Theron sat with it.

"The accent," she said.

"The harbor vowel."

"Sea-town vowel, flat and forward. He reached for it the first night at dinner when he was tired, said it wrong, caught himself. I didn't say anything. You file a thing like that away and don't mention it because correcting a lord at dinner is rude. I had it in the back of the room and didn't pull it." She set her right hand on the desk, the way she set things down when they had finished being held. "Coastal town. Two or three stretches it could be. Now it belongs to where Joren was from, not to where Vannic's estate is. The vowel was his."

He wrote nothing. He had no slate. He was aware of the absence of the slate in the way he had been aware, at the beginning, of the weight of the pin. He was not sure whether to name the absence out loud and decided not to.

"The callus," she said, and looked at him. "That one is yours."

"Index finger, right hand," he said. "Working-mage ridge. Not a hobby mark — the kind built over years, ten hours a day. I registered it at the handshake on the boarding morning. I filed it as a noble who practices more seriously than idle suggests."

"And he did practice seriously."

"Yes. Because he was working mage, not a noble who dabbled." He heard himself say it plainly and did not look away from it. "I had the right fact and the wrong filing. The ridge was accurate. The explanation was wrong. The man who built that callus spent years on a mage's work. Joren Aldis's hands."

"Joren Aldis's hands."

He waited.

"The fourth," she said. "You were at the table. The third dinner, the saloon on deck three." She paused for long enough that he heard the silence not as hesitation but as respect for the distance between them at that table. "He spoke about his niece. He said she had carved a small object for him when she was six, and that she would be unimpressed that he hadn't lost it."

"I heard the sentence."

"I heard the voice." She turned her left hand over, looked at it. "The voice changed. You heard the words and they went into the social texture of a man whose body was in the cabin, and you moved on. What I heard was a shift — here and then gone, the way a room shifts in temperature when someone opens a window and closes it again. That tone doesn't come from a performance. A man performing a character at a dinner does not slip sideways into that tone by accident. He slipped into it because he wasn't trying, for four seconds, and what came out was Joren."

She stopped.

"She's eight now," she said, quietly. "If she was six at the carving." Not interiority; a fact, stated plainly, for the record. "She's somewhere this morning. Her mother is somewhere."

The room was very still.

He held the stillness. He was aware of the window, the harbor, the coal barge making its slow progress across the slip. He was aware of the cloth bag on the desk. He was aware of the specific texture of the morning three days ago in the stern alcove, the bronzed light, Sable's cup turned a quarter, the way she had said the line without looking at him.

*He wasn't as bad as I expected.*

It came back to him not as her voice but as his own register of it, in the particular flat tone the part of his mind that stored things without examining them used. He had heard it. He had filed it under *small talk between two people who have been thinking about a man.* He had turned the slate over.

He had not asked why.

She had told him who Joren was — not in the language of an accusation, not as a clue she was pressing into his hand, but in the simple way she said true things when she knew she was the only person in the room who would say them. She had said *he wasn't as bad as I expected* and he had heard it and filed it and moved on to *Esherel*, and the line had waited there in its column, correctly filed, never asked about, and here they were.

He sat with it.

The time it took him to sit with it was the time it took the coal barge to clear the end of the slip through the porthole glass. He watched it. He did not say anything about the coal barge. He did not say anything about the line.

Sable watched him sit with it and said nothing, because she did not need to.

After a while, he reached for the cloth bag.

He did not open it for a moment. He held the shape of it — the handkerchief and the nib against the palm of his hand, the fish underneath them, which he could feel through the cloth by its specific weight and its specific lack of sharp edges. A small thing. Longer than it was wide, the tail end rounded, the fin line worn almost smooth where it met the body.

He untied the bag and set the handkerchief and the nib on the desk and took out the fish.

It sat in his palm.

It was pale wood — birch, maybe, or ash. The length of his middle two fingers together. The carving was careful but not precise: the fin rose at an angle that was not quite level, the tail had one side fuller than the other, the mouth was a confident groove that ran slightly deep on the left edge. The kind of confident imprecision that came from a hand that knew what it intended but was not yet old enough to have closed all the gaps between intention and execution.

An eight-year-old had made this. Six years ago. A child with a knife or a chisel and the determination to make a thing for a person she wanted to keep.

The grain of the wood was warm from the cloth.

He had logged it at the body. Outer coat pocket, between a handkerchief and a pen-nib, logged as *odd personal effect, note for return to estate*. He had held it for the thirty seconds it took to log it and put it in the bag and moved on to the geometry of the porthole latch. He had not thought about it again until the stern alcove when he had handed the bag to Calden and taken it back and put it in his own pocket, and it had been in his pocket since.

Joren Aldis had gone into the run with this in his coat pocket. Not in the luggage. On his body, on the person it reached him through the fabric of the outer coat when he reached the pocket for something, which he must have done a hundred times on the run without taking the fish out. The coat pocket with the handkerchief and the nib. The pocket you put things in when you wanted them to be findable but not on view. The pocket where a person keeps the thing they carry because it is theirs.

Sable was looking at it.

He set it on the desk between them, where she could look at it.

The fish sat there. Worn smooth at the body where hands had held it. Careful and imprecise in its carving, the fin at its small angle, the mouth with its deep left groove. An eight-year-old's handiwork, traveling.

*[Investigator — Quest updated: Solve the Death of Joren Aldis.]*

He read it. He looked at it for a longer moment than he usually looked at a System line.

Joren Aldis. Joren Aldis. The name was in the log now. The name was where the case was.

He picked up the fish again, gently, and held it.

Outside, the harbor rocked the gondola on the lines. The coal barge was gone. The fishing boats knocked softly at their moorings, and somewhere two decks up the magistrate's bell would ring at the second hour, and the work would begin again, and the name on the page would stay there.

He kept the fish in his hand.
