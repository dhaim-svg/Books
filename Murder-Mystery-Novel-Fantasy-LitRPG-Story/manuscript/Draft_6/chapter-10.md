# Chapter 10 — The Cleanest Page

He woke at the bell and at the place his mind had been waiting on overnight, which was the underline on *candid* and the question of whether he had been pleased with it for the right reasons.

He sat at the desk with the coffee Calden had left under its cloth and read the page through. Motive. Opportunity. Demeanor. The letter, sitting where Sable had set it next to the other three and refused to make smaller than they were. The early window beside the late one, unaccounted for in Esherel's column on both sides of the gap. He had slept the way Sable had told him he would sleep, which was the kind of sleep a person had when a conversation had finished without anyone being the one who'd had to give. He found, on inspection, that he was still pleased with the underline.

He capped the pencil and put the slate in his coat pocket and went out to walk.

The first-class corridor at the early bell had the lamp-trimmed quiet of a space staff had passed through and not yet returned to. Calden was somewhere two decks down. The cabin board at the stair had the small chalk tick beside fourteen that meant *occupant up,* drawn in by Calden some minutes before Theron had risen. Calden saw a lot of risings.

He went down the corridor at the pace he walked when he was thinking around a problem rather than at it, which was slower than his usual and harder on his thumb. He passed cabin twelve and made the registration he had made eleven times now of the wax seal and the folded note. He passed the place where the corridor's slight kink put the porthole rail half a step into the floor plan. He stopped at it because the geometry had become a habit of stopping, and looked down the long line of doors.

The memory surfaced unbidden, which was a useful word for it because he hadn't bid for it.

*Boarding morning. The gangway threshold. Vannic stopped for an elderly passenger finishing the step down. Not with any performance — not the wide gesture or the produced smile — just a pause, the three seconds required, and then the step up. No one had been watching except Theron, who had been looking at the lock on his cabin key, and Esherel, whose attention had been elsewhere. Vannic had not looked at the man.*

He had kept it that morning under *manners that did not announce themselves.* He had held it again, a week later, in the part of his attention that kept the lord-shaped behavior of a man with a working mage's calluses on the side of his index finger, and had decided then that the courtesy was *idle*, in the technical sense — the small unspent thing a man with too much room did instinctively, the way a tall man ducked. A lord who practices seriously, he had written in the early margin. A lord who practices seriously will also have the unspent space.

He looked at the corridor. The corridor offered no further information.

He went on.

---

The dining room at the open of the breakfast was warmer than the corridor and louder than it had been any morning of the run so far, which was the thing he noticed before he noticed what was making it loud.

The wine steward stood at Esherel's table with both hands at his sides and the careful neutral face of a man who had been asked a question he did not yet know how to answer. Esherel had not stood. The folio was open on the table at a page he recognized from yesterday by the bend of its corner. Her finger was on a line of figures halfway down.

"Crates," she said. Not raised. Exact. "Two hundred and seven, by your manifest. Two hundred and eleven, by the carrier's mark in the customhouse at Veller. The discrepancy is in the gondola's hold at this moment. I am not asking you to find it. I am asking you to confirm to me that the manifest copy in this folio is the manifest copy in your office."

"Madam Esherel—"

"It is a yes or a no."

The Henners, two tables over, had stopped talking. Vellaine had not looked up from her book. The second-class passenger up to fetch coffee, a tall man Theron had registered yesterday by his patience at the urn, had stopped at the urn with the cup not yet poured.

Esherel stood.

She did not raise her voice. She raised her body, which on Esherel was the same instrument tuned a half-step higher. "You sent the substitute Tenner on the first morning. You will recall that I asked you to note the substitution. I noted it as well. We are now four mornings past that note, and I have spent those four mornings looking through the folio at the night manifest as it traveled with me from Veller, and I am sitting at this table holding a four-crate discrepancy that I will, if I leave the table, have to write to your bonded house about with the formal phrase that means *I am no longer prepared to accept errors of this kind.* I will write that letter from Halverstow. Lord Vannic is dead in cabin twelve and I am sorry he is dead and I am sorrier that I am about to lose the gondola's wine ledger on the same run because there is a fairness to having both losses count and the fairness will not survive the gondola landing without a manifest match."

She stopped.

The room was quiet.

"Yes or no," she said.

"Madam Esherel." The steward inclined his head a half-degree. "I will check. With your leave."

"Check."

He went. The room did not, immediately, return to its noise. Esherel sat back down and opened the folio at a different page and did not, that Theron could see, read it.

He had not moved. He was at the dining-room arch with his coffee cup still in his hand from the corridor outside, which he had brought because it was warmer than the cabin's. He felt the small clean sensation of a category landing on the page.

*Demeanor under stress.*

He stepped into the room, took the chair Vellaine did not look up to acknowledge, and poured himself a second cup he did not need.

"She's having," Vellaine said, "a morning."

"She's having a four-crate morning."

"The crate is real." She turned a page. "I checked when she said *two hundred and seven* on the third syllable."

"You can read a manifest at six tables' distance."

"I can read Esherel's finger at six tables' distance." She closed the book on her thumb. "There is a thing I would like to say to you which I will say only once."

He looked at her.

"That woman is angry," Vellaine said, "and she is correct. Those are not always the same thing in the same body. I have seen the combination twice in my professional life, and I have been wrong about it once, and I will tell you that the wrongness cost me. Be careful with the cleanest column."

She opened the book again.

He drank the coffee he did not need.

---

The captain's gallery ran across the gondola's bow above the chartroom, a narrow railed walk with a window the full width of the hull and the small bench Joriss had told him at second-watch end that he was welcome to use if he didn't sit on the brass. He sat at the end of the bench that was not the brass, with the slate flat on his knee, and worked the page.

He had not written *opportunity confirmed* yet. He wrote it now, in pencil, in a careful hand, and looked at it.

The early window was three minutes in which she had been, by her own account, asleep, on a bed Calden had not seen her on at that minute. The late window was eight minutes in which she had not, by her own account, left her cabin, and which Calden had not been in the corridor to verify. The combined gap was sufficient for the corridor she would have had to walk, the door she would have had to enter, and the cabin she would have had to leave again on a half-step out to read.

He drew the corridor on the slate from memory. He drew the door of cabin twelve and the half-step pass-off that Sable had walked with him the night they'd mapped the upper-deck geometry, which had given the corridor its small clean shape. The line through the columns was uninterrupted. The early window covered the approach. The late window covered the return.

He sat with that.

Motive, on the page, at forty-one and a half thousand crowns and two years of unanswered letters and a folio of drafts. Opportunity, on the page, in two windows in series with a corridor's geometry that admitted her. Demeanor under stress, on the page, in a dining room twenty minutes earlier, in a voice that had not been raised but in a body that had stood; in a four-crate discrepancy that was real and that she had nonetheless escalated in front of three families and a second-class coffee fetcher and a juridical theorist who had read his paper.

Sable's reading sat in the column beside, in the small careful hand she did not have but which his own hand made for her sentences when he wrote them down. *Composing the not-replying. The room she lives in alone. Two days, four rewrites, half a page stopped mid-sentence.*

He looked at both columns. He became, briefly and with no productive result, aware of how much he wanted the underline to be there already.

The pencil capped in his hand, he had not written the conclusion. He had built the page that would carry the conclusion. There was a difference, in his training, between the page and the underline at the bottom of it, and he was not yet at the underline.

He went to find Sable, who would either approve of the discipline or tease him about it. Either would be useful.

---

The stern alcove had been hers in the late afternoon for three days running, in the bronzed light he had begun to recognize as the gondola's port-side hour. She was at the window bench when he came in, with the property papers folded smaller than they had been at any previous sitting and her cup at her elbow at the angle that meant she had drunk from it recently and had not yet decided whether to drink from it again.

He took the chair at her working angle.

"How was it," she said.

"How was what."

"The four-crate morning."

"You heard?"

"The galley boy heard. The galley boy tells me things he isn't supposed to tell anyone because I asked the cook about Calden eating standing up, and the cook has decided that I am a person who hears about the second-class corridor's gossip in exchange for the information getting passed back through Calden by way of a clean corridor. There is a small economy. I am being paid in galley reports."

"She stood up."

"She stood up." Sable did not phrase it as a question.

"She did not raise her voice. She raised the body, and the room heard her."

"Yes."

"The four crates are real."

"Vellaine clocked them at six tables."

"Vellaine told me to be careful with the cleanest column." He set the slate on the small reading table face-down. "I am, for the record, being careful."

"Good."

"I have not written the conclusion."

"Good."

"I have built the page that would carry it."

"Also good."

"You're not going to push back."

"On the page-building? No. The page-building is the right shape of the next two hours. Push back at the page-building and the underline at the bottom comes faster, not slower." She turned the cup a quarter so the handle pointed away from him. "Push back at the underline when the underline is on the slate. Not before."

He sat with that. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the bench. Mottled was still settled, three days into it now; the slight ease in the deck plates was the rhythm's working rhythm, no longer a correction. Wisp had been out at the bow at first watch with the gauge and was, presumably, asleep somewhere now.

"The morning has another thing in it," he said.

"What."

"Nothing useful. The kind of thing you put into the part of the page where you don't write anything down."

"All right."

"Vannic — the *I have been thinking about him in the past tense* Vannic — stepped aside in the corridor on the boarding morning for an old man finishing the step at the gangway. I had filed it at the time as a noble's idle courtesy. It came back to me in the corridor at the early bell because I was walking past the place where I'd seen it."

"Mm."

"I have set it back under the same heading and decided it is still not on the page."

"That's fine."

She drank from the cup. She set it down. The galley boy passed the alcove arch with the tray he was now passing every afternoon at this hour, and Sable lifted a hand at him in the way that meant *not yet, thank you,* and he went on.

"He wasn't as bad as I expected," she said, half-shrug, looking at the window.

"Vannic?"

"Vannic. I don't know what I'd expected exactly. Whatever it was, he wasn't it."

"He insulted Esherel within four minutes of the gangway."

"Yes."

"He raised his voice at Calden on the first afternoon over a wardrobe latch."

"Yes."

"He's the kind of man Esherel's letter is about."

"He is."

She did not say anything else.

He held the slate's edge with his thumb on the corner where the chalk had worn the wood smoother than the rest. He was, in a part of his attention he was not at this moment giving an audit to, aware of the small still moment Sable had a way of making when she had said a thing she did not intend to expand on. Three times now over the run he had marked the moment, and was holding it the third time the way he had held it the first two, because he had no better column to put it in.

The line was, he thought, a thing she had said about a man whose body had been in cabin twelve for two days. People said small generous things about the recently dead. The room she lived in alone was Esherel's room. The room Sable lived in was, by all the evidence on the page so far, a room he had not been invited to look at and did not need to look at to do the work of the next two hours.

He stored away *Vannic wasn't as bad as I expected* under *small talk between two people who have been thinking about a man,* and turned the slate over.

"All right," he said. "Esherel."

"All right."

"I'm going to walk it for you the way I walked it for myself this morning, and you can tell me whether I've left anything out, and then I'm going to take it up to my cabin and not write the underline."

"That is the right shape of the next two hours."

"You said."

"I'm saying it again."

He walked it for her the way he had for himself, top to bottom. Motive. Opportunity. Demeanor under stress. The four-crate scene at the breakfast and the small clean way the body had risen in a voice that had not. The two windows in series, and the corridor geometry that admitted both. Against all of it, the letter as one weight on one side of a beam that had three weights on the other. He kept Sable's reading in the column it sat in. He did not collapse it. He named it as the reading he was holding against, and he named, in plain words, the three things he was holding it against.

She listened. She did not interrupt. At one point she turned the cup and set it back where it had been, which he had begun to understand was her version of his thumb on the worn corner of the slate.

When he finished, she said, "And the underline."

"At my cabin, after dinner. Or in the morning."

"In the morning."

"Probably in the morning."

"Good. The morning page is the page that has slept."

"You said that on the night we walked the corridor."

"I'll keep saying it."

He almost smiled. He did not, but the not-smiling was the kind the room got the use of.

---

He took the slate up to his cabin at the close of the bronzed hour and put it on the writing desk.

The page was the page he had built in the gallery. Esherel at the top. Tannin at the second tier. Vellaine at the third. The two windows in series. The corridor in pencil. The four-crate scene noted under *demeanor under stress* with a small clean tick. Sable's reading in the column beside, named honestly, weighed honestly, held against three other things.

He did not write the underline.

He thought about it. The pencil sat capped in his hand for the length of time it took the lamp oil to warm the way it warmed once the wick had been at trim for a few minutes. He decided that the morning was the right shape and that the morning page was the page that had slept. Then he put the pencil flat across the slate at the angle Sable put hers at, parallel to the top edge.

The cabin smelled faintly of the flame oil from his father's dispensary, the way it had on the first night. He had stopped being surprised by the smell sometime around the third evening, and found, now, that he was comfortable.

He left the slate on the desk and went to find dinner.
