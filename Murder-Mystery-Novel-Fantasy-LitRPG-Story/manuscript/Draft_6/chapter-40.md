# Chapter 40 — The Tuesday Coach

Sable's coach had gone at second bell on the Monday and Theron had walked back from the coach-house yard at the slow working pace of a man who had walked a friend to a coach and had been told to walk back alone, and the barman had a pot of tea on the table where Theron had eaten his breakfast and had not cleared the second cup, which was the courtesy of a man who had been keeping a working inn at the working pace of a working harbor for the years it took to learn that the second cup at a table was a thing one left until the table itself was cleared. Theron sat at his side of the table. He drank his tea. He left the second cup where it was, and he opened his strap, and he laid the personal notebook flat on the wood and he set the case book beside it, and he took out the pencil and he uncapped it, and he began to write the report Warden Davren had asked of him.

The report ran to Monday afternoon and into Monday evening and into Tuesday at first bell.

Davren's stipulated form was the form the Halverstow office had been using since before Theron was at the Conservatory — date and place at the top, party and party, the matter as a single declarative sentence, the procedural chain as numbered paragraphs. He numbered them. He wrote *H. Renn* at the top of paragraph one and *J. Aldis* at the top of paragraph two. He used the names. He wrote *Miss Verrin south on the Monday coach, per her own arrangement* in the place that asked for the disposition of consulting parties, and he did not write anything beside that line.

The barman cleared the second cup at last when he came past with the noon plates on the Monday. He did not say anything about clearing it. He took it and went, and Theron wrote the next paragraph.

He sealed the report at first bell on the Tuesday with the small wax pin he had carried in the strap since Halverstow. He took it across the lane to Pessel's letter-pouch, which hung on a hook by the chandler's door, and he tied the report into the pouch with the cord kept there for the purpose. Pessel was at his counter and inclined his half-inch, and Theron inclined his back.

"The afternoon coach," Pessel said. "It goes at the half of one. The boy takes the pouch over at the quarter."

"All right."

"I'll have a thing for you, if you're at the inn at midmorning."

"All right."

"It's not for the pouch."

"All right."

Theron inclined his half-inch and went back across the lane. The bell at the inside of the lintel rang once on his going out, and once on his going in at the inn door, and the barman, not looking up from the keg he was tapping, tipped his chin over the tap-arm.

---

Pessel came at the quarter past first bell.

He came himself this time, not the boy. He had the satchel under his left arm and a small leather bundle under his right, and the bundle was tied with a length of the chandler's cord, and he came to the corner of the table where he had stood the night Sable had set the date for the morning meeting, and he set his hand on the back of the empty chair and did not pull it out.

"Mr. Mavik."

"Mr. Pessel."

"This is the rest of what Joren had left with me to hold."

He set the bundle on the table. It was small — the length of a hand, the depth of a clasped fist — and tied as a chandler tied a bundle, which was twice across and once around, the knot in the middle of the upper face.

"For the family," Pessel said. "I gave Miss Verrin the letter on the Sunday and the address on the Sunday, and these were the rest. Miss Verrin is south and the family is north. I am not in the habit of forwarding a man's papers to his sister sight unseen by a person who knows the work the papers were done in. I came to you for the reading. I will take it back if you have it read by midday, and I will put it in the next pouch north myself."

"That'll do."

"It's mostly nothing. A book he had been reading. Two receipts for a coach he had paid for himself for a previous run. The letter I held you have already heard of. There is one slip I did not place, which is the slip I am bringing you for. You will see it. I would not have known the slip from a chandler's receipt if I had not been holding a man's papers for a year, and I have been."

"All right."

Pessel did not sit. He did not appear to want to sit. He set his hand on the back of the chair and he waited the count it took Theron to undo the chandler's cord, and the cord came undone with the small dry sound a chandler's cord came undone with on a working morning, and the bundle opened on the table.

Theron laid the contents in their working order.

The clothbound book first, set at his left. The two coach receipts in a fold beside it. The held letter from Joren to the family — a single folded sheet, the outer face inked in Joren's hand with the family name and the parish — set at the center. He did not open the letter. The letter was Sable's to give and was not his to read. He set it back into the bundle's wrap with the book and the receipts on either side of it.

And the slip.

It was a single fold, smaller than the receipts and smaller than the letter, the size of a calling card opened once. The paper was a clerk's paper, not a chandler's, and the fold was a clerk's fold. Inside, the lines were two — a fee in a number that meant nothing to Theron without the unit at its head, and a delivery clause for a thing that was not named, only signed for with an initial and a date. The date was the third of July of the same year. The Wyrm run had been the last week of September. As for the initial, it was not Joren's. It was not Halsa's. It was a single capital letter that Theron read once and did not recognize and read a second time and did not recognize the second time either.

At the upper corner of the slip, stamped not inked, was a small device.

The device was a rondel of three lines folded inward toward a center, not quite a knot, not quite a wheel. It was the device he had seen on a sheet at Davren's table on the Friday afternoon of the previous week, in among Halsa's papers, in the working pile, and he had registered it without asking after it because the cabin had been the cabin and the case had been the case and the device had been a separate question.

The device on the slip on the table was the same device.

Theron did not move.

He sat at the table with his palm on the wood beside the slip and he read the slip a second time and a third and the device a fourth, and the room around him was the front room of the inn at the quarter past first bell on a working Tuesday in October, and the barman was at the keg, and the older woman with the basket was at the table by the door, and the dog at her foot had the chin on the wood it had had on the Sunday morning, and Pessel was at the back of the chair and was waiting.

"Mr. Pessel."

"Mr. Mavik."

"Did Joren say anything to you about this slip."

"He did not."

"Did he ask you to hold it separately."

"He did not. It was in the bundle. He gave me the bundle in May. He said the bundle was for the family if he did not come south himself by the year's end. He did not separate any of it."

"Did anyone come asking after the bundle since."

"No."

"No one."

"No one."

"All right."

Theron looked at the slip a moment longer.

He did not pick it up. He let it sit on the table beside the wrapped bundle.

*[Investigator — note: the corner of the slip.]*

He read the line once. He did not nod for it. He set it down inside his head where he had set the line on the geography in the front room on the Sunday and the line on the device at Davren's table on the Friday, which was a shelf that had begun to fill up in the past week and was, he thought, the kind of shelf that would need its own page in a different book one day, and he was not going to begin that page on a Tuesday morning at a chandler's letter-pouch with a coach to catch at the half of one.

He took the slip off the table.

Instead of returning it to the bundle, he opened the personal notebook to the back cover, where the cover-fold made a small pocket against the spine, and he slid the slip into the pocket. He closed the notebook and set his hand flat on the cover.

"The rest is for Miss Verrin to take north," he said. "I'd ask you to hold the bundle until she sends word."

"I'll hold it."

"She'll write you from the cottage."

"She said so."

"All right."

"And the slip?"

"The slip stays with me."

Pessel looked at him a count. Then he inclined his half-inch.

"All right."

He took the wrapped bundle off the table — the book and the receipts and the letter, tied again in his hand in the chandler's twice across and once around, the knot at the middle of the upper face — and he set his hand on the back of the chair the half-second a man set his hand on the back of a chair when he had finished what he had come for, and he went.

The bell at the door rang once on the going out.

---

The coach office was across the lane and three doors up from the chandlery, between the oil-merchant's and a small shop that sold bone buttons. The clerk was a thin man at a high desk with a ledger open in front of him and a ruled board of departures hung on the inner wall behind him, the names of the inland stops written in chalk and the times of the coaches written in chalk beside them, and the names of the coastal stops written above. The chalk for Mirren Vale was clean. The time beside it was *one and a half.*

"Mirren Vale," Theron said.

"Inland coach. The half of one."

"That."

"Outside or inside."

"Outside."

The clerk looked at him over the high desk. He was, Theron read, a clerk who had been told *outside* by enough men who had then complained about the wind that he had a clerk's small dry pause for it.

"It's cold on the box at this hour."

"All right."

"Two days to the Vale. There's an overnight at the half-mark at Hessler's Crossing. The Vale's the spring-houses. You'll want the one on the upper terrace. The lower's all coach traffic."

"The upper."

"I'll mark it in the chit," he said, and marked it. Theron paid the fare. The clerk took the coin, gave him the chit, and inclined his half-inch over the high desk, and Theron took the chit and inclined his back, and the bell at the coach-office door rang once on his going out.

He went back to the inn.

The barman had bread on the bar and the cold midday cider in the chipped mug he had been using for Theron's afternoon cider since the Sunday, which Theron read as the barman's settled decision about which mug was Theron's mug, and Theron took the mug to the table he had drafted the report at and drank the first swallow without tasting it and the second more slowly.

The strap was on the bench beside him.

The personal notebook was in the strap.

The slip with the device on the corner was in the back pocket of the notebook, in the small fold against the spine.

He drank the cider and ate the bread. The older woman with the basket finished her tea and tipped her chin to the barman and went, the dog at her heel. The dog looked back at the barman at the door. The barman did not look up from the lamp, whose wick was being trimmed.

At the half-past noon he buckled the strap.

He inclined his half-inch to the barman without thinking about inclining it, and the barman inclined his back without looking up from the wick, and Theron went out into the lane.

---

The coach was at the office door at the quarter past, and the driver counted heads at twenty past, and at the half he looked at the coach clock above the office door and clucked once and shook the reins.

Theron was on the outside bench.

He had his strap across his knees. The strap was warm in the cold of the early afternoon, the leather having taken the warm of his hand at the inn, and the bench under him was the cold of a bench that had stood out in the lane since first bell.

The driver's elbow found the rest on the door rail with the small settled motion of a man whose elbow had found the rest on the door rail at the half of one on a Tuesday for enough Tuesdays that the rest knew the elbow. The coach moved, and the lane began to slide back at the slow working pace of a coach starting on a cold cobble. The wheels found their rhythm at the second turn of the lane and held it.

The painted board over the chandlery went past on the right at the slow pace.

It said *Pessel & Son — Chandlers & Ropework* in white over older white, and the older white was the older white it had been on the Saturday Sable had walked Theron down the lane to Pessel's door, and the new white was the new white it had been then, and the board went past the coach at the pace of a coach turning at the foot of a harbor lane and was behind him.

The harbor bell at the customs slip struck once for the hour of the afternoon.

Theron sat with the strap across his knees and the slip in the back fold of the notebook in the strap, and the inland road came up at the bend at the top of the lane, and the driver's elbow stayed on the rest at the door rail, and the coach took the bend at the slow working pace of a coach taking the first bend of an inland run, and the southern light was on the cobbles ahead and on the back of the driver's coat and on the leather of the strap warm under Theron's hand.

He moved his thumb off the strap, and left it there.
