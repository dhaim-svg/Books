# Chapter 38 — The Case Settles

The front room of the inn took its evening occupants the way an inn took its evening occupants in a working harbor, which was without ceremony.

Two men in oil-merchant aprons sat at the table by the door with a jug between them and were not speaking and did not appear to require speech. A woman whose name Theron did not know was at the till with a ledger laid open and a small pair of brass scales, and the scales were the kind that weighed coin rather than goods, and she was running the day's take. The barman had taken off the apron and put on a clean one and was wiping the same glass with a different dishcloth, white in the manner of a cloth lately come off a line and not yet put to a day's work. The light in the room was the lamp light of an inn that had decided the southern afternoon had gone enough.

Theron and Sable had come in from the back porch through the bar door and had taken a table at the inside wall, not the window, because the porch was the porch and the front room was a different room.

The barman brought bread.

He brought it without being asked, on a wooden board, with a knife laid across it and a small dish of salt to the side of the board, and he set the board between them and inclined his half-inch.

"Stew's done for the day," he said. "There's a pie in the oven."

"Pie," Sable said.

"Two."

"Two."

He gave the same small nod and went.

Sable did not tear the bread this time. She left it on the board between them and put both hands in her lap. Her notebook was on the table at her left, the pencil through the spine. Theron's strap was on the bench beside him with the personal notebook inside it. The case book had gone to Ottren at the warehouse. What was in the strap now was the run in his own hand, week by week, which was the lighter book.

The door from the lane opened.

A man came in who was not one of the oil-merchant pair and was not a working local in the manner the oil merchants were. He was a small spare man in a coat that had been good once and was still respectable, with a leather satchel under his left arm of the kind clerks carried, and he stopped just inside the door and looked across the room until he found their table.

"Mr. Mavik. Miss Verrin."

"Mr. Pessel."

"Mr. Pessel."

He did not come to the table. He came to the corner of it, which was the courtesy a man used who had a thing to say and not a meal to take, and he set his hand on the back of the empty chair and did not pull it out.

"The rooms are settled with the house. I spoke to him at the door."

"Thank you."

"It's the two upstairs, the back ones. They look on the lane, not the harbor. The harbor rooms were taken before the gondola was down."

"That'll do."

"It will."

He looked at Sable.

"Miss Verrin."

"Mr. Pessel. The address."

He had been waiting for it. Theron read it in his shoulders before he read it in his face — the small settling of a man who had been carrying a thing in his pocket since the morning and was glad to set it on the table.

"I have it. I have had it since the twenty-eighth. It's in the inside flap of the book on my desk in the back of the chandlery."

"I'll come by in the morning."

"Come early. I open the chandlery at first bell and there's a man from the rigging house who comes at quarter past, and after him there's a woman who has a question about a length of cordage I have not been able to satisfy in four visits, and after her the morning is gone."

"First bell."

"First bell."

He looked at her a moment longer, and he did not look at Theron. The look was the look of a man who had been holding a piece of paper for a man and was being asked, now, to give the piece of paper to the woman the man had not lived to give it to himself, and the look did not require any naming.

"Mr. Mavik."

"Mr. Pessel."

"The morning, then."

"The morning."

He inclined his half-inch and went out the way he had come in, and the door closed, and the woman at the till did not look up from her ledger and the oil merchants did not look up from their jug, and the barman set down the white cloth and took up a different one without changing the rhythm of his wiping.

Sable put her hands on the table.

She did not say anything. She picked up the knife from the bread board and cut the loaf in halves, an even courtesy, one half on her side and one half on his, and she set the knife back down with the blade pointing in toward the center of the board. Theron tore a piece off his half.

The pie came after a while.

It was a pie of the kind a coastal kitchen made at the end of a day with whatever the day had not used — fish, potato, a little of the green that grew at the back of the kitchen garden — and it was hot and the crust was good and they ate without making a thing of it. Sable poured water from the jug on the table into both cups and drank her cup down and poured it again. Theron drank his without noticing he had drunk it.

"Tomorrow," she said.

"Tomorrow."

"After Pessel I'll need a day or two to think on it. I don't want to write the letter and post it and have it arrive ahead of me. I want to be there."

"You said."

"I'm saying it again."

"All right."

She took a piece of bread and used it to clean the side of her plate and put it in her mouth and chewed it and swallowed it.

"You don't have to come to Pessel's with me."

"I know."

"I didn't say I didn't want you to. I said you don't have to."

"I'll walk you over. I won't go in."

"Mm."

That was the whole of it.

---

She went up before he did.

She took her notebook in her left hand with the pencil through the spine and the cup of water in her right and she inclined her half-inch to the barman at the bar, who had stopped wiping glasses at some point and was reading a folded sheet by the lamp, and she went up the inn stairs at the pace of a person who had eaten and was tired and was not making a performance of either.

Theron sat at the table with the empty plates.

The barman came and took the plates and the board and left the salt and the water jug, and Theron asked for a cup of the cider that had been hot in the afternoon and was now whatever it was, and the barman brought it in the same chipped mug, which Theron read as the barman's small particular courtesy of returning the cup that was his.

He set the strap on the table. The buckle came undone under his thumb, and he took out the personal notebook and laid it square in front of him with the spine at his left. He took the pencil from where it had been tucked in the strap-fold and rolled it once on the table to settle it under his fingers.

The notebook was open to where he had last written, which was at Pessel's table on the twenty-eighth. There were seven working pages between the twenty-eighth and the morning at Ottren's warehouse — interviews, the reading at Davren's table, the long Friday at the Arms, the writing-desk pages in cabin six. He turned through them once, not reading them, the way a man turned the pages of a book he had written to remind his hand where it had been. He came to the first blank.

He wrote the date at the top of the page in the small hand he used for closing entries.

> *Closing — Sannholt, the seventeenth of November.*
>
> *H. Renn delivered to Master Ottren, Sannholt magistrate's office, second warehouse from the slip, by Mr. Davren's order of yesterday. Order countersigned. The working case book is with Ottren. The matter is with the Sannholt office. The quest closed at the door of the warehouse.*

He stopped.

His eye went to the line he had written and to the white space below it. He had written the procedure. He had not written the case. The procedure was the easy part to write and the case was the part he had been carrying since cabin twelve, and the white space sat under his pencil and asked the question whether he was going to write any of it down.

He did not write it down. Instead he wrote one more line.

> *J. Aldis. Coastal. The work was not for himself.*

Then he closed the notebook.

For a moment his hand stayed on the cover. He was aware, in the way a man was aware of a thing he had set on a shelf and had not yet taken back down, of the other thing — the sheet with the small mark on it that had been in Halsa's papers at Davren's table, the device he had not recognized and had not asked her about, because the cabin had been the cabin and the case had been the case and the device was a separate question and Davren had taken the papers. He did not name the thing in the notebook, or to himself in any sentence longer than the registering of it. He left it where it was, which was on the shelf, which was a place a thing could be while a man finished his cider in a working inn and went up to a room on the second floor.

He drank the cider down and buckled the strap.

He inclined his half-inch to the barman without thinking about inclining it, which was a thing he had begun doing in the last two days of the week and had not noticed himself begin doing, and the barman inclined his back and did not look up from the folded sheet by the lamp.

Theron went up.

---

The morning came in at the back window of his room as the southern morning came into a back window on a lane, which was sideways, off the wall of the chandlery opposite, with the color of an early sky reflected in the whitewash. He had slept in a way he had not slept on the gondola, which was without listening for the deck. He had woken once in the middle of the night and had not known where he was and had then known and had gone back to sleep, and that was the whole of the night's account.

He went down.

Sable was at the front-room table already. She had her traveling coat on and her notebook beside her cup and the pencil through the spine. Her right hand was around the cup and her left flat on the table. She had not buttoned the coat. The barman had brought bread and a small pot of preserve and a pot of tea, and she had poured a cup for the place across from her where Theron had not yet been sitting.

"Morning," she said.

"Morning."

He sat and picked up the cup. The tea was hot. He drank the first swallow without tasting it and the second more slowly and set the cup down.

"First bell soon," she said.

"Soon."

"I'll go over after this."

"All right."

She put preserve on a piece of bread with the back of the spoon and ate it. She did not eat fast. She drank her tea. He drank his. The oil merchants from the night before were not in the front room this morning; in their place at the door table was an older woman with a basket on her lap and a small dog at her foot, and the dog was watching the barman with the considered attention of a dog that had been given a piece of bread on a previous morning and had not forgotten the precedent.

"Walk with me as far as the door," Sable said.

"As far as the door."

"That's what I'm asking."

"That's what I said."

She finished her tea, then tore a small piece of bread and ate it without preserve. She set her cup down the half-inch back from the rim of the table that meant the cup was done. Her right hand went into her coat pocket, briefly, and two fingers rested against the inside lining of the inside pocket where the carved fish had been since the dock. She took her hand back out and picked up her notebook.

"Ready," she said.

"Ready."

They went out.

The lane in front of the inn was a working lane at first bell with two carters loading at a yard three doors up and a boy with a bucket sluicing a step further down and the early gulls already at the harbor. The southern morning light was lower and harder than the afternoon's had been, and it laid itself across the cobbles in long stripes broken by the shadows of the buildings opposite, and the smell of the harbor was the smell of a harbor at first bell, which was cold water and rope and a little of yesterday's fish. On the inn step, where the cat from the day before usually sat, there was no cat this morning. The step was wet from the boy's bucket and the cat had taken itself elsewhere.

Sable turned left along the lane. Theron walked on her left, the side he had walked on at Davren's table and at the foot of the gangway and at the rail of the pier and at the porch table the afternoon before, and the placement was the placement it had been. Pessel's chandlery was four doors down on the harbor side, and the painted board outside its door said *Pessel & Son — Chandlers & Ropework* in a hand that had been done in white over an older hand that had said the same thing in a different white some years ago.

They stopped at the door.

Sable did not put her hand on the latch. She did not look at the board. She looked at the harbor at the end of the lane — the customs boat at its tie, the gull on the pile, the gondola at the visitor slip with its two drakes on their tethered rest — the way a person looked at a view she had decided to take a count of before she did the next thing she had come to do.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

"I'll find you at the inn after."

"You'll find me at the inn after."

She put her hand on the latch and lifted it. The door went open under her hand and the small bell at the inside of the lintel rang one note, which was the note a chandler's bell rang at first bell on a working morning in a working harbor, and Pessel was at the back of the shop already with a ledger on the counter and his hand on a pen, and he looked up and saw her and inclined his half-inch.

Sable went in.

The door closed behind her with the small definite sound a door closed with on a working morning, and the bell rang the second note, which was the note it rang on the closing, and the lane was a lane again with the carters at their yard and the boy at his bucket and the early gulls at the harbor.

Theron stood in the lane a moment.

He looked at the board with the white-over-white and at the gull on the pile and at the visitor slip and at the harbor-side inn three doors back, and he turned and walked back at the slow pace of a person who had walked a friend to a door and had been told to walk back alone, and the southern morning light went sideways across the cobbles ahead of him, and the day had work in it that was not his to do for the next part of the morning.

He inclined his half-inch to the barman as he came back through the inn door, without thinking about it, and the barman inclined his back.
