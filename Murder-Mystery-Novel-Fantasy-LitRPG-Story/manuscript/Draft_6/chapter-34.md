# Chapter 34 — The Door of Cabin Six

The cabin was small and well-considered. Two portholes, a writing desk with a worn drawer-pull, a chair, a padded bench under the porthole. The lamp bracket had been angled by someone who had thought about reading in bed. The wardrobe was honest about itself. The hum through the deck was the Wyrm's hum at a lower note, because there were two drakes pulling and not three.

Theron sat on the bench with the case book on the desk and did not look at it.

He thought briefly about the page that was not in the book.

The requirements page was in Davren's case-room. He had agreed to that in the office on Friday and the agreement had been the right shape then and was the right shape now. The page was the page; the page named her at its foot; the page would be read to her, in time, by the magistrate at his table in Sannholt, in the room a magistrate built for that work. It was not for a cabin on a gondola.

What he had on the desk was the abstract, the scry log, the transcripts, the manifest with its forty flat lines and its one unmarked row. The public-facing record. What he had on the desk was what he had said he would carry.

He stood up.

He put on the coat he had hung on the hook by the door. He picked the leather strap off the desk and slid the case book into it and tied the strap, not because the strap needed tying — the cabin was three doors from the cabin he was going to — but because the act of tying it was the act of saying *now*, and he had used the same small ritual every morning of the week.

He left the cabin.

The corridor was empty. The wet-slate green of the doors matched the lacquer of the hull, which was a choice somebody had made about how this gondola was supposed to feel, and the choice was a different choice than the Wyrm's bone-colored doors had been, and he could not, this morning, say which choice he liked better. He passed cabin three, then cabin four. Sable's door opened as he came up to it, which meant she had been at the door waiting, which meant she had set herself to come out when she heard him.

"Ready," she said.

"Ready."

She came out and closed the door behind her without locking it, because the corridor was the corridor of a gondola under a magistral writ and the only people on it were the people who had been named on the order. They walked together. He carried the strap. She carried her notebook closed in her left hand, the pencil through the spine the way she carried it when she had decided she would not be writing.

They came to cabin six.

He knocked.

He knocked once and waited the count he had decided in his cabin he would wait, which was three, and then he knocked again, because the second knock was a courtesy the first had not been. He heard, beyond the door, a small specific movement — a chair, set carefully back from a table — and then the door opened.

Halsa Renn was in her traveling coat with the buttons done up. Her hair was pinned. She was holding the door at the latch with her right hand and she had her left hand in the pocket of her coat. She looked at Theron for the length of a breath, and at Sable for the length of another, and at the strap under Theron's arm for the length of a third. She did not speak first.

"Miss Renn," he said.

"Mr. Mavik."

She had the small steady voice of someone who had been awake since before the morning bell. The voice did not waver and did not perform not-wavering, which were different things.

"I'm here under the magistral order you were given last night."

"I know what you're here under."

"I'd like to lay out what's in the case book to you, if you'll sit."

She looked at him, then at Sable. She stepped back from the door.

"Come in," she said.

Her cabin was the cabin of someone who had unpacked the way a person unpacked who meant to be in a room for one night. The bag was on the floor by the wardrobe with the buckle done up. The coat she was not wearing was on the hook. There was no book on the writing desk. There was a cup on the desk and a small folded sheet beside the cup, sealed, the seal not broken. The wax was Davren's. The sheet was the note from sixth bell yesterday.

She had not opened it.

Theron noted the un-opened note in the part of his head that kept such things and did not, for the moment, do anything with it. He set the leather strap on the desk beside the cup. He pulled the chair out from the desk and turned it ninety degrees so that it faced the porthole bench and not the desk, and sat. The case book he laid on the desk, one hand flat on it without opening it.

"Sable will sit by the wall, if that's all right with you."

"That's all right."

Sable went to the wall and took the small chair that was there for taking off boots and sat in it with the notebook on her knee. The notebook stayed closed. Halsa watched her sit. Then Halsa went to the porthole bench and sat in the center of it with her hands folded in her lap, the left hand still in the pocket and the right one over the wrist of the left, the way a person sat who meant to listen.

He opened the case book.

He did not open it to the abstract first. He had decided in his cabin to open it to the manifest, because the manifest was the public-facing shape of what he had come to lay out, and the abstract could come after. The abstract was a paragraph. The manifest was forty-one rows.

He turned the book around so that the manifest faced her.

"This is the Wyrm manifest," he said. "It's a copy from the magistrate's office at Halverstow. Three columns: cabin, name, place of disembarkation. Forty-one entries. You'll find your row at the head of the third-class block."

She looked at it. She did not lean forward, and did not need to; the desk was small and she could read it from the bench. She read it, found her row, then the four other rows in the third-class block. Up through second class and through first class she went. The cabin-one row was the Mirren factor she had heard about by name in the dining room on the Wyrm and had not, then, given a face to. The cabin-eight row was Esherel. The cabin-three row was Vellaine.

"I'm reading it," she said.

"Take what you need."

She read it.

When she had read it she looked up at him without lifting her chin.

"All right," she said.

"In the case book," he said, "behind the manifest, there's an abstract of the working theory of the method on Joren's killing. I'll walk you through what's in it. I'll stop when you'd like me to stop. If you'd rather read it for yourself I'll turn the page and let you read."

"You walk me through it."

"All right."

He did not turn to the abstract. He had it by heart. He laid his hand flat on the closed face of the case book the way he had laid it flat on the requirements page at Davren's table the morning before, and he began, in the unraised voice he had used at Davren's table, with the booking.

"The booking," he said. "You came onto the Wyrm passenger list through the Kolach channel. The Kolach channel is the booking route the magistrate's office reads as the channel that places travelers who are not placing themselves through the open exchanges. The booking was personally referred eleven years ago by Joren Aldis."

"Yes."

"You worked at the Copper Finch in Sannholt for nine years. Quiet rooms. Cash. No professional reference logged on the Wyrm manifest. Picked up the work; didn't write it down."

"Yes."

"Joren Aldis took you as his apprentice on a coastal placement when you were eighteen. The placement was a Vantrel routing through the Pessel apprentice-line. You worked with him for eleven years."

"Eleven years."

"Pessel's letter dated Tuesday confirms the eleven-year apprenticeship and the working hours. Pessel's letter dated this Thursday confirms a further line about the master side of the work. I have the letters in the book. I'll read you the line, if you'd like."

"Read it."

He turned to the letter. He had set Pessel's two letters in the book in the order they had arrived, with a strip of tied ribbon between them, which was his own small ritual of order and was not in any examination's instruction sheet. He found Thursday's letter. He found the line.

"*The seven years past sufficiency were a deliberate teaching. He kept her because there was a thing he meant to show her that he showed nobody else. He called it the late-stage work, when he called it anything. Quiet method, mostly. He did not write it down.*"

He set the letter back down without looking up, and turned to the next page.

"The method on the cabin," he said. "A sustained scryproof glamour, held through the seating phase. The seating phase is not in any standard text on illusion. It is the point in an apprenticeship where the apprentice's signature becomes durable against the master's. After the seating phase the working between two illusionists is signature-compatible in a way that nobody else is. The signature compatibility is the apprenticeship's working product, not its credential."

"That's the credential nobody writes down," she said.

"Yes."

"Go on."

"The trigger architecture for a sustained scryproof glamour, held to a seating-compatible signature, is a piece of late-stage practical theory that, as far as my year and the year behind me at the Conservatory knew, had been written down in one paper, written down badly, and dismissed at examination as exam fodder. It has no commercial application. It has no defensive application. It has, on the face of it, no application at all. It does have the application of doing exactly what was done in cabin twelve on the Wyrm."

She nodded once.

"In a closed cabin," he said. "Through a sealed bolt and a sealed porthole and a sealed deck hatch. Without the caster needing to be near the target at the moment of activation. With the trigger known to the caster alone."

"Without leaving a scry log."

"Without leaving a scry log."

He turned the page.

"The manifest," he said. "I went through it on Thursday morning with Sable. Cabin by cabin. The requirements on the method are six structural requirements; I wrote them out on a page that is not in this book. The page is in Davren's case-room at Halverstow. The page is going to be read to you in Sannholt, on a table that is not this one. I am not going to read it to you here."

"All right."

"The manifest has forty-one entries. Forty fail at one or another of the six lines on the page that is not in this book. Some fail on the first line. Some fail on the second and the fourth. Three or four pass the first line and fail on the second. One row passes all six lines."

"That row is mine."

"That row is yours."

She did not say *yes*, and did not need to. She had said the sentence first, which was a courtesy of a particular kind, and he heard the courtesy without making anything of it.

He closed the case book.

He did not push it across the desk. He left it where it was, square between them on the small writing desk of cabin six on the southbound morning service of the seventeenth of November, with the manifest closed inside it and the abstract closed inside it and Pessel's two letters tied in their ribbon inside it. He sat with his hands flat on the closed face of the book.

"That's what's in the book," he said.

"That's what's in the book."

"That's what I came in to lay out."

"You've laid it out."

He waited. He had decided in his cabin that the waiting would not be a tactic. The decision had also been that he would put the work down on the desk and would not, after he had put it down, ask her a question. In the slow count he had done with his hands flat on the case book at sixth bell yesterday, he had decided that the work would speak its own sentence and that he would not stand over the work in a way that asked anything of her except what the work itself asked.

He waited.

Halsa was looking at the closed case book.

She had not, in the time he had been speaking, taken her left hand out of the pocket of her coat. She took it out now. She set it on her right wrist where the right hand had been before. She looked at the case book a moment longer. Then she looked up at him.

"He taught me the seating phase in the back room of a working-house in Sannholt that doesn't stand any more," she said. "It was a Tuesday. He had not slept. He had come back from a job he did not name, and he had walked me through the architecture twice on the chalk-wall and a third time on a folded scry-sheet, and at the end of it he had said: *don't write this down*. I asked him why. He said because the only person it was useful against was him."

Theron did not speak.

"I have been alive," she said, "with that sentence in my head, for seven years."

She looked at the case book.

"You have it right," she said. "What's in the book. You have it right."

"I'm not asking you to say it."

"I know you're not asking me to say it."

"I'm laying it out so that you've seen it laid out."

"I've seen it."

She did not look away from the case book.

"I'm not going to dispute the page," she said. "I'm not going to dispute the manifest. I'm not going to dispute the Pessel letter. I'm not going to dispute the Kolach channel. I've had a night to think about what would be a dispute and what would not, and a dispute is not what I have."

"All right."

"All right."

She let the right hand come off the wrist. She set both hands flat on her knees, palms down, the way a person set hands flat on knees who had decided to sit through what was coming and not move.

He registered that posture, and did not, in the registering, say anything.

He read the System line through once and gave it the small dry acknowledgment he had given the System every morning of the week. The System had read the room with him. It had not, this morning, used a word he was not willing to use himself.

Sable, at the wall, had not moved.

Halsa looked up from the case book and looked at her for the second time since the door had opened.

"Miss Verrin," she said.

"Miss Renn."

"You're named on the order."

"I am."

"In your own capacity."

"In my own capacity."

"All right." She looked back at Theron. "I have something I'd like to ask you," she said. "Before we go on with whatever's after this."

"Ask."

She did not ask. She held the question for a moment, the way a person held a question that she had been carrying since a Friday evening when a magistrate's clerk had handed her a sealed note at her boarding-house door, and the small folded sheet was still on the desk beside the cup with Davren's wax intact on it, and her left hand had been in her coat pocket the whole of the laying-out, and the case book on the desk was closed.

"Not yet," she said.

"All right."

"I'll ask it when I'm ready to."

"All right."

He sat back in the chair. He let his hands come off the case book and rest on his knees the way her hands had come to rest on hers. The cabin was small and the hum through the deck was the hum of two drakes finding their working thermal, and there was a great deal of the morning left in the air ahead of them and the gondola was a third the size of the Wyrm and the corridor outside the door was empty.

The case book sat closed on the desk between them.

Halsa Renn was not denying it.

She had not denied it.

She had said she would not dispute the page, and she had not disputed the page, and she had said she would not dispute the manifest, and she had not disputed the manifest, and she had said she had a question to ask and would ask it when she was ready, and he was sitting on the other side of the desk and waiting for her to be ready.

He did not ask her to be ready.

He let the cabin be quiet for a slow count he did not count. Sable at the wall was quiet. Halsa on the bench was quiet. The hum through the deck went on under everything, and the cup on the desk sat where it had sat, and the sealed sheet beside it had not been opened, and the case book was closed.

He had laid it out.

The page had been laid out.

The room had heard it.

The room was now Halsa's room to make what shape she would make of it, and he was here to wait until she made it, and he did not, this morning, have anywhere else to be.
