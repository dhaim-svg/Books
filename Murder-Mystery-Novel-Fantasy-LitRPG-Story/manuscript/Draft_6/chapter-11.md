# Chapter 11 — Three Drakes in Formation

He was on the starboard promenade at the open of late morning because the page in his pocket had begun to want air, which was not a thing the page knew how to want but was, in his experience, a thing he knew how to translate for it.

Wisp was at the bow end of the railing with the gauge.

"She's stopped," he said, by way of arriving.

"Three days." Wisp did not look up. "I want to enjoy it for at least two more before I tell her."

"You're not telling her."

"I told her this morning. She is choosing not to register it. She is showing me by holding the downbeat at the count for a third day that she has decided I never said anything to her about rushing it. We are in a small period of mutual professional fiction."

"And Petal."

"Petal," Wisp said, with the small pleased authority of someone reporting on a favorite student, "has a new line on the upswing. Watch her hand the slot to Gravel on the return. The slot is two breaths wider than it needs to be. She is teaching him to take a wider corner."

Theron watched. The three of them were off the starboard bow, drawn forward against the rigging, the slot Wisp had named opening and closing between Petal's wing tip and Gravel's shoulder on the bank. Wider than was needed. By two breaths, if you were counting breaths.

"Does he know."

"He is choosing not to register it. He is showing her that he is taking a wider corner because he has decided to. We are in a small period of mutual professional fiction at the harness end of the chain also."

"Two fictions."

"Three." Wisp made a small notation. "The third is between me and the long bolt on the port runner about whether it loosened a quarter turn since yesterday's trim. The bolt has decided it did not. I have decided to check at second watch."

Theron stood at the brass rail with his hand on it and felt the slipstream slip the cold across his knuckles. The chain ran ten feet from his head in its long curving line out to the boom, the harness's note carrying back along it the way a struck wire carried back along a length — the same constant low hum he had felt through every wall and bench on the gondola since boarding.

Underneath the main note, today, there was another one.

It was, he thought, a faintly off note. A fraction below the main. A thin secondary hum, carried along the chain.

He did not say so.

"You're listening for something," Wisp said.

"I'm listening to it."

"All right." Wisp made another notation.

The slot opened in the formation again, the same wider two breaths. Petal banked the inside and let Gravel come through it with the air she had freed for him. Gravel took the air. He did not acknowledge.

"Petal's a professional," Theron said.

"She is. Mottled has a theory I side with her. That is the opposite of the truth. I am siding with Gravel. He is not getting it; I am giving him time." Wisp capped the gauge. "Long bolt at second watch. I'll be at the boom if you want to bring the slate by."

He had not said anything about the slate. He had it in his coat pocket. He decided not to ask how Wisp had decided he had it, and went back along the promenade with the chain's two notes still in his ear.

---

The dining room at the open of the lunch service was warmer than the corridor and quieter than it had been at breakfast, which was its usual order for the second meal — the morning crowd thinned out, the afternoon crowd not yet up from their reading. Vellaine was at her window table with the book and the cold coffee. The Henners were at the far end with their daughter, who was eating a soft-boiled egg with the careful attention of a child who had been told it was a treat. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the deck plates.

Esherel was at her table with the folio closed.

He noticed it as he came in. The folio had been open at every meal since the first morning. It was closed now, set squarely at her right hand with one finger flat on its cover, the way she set things down when she had finished thinking about them for the present session. Her coffee was at her left. Her plate had bread on it that she had not yet broken.

He took the chair across from Vellaine, who did not look up.

"She's closed it," he said, quietly.

"She closed it on her way in." Vellaine turned a page. "I would not be at peace about that, in your position."

He had not asked her to be at peace about it. He did not say so. He poured himself a cup of the lunch pot, a different blend from the breakfast, less bright, more useful, and reached for one of the small breads at the center of the table.

Esherel turned a quarter on her chair.

Not the whole way. The right shoulder. The face followed; the chair did not. She was looking at him across six feet of floor with the careful neutral attention of a merchant taking a new shipment, and the room — Vellaine's page-turn paused, the Henners' fork stopped above the egg, the silence at the urn where the second-class passenger up for the second pot had stopped pouring — adjusted around her in the quiet recalibration he had felt the room do twice now on this run.

"Investigator," she said.

His name was nowhere in the sentence. He set the bread down.

"Madam Esherel."

"I would like to ask you a question in front of the room, because I would like the room to have a record of the answer." Her voice was not raised. She had not stood. The chair had not moved. The hand on the folio had not moved. "Your case slate is out at every meal. Your line of sight at my table is a degree longer than at the Henners' or Dr. Vellaine's. The galley boy mentioned to the cook on the second morning that you took the seat at this table that has the longest sight onto my table, and the cook mentioned it to me as a courtesy. I would like to know whether you have a question you would like to put to me in the open, before the gondola lands, in the presence of witnesses. I would prefer to answer it here than in a port magistrate's office where the witnesses are paid for the writing."

The room held still.

He had a moment, in the back of his teeth, where the entire structure of the case-page he had built in the gallery — motive, two windows, demeanor under stress, the careful column, the parallel pencil — rose up and offered itself to him as a sentence he could speak. He could ask her now, in this room, about the early window. He could ask her about the late one. He could ask her about the letter in the folio she had just set her hand on. He could underline *opportunity* in front of the Henners and the second-class coffee fetcher and Dr. Vellaine of the Larent Institute.

He did not.

He took a breath, the brief clean breath he had been taught to take in his oral defense when the question had been one he intended to answer, and not the question he had been preparing the answer to.

"I am working the page, Madam Esherel," he said. "I have not finished working it. When I have finished, I will speak to you. I would rather speak to you with the page finished than half-finished in front of the room."

She held the look a beat.

"That is a fair answer."

"Thank you."

"I do not require the room to record an answer I find fair." She turned a quarter back. The chair had still not moved. "Continue your lunch."

The Henners' fork resumed. The second-class passenger at the urn resumed his pour. Vellaine turned the page she had paused on. Theron broke the bread.

He drank half a cup of coffee that he could not taste, and waited for his pulse to return to the count he was usually carrying it at in a dining room. It took a count and a half.

"Hm," Vellaine said, without looking up. "She closed the folio because she knew she was going to do that."

"She knew at breakfast?"

"She knew at the third bell, I would guess. The galley boy is paid in pleasantness, not silence. The cook is paid in silence, occasionally."

"And you."

"I am paid for symposia, Investigator. I am here because the route was scenic."

He looked at her. She did not look back. The corner of her mouth had moved, the way it had moved on the first morning when he had read the wine substitute correctly.

He sat with the quiet sensation of a category landing on the page, the second one in three days.

*Demeanor under stress, escalating.*

He filed it. He drank the second half of the coffee. It was, on second pass, very good.

---

The captain's gallery in the early afternoon had the slow pleasure of the gondola's bow turning slightly into the long approach, the drakes still in their formation against a sky that had thinned to the pale clean blue of an afternoon that intended to behave. He took the bench at the end that was not the brass and put the slate flat on his knee.

He worked the page.

He drew Esherel's column with a finer hand than yesterday. The corridor's geometry he drew from the long memory of having walked it now twenty times. He drew the early window and the late window on a single line at the top of the slate and the corridor under it, and he drew, in the margin under cabin twelve in three small lines, the harness-chain's secondary note as a possibility he had not yet decided how to use.

*Carry-frame,* he wrote in the margin. He capped the pencil. He thought about it.

The textbook had been mostly interested in the carry-frame as a problem for long-distance courier glamours. The example had been *the unattended sustained illusion field*. He did not yet know what to do with that example. The sealed cabin had no wound, no powder, no bottle, and a body without a mark on it. A sustained field that did not need its caster's presence to continue running was a thing he had written in the margin of his notes on a Tuesday afternoon and had not had a use for since. He left the three small lines in the margin and did not connect them to anything. He was not at the underline yet.

He did not connect them to Esherel either. They sat in their margin and were patient.

He drew the lunch scene under *demeanor under stress* with a tick. He drew the four-crate scene under the same heading with a tick. He drew the dining-room arch in pencil and the merchant's table at six feet from the arch and the chair-quarter turn at one finger flat on the folio. He underlined *demeanor* once and looked at the underline, and decided he had been right to underline it, and was faintly pleased with himself for the careful hand.

> *[Investigator — Insight +1. A page can look like a face from far enough away.]*

He read it. He read it twice. Flinching at it occurred to him; he chose, again, not to.

He capped the pencil and set it parallel to the slate's top edge, the way Sable set hers. He looked at the column for the small honest beat he had begun to require of himself before he left the slate, the beat in which the page was a tool and not the room.

The page was the page. He had built it cleanly. He was, on examination, comfortable.

Below the gallery window, Petal handed Gravel the slot again. Gravel took it. He did not acknowledge.

---

Sable came up to the gallery at the open of the bronzed hour and stood at the door without coming in, which was her habit when she had come to find him and was prepared to be found doing nothing.

"Saloon," she said. "There is a soup and there is the lemon bread and Calden is on the second-watch rota tonight so he will not be eating standing up if I am at the door."

"Saloon."

"Bring the slate."

He brought the slate. They went down the aft stair together at the pace they walked when they had nowhere to be, which was slower than his step and slightly faster than hers, and arrived in a rhythm neither of them had settled.

The forward room on deck three was the narrow one — six tables, a brass urn, a porthole on the long wall and a porthole on the short — and it was warm at the open of the second-watch service in the way the narrow rooms on the Wyrm were warm, the smell of the bread and the soup and the lemon coming up through the floor from the galley one deck down. Calden was at the door. He nodded at Sable. He nodded at Theron. He did not nod again.

They took the corner table at the long porthole.

"Lemon bread," he said.

"Lemon bread."

The soup came. It was the kind of soup the cook did on the gondola's third day out, when the morning fish had been good — a clear broth with the small white pieces in it and the green of the herb on top — and they ate it the way two people ate a soup they did not have to talk through. The bread came after. The lemon biscuits, when they arrived on the small plate Sable had been expecting them on, arrived as four and not as six, which was the cook's quiet joke at Sable for the bread schedule remark the night before. She moved two onto Theron's side without comment.

"The lunch," she said.

"You heard."

"The galley boy heard. The cook heard. The cook told me, this time, not the galley boy. I am being upgraded."

"She closed the folio."

"She closed it at the third bell, Vellaine thinks."

"Vellaine said that to you?"

"Vellaine said it to a place in the room she knew I would be in by the open of the second-watch service. There is a slight economy. I am being paid in observations." She broke the bread. "You answered her well."

"I did not say what was on the page."

"You said you were working the page. Which was true."

"It was true."

"True is a useful answer in a room with witnesses."

"It is."

He drank from the cup of water at his elbow. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the bench. The harness note came up through the floor in the same way it came up everywhere on the gondola, and underneath it, when he listened for it, the secondary note was still there, a fraction below the main.

"The cook," he said, after a beat. "He told you about the bread schedule last night. He's been talking to you."

"He talks to me because I asked him about Calden eating standing up. He is the kind of man who, having opened a door, holds it open. It is restful."

"It is restful."

"You should try it sometime."

He almost smiled. He did not, but the not-smiling was the kind the table got the use of.

She turned her cup a quarter so the handle pointed away from him. The still moment after the cup-turn was a moment he had begun to file and was, on the third day of filing it, not yet ready to put a column on.

"The third night," he said.

She looked at him.

"Vannic was at his wall-facing table behind us. I had taken the seat with the saloon's long window, you were at the next table over. He was talking to the wine steward about something — not the substitute, this was after the substitute — and he said, in a part of the conversation I had not really been listening to and was nonetheless storing because the page does that —" he set the soup spoon down — "he said, *my niece carved this for me when she was six and she would be unimpressed that I have not lost it yet.*"

The bread was half to her plate. She held it there. She glanced at the porthole. She brought the bread the rest of the way down.

"That's the sentence?"

"That's the sentence."

"Mm."

He sat with the words for the brief honest beat the words required, which was the beat the social texture of a man whose body was in cabin twelve had earned at any table he had been at in the run. Vannic had been at a table on the third night and had said a sentence about a small carved object and a child and the gentle comic indignity of the child being older now than she had been when she had carved the object. Theron had not been particularly listening. The sentence had stored itself anyway.

He picked the spoon up.

"He had a small wooden fish on him," he said. "In the coat pocket. I logged it the morning of the body."

"I remember."

He filed the sentence under *social texture of a man whose body is in cabin twelve* and moved on, because it was not on the case-page and he was not at the underline. The cook brought a second small plate over with two more lemon biscuits on it, in the unfussy correction of a man who had counted four and meant six.

"He's correcting the bread schedule," Sable said, as the cook withdrew.

"By giving us more biscuits than the bread schedule requires."

"Same correction."

"You'll write him a thank-you note."

"I will say *noted* at him when I see him at the urn, which is the correct form."

"Noted."

He did smile, this time, briefly. She did not look at him while he did, which he had begun to understand was its own kind of looking. The cup was a quarter further on its handle than it had been a moment ago. They finished the meal. He had not, in the event, opened the slate.

---

He took the slate up to his cabin at the close of the bronzed hour and set it on the writing desk.

The page was the page he had built in the gallery. Esherel at the top, with the underline once now on *demeanor*. Tannin at the second tier, Vellaine at the third. The two windows in series, the corridor in pencil; the lunch scene noted with a tick under *demeanor under stress*, the four-crate scene with a second tick beside it. The three small lines in the margin under cabin twelve that said *carry-frame*, sitting in their margin and not yet connected to anything.

He looked at the underline on *demeanor*.

He capped the pencil and laid it parallel to the slate's top edge at the angle Sable laid hers.

> *[Investigator — Quest active. Ten hours, seven minutes to Halverstow Port.]*

He read it. He moved his thumb off the graduation pin. He had not noticed, again, his thumb arriving at the pin.

The cabin smelled faintly of his father's dispensary oil. The drakes were on their long approach. The Wyrm carried its low constant through the floor. He was, on inspection, comfortable.

He did not write the underline on opportunity. He turned it over for the length of time it took the lamp oil to warm at trim, decided the morning was still the right shape, and set the slate where he had set it the night before, parallel to the desk's far edge.

He went to find dinner.
