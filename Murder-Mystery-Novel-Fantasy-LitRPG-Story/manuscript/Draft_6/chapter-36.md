# Chapter 36 — The Title at the Foot of the Quest

Second bell sounded through the deck.

Theron heard it the way a person heard a bell on a gondola he had been on the better part of a morning — not as an event but as the deck answering itself. He had been sitting on the bench under the porthole of cabin two with the case book on the desk and his hands flat on his knees in the posture Halsa had used in cabin six. He had not been doing anything in particular, and he had not been working the case. There was no case left to work in the morning's laboring sense; the labor had been done at Pessel's table and at his own table at the Arms and at Davren's reading-table and at the small writing desk in cabin six, and what was in front of him now was a procedure, and the procedure did not need rehearsing.

He stood.

He put on the coat he had hung on the hook by the door, then picked up the leather strap. The case book slid into the strap, and he tied the strap with the small ritual tying he had used every morning of the week, not because the strap needed tying but because the act of tying was the act of saying *now*. He put his hand on the door latch and waited a count he did not count. Then he went out.

The corridor was empty.

Cabin four's door opened as he came up to it, which meant Sable had been at the door waiting, which meant she had set herself to come out when she heard him. Her notebook was closed in her left hand with the pencil through the spine the way she carried it when she had decided she would not be writing. She had her traveling coat on. She had not buttoned it.

"Ready," she said.

"Ready."

She closed the door behind her without locking it. They walked together. He carried the strap. She came on his left hand, which was the side she had been on at Davren's table and at the door of cabin six and at the foot of the gangway at Halverstow, and the placement had stopped being a placement and become a fact about the corridor.

The porthole in the bend of the corridor looked out on Sannholt harbor.

The harbor was a smaller, southern thing than Halverstow's: two slip-cranes working at the eastern arm, a customs boat at the inner mole with a single oarsman backing it onto a pile, the water flat and the light long across it the way southern afternoon light went long. A gull stood on the pile beside the customs boat. The gondola was descending. The hum through the deck had gone down a note, the way it went down when a drake throttled back for the set-down phase, and Theron registered the note without turning it over.

"Sannholt," Sable said.

"Sannholt."

They came to cabin six.

He knocked once and waited the count he had decided he would wait, which was three, and then he knocked again, because the second knock was a courtesy the first had not been. He heard, beyond the door, a small specific movement — a chair, set carefully back from a table — and then the door opened.

Halsa Renn was in her traveling coat with the buttons done up. Her hair was pinned. Her bag was on the floor by her left foot, the buckle done up, the strap looped through her hand, her right hand free. She looked at Theron and at Sable and at the strap under Theron's arm in the same order she had looked at them yesterday, and she stepped out into the corridor without speaking. To close the door of cabin six she set the bag down beside her foot, left the door unlocked, and picked the bag back up.

"Mr. Mavik."

"Miss Renn."

"Miss Verrin."

"Miss Renn."

That was the whole of what was said in the corridor.

They walked together to the disembarkation gangway. The Sannholt steward — a different steward from the Wyrm's Calden, a long-faced quiet man named on the brass plate of his station as Master Pell — was at the head of the gangway with his hands behind his back. He looked at Theron, who was first, and at the strap under Theron's arm, and at the magistral seal that was visible on the cover edge of the strap because Theron had set it visible. He inclined his head a half-inch.

"Mr. Mavik."

"Master Pell."

"Mr. Davren wired the harbor-master at first bell. The receiving room is the second warehouse from the slip. The constables are at the door of it. Master Ottren's at the table inside."

"Thank you."

"Three of you stepping off together."

"Three of us."

Pell inclined his head a second time and stepped aside.

The gondola had set down while they were in the corridor, and the gangway was already laid. The Sannholt slip was a working slip, not a passenger pier — paint-marks on the stone for the loading stations, a row of fenders done in a coastal hand, a customs boat tied off forward of the gondola's mooring with the same single oarsman now coiling his line. On the pile the gull had not moved. The light on the slip was the southern afternoon's long light. Theron registered each of these the way Wisp had taught him without teaching him to register a yard at its labor.

They walked down the gangway together. Halsa was between them — Theron on her left, Sable on her right. The order had not been arranged. The order had set itself in the corridor.

The receiving room was the second warehouse from the slip.

It was a warehouse in use, with a customs office at one end, partitioned off in painted board, with a door in the partition and the door open. Two constables in the Sannholt magistrate's plain blue stood at the door. They did not move when the three of them came up. The constable on the left had a clipboard and the constable on the right had nothing in his hands, which Theron read as the courtesy of the arrangement Davren had set up — the constable on the right was the constable who would walk Halsa through, and his hands were free in the way a man kept his hands free who did not mean to use them.

Inside the partitioned office a small writing table was set against the inner wall.

Behind the table sat a man in his early fifties in a worn gray magistrate's coat, with reading glasses pushed up onto his forehead and a single sheet of paper laid square in front of him. His hands were on the paper. He looked up as they came in and he did not stand, because standing would have been a performance the room had been arranged not to perform.

"Mr. Mavik."

"Master Ottren."

"Mr. Davren is well, I hope."

"He is."

"You're under his deputation for this delivery."

"I am."

"The order."

Theron unbuckled the strap. He took out the magistral travel-order Davren had signed at the close of Friday and laid it on the table in front of Ottren without sliding it. He took out the case book and laid it beside the order, also without sliding it. He stepped back a half-step. Halsa stood where she had been standing. Sable stood where she had been standing.

Ottren read the order. He read it through once. He set his hand on the page in a flat reading hand and he looked up at Halsa.

"Miss Renn."

"Master Ottren."

"You're presented to me at this table under Mr. Davren's order of yesterday. You are not under arrest as of this moment. You are, as of my countersignature, in the custody of the Sannholt magistrate's office for the purpose of giving a statement under writ. The constable on my left will walk you through to the inner room. There is tea in the inner room and there is a chair. The statement will begin when I come through. Do you understand the shape of what I have just said."

"I do."

"Anything to say to Mr. Mavik or to Miss Verrin before we begin."

Halsa looked at Theron. She looked at Sable. She did not look long at either.

"No," she said. "I said it in the cabin. I have nothing to add to it on the dock."

"Thank you."

She did not say *thank you* back. She picked up her bag in her right hand and she walked past the table and past the constable on the right and through the inner door, which the constable held for her without making anything of holding it. The inner door closed behind her with a small definite sound.

Ottren took up his pen.

He countersigned the order. He turned the order and slid it back across the table to Theron, who took it without looking at it and folded it once and put it back in the case book and tied the strap. Ottren laid his hand on the case book itself.

"This is the book."

"This is the book."

"It stays with me."

"It does."

"Thank you for the manner of the delivery, Mr. Mavik."

"It was Mr. Davren's manner. I was only carrying it."

"You were carrying it."

Ottren did not stand to see them out. He had not stood to see them in. He inclined his head the half-inch Pell had used at the gangway, which was the measure this part of the country kept for the work of magistrates, and he opened the case book and did not look up again.

Theron stepped back.

Sable stepped back.

They turned and went out through the partition door past the two constables, who did not look at them, into the warehouse, and from the warehouse out into the southern afternoon on the slip.

> *Quest closed: Solve the Death of Joren Aldis.*
> *Title at first issue (Wyrm, cabin twelve, second watch, the fifth of October): Solve the Death of Joren Aldis.*
> *Title carried through the run.*

He read the screen.

He read it a second time, slower, the way a man read a sheet of paper that had a thing on it he had thought was on a different sheet of paper.

He had read the quest line in cabin twelve as *Solve the Death Aboard the Skybound Wyrm.* He had carried that title in his head for the better part of a fortnight. He had read in the captain's day-cabin at Halverstow a System line that said *Quest updated: Solve the Death of Joren Aldis* and he had read the word *updated* and not paused on it because the line was already doing a different work in him on that morning. The title had not been updated. The title had been the title. What had been updated was the part of the title the screen had been willing, on that morning, to show him.

He stood with the strap under his arm.

He gave the screen the small dry acknowledgment he had given the System every morning of the week.

He gave it a half-count longer than the morning nod.

Sable was beside him. She was looking at the gondola in its mooring at the visitor slip. She did not look at him. She had not seen the screen and she was not going to ask.

"All right," he said.

"All right."

They walked together away from the warehouse, along the working edge of the slip, past the customs boat with the oarsman now sitting on a bollard and eating a piece of bread, past the gull on the pile that had not moved, past the gondola whose two drakes were on their tethered rest with their heads down on their forepaws in the way Wisp had told him drakes rested when a set-down had been clean. The slip-cranes worked at the eastern arm. The light went long across the water.

At the end of the slip the stone became a wider pier with a low rail at the harbor-side. They stopped at the rail. Theron set his free hand on the rail. The rail was warm from the southern afternoon. Sable put her right hand into her coat pocket and rested two fingers against the inside lining of the inside pocket where the carved fish had been since the twenty-eighth of October. She did not take anything out.

"Sannholt," she said.

"Sannholt."

"The room behind us is the room behind us."

"It is."

"There's a thing or two ahead of us that isn't the room behind us."

"There is."

She did not name it. He did not ask her to.

He took the strap off his shoulder and resettled it. The case book inside it was lighter than it had been at the head of the gangway, because Ottren had the part of it that was Ottren's now, and what Theron carried back to the harbor-side inn was what Theron would carry from here on, which was a different weight.

The gull on the pile lifted off and went south, a bird about its own afternoon and none of his business.

"All right," he said.

"All right," Sable said.

They walked off the pier toward the harbor-side inn at the slow pace of two people who had finished one thing and had not yet begun the next.
