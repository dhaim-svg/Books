# Chapter 31 — The Person-Shaped Hole

Wednesday morning started in a thin gray light from the harbor side of the dining room and the kind of pot the host put down at first bell when the second loaves were still ten minutes off. Theron had been at the corner table since the lamp went on. The case book was open at the method page. The five working-theory lines stood where they had stood last night.

He had not yet written anything new.

Sable came down at the half-bell. She had her hair pulled back the way she pulled it back when she had slept enough to bother, which was a smaller margin than not pulling it back at all. She had the brown coat over her arm and put it on the chair before she sat. The host arrived with the second cup before she had finished sitting.

"Bread in a minute."

"Thank you."

The host went on. Sable drew her own notebook out of the inner pocket of the coat and set it beside Theron's case book without opening either. She drank a quarter of the cup first. Then she looked at him over the rim.

"You haven't written."

"No."

"You're waiting." She set two fingers flat on her unopened notebook.

"I was waiting for you. Now I'm not."

"Then write."

He did not write yet. He turned the case book around so the method page was right-side up for her, the way he had turned it last night, and put his finger on the second line: *sustained contact during a seating phase*.

"I want to say something about this line." He kept his voice in the register the corner of the room allowed. "Last night I said an apprenticeship of any length would satisfy it many times over. That's true. It satisfies the *contact-time* requirement. It satisfies the *signature* requirement, by inference. It does not satisfy a third thing I did not write down."

She set the cup down. "Go on."

"The seating phase, as a method of laying a glamour, is one thing. The seating phase, as a *trigger architecture* — meaning, set up so that the seating itself prepares a later remote activation — is another thing. They use the same words. The work is different. The first is in any practical illusion textbook. The second is in three papers, one of which is mine, none of which are in the trade."

"So an apprentice would learn the first."

"An apprentice would learn the first as part of any working trade. The second she would only learn from a master who knew it and chose to show it to her."

She did not nod. She turned a page in her own notebook and ran her finger down the column and stopped at a line he could not read upside-down from where he was sitting.

"Joren knew it."

"Joren knew it enough to use it. He would have had to teach it for an apprentice to learn it. There is no other path."

"And he would not have taught it to a passing apprentice."

"He would not have taught it to a competent apprentice. He would have taught it to a particular one."

She read her own line again. She set the finger on it and lifted the cup with the other hand and drank without looking up.

"All right."

The host brought the bread.

---

She did not open her notebook to him.

She read from it instead, two sentences, in the slightly careful voice she used when she was reading her own field notes aloud and she wanted both of them to hear what the field notes said and not what either of them wanted the field notes to say.

"From Pessel, Sunday afternoon, second sitting. *She could do the work after about three years, but Joren kept her seven more.*"

He waited.

"Same conversation, fifteen minutes later. *He sent her south alone for the work that needed her hand more than his.*"

She set the notebook down so the page faced him. The two sentences were in her small clear hand, one above the other, with a thin pencil line between them. Underneath the second one she had written, in a hand that was even smaller, *training arrangement / trust.* That note had a thin pencil line through it. The line was not from this morning. The pencil was paler than the pencil she had this morning. She had struck it out at some point between Sunday and now and had not told him she had struck it out.

He read both sentences twice.

"Three years to do the work." He set his finger on the first sentence. "Seven more to do — "

"Not the work."

"No."

"Something he kept her for that the work didn't require."

"And then she went south, for work he didn't go with her on, that the work-she-could-do-alone needed her hand more than his." He set his finger on the second sentence. "Pessel said *more than* and not *instead of*. Which is the way Pessel said most things — she chose the smaller word. So in some part of it, his hand was still in it. The work was still partly his, but the seating was hers."

"Yes." She drew the notebook back. "That's the line I read three times on the coach yesterday and didn't tell you about because I didn't know what it was for."

"It was for this." He did not say it as triumph. He said it the way he had said *that's the geometry* last night, in the flat register of a working sentence sitting on the page where it belonged. "The apprenticeship was longer than competence required. That's the seating phase she's describing without knowing she's describing it. He kept her near him for seven years past sufficiency because he was teaching her something the work itself didn't ask for."

Sable said nothing for the length of a slow pull from the cup.

She said it the way she came to conclusions, not as arrival but as settling: the sentence already present, set down without ceremony.

"Then she came south alone for work he could no longer do himself."

"Or did not want to do himself. We don't know which."

"No. We don't."

She turned the page back to the column where the Pessel notes lived and closed the notebook on her finger and looked at him with the steady look from last night, the one that was not pity and not satisfaction. He had seen her give it twice now. He was beginning to recognize the muscles in her face that produced it. There were not many.

"Write your list."

---

He turned to a fresh page in the case book.

He did not write *Halsa Renn* across the top, nor a date. He wrote, in the unindented hand he used for working theory not yet earned: *Person who could have performed the method.*

He wrote, under it:

*(a) Sustained-contact apprenticeship with the victim, of seating-phase duration. Not less than the laboratory floor. In practice many years.*

He read it back. He moved on.

*(b) Signature compatibility with the victim. Produced by (a) as a matter of the apprenticeship's working product.*

He read that back. He moved on.

*(c) Instruction in the trigger architecture, not only in the lay-down of glamour. Given by the master, not learned from books. Not standard apprenticeship content.*

He held the pencil over the page. He wrote:

*(d) Retention of the trigger after the apprenticeship ended. The trigger does not transfer back unless the master demands it back. The master did not demand it back, or did not know to.*

Sable read the line as he wrote it.

"He didn't know to."

"He didn't know it had been completed. The seating phase doesn't announce itself to the host. The trigger does not exist for him. He carried the glamour and not the awareness of the glamour."

"Then the apprentice walked out with the trigger and the master did not know what she walked out with."

"That's what (d) says."

"That's not common."

"It is not common." He set the pencil down for a moment. "It is the kind of thing that happens when the master has not done the work himself and the apprentice has. When she has worked alone on the technique past where he was working. The competence ran ahead of the relationship at some point. The relationship did not catch up."

She did not say *prodigy*. She did not say *unusual*. She did not say anything. She turned her cup so the handle pointed away from the table edge and sat back fractionally in the chair. He saw the small adjustment and noted it without comment.

He wrote:

*(e) Voluntary, repeated proximity from victim to caster late in the apprenticeship, of the kind that allows the seating to be completed without the victim's awareness. Required from the victim's side. Trust-shaped, not coercion-shaped.*

He read that back twice.

He did not amend it.

He wrote:

*(f) A working life after the apprenticeship that did not register on the standing-detection rigs the trade ordinarily checks. Required so that the method could be carried into the field without prior detection elsewhere. Consistent with the Copper Finch note: "did work that needed quiet rooms."*

He set the pencil down.

Six lines.

He read the page through.

The six lines did not name a person. The six lines said what the person would have had to be. He read each line and felt the case book lie under his hand the way it had been lying since Friday. Heavier on one side, where the column page was. Lighter under the method page. Lighter still under this new page, because the new page was a shape and the shape was already mostly filled in from work he and Sable had done in other rooms in other weeks.

The first line: Pessel, eleven years, Sunday afternoon. The second line: Pessel again, *because that was what an apprentice meant in their trade.* The third line: the thesis abstract and the working-theory four lines from yesterday morning and Pessel's *he sent her south alone for the work that needed her hand more than his* read against them. The fourth line: trigger known to caster, not to target — the defining property of the exploit. The fifth line: Joren-Aldis-eleven-years-of-quiet, which the case book held in three places. The sixth line: Hesh at the Copper Finch, *did work that needed quiet rooms,* and nine years of southbound visits without registration anywhere the trade would look.

He did not write the name at the bottom of the page.

He did not need to.

He sat with the six lines for a slow minute. The host was carrying a pot to the second table. The factor from yesterday had not come in. The harbor was making its Wednesday-morning sound through the window, a hand quieter than Tuesday because the wind was off the land.

Sable was looking at the page. He was looking at her looking at the page.

She did not name the person.

"It's a shape."

"It's a shape."

"And there's one person on the manifest who fits the shape."

"Yes."

"That's not the same as no one else could fit it."

"No. It's not."

"But the shape doesn't argue for anyone else."

"No. It doesn't."

She did not push past that line. She did what she had been doing for three days now, which was to keep the working sentence to its working width and not let it grow. He had a quiet appreciation of her for it that he was not going to say out loud and that he suspected she would not have accepted if he had.

She picked up her cup and finished it.

---

He turned to the back-of-book page.

The page held the careful sentence about the original placement from Saturday and, under it, last night's line in his own hand: *Exclusivity to Renn — not yet established. Joren may have trained other apprentices over a thirty-four-year working life. The theory holds against the case as known; the theory does not yet specify that Renn is the only person on the Wyrm capable of having performed it.*

He read the line. He did not strike it out. He did not want to strike it out — striking it out would have been the cheap pleasure of yesterday's certainty over today's, and he had been at a graduation table four months ago and had a sense of what cheap pleasures cost a year later.

He picked up the pencil.

Under the line, in the smallest shadow-note hand he had — the hand for things he wanted on the page and not in the center of the page — he wrote: *Requirements list converges to one person on the manifest. Exclusivity not proven. Structural fit is.*

He set the pencil down.

He read the two lines together. Last night's and this morning's, the open question and the morning's narrow answer. They sat on the page the way the lines had been sitting all week: not as conclusions, as the small steady accumulation of what the working pages allowed him to say.

He did not feel triumphant.

He felt the specific quiet that came after a piece of structural work had finished placing itself on a page. He had felt it once before, in his thesis defense, at the moment when the second examiner had asked the follow-up question and he had heard the answer in his own head before he said it and had known, on the way to saying it, that the answer was the answer. The feeling now was the same feeling. The room was different.

He turned to the new page, the requirements list, and read the six lines through one more time.

The shape on the page had a person in it. The person was not on the page. The person was, however, on the passenger manifest at the magistrate's office two streets over, in cabin nine-T, marked Sannholt-bound, cash, no professional reference, a creak near the second-class passage after second watch on the third night and a quieter walk on the third morning. Eleven years of Pessel's testimony stood behind her, and a back room at the Copper Finch with three or four southbound visits a year for nine years, and a placement made eleven years ago through a network now unwound.

The person fit the shape.

He closed the case book on the requirements page, with his thumb in to hold it, and let it sit closed for a moment under his hand.

Sable was watching the page-edge of his thumb. She had been quiet for the length of his reading.

"The work today." She set her own notebook on the table. "Was the work today."

"Yes."

"Tomorrow is the exclusivity."

"Tomorrow is the exclusivity."

"How do you do that?"

He had not, until she asked, thought of the answer in plain words. He thought now.

"I show that no other person on the manifest could stand in the shape on the page. I take each of them — the Mirren passenger list, all of them, every cabin — and I lay the requirements list against the manifest entry and I see which lines fail and where. The work is mostly subtraction. Most of them will fail at line (a) before I have to check the others. A few will need two or three lines to clear them out. One will not be cleared out by any line."

"And the one that isn't cleared out — "

"Is not proven. It is what is left after the subtraction. Which is a different thing than proven, and a thing I will name as that thing when I give the page to Davren."

"All right." She stood up. She put the cup on the bread plate and picked up her own notebook and tucked it back into the inner pocket of the coat. She left the coat on the chair without taking it up. "I have to write to Pessel."

"What about?"

"About the seven years."

She did not elaborate. He did not press. He had a sense, by now, of what Sable wrote letters about and the order she wrote them in, and *the seven years* was a phrase that would sit with her on the way to her room as cleanly as it had sat on the table between them.

"Bring it to me before you post it?"

"Yes."

"Thank you."

She did not say *of course* the way Calden said it and the host said it and the people on the gondola corridors had said it. She said *yes,* and started for the stair, and stopped at the foot of the stair and turned half around.

"Theron."

"Yes."

"The list is good."

He looked up from the case book. "Thank you."

She went up. He did not watch her go up. He turned back to the case book and opened it on the requirements page and read it through a third time, because that was the count he had been keeping for the morning's pages and he had not let himself get to three with it yet.

*[Investigator — Working page added: Method-of-perpetrator structural requirements. Six lines. Status: structural fit narrowed to one named candidate on manifest; exclusivity check pending against full passenger list.]*

The System line came in over the page in its laconic way. He read it through once. He noticed that the System had said *one named candidate* and not *Halsa Renn* — the System had the same caution he had about putting a name at the bottom of a structural page before the subtraction was done — and he gave the System the brief, dry acknowledgment that was all it ever wanted from him.

He sat at the table for a while longer.

The harbor wind shifted once on the window. The host carried the second pot through. A pair of road-coats came in and were settled at a table by the front-room door. The case book sat under his hand on the requirements page.

He had been told, at the Conservatory, that a working investigator would have a day in the first year when a piece of his own past work would lie on the table beside the case and the two of them would have nothing left to say to each other but yes. The Warden had said it on the ceremony day. He had not believed it that day, because most ceremony-day sentences were ceremony-day sentences and not working sentences.

The Warden had been right.

He closed the case book on the requirements page with his thumb still in the place where he had been reading.

He took his thumb out. He let the page lie.

The host came past with the third pot and set it down without saying anything, and Theron drank what was in the cup and did not pour another, and the morning's work, for the morning, was done.
