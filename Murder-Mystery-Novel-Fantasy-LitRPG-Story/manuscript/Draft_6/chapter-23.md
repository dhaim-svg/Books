# Chapter 23 — Everyone Walks Out

The magistrate's clerk had two speeds: one for documents she considered appropriate to her office and one for everything else. She ran a finger down the reference column at the appropriate speed, which was no speed at all, while Theron stood at the counter and watched.

"TL-9," he said, in case she had forgotten.

"I know what I'm looking for," she said, without looking up.

He waited. The clerk's office smelled of sealing wax and something camphor-adjacent that was probably the lining of the ledger cabinet. Through the window to his left, the harbor's morning light came in at a low angle and put a long rectangle of gold across the flagstone floor, which moved imperceptibly while he waited. A second clerk at the back of the room was copying from one register into another with the serene patience of a woman entirely at peace with the world.

The finger stopped. "There," she said, and turned the ledger to face him.

---

He walked back to the Arms with the clerk's notation in his case book: two spring customs-assist jobs, reference TL-9, both connecting through a Mirren west-harbor account registered to a cargo storage firm on the harbor's outer ring. The firm's name was Vantrel Secured Storage, registered agent listed as one H. Marbeth. Not Toll. Not Roven. A name Theron had not seen in any previous interview, any letter, any yard log.

He stopped at the corner of the harbor road and wrote it out in full. *Vantrel Secured Storage, outer ring, west harbor. Registered agent: H. Marbeth.*

Another layer. He put the case book in his coat pocket and kept walking.

---

The problem he had been sitting with since yesterday had not gone away for being slept on.

He brought it out properly at the small table in the Arms dining room, over coffee that arrived without being ordered, which was one of the benefits of staying somewhere long enough. His notes from the early stages of the case lay beside the cup: the categorization he had made in the first hour, the suspect columns, the framework he had built in the captain's gallery with his method organized and precise and, as it turned out, organized and precise around the wrong question.

He had asked: who had motive against Lord Vannic?

The question was correct. It was the correct question for the case he had been given. A man found dead in a locked cabin, the man identified to all present as Lord Petric Vannic: the question is who among his known circle had reason to want Lord Vannic dead. He had built that case with care, and the case held, and it was the wrong case entirely.

The real question, the one that would have led him to Roven, to Toll, to a working illusionist named Joren Aldis and the professional network that had put him in that specific cabin on that specific passage, was: who had reason to stop Joren Aldis?

He turned the page and wrote that at the top of a clean sheet. Let it sit. Then he looked back at his first-class suspect columns.

Esherel: forty-one thousand five hundred crowns of motive, clear opportunity window, demeanor that had looked like calculated presentation. He had nearly committed to a conclusion that would have been wrong in every particular. Not because his method was flawed. Because his method was working on a case that didn't exist.

Vellaine: a juridical theorist, a witness with a lamp-timing discrepancy, a woman who had told him to watch his cleanest column. She'd known the column looked clean because she'd seen cases built on clean columns before. Not a critique of his conclusion. An observation about his frame.

The second-class field: three merchants with dull alibis, a wool-trade agent, a woman traveling to see her sister. He had built brackets around all of them because they were on the same gondola as the name Vannic and might have had reasons he couldn't yet see. None of them had known anything about Joren Aldis. None of them had been near that network.

The method was correct. He had run the method correctly. What the method had needed was a different set of inputs, and he hadn't had them until cabin three-T.

He wrote, in the margin of the original categorization page: *case one and case two. Wrong shape for case two; correct procedure throughout.*

Then he closed the early notes and set them at the far edge of the table.

*[Investigator — Annotation flagged: original case file reclassified as wrong-case documentation. Not error. Frame shift.]*

He looked at that for a moment. Then he finished his coffee.

---

The cargo numbers had traced to Vantrel Secured Storage and stopped. He spent the late morning writing to the magistrate's office to request the full year's registered-agent records for the firm. They would need a search warrant to go further, and the warrant would take time he didn't yet have. He noted *Marbeth* as a thread to pull once the warrant came through, and left it there.

By mid-afternoon, the case had the shape of something in progress. Roven placed Joren. Toll chose the passage. Fell was on the cargo side, connected but not at the center of it. Dosseren processed the money. Marbeth, or the firm called Vantrel, or whoever sat behind both, was another wall that needed time to come through. Sable was leaving in the morning for a Sannholt conversation that would not travel by post.

He sat with all of it in the dining room when the lunch hour cleared out, the way he sat with cases when the work was in the stage of becoming clear rather than the stage of being done. Progress was real. The shape of what had happened was comprehensible now in outline. What was still missing was the part that turned outline into evidence.

---

Esherel came down the front steps of the Arms just as he was going up.

She pulled on her glove — one finger, then the next, with the practiced precision of old formal habit — and said, without preamble, "You've been at the magistrate's office this morning."

"I have."

"The cargo trace," she said, and it wasn't a question.

"Partial result," he said. "Useful, not conclusive."

She looked at him steadily. "Are you going to get there?"

"I think so." He paused on the step. "Not quickly."

Esherel put the second glove on and smoothed it at the wrist. "Joren's family," she said. "Does anyone know — has anyone contacted them?"

"Sable is going to Sannholt tomorrow. There's a contact there who knew him." He stopped. "The family would be elsewhere — he came from the coast, from a fishing town somewhere in Joren's home region. We don't have the specific address yet."

Esherel's expression shifted slightly. Not softer, exactly — more settled. "I wanted to ask because I intend to say something, when there's a proper address and a proper moment. I was clear-eyed about what he was. A professional deceiver. That doesn't mean his people shouldn't hear from someone who was in the room."

Theron didn't say anything for a moment. "That's —" He stopped himself. The word he had been about to use was *generous*, and it was wrong, or at least incomplete. "That would mean something," he said.

She made a small sound that was not a dismissal. "I have a letter to the solicitor's to see about, and you have whatever you have." She went down the last step and then paused. "Come and find me if the address turns up. I'll be at the Arms through the end of the week."

He watched her go across the harbor square.

Then he went up the steps and inside.

---

He was arranging the afternoon's notes into the case book when someone knocked at the parlor door.

It was Esherel again — he thought, half a second before the door opened and the thought corrected itself. The step was different.

Madam Esherel's hair was steel-gray and entirely assembled; the woman in the doorway had light-brown hair wound up with the slight carelessness of someone who had done it themselves this morning without a mirror. She was neither young nor old. Her coat was the practical heavy wool of the harbor quarters, not the harbor-walk coat of someone passing through.

"Mr. Mavik," she said. "I was told you were here. I'm sorry to come without sending word first."

He didn't recognize her. Then, the fraction of a second later, he did: the shape of the face, the directness, the slight formal stiffness around the edges of her voice that said *this matters and I am aware it is slightly awkward that it matters*.

"Madam Esherel," he said, and caught himself. The woman in the doorway was not Esherel. He knew perfectly well who Esherel was. He had just watched Esherel cross the harbor square with her gloves on.

The confusion lasted one more beat before he had it — the voice. Not Esherel's voice. Similar precision, different register.

"I'm sorry," he said. "Please come in. You're —"

"Norra Esherel," she said. "Fellin's —" She paused. "Madam Esherel's niece. I came down from the city when she sent word. She said — she thought you might be willing to speak to me. About the investigation."

He gestured to the chair across from him. "Please sit."

She sat with the careful economy of someone managing her own dignity without a great deal of practice at it. Her hands went to her lap, then to the chair arms, then back to her lap.

He waited.

"She told me about the man," Norra Esherel said. "The illusionist. Joren Aldis." She said the name with a slight pause around it, the pause of someone putting careful weight on a word they'd recently learned. "She told me what he was like on the gondola — from her perspective. What she noticed." She looked at the case book. "She also told me she gave you three observations. But she didn't tell me everything she observed."

Theron looked at her. "How do you mean?"

"Because she didn't think it mattered," Norra Esherel said. "At the time." Her hands settled finally on her lap and stayed there. "She told me when she got in — not the first night, the second night, after she'd had time to think about it. She saw something that she'd — she'd filed it away. As nothing. As a private moment that she hadn't understood and hadn't wanted to intrude on."

"What did she see?"

The young woman looked at the case book for a moment, not as though she expected it to answer, but as though looking at an object somewhere below eye level helped her put the words in the right order. "On the second evening of the passage," she said. "She was coming back along the first-class corridor. Late — past the second watch. She nearly ran into him. He was standing in the corridor on his own, half-turned toward the wall, and he had something in his hand." A pause. "He'd taken it from his coat pocket. Something small. He was just — looking at it. Quietly. She said he looked like a man in a conversation with himself that he'd had many times before."

Theron set his pencil down.

"She almost said something," Norra Esherel continued. "She didn't, because it was private. He hadn't seen her — or if he had, he hadn't acknowledged it. He put whatever it was back in his pocket after a moment and walked on toward the dining room. She thought it was a letter, or a small token of some kind. Something kept rather than used."

The corridor. Second watch. Joren in the first-class passage that connected his cabin to the dining room, and something small taken from an outer pocket, looked at, put away. The outer coat pocket — the right one.

"She knows now," Norra Esherel said quietly, "that it was the fish. The carved one." Her voice was level. "She was told about that — about what was in his pocket. And she wanted — she asked me to come because she didn't want to come herself and make it — she said she didn't want to make it about her feelings, which aren't relevant, and she was afraid she would." She looked at Theron. "So she sent me to tell you."

"Why was it important to tell me?"

Norra Esherel's hands tightened slightly, then released. "Because someone is going to go and tell his family. And Aunt Fellin thought — they should know. That he had it with him. That he reached for it." A slight pause. "That at the end, or near it, there was a private moment where he looked at something a child had made for him and carried for two years. And that it was quiet. She said — she said he didn't look sad. He just looked like a man who knew what mattered."

Theron looked at his case book.

The carved fish had been in his notes since the first examination of the body. Outer coat pocket, between a used handkerchief and a steel pen-nib. Filed as *odd personal effect, note for return to estate*. Re-filed after the reveal, after the recall passage, after Sable had named what the fish was and who had made it. When the quest updated, he had written Joren's name across the top of the case file, and had not flinched at it, because that was what you did once you knew the name.

He had not known about the corridor, the second-watch moment, the small private looking, the thing put back into a pocket and carried on.

He closed the case book.

"Thank you," he said. "Please thank Madam Esherel." He looked at Norra Esherel steadily. "The family — when we have an address. She'll be contacted directly, with her permission."

"She'd like that," Norra Esherel said.

She stood. He stood. She went to the door with the same careful economy she'd come in with, and paused there, her hand on the frame. "She said one more thing," she said. "I almost forgot. She said — he didn't look like a man afraid of being caught. In the corridor. He just looked like himself."

Then she went.

---

Theron sat for a while. Not long — the afternoon light still had time in it, and the Marbeth note wanted a follow-up letter to the harbor registry before end of business. But long enough to let the corridor image settle into the case: the first-class passage on the second evening, Joren Aldis standing half-turned to the wall, the fish in his palm, looking at it the way a man looks at something that has been the reason for a very long stretch of difficult work. Put it away. Walk on toward the dining room.

He picked up his pencil. At the top of a fresh page he wrote: *What Esherel saw — corridor, second watch, second evening. Small carved object from outer coat pocket. Returned to pocket. Private.*

Below that: *Not afraid. Himself.*

He underlined neither.

*[Investigator — Witness addition logged: Esherel observation, second-evening corridor. Corroborates known-facts entry on the carved fish. New detail: pre-death, private moment, deliberate.]*

He looked at the line. Then he set the pencil down, picked up his coat, and went to find the harbor registry before it closed.

The fish had been there in his pocket the whole passage. He had reached for it in a quiet corridor and looked at it, and then he had gone into the dining room and eaten supper with a fork in his hand and a dead lord's voice in his throat, and none of the first-class passengers had known what he was, or what he was carrying, or what two years of careful waiting felt like when the time was finally nearly right.

Theron went out into the afternoon. The harbor road was busy with the end of the working day, the carter's children running the edges of the cobbles, the smell of the day's tide coming in off the water. He walked quickly. He had letters to write before evening.
