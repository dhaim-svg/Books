# Chapter 3 — The Manifest

The case slate had been face-down on the bench for a little over an hour when Theron picked it up again, and he was aware, picking it up, that the hour had not been a rest.

He'd told himself it was. He had read three pages of a novel about a clockwork-maker in a port city that wasn't doing anything wrong but also wasn't competing with the view, and he had eaten the apple he'd taken from the dining-room sideboard, and he had even closed his eyes for what he assumed had been longer than it had been. Then his hand had reached for the slate.

The slate was for emergencies. That was the standing rule. The slate was for emergencies and any other use was a courtesy the System extended to a class user with otherwise unoccupied attention. He turned it over and brought up the full first-class manifest with a tap.

*[Passenger manifest — first class — 12 entries (booked manifest).]*

He read it down the column once without categorizing — that was the discipline. Just the names. Just whose holiday this was, alongside his.

Then, on the second pass, the categorizing happened without him.

The twelfth row was the amendment.

*[Booking amended post-departure: M. Tannin, first class, cabin three. Original booking name on record under sealed administrative note. Steward's discretion.]*

He read it twice. He'd read it twice yesterday.

The amendment was a name change made after the boarding manifest was finalized but before the gondola had cleared the dock. That much was new since the morning. *Sealed administrative note* meant a clerk on the ground had recorded the original entry and locked it under Calden's office, not the System's. The kind of paperwork that happened when a passenger had a reasonable expectation of privacy. A merchant moving under a trade pseudonym. A widow not wanting to be addressed by her late husband's title. An academic incognito.

There were probably six legitimate reasons.

He could think of three illegitimate ones too, and he watched himself doing it, and he made himself stop. He picked one at random — the merchant — and filed it there. He pulled the intake-notes page up over the manifest and wrote, underneath yesterday's *day one: no active queries*, the line *day two: still no active queries.*

He looked at the words. Then he closed the slate. Then, and this was the part that he wanted on the record even just for himself, he stood up, walked the slate to the writing desk, set it inside the desk drawer, and closed the drawer.

Unlocked, the drawer left the slate within reach. The exercise was not for the slate. The exercise was for him.

He sat back down on the bench and went back to the clockwork-maker.

The clockwork-maker, it turned out, was also failing to do anything about his problems. They had something in common.

---

By late afternoon the cabin had warmed past comfortable, and Theron went looking for air.

The exterior promenade was empty when he got there — at this hour, second watch by the slow ringing in the galley wall, most of the passengers were doing whatever passengers did in the slack period before dinner. The drakes were out on long tether, half a sky from the gondola, three small wedge shapes against a high sun.

He went to the stern, where the rigging house squatted at the back of the deck like a small, opinionated shed. The door stood open. Wisp was inside, sitting on an upended crate with a coil of harness chain in her lap, working a wax-impregnated cloth along the links one by one.

"Mr. Mavik."

"How does she know who I am from the bootsteps."

"You walk like a man who's read a book about boats." Wisp didn't look up. "It's a particular gait. The crew walk like the deck is the floor. Passengers walk like the deck is a deck." She rotated the chain a half-link and went on cleaning. "Sit, if you're sitting."

There was a second crate. He sat.

The rigging house smelled of beeswax and harness leather and the warm-iron smell of chain that had been working all morning. Inside the doorway, a row of canvas-covered tools hung on the wall in the order they were used — he could tell because the handles of the first three were a shade darker than the rest from grip-wear, and the fourth was almost untouched. He didn't ask. It was the kind of detail that belonged to Wisp.

"She's holding her downbeat now," he said.

"She's pretending. She'll go back to it after dinner when she thinks I've stopped watching." Wisp moved to the next link. "Mottled does her best work when she believes she's getting away with something. I've found it's easier to let her believe it."

"That seems like a generous theory of drake."

"It's the only theory of drake I've got that works." She held a link up to the light, turned it, set it back in the lap. "Gravel. Gravel is the opposite. Gravel does his best work when he's being watched and his worst when he isn't. I park a junior crewman by his harness pin on long runs and tell the crewman it's a safety post. It isn't. It's so Gravel performs."

"And Petal."

"Petal does her work." Wisp said it the way people said *the sun rises in the east*. "She doesn't need a theory. She has a job and she has weather and that's enough for her."

"You like Petal best."

"I like Petal *most peacefully*." Wisp turned a link over and set it back. "I don't have favorites. Don't write that down."

"I'm not writing it down."

"You have the slate in your jacket."

He had the slate in his jacket. He'd forgotten putting it there. He must have collected it from the desk drawer on the way out without thinking. The hand had done it; the head had not been consulted.

"It's not for notes," he said. "Today."

"All right." She did not look up. Then, after a few seconds of the cloth working: "What did you study?"

"Investigation. Theory mostly."

"What's an investigator do on a holiday?"

"Mine is going to find out."

Wisp made a small noise that might have been a laugh and might have been the rag catching on a link. The chain moved through her hands one piece at a time. Outside the rigging house door, the drakes had begun their slow turn back toward the gondola; he could see Petal in profile, banking with the unconcerned ease of something that had not had its competence questioned by anyone and never would.

"How do they sleep," he said. "In flight."

"They don't. Not the way you mean." Wisp set the chain aside and stood, brushing wax off her apron. "Two of them sleep at a time, in the harness, on the long tether. One stays up. They rotate by light, mostly. Petal goes second watch and last. Mottled won't sleep unless Gravel is the one awake, which is funny because she hates him. Gravel sleeps anytime. He's not a complicated drake."

"And you?"

"I sleep when the harness tells me to." She pulled the door wider with her shoulder. "Tonight I'll be up at fourth, that's when the long thermals come off the Brindle Range and the lines want re-tensioning. Come and watch if you can't sleep. People who can't sleep are useful at fourth watch."

"I'm going to sleep."

"All right." She didn't argue, only glanced at the angle of the light as if to confirm the hour. "Then dinner's in an hour, sir. Cook's done a pie. He's only done it once before, which is why he's doing it again."

He left her there with the chain.

On the walk back to the cabin, the slate stayed where he'd put it, in the inside breast pocket of his jacket. He didn't take it out. The promenade had taken on the early-evening color that meant the sun was past its working hours, and the harness chains were singing a slightly different note in the slipstream — lower, fuller, the way a string settled into a long sustain after the bow had stopped pressing.

He put a hand on the railing. The brass had gone warm in the sun and the warmth was still there.

He stood for a minute, doing nothing about it.

---

Dinner was a quieter assembly than breakfast.

The family of four had taken the long table and were working through what looked like a serious negotiation about whose turn it was to sit by the window. Esherel was in her corner again, the folio nowhere visible, which Theron read as: she had finished what she came to finish, or shelved it. The two scholars were leaning toward each other across their soup with the focused intimacy of an argument that had moved into its productive phase. Vellaine was at the small window table, her book gone and a glass of water in front of her instead, watching the dusk arrive over the Brindles like someone in no particular hurry to leave the chair.

"Vannic" came in late.

He sat at the same place he had at breakfast, corner, back to the room, plate-and-porthole, and ate steadily and without expression, and then before the dessert course had reached anyone else he was up again and gone. Calden, refilling water at the next table, did not appear surprised. The man's preferences had been registered and would be accommodated, and the dining room continued its quiet conversation around the small absence.

Vellaine looked over when Theron came in. She did not invite him; she did not have to.

He sat down across from her. She watched the dusk for another moment and then said, without turning, "Have you done anything reckless yet."

"I closed a drawer."

"That's progress."

The cook's pie was lamb and root vegetables in a crust the color of an autumn field, and it was, the cook had been correct to want to try again, very good. They ate without much conversation. Vellaine read three more pages of the untitled book between courses, and at one point looked up to point a finger at a passing carafe and have it brought to her without anyone needing to interpret the gesture. The room was, for an hour, the kind of room that did not require its occupants to perform.

When the lamps in the dining room were lit, the windows turned black and the gondola became a brighter, smaller place than it had been all day. The drakes were invisible now beyond the glass except as occasional silver flashes when the running lights caught them.

"I'm going to bed," he said, eventually.

Vellaine turned a page. "It's not even third watch."

"I know."

"Then you'll lie there and think." She did not look up from the page.

"I might. I'm going to try not to."

She closed the book on her thumb again, that small bookmark she didn't seem to need a strip of paper for, and looked at him properly for what might have been the first time since the morning's first words about his pin. "You should know," she said, "that nobody ever holds their pin straight in the third week. Either it slips, or you stop touching it. Either is fine."

"Which was yours."

"That's a question you ask in the second year. Have a good night, Mr. Mavik."

He took the long way back to his cabin, past the lounge, which was empty now, the brown-coat woman gone, her papers gone with her, the corner table reset for breakfast, and up the corridor. Calden was at his station, writing something in the steward's book. He looked up.

"Good evening, sir."

"Good evening."

"Drakes are settling. The crossing should be a smooth one tonight."

"Wisp said something about fourth watch."

"She always does." Calden returned to the book. "If you want a cup of something brought up, sir, the kettle's on at first and fourth watch. Pull the bell."

"Thank you."

He let himself into his cabin and closed the door.

The lamp went on at the first turn of the wick. He hung his coat, then took the slate out of the breast pocket and laid it on the writing desk — face-down, not in the drawer this time, because the drawer business had been the afternoon's exercise and he had passed it. The collar of his shirt came undone next. He sat on the edge of the bed.

He picked the slate up and turned it over.

For a count he didn't keep he looked at it, and then he set it face-down again.

*[Investigator — Off duty. Sleep well.]*

He couldn't tell, again, whether the System was being dry on purpose, and chose, again, to read it as such. What he found was that he was smiling at the slate, and that he didn't particularly mind being seen doing it.

He turned the lamp down.

In the dark, the harness chains' song was a softer, slower thing, settling into the long line of the night flight. He could feel Mottled's downbeat through the deck, early, faithful to her temperament, and Gravel's correction half a breath after it, and Petal underneath both of them, doing her work.

He turned over once and slept.
