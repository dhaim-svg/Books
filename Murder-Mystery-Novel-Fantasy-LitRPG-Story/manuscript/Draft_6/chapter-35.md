# Chapter 35 — The Question She Came to Ask

Halsa took a slow breath. Not for the count of it — for the shape.

"All right," she said. "I'll ask it now."

"Ask it."

She did not look at the case book. She looked at the wall above Theron's left shoulder, the way people looked at a wall when they were not borrowing strength from a face. Her hands stayed flat on her knees. The cup on the desk was a cup. The sealed sheet beside it was still sealed.

"On the Wyrm," she said. "After it landed. Or in the days after, in Halverstow, with the magistrate. Or with the captain. Or between the two of you, in a room with a door shut on it. Did anyone use his real name."

For the space of a count, Theron said nothing.

"His real name," he said.

"Joren Aldis." She said it without effort. "Not the lord's name. Not the man he was wearing. The name he had when he wasn't working. Did anyone use it. To his face if there had still been a face to use it to, or in a room about him."

He did not answer at once. He set his hands on his knees the way her hands were set on hers. He looked at Sable at the wall.

Sable looked at him.

"Yes," he said. "Someone did."

"Who."

"Sable."

Halsa moved her eyes from the wall to Sable, slowly, the way a person moved their eyes who had been holding a particular question for a long time and did not want to spend it badly.

"Miss Verrin," she said.

"Yes."

"You used it."

"I used it."

"When."

Sable did not look at her notebook. Her notebook was closed on her knee where it had been the whole laying-out, the pencil through the spine, and she did not move to open it. She set it on the small chair beside her instead, the way a person set a thing down who meant her hands free.

"The morning the Wyrm landed at Halverstow," she said. "The fifth of October. In the captain's day-cabin. Theron came in from the aft rail. The harbor was quiet. There was a coal barge working the far slip — I remember the barge because it was the thing the light kept catching on while we were talking."

"Go on."

"I had taken his surname out of the Berric Toll correspondence the night before. Toll uses the full name to sign off the contract particulars. I had read it at the alcove on the third night without saying it; I had wanted Theron to find it himself. He hadn't, by the morning we landed, and I had decided I was not going to wait for him to."

"You said the name first."

"I said it first. He sat down. He took the small cloth bag out of his coat pocket — the bag with the things from the cabin pocket — and put it on the desk between us, and I said the name again, and he said it, and after that he used it."

"And then."

"And then I asked if I could walk through what I had seen." She had not raised her voice. She had not lowered it, either. "He said yes. I walked through the three kindnesses first. The steward thanked by name at boarding. The ginger sweet to the airsick child at the second dinner. The elderly passenger at the gangway step. Then I walked through the accent — the harbor vowel in *harbor* at the first dinner. Then the working-mage callus that Theron had registered at boarding and had not asked himself why a lord would have. Then the sentence about the niece, which he had said at the third dinner."

Halsa's hands did not move.

"The niece," she said.

"He had said one sentence about her at a meal. About a small object she had carved for him at six. He had said she would be unimpressed he hadn't lost it yet."

"That was the wooden fish."

"That was the wooden fish."

"You knew it was the niece's."

"Not on the day," Sable said. "I knew the tone. The tone wasn't a lord's tone for any of the things a lord would have a tone like that for. The day I named the fish was after Pessel."

Halsa nodded once. She did not say *Pessel*; she had heard it through the laying-out, and she was not going to ask anything she did not need to ask now.

"Anything else."

"Two things," Sable said. "After the five details, the System updated the quest. It had been *Solve the Death Aboard the Skybound Wyrm.* It became *Solve the Death of Joren Aldis.* Theron looked at the line. He did not say anything about it. He turned the page on his slate and we kept going."

"That was the first time the System used his name."

"Yes."

"To him."

"To both of us. He read it; I saw him read it."

Halsa took that. She did not nod for it. She gave it the small still space a person gave a piece of information that mattered and was not for showing it mattered.

"You said two things."

"The second thing was a promise." Sable's left hand went, without her looking down, to the inside of her coat where the inside pocket was. She did not take anything out. She rested two fingers against the cloth there and left them. "Two days after the landing, at the foot of the Halverstow gangway, I told Theron that someone had to tell Joren's family properly. Not a magistrate's letter. A person. He heard it, and he did not argue."

"You said his family."

"His family."

"You did not say *the deceased's family*. You did not say *the man's family*."

"I did not."

"Are you still going."

"I am still going."

Halsa took that, too. She moved her right hand, the first move of her hands since they had gone flat on her knees, and she put it on the back of her left hand on her left knee. She did not look at Theron. She looked at Sable.

"Where to."

"His sister and his niece," Sable said. "Pessel has the box. I have a contact in Sannholt, through a separate post, with the magistrate's permission. I'm going down after Halverstow as the case allows. I have a thing of his to give back."

She did not name the fish. She did not need to.

Halsa looked at her two fingers against the inside of her coat.

"You have it on you."

"I do."

"You've been carrying it."

"Since the twenty-eighth of October, when Theron handed it to me at the clearing-house yard. It traveled in this pocket to Sannholt with me, and back up the coach road, and it is in the pocket now."

Halsa was quiet for a count.

"All right," she said. "Thank you."

"You're welcome."

She had said *thank you* the way a person said *thank you* who had been asked an unanswerable question for a long time and had been answered. It was not a thanks for kindness. It was a thanks for the absence of the wrong answer, which was a thanks of a different shape.

---

Halsa looked at her hands. Then she looked at the case book. Then she looked at Theron.

"I'm going to give you something," she said. "Not for the page. I am not going to dispute the page. The page is what it is, and we both know it is, and I have already said I will not dispute it, and I am not going back on that. This is not for the page. This is for the question I asked."

"All right."

"I had a brother," she said. "He was seventeen. His name was Tarrin."

Theron did not write the name down. The case book stayed closed under his hands.

"Eight years ago," she said, "Joren took a scare-job in a hill village three days' ride north of Mirren. Magical intimidation. Frighten a debtor into paying. He was not the only mage on the work — there were two of them — and the work was the kind of work that occasionally spilled. He knew it was the kind of work that occasionally spilled. He took it. He had been trying to put aside enough to stop taking that kind of work for two years before that, and he wasn't there yet, and his sister's children were eating, and he took the rest of the fee."

"All right."

"It spilled. The debtor was a man named Hemmel. The debtor's son was eighteen and was at the door of the house when the work went in. My brother was not the debtor's son. He was the boy from the next house. He came over because he had heard a noise and the Hemmel boy was a year older than him and they had been in school together, and the working caught both of them, and the Hemmel boy lived and my brother did not."

"All right."

"Joren was not in the room. He was in the yard. The other mage on the work was the one who set the glamour. Joren took the rest of the fee that night. He took it knowing the work had gone messy, and he did not ask the name of the boy who had died. He did not, in the eight years between that and the Wyrm, ever ask the name. I checked. Pessel did not have the name. The Copper Finch did not have the name. The Sannholt landlady did not have the name. He did not write it anywhere. He did not look it up. He carried the general weight of *a job that had spilled* and he carried it the rest of his working life, and he did the careful thing with the next eight years of money on behalf of his sister and his niece, and what he never did was ask whose blood the eighth year of that money had started in."

She stopped.

She did not look at Theron when she stopped. She looked at the wall above his left shoulder again.

"That is the part of it that is mine to say," she said. "I am not telling you to think anything about it. I am not telling Miss Verrin to think anything about it. I am telling you it because you asked for the page and the page does not say it and I asked for the name and the name was used. The book is even now."

"All right."

"It is not even on his side. It will not ever be even on his side. He is dead. That is the work I did, and the work I did is the work I did, and I am not telling you it was anything but. I am telling you the shape of the grievance because I asked you a question and you answered it and the answer was *yes*, and the answer being *yes* is what makes me able to sit in this cabin and not make this morning any harder than it has to be."

"All right."

"That is all."

"All right."

She looked back down at her hands. Then she looked at the sealed sheet beside the cup.

She picked it up.

She broke the wax with her thumb the way a person broke the wax of a note they had been carrying since Friday evening at a boarding-house door. She unfolded the sheet and read it once. The reading took the count of a long breath. She folded it again and set it on the desk beside the cup.

"Davren is courteous," she said.

"He is."

"He's set out what the writ does and what the writ doesn't. He has set out that I am not under arrest aboard this gondola. He has set out that I am asked to remain in cabin six for the remainder of the morning service and to disembark with the two of you at Sannholt, where the magistrate's table will be ready." She paused. "He closes it: *I am obliged to you for the difficulty of this morning, Miss Renn.* He didn't have to write that."

"No," Theron said. "He didn't."

"I'll do that."

"All right."

She put her right hand into the right pocket of her coat and brought out a small flat object — a piece of folded paper, folded once, the size of a calling card — and set it on the desk beside the case book. She did not slide it toward him.

"That is the address in Sannholt where the trigger architecture is written down," she said. "It is written down because Joren told me not to write it down, and I did not write it down for six years, and then in the seventh year I wrote it down once, on a single sheet, kept folded, in a stone box, in a back room of a house that does not stand any more. The page exists. Miss Verrin can collect it on her way south, or Davren can send a clerk, or it can stay where it is, and I am telling you it is there because the page being uncollected is the only piece of me that would still be working after the morning service today, and I am not leaving working pieces."

Theron did not pick it up.

"All right."

"You can have the address read to you and you can decide what to do with it."

"All right."

She set both hands flat on her knees again, palms down, the way she had set them at the close of the laying-out. She looked at the case book, then at the sealed-and-now-unsealed note, then at the folded paper.

"That's everything I had to put down," she said. "I am ready to go where you say I am to go for the rest of the morning."

"You're to stay in this cabin," Theron said. "Sable will be in cabin four. I'll be in cabin two. The corridor will be the corridor. The morning service runs to second bell. The gondola sets down at Sannholt at the third bell. At the third bell I'll knock on this door and we will walk off together to the magistrate's table that Davren has been good enough to arrange."

"All right."

"Is there anything in the cabin you'd like, between now and the third bell."

"No."

"Is there anything you'd like asked of the steward."

"No."

"Is there anything you'd like Miss Verrin or me to bring you from our own cabins."

"No."

"All right."

He read the line through once. He gave it the same spare nod he had given the System every morning of the week. He stood.

Sable stood with him.

He took the leather strap off the chair-back where he had set it when he sat down. He picked up the case book and slid it into the strap and tied the strap. He did not pick up the folded paper on the desk. He left it where Halsa had set it.

"At the third bell," he said.

"At the third bell."

Sable did not say anything. She picked up her notebook from the small chair and put it under her arm with the pencil still through the spine. At the door she stopped and opened it.

Halsa did not stand. She stayed on the porthole bench with her hands flat on her knees. She looked at Theron once. She did not look at Sable.

"Thank you," she said, again, and this time she was saying it to both of them, and this time the thanks was for the morning that had not been made harder than it had to be, which was a thanks of a different shape again.

"You're welcome," Theron said.

He went out.

Sable followed.

The door of cabin six closed behind them with the small definite sound of a latch falling into its keep, which was the sound the door of a cabin made on a small southbound gondola at the hour past first bell on the seventeenth of November when the hum through the deck was the hum of two drakes finding their working thermal and the corridor was empty and the morning service had four and a half hours to run.

Theron stood in the corridor a count.

He set the case book under his arm.

"Cabin two," he said.

"Cabin four," Sable said.

They walked together to the doors of their own cabins. At his door he stopped. He looked at her over the strap of the case book.

"That was well asked, and well answered," he said. "Both of you."

"It was a question," Sable said. "It needed the answer it needed."

He considered that.

"Yes."

She went into cabin four. He went into cabin two.

The cabin was small. Two portholes, a writing desk, a chair, a padded bench under the porthole. The hum through the deck was the Wyrm's hum at a lower note, because there were two drakes pulling and not three.

He set the case book on the desk.

He sat on the bench.

He did not open the book.
