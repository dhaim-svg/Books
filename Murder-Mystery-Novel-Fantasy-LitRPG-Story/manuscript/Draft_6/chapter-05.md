# Chapter 5 — Academy Mode

The captain's chartroom was at the top of the forward stair, behind a door with a brass plate that read *Capt. M. Joriss* and below that, in a smaller hand, *please knock before entering*. Theron knocked.

"Mr. Mavik."

The voice came through the door already placing him. He let himself in.

The chartroom was the size of a generous wardrobe and ran the long way back into the gondola's nose, two chart tables along one wall, a narrow window the captain could put his elbow in if he wanted, and the smell of pipe smoke that had not been smoked in this room but was carried in on the captain's coat. Captain Joriss was perhaps fifty, with the shoulders of a man who had spent twenty years correcting other men's lines, and the steady, unsurprised face of someone who had been told the worst possible news on three previous flights and found this one was not quite that.

"Calden has given me the shape of it," he said. "I'd like it from you."

Theron gave it to him. Cabin twelve, deck four. Interior bolt drawn. Porthole, deck hatch, chain trunk all sealed. No bottle, no glass used, no powder, no visible wound. Three to five hours dead at discovery. He gave it in the order he had written it down, not the order he had observed it, because the written order was the one the captain would carry to port.

Joriss listened without interrupting. When Theron was finished he set both hands flat on the chart table and was quiet for a moment.

"You'll want the wireless held."

"Until the last hour, if you can. A general message ahead would invite a magistrate's runner aboard at port before I'd finished. I'd rather hand them a closed case than an open one."

"You think you can close it in," the captain said, and glanced at the deck-watch clock above the door, "eighteen hours."

"I think I can build the case I'd give a magistrate's office in eighteen hours."

"That's a different sentence."

"Yes, sir."

Joriss looked at him a moment longer. Whatever he had expected of a first-posting Investigator, this was either close to it or far enough off that he had stopped measuring. He nodded once.

"You'll have your wireless silence to the last hour. I'll have Calden close the first-class corridor at both ends. The cabin stays sealed under the master, off the ring. The manifest is being brought to you." He paused. "I will need to address the passengers at noon. They've already started asking my stewards questions I would prefer my stewards not be asked."

"Noon is acceptable."

"What would you like me to say."

Theron had thought about this on the stair. "That Lord Vannic has died in his cabin. That the cabin was sealed and that the cause is not immediately apparent. That an Investigator class is aboard and is working the case. That the gondola will continue its scheduled flight to Halverstow." A small breath. "That further questions should be brought to me directly. Not to your stewards."

"Plain."

"It travels best plain, sir."

The captain almost smiled — a man hearing one of his own working principles repeated back to him by someone he hadn't expected to know it.

"Eighteen hours, Mr. Mavik."

"Sir."

---

His cabin was warmer than he'd left it. Calden had already been by; the second pot of coffee stood on the writing desk under a cloth, and beside it, in a flat leather wallet, the passenger manifest copied out in the steward's careful hand for the first and second classes both.

Under the manifest was a second, smaller sheet the captain had sent up with it: the Wyrm's standing-detection record for the night just past, the kind of routine sheet a ship carried and handed to an investigator boarding a death as a matter of course. He read it down. The rig had logged the cabins along deck four through the third watch and turned up nothing it knew how to call cast magic — cabin twelve carried the same three letters as the empty rooms either side of it, *N.R.*, no registration. He filed it with the porthole and the deck hatch and the unbroken dust seals, one more way the room had stayed shut, and set it under the manifest again.

He hung his coat. He poured. He sat down.

The manifest was twenty-eight names on two sheets, and he read each sheet down the column once without categorizing. That was the discipline, the same one he had used yesterday on the smaller list. On the second pass the categorizing happened without him.

Motive against Lord Petric Vannic, taken at the surface and in working categories. He wrote them at the top of a fresh page in the small notebook and underlined them: *commercial. familial. romantic. financial. political. personal.* Then he started populating.

*Esherel, Mdm.* Commercial. The folio yesterday morning at breakfast, the wine dispute, the look on the dock when "Vannic" had crossed past her without greeting. A working merchant with an active grievance and a registered firm at risk; he had not yet found the specific shape of the dispute, but he could find it. He underlined the name once and put a small *1* beside it, meaning first to interview.

*Henner family.* Improbable but recordable. A family of four traveling to a wedding, the wife's cousin's. He could see no reason for any of them to be on the manifest for Lord Vannic's sake. He put a *5* beside the row, meaning a clearing interview rather than a working one, and moved on.

*Lerren and Stoll, university press.* Soil chemistry. He set the pencil down for a second and considered the sentence *soil chemistry as motive for the murder of a central-plains lord* and put a *5* beside both names without further comment.

*Brun, courier.* Contract delivery. He looked up the license code on the slate. The contract was for the delivery of bound proofs to a printer in Halverstow and had been let three weeks before sailing. Brun's previous twelve runs were in the registry, all clean. He put a *4* beside the name, which was *plausibly nothing but verify*.

*Verrin, S.* He looked at the entry for some seconds. The booking note still read *property*. Brown coat. Pencil in left hand. Eight days' prior booking. He could think of no version of *property paperwork as motive against Lord Vannic* that did not require a chain of coincidence he was unwilling to invent in advance of evidence. He put a *4* beside the name, the same as Brun. The category *verify, not pursue*.

Sable. He thought it deliberately, the name as the dining-room list had given it. He went on.

*Vellaine, Dr.* He paused on the entry. Juridical theory. Larent Institute. The Halveth Compact symposium, a verifiable destination booked three months prior. The look in the dining room yesterday morning when she had registered his pin. The *some of them favorable* yesterday at breakfast. She had been on the gondola two days and had asked nothing about Lord Vannic, which meant either that the man had nothing to do with her or that the man had everything to do with her and she was the kind of theorist who knew when to be quiet. He put a *3* beside the name. *Verify and ask once.*

*Tannin, M.* The post-departure amendment, cabin three, original name sealed under steward's discretion. He stopped on this row for longer than the others. A clerk on the ground had recorded an original entry and locked it. The amended row had been in the manifest since before Vannic boarded. The amendment was older than the death. That did not make it innocent. He put a *2* beside the name and underneath, in small letters, *speak to Calden re: sealed admin note.*

The second-class manifest then. Sixteen names. He worked the column the same way. Three he set aside outright — a clergy party of two and an apprentice carpenter traveling for a piece of work. Two merchants he put at *3*: one whose firm had crossed Vannic's in the registry, no current dispute on record but a history; the second whose registered cargo on the lower deck was high-value enough to want to know about. A small group of four sharing a cabin block at the stern — he wrote *check whether group travels together by booking or by reassignment*. Three solo male travelers between forty and sixty he put at *3* by default — the bracket the Conservatory had taught him filled the longest list and resolved the cleanest.

He read the page once. He put a fresh sheet over it. He copied the *1*s and the *2*s onto the fresh sheet, with one line of working note beside each. Esherel, Tannin. Two names in the top working bracket.

It was a working list. Not a conclusion. Categories, not certainties. The Conservatory's *Investigation in Closed Environments*, second year, Hadrik's seminar, had been very firm on the distinction, and Theron held to it now, conscious of the holding the way a man at a banister was conscious of holding the banister.

The System had been quiet through all of this. He waited until he had set the pencil down before he looked at the slate.

> *[Investigator — Insight +1. The shortlist is the easy work. The clearing list is the rest of it.]*

He read it twice and couldn't tell whether his class was being instructive or dry. He chose to read it as both. There were nine hours to do the rest of it before he had any business calling the list a case.

A knock at the door, two raps and light.

"Mr. Mavik?"

Sable's voice. He stood.

She was at the threshold with the second copy of the manifest in one hand, the cover folded open against her wrist, her thumb keeping it at the third page. Not the courier's manner. A reader's.

"Calden asked whether you wanted the second-class copy now or after lunch." She glanced past him at the desk and the underlined pages on it. "I told him I'd bring it up since I was on my way back to my cabin."

She had brought it herself. Something in the part of his head that filed corridor sounds without consulting him set down a mark against her name — she had walked past three cabin doors and a steward's station to bring the second copy of the manifest to a man she did not yet have any working relationship with. The part of his head that filed it offered no immediate interpretation. He took the copy.

"Thank you."

"Mm."

Her thumb came off the third page. He glanced down. She had stopped on the page that listed the second-class single-men bracket, which was the page he had spent the longest on.

He looked up. She was already turning to go.

"Miss Verrin."

She turned back.

"If anything has occurred to you about yesterday," he said, "about the corridor, about the rounds, that you would want a person to have. I'm a person who would want it."

She looked at him for the count of two. Then she said, "If anything occurs to me I'll bring it."

"Thank you."

"Mm."

She went. The door pulled to behind her. He stood with the second copy in his hand and did not, for a count he didn't keep, open it.

He was filing more than he was reading. He knew it, the way a man at the banister knows his grip is too tight without quite being ready to loosen it.

Then he sat back down and opened it.

---

The first-class lounge at noon was the quietest noon it had ever been.

The captain had made his address at the forward arch ten minutes before — Theron had stood at the back of the dining room and watched it land — and the news had gone through the room the way news did, in small movements of weight from chair to chair. The wife of the Henner family had taken her two children to the porthole bench and was talking to them in the steady undertone parents used for explanations they did not entirely believe themselves. Her husband had stayed at the table with his hands flat on the wood. Lerren and Stoll had stopped arguing about their briefcase and were sitting with it between them, not opening it.

Madam Esherel was at her corner table with the folio out again, and she was, for the first time since Theron had seen her, not reading it. The folio was open. Her hand was on the page. She was looking at the porthole.

He crossed the lounge to her without hurry.

"Madam Esherel."

She looked up. The look took a moment to arrive. She had been some distance away. Then it arrived all at once, and there it was, the merchant's face he had seen yesterday over the wine.

"Mr. Mavik. Sit, if you're sitting."

He sat across from her.

"I'll be brief." He waited a beat. "I'll need a conversation with you at some point today about Lord Vannic. Not now. I'd like the proper sit-down. I wanted you to know that the conversation is on the list."

"You wanted me to know that the conversation is on the list," she repeated.

"Yes."

She held his eye. Then she made a small sound that was either a laugh or an acknowledgment that a laugh would have been the wrong shape for the moment.

"You're being very tidy about it."

"I'd like to be."

"Was it him."

The question landed before he had quite expected it. He looked at her for a moment, then said, "I'll explain what I can when I sit with you. I can't yet."

"Was it me."

"Madam Esherel."

"I'm asking because I'd like to know how the rest of my day works, Mr. Mavik. If your answer is *that's also on the list*, that is a fine answer. I'd like it now."

"That's also on the list."

She set her hand flat over the open folio page and closed it. "Then I'll be in my cabin or here until you want me. I'd be grateful if it were soon, because I have a small business that does not run itself and I would like to know within the hour whether I am answering questions or working." She paused. "I disliked the man, Mr. Mavik. I made it known. I did not, in case it is helpful in your tidying, kill him."

"Noted."

"That isn't a yes."

"It's a noted."

She nodded once. She looked, briefly, almost amused. Then the amusement went away and she opened the folio back up and went back to looking at the porthole, the folio open under her hand and unread.

He stood.

Vellaine was at the small window table he had sat at with her the previous morning. She had her book closed in front of her and was watching the room the way she watched dusk — with no particular hurry about it.

He went over without sitting.

"Dr. Vellaine."

"Mr. Mavik."

"I'll want a conversation with you at some point today."

"Of course."

"Was there anything you noticed last night or this morning that you would tell a person who would want it."

She thought about that for a moment. The thinking was visible — the pause she made at a page when she was deciding whether a line was true.

"I heard Calden's first round at first watch," she said. "I heard his second. I did not hear his third. I noticed the third only because there wasn't a fourth." She looked up at him. "I had been awake since first watch. I was at the desk. I'm working on a paper. I heard the corridor as I'd hear it any morning, until I didn't."

"Did you hear anything from cabin twelve overnight."

"No."

"Did you hear the cabin's bolt."

She considered. "I would not have noticed the bolt. I do not know whether I would have heard it. I was at the desk with the lamp on and a particular section of a difficult argument under my hand. I was not listening for the corridor."

"Thank you."

"Mm."

He left her and crossed the lounge to the corner table by the window.

Sable had her papers spread again. The pencil was in her left hand. She had not been reading when he came in; she had been looking at the porthole, the same direction as Esherel, but with a more specifically directed look. At the harness chain where it crossed the porthole's line, perhaps, or at the angle of the light along the brass.

She looked up when his shadow reached the table.

"Mr. Mavik."

"Miss Verrin." He didn't sit. "I'm going to come and ask you formally about yesterday and last night when I've finished the corridor."

"All right."

"Is now bad."

"Now is fine," she said. "Now is when I would have rather you came."

He looked at her. She did not elaborate.

"Then I'll come back," he said, "in about an hour."

"Mm."

He turned to go. He had taken two steps before her last answer caught up with him — *now is when I would have rather you came* — the kind of answer a person said when they wanted the conversation done with so they could go back to the work they were doing, which was either property paperwork or something a witness wanted off her chest. Without turning around, and without the second pot of categorical thinking in him yet for whichever it was, he filed the line under *come back*, and went.

---

He passed cabin twelve on the way back to his own.

Calden was at the corridor's far end on his folding stool, his book open on his knee in the deliberate way of a steward who had been told to be visible and had elected to be useful as well. He looked up at Theron, did not move otherwise, and acknowledged him with the small inclination of head that on Calden meant *I have not left this post and will not leave this post*.

Theron set his hand flat against the brass plate of cabin twelve.

The wood was the temperature of the corridor. The master had been turned in the lock and the bolt could not be drawn, not from inside or out, because the body inside had drawn it once and would not draw it again. He held his palm against the plate a moment longer than he needed to.

The working list was made. He had a captain's silence on the wireless to the last hour, Esherel at *1* and a sealed administrative amendment at *2* and a clearing column to walk through before either of them. He had the method his class had been built to use, and he had eighteen hours.

He took his hand off the plate.

He went to find Calden, who had a sealed administrative note to discuss with him, and the second pot of coffee in his cabin would be cold by the time he got back to it, which was fine, because he had not poured himself the first cup yet.

> *[Investigator — Quest active. Seventeen hours, fifty-one minutes to Halverstow Port.]*

He read it. He went.
