# Chapter 13 — Petor Vann

Sable was already at the bottom of the aft stairwell when Theron came down, and she had a small square of paper in her hand folded twice at right angles.

"Third-class register," she said. "Calden had the third-class copy in a different box." She held the paper out without explanation.

Theron took it. The fold opened on a passenger name entered in the booking officer's round, hurried hand: *Petor Vann, cabin three-T, paid passage Mirren Dock to Halverstow Port, second-class berth declined, third-class confirmed.*

"Petor Vann," he said.

"Mm."

He turned it over. The reverse was blank. "The register was Calden's, you said — when did he—"

"When I asked him about the amendment. He went to his station box, came back with this one." She took the paper back, folded it the same way she'd had it. "He'd been holding on to it."

Theron put a hand on the stairwell railing. Below them the third-class corridor ran level and close, its lamp brackets lower than on the upper decks, the brass less polished. The dragon's pull was steadier down here, closer to the belly of the gondola. You felt it through the soles of your feet more than through your legs.

"And Calden knew," Theron said.

"Calden knew someone had paid cash for a third-class berth the same week the first-class booking under M. Tannin came in." Sable tucked the paper inside her coat. "I don't think he put it together. I think he was holding it because it didn't fit, and he was waiting for someone to tell him what it meant."

"Three-T," he said.

"Third class, starboard side."

He had never been in the third-class corridor. He knew it from the manifest, from the geometry of the gondola's structural drawings on the captain's gallery wall. It had twelve berths rather than four, a shared observation window at the far end, and no individual porthole allocation. The passengers booked there were the kind the manifest categorized as *economy, single travel* and that Theron had ranked in the fourth tier: too far from the first-class corridor to have reached cabin twelve, alibi by architecture.

"Lead," he said.

---

The third-class starboard passage was painted a yellower white than the upper decks, and it smelled of boot leather and last night's soup from the galley two compartments over. The lamp at the far end needed trimming. The gondola's hang had a slight asymmetry here, a lean in the deck plates, barely measurable but present under the boot.

Sable walked ahead of him, not quickly. She stopped at the third door on the right and knocked twice with her knuckle — not the flat of her hand.

From inside, silence. Then the sound of something being moved.

Theron had the manifest page in his head: Petor Vann, cabin three-T, third class. He had the paper Sable had folded in her pocket, and the whole first-class column arranged in his mind like a set of slides in a lecture lantern. None of it had prepared him for the door opening.

The man who opened it was thirty years old at the outside, with the wrinkled look of someone who hadn't slept well in three days and was still wearing yesterday's waistcoat. He was medium height, medium brown hair, and he had the face of a person who had been hoping this particular knock would not come. He was recognisably the same person as the composed mage on the dock — but three days in a third-class bunk, in a cabin the size of a wardrobe, had stripped away the controlled setting that had made that register sustainable.

Theron looked at him.

The man looked at Theron.

"Lord Vannic," Theron said.

The name had come out of him with no drama, no deduction, no moment of working backward through evidence — it had arrived the way recognition always arrives, complete and sudden, the face fitting itself over memory while his lungs were still catching up. Lord Petric Vannic, alive. The dead man in cabin twelve was not Vannic. Theron could feel the entire column of his case rearranging itself like a stack of papers that had been held at one corner and released.

Sable said nothing. She was watching the door.

The man who was not Petor Vann made a sound that was not quite a word and stepped backward. He did not shut the door.

---

The cabin was barely wide enough for the three of them. It had one bunk, a small table screwed to the bulkhead, and a peg on the wall with a coat Theron recognized: pale grey, a half-cape at the shoulder, too fine for third class by two price brackets at minimum. The coat had been Vannic's. The man now wearing a wrinkled waistcoat and no coat had, apparently, been wearing that one when he boarded.

"Sit down," Theron said. It came out more gently than he intended.

Vannic sat on the bunk. He had large hands, and he pressed them flat against his knees in the way of someone whose hands have nothing else to do.

"I don't know what—" he started.

"The dead man in cabin twelve is not you," Theron said. "That much I've established on my own in the last thirty seconds. Who he is, and who hired him to perform the role, I will need you to explain. Start with his name."

Vannic put his face in his hands for a moment. Not dramatic — tired. When he looked up, he said, "Joren. He said I could call him Joren."

*[Investigator — Quest updated. The dead man has a name. Cabin twelve is not a locked room. It is Joren's room.]*

Theron read it twice without moving. He was aware of Sable settling against the bulkhead behind him with her arms crossed, and he was aware that she was not going to say anything yet, and he was glad of it.

"Joren," he repeated. "Last name?"

"He didn't give one. He — we arranged it through a factor, not directly. The factor said his name was Joren and that he was reliable. He was." Vannic's hands pressed harder against his knees. "He was extremely professional. He said he'd done impersonations before, that the illusion work was straightforward, that as long as I stayed out of the first-class corridor for the duration of the flight—"

"Why the gondola?" Theron asked.

Vannic looked up.

"A closed transit with a fixed passenger list and a three-day window — that's an unusual choice for a disappearance. Why not go overland?"

Vannic's mouth pressed together. "The engagement announcement goes into the Mirren Gazette on the twenty-second. If I'm not in Mirren on the twenty-second, I am officially — the contract specifies that a failure to appear at the formal introduction constitutes a withdrawal, which means—"

"Which means if you're not there, the contract dissolves," Theron said.

"On the twenty-third," Vannic said. "I need to not be findable until the twenty-third. After that, the Halveth Compact symposium is — Halverstow is very far from Mirren. I thought, if there was a record of Lord Vannic aboard the Wyrm, an official manifest entry, then no one would think to look for me in Halverstow. That I'd simply have travelled on. Taken the air. Shown my face." He looked at his hands. "I didn't account for — I didn't think anyone would die."

Theron had no immediate response to this, which was unusual. He filed the engagement-contract timeline in the space where Esherel's motive had been and noted, distantly, that the column he'd spent three days building was not so much wrong as it was about a man who was currently sitting in front of him with both hands on his knees.

"The forced betrothal," Sable said, from behind Theron. "Tell him the terms."

Vannic looked at her as if he'd momentarily forgotten she was there. Then he looked at Theron. "Duke Esseven's daughter. She is forty-four. I am twenty-eight. The match was arranged by my father's estate board while my father was ill, and I was not consulted, and I — I know how it sounds." He stopped.

"It sounds like a reason," Theron said.

Vannic seemed not to have expected that. He blinked.

"What I need to understand," Theron continued, "is the arrangement. You hired Joren through a factor. He boarded the Wyrm in your place. You boarded separately as Petor Vann. Who knew?"

"The factor. Joren, obviously. And—" Vannic stopped. A muscle in his jaw moved. "He was supposed to stay in the cabin. He was supposed to be reclusive and difficult and eat alone and leave on arrival. He wasn't supposed to—" He stopped again.

"He was supposed to not die," Theron said.

"Yes."

Theron thought about the dead man's coat, which was Vannic's coat, and which Joren had apparently worn with Vannic's face and Vannic's manner and, as Sable had reported at the dining-room table, an occasional slip of a coastal accent when he was tired. He thought about the carved wooden fish in the outer pocket — small, worn smooth, the kind of thing a person carries and forgets they are carrying.

"The factor's name," he said.

"Berric. Berric Toll. He operates out of the Mirren west harbour." Vannic gave the address in a flat voice that suggested he had made his decision about cooperation somewhere around the moment he opened the door. "He can confirm everything. I have his correspondence."

"I'll want the correspondence."

Vannic nodded. He reached under the bunk and produced a small oilcloth packet, the careful preservation of a man who was nervous about a great number of things.

Theron took it without opening it.

"One more question," Sable said. She said it to Vannic with the straightforward courtesy she brought to most things, not unkind. "Joren. You only met him the once, through the factor?"

"Twice," Vannic said. "Once at the factor's office, to be measured and matched and photographed for the glamour work. Once at Mirren Dock, to confirm the boarding." He looked at her directly. "He shook my hand and told me to enjoy Halverstow and not to worry about the paperwork end. I thought he was being professional. He probably was."

Sable made a small sound that was not quite agreement and not quite grief and didn't require a response.

Theron looked at the oilcloth packet in his hand, and then at Vannic, and said: "You will stay in this cabin until the gondola lands. You will not correspond with anyone on this vessel. You will not contact the factor before I have spoken to the captain." He paused. "You will eat breakfast. There's a tray service for third class at seventh bell."

Vannic stared at him. "Is that — are you giving me instructions about breakfast?"

"The case runs better if everyone on this gondola is fed," Theron said. "It is a practical consideration."

---

They came back up the aft stairwell in the direction they'd descended, and Theron stopped on the landing where the second-class passage met the service corridor, because the oilcloth packet was in his hand and the column in his mind had completely changed shape and he needed to stand in one place for a moment before he did the next thing.

Sable stopped beside him. She did not say anything. The lamp above the landing ticked as the gondola shifted on a thermal.

"Joren," Theron said. The name sat differently in his mouth than *the dead man* had. He was aware of this. He did not remark on it.

"Yes," Sable said.

"The case is not what I thought it was." He said this without inflection, by way of inventory. The corpse in cabin twelve was an illusionist named Joren who had taken contract work impersonating a lord who was alive and hiding two decks below from a marriage he had not chosen. Esherel's forty-one thousand crowns were still real. Calden's twelve-minute absence was still real. The locked geometry of cabin twelve was still real. The dead man's method of death remained unknown. All of that was still on the page. The page had simply acquired a new first line.

"Esherel didn't kill him," Sable said.

"No."

"Because she didn't know he wasn't the lord."

"No." Theron considered this. "Which means whoever killed him did know. If you don't know who Joren actually is, killing him is coincidence at best, collateral at worst. Knowing he's Joren — knowing the man under the glamour — that's a target."

Sable tilted her head. "Someone killed Joren knowing Joren was Joren."

"And wanted the body to be found as Vannic. Or didn't care which it was found as." He pressed his thumbnail against the edge of the oilcloth packet. "The factor. Or someone who knew the factor. Or someone in Joren's working history who knew his face under the glamour."

"Third class," Sable said.

"Yes. I've been looking at the wrong manifest." He looked up. The lamp ticked again. "I owe Esherel a conversation."

"You owe her an apology," Sable said. Not unkindly.

"I owe her accurate information delivered in a professional manner," Theron said. "The apology is implicit."

The corner of Sable's mouth moved. "Start with the word *sorry*. It takes four seconds."

Theron considered this. "I'll start with *I have new information that I think you'll find significant*."

"Mm."

"That is also accurate."

"It is," she said. "It's also going to make her laugh at you, and then she'll be in a better mood for the rest of it, so I suppose it works either way."

*[Investigator — Insight +1. Two manifests, two worlds, one locked room. The question is not who had access. The question is who had reason.]*

Theron read it once, tucked the notation away, and went up the stairs carrying Vannic's oilcloth packet toward the captain's gallery, with the name Joren now occupying the space in the case that had previously belonged to the dead man, and the dead man having acquired, in the space of twenty minutes, a face and two handshakes and a factor and a coastal town he had presumably come from.

The lamp at the top of the landing was well-trimmed and bright. The one at the bottom had needed work. He had not noticed until now.
