# Chapter 22 — Vannic's Full Accounting

Theron had spread the Fell interview notes across the small writing table by the window before he remembered there was no window. The room at the Arms was one of the interior ones. Cheaper by the week, Vannic had explained when they'd first visited, as though the admission embarrassed him and he wanted credit for the candor.

He restacked the notes along the edge of the table and knocked twice on the door to the room at the end of the corridor.

---

Vannic was eating breakfast at eleven in the morning, which Theron filed under *consistent character*, and had not shaved, which he filed under *difficult week*. He stood when they came in and then appeared to decide this was excessive and sat back down. The plate in front of him held what had been poached eggs and was now the evidence of poached eggs.

"The note said this morning," Vannic said.

"It's still morning," Theron said.

Sable took the chair by the window. Theron sat across from Vannic and opened his notebook.

"I want to run through the contract process again," Theron said. "From the beginning. Toll's office — when you first contacted him."

Vannic's expression shifted in the way of someone who had been hoping the subject was already done. "I went over this."

"You went over parts of it. I need the full picture." Theron set his pencil on the notebook. "The payment terms specifically, and what Toll told you about how the operation worked — who was responsible for what."

Vannic looked at his egg plate. A passing impulse, quickly suppressed. "I hired Toll through the estate management — he'd been my factor for three years at that point. I went to his office in Mirren west harbour personally, in the sixth month. The usual estate business first, then the other matter."

"Meaning the impersonation."

"Meaning that." Vannic shifted in his chair. "He took it in stride, which told me this wasn't his first such commission."

Theron noted: *not first commission*.

"He named a fee," Vannic continued. "I won't tell you the number."

"You will, actually."

A pause. "Three thousand crowns. Half at signing, half on completion of the passage."

"Paid to Toll directly?"

"Paid to Toll's account, yes. What he paid the — what he paid Joren I was not told."

*Joren.* The name came out with the kind of deliberate, controlled care that meant Vannic had been practicing using it.

"What other terms were in the contract?"

"Discretion was explicit — a standard clause, nothing unusual. And there was a clause about oversight." Vannic looked at his hands. "Toll said there would be a person on the passage who was responsible for ensuring the arrangement ran without difficulty. He described it as standard practice for a commission of this kind."

Theron looked up from his notes. "Did Toll name this person?"

"Not at signing." Vannic met his eyes, briefly. "He said the name after I pressed him. At the end of the meeting, when I was already at the door. As a — reassurance, I took it. He said Roven would be keeping watch."

"*Keeping watch.* Those are the words he used?"

"He said, 'Roven will have an eye on the passage.' I thought he meant —" Vannic gestured with his fork, which was still in his hand for no reason either of them acknowledged. "I thought he meant generally. Administrative oversight. I didn't think he meant *on the gondola*."

"When did you start thinking it might mean on the gondola?"

"When you told me Joren was dead on the gondola." The fork went back to the table. "Before that, I confess, the nature of Roven's oversight was not my primary concern."

*[Investigator — Insight +1. He genuinely didn't know Roven was aboard. The surprise in Ch. 20 was real.]*

"Did Toll say anything about what Roven's role was in the network? What kind of work he did normally?"

Vannic considered this with more care than he usually gave questions. "He said Roven was the person who had found the illusionist — had, as he put it, *vouched for the quality of the work*. And that they'd worked together before on several occasions and the arrangement had always been satisfactory."

"Toll described Roven as the person who sourced Joren."

"More than that." Vannic frowned. "He said Roven was responsible for the — the talent side. Meaning the people. Toll handled the brokering and the contractual structure. Roven supplied the names."

Theron wrote: *Toll = broker/contracts. Roven = talent source. Joren was Roven's placement.* He underlined the last line, then thought better of it and set the pencil down.

Sable had said nothing. She was looking at the window, which let in the street noise from below: someone arguing about a cart, a child, the intermittent clop of a horse being led. Her notebook was open on her knee.

"How many names?" Theron asked.

Vannic looked at him.

"You said Roven supplied names. Did Toll tell you how many people were in that part of the network? How many illusionists Roven was placing?"

"He said — he implied it was more than one. A roster, he called it. People with specific skills, specific track records." Vannic picked up his knife, put it down again. "He said Joren was the best he had for this particular commission because Joren had worked this class of client before. Meaning quality of performance. He was reassuring me."

"What else did Toll say about Roven? Any other details — where Roven worked, how to contact him?"

"No. Toll said quite specifically that Roven's contact was Toll's concern and not mine." A careful pause. "He said this after I asked a similar question. He was not hostile about it, but he was clear. Roven was not my contact."

"And on the payment side — was there anyone else named? Any other parties to the arrangement?"

"Not by name." Vannic thought. "Toll said there were people on the cargo side of the passage who were Toll's regular accounts, and that the gondola run had been partly selected for that reason. That was the only reference to anyone else involved."

Theron noted: *cargo side — Toll regular accounts, gondola selected partly because of it.*

He looked at the note. Then at Vannic. "When you say the gondola run was partly selected for that reason — whose decision was it? Did you choose the Wyrm, or did Toll?"

Vannic had the grace to look uncomfortable. "Toll proposed the Halverstow route. I had a version of my own in mind, but his was — he made a case for it, and his case was reasonable."

Theron picked up his pencil. "Toll chose the passage."

"He proposed it," Vannic said, with what was clearly a distinction that mattered to him and no one else.

"He chose the passage," Theron said, and wrote it down.

He closed the notebook at the bottom of the second page. Vannic had given him the full accounting: the payment structure, the Roven description, the cargo-side note. Theron's question about additional network names had produced one more name, offered under light pressure: *Dosseren*, a clearing broker in Mirren west harbour who had processed the contract's fee payment at Toll's direction. Not a new thread, Theron thought, but confirmation of the financial shape.

"Is there anything else you haven't told me," Theron said, "that you've been deciding whether to tell me?"

Vannic looked at him steadily. "I was trying to decide about the cargo-side comment since you first asked about Fell."

"What about it?"

"Toll mentioned a name. When he was talking about the regular accounts on the cargo side." Vannic folded his hands on the table, a conscious gesture. "He said Fell was reliable. That she had done two jobs for him in spring and was known to him."

Theron looked at his notes. Fell. Toll calling Fell reliable to a client at the contract signing, not concealed, just not flagged as significant, filed away until pressed.

"You didn't mention this in earlier interviews."

"You asked about the people Toll told me about in the network. Fell's name wasn't in the network. She was a reference to competent regular work. I didn't connect it."

"You're connecting it now."

"You have Fell's name on your list. I have eyes." Vannic looked at the egg plate. "I'm trying to be useful. You've made it fairly clear what happens if I'm not."

*[Investigator — Quest progress: Toll-Roven-Fell triangle fully confirmed. Payment chain traceable. The gondola was not incidental.]*

Theron looked at his notebook for a moment. Then he said, "One more thing. Did Toll name anyone called Dosseren to you as a principal contact, or is that the only reference?"

"Only the reference," Vannic said. "He handled the fee. That's all I know of him."

Theron nodded, made a final note, and closed the notebook.

---

There was a small sitting room at the end of the Arms corridor that was empty at this hour: two armchairs angled toward a cold grate, a table with a crack running across its near edge. They took it because the corridor wasn't the place, and the street wasn't the place, and Sable said this was fine with a gesture that indicated she had made worse work-spaces serve.

"Roven placed Joren," Theron said. "Not as a one-off. As Roven's usual work — sourcing talent, vouching them into Toll's commissions. Joren had a track record with Roven. That means Roven knew who Joren really was."

"He knew what Joren looked like," Sable said. "Knew his real work. Not the face he wore on the Wyrm."

"Toll chose the Wyrm because of the cargo side. Because Fell was already in the manifest — as a regular account, not as a coincidence." He set his notebook on the cracked table and looked at it. "Joren didn't end up on a passage with a Toll contact by accident. The whole thing was threaded together."

"From Roven's end or from Toll's?"

"Both, I think." He turned the notebook over. "Toll picked the route for business reasons — the cargo accounts. Roven had Joren as a ready placement for the impersonation. The two ends of the arrangement fit together because Toll was managing the whole commercial picture." He looked at the grate. "The question is whether Roven knew Joren was going to die."

Sable didn't answer that. It wasn't a question for the sitting room at the Arms.

"Fell's cargo job numbers," he said. "When those trace through Toll's operation, they should confirm the freight-side connection to the Wyrm's Mirren departure. That tells us Fell's spring work wasn't incidental — she was being set up as a regular account for the passage."

"By Toll. Or Roven."

"More likely Toll." He looked at the notebook, turned a page. "Roven handles talent. Fell's work isn't talent work — it's cargo-verification, customs-assist. That's Toll's side of the arrangement." He paused. "Which means Roven and Toll had a clean division of their functions. They weren't interchangeable."

Sable said, "Joren was Roven's asset. Toll brokered the commission, but Joren was Roven's."

"And Roven was on the passage," Theron said quietly. "Aware of it. Watching it."

The sitting room was cold. Someone had let the fire go out mid-morning and not bothered to reset it. Through the floor came the distant rhythm of the Arms kitchen, a steady percussion that had been running since they sat down.

He picked up his pencil, set it beside the notebook, and let the full shape of it sit there for a moment. The Wyrm's Mirren departure was not a coincidence. Joren's presence on that specific passage was not incidental. Roven, who knew what Joren was, had been on the same gondola. Framed as oversight. Framed as reassurance.

The answer to why Joren had died was somewhere in that.

There was a knock at the sitting-room door. The Arms boy put his head in. "Post for Mavik," he said, and held out a sealed envelope.

Theron took it.

The seal was a small wax impression he didn't recognize. The direction on the outside was his name at the Arms, in a hand he didn't recognize either: careful, deliberate, the hand of someone who had been taught to write and had stopped practicing regularly somewhere along the way. The postmark was Sannholt.

He broke the seal.

---

*Mr. Mavik —*

*Your letter arrived yesterday. I am glad someone has written, though the circumstances are not what I hoped for when I first began to watch for Joren's post.*

*Joren Aldis was expected in Sannholt. He had written to say he would be here by the third week of the month at the latest, and I have been holding the box for him since. He is now past his expected date by eleven days, which I had put down to a delay on the passage, or a delay at port, as these things happen. Your letter tells me it was not a delay.*

*I cannot write the rest of what I have to say here. It is not post-coach business. What I can tell you is that Joren was coming to Sannholt for a reason that had nothing to do with the season or the coast roads, and that I have been waiting for him to arrive for two years, not three weeks. He wrote to me in spring to say the time was nearly right. I have a great deal to tell him and apparently can no longer do that.*

*If someone intends to come to Sannholt — and I think someone should — I am at the Copper Finch most evenings after the fourth bell, and all day on the days the post-coach runs.*

*— Pessel*

He read it twice. Then he set it down.

Sable read it and handed it back without comment.

"Two years," he said. "Pessel has been waiting for Joren for two years. Whatever Joren's business in Sannholt was, it wasn't personal in the way I was reading it — a friend, a contact, something convenient. Pessel says the time was *nearly right*."

"That phrase could mean anything."

"It could." He folded the letter carefully, running his thumbnail along the fold. "The address has been maintaining a registered box, paying fees, holding post for a man for two years. That is a specific kind of waiting." He put the letter in his notebook. "And Pessel won't say what it is. Not in writing."

The kitchen percussion downstairs continued its steady rhythm. He looked at his notes, at the Roven summary, at the Fell cargo numbers, at Pessel's letter folded into the back page.

Sable said, "I'll take the next post-coach."

He looked at her.

"Friday run," she said. "It's Wednesday. That's two days. I'll be in Sannholt by the weekend." She had her notebook open, not looking at it. "I need to tell Pessel in person before any magistrate's letter gets there, and Pessel clearly has something to say that won't travel by post. That's two good reasons and I only need one."

"I know," he said.

"You don't need to come." She said it without edge. Purely practical. "You need to stay here and follow the cargo numbers when they trace. Someone has to be in Halverstow when that comes back."

She was right. He turned this over for a moment, which was all the time it took to confirm she was right.

"The clearing house has the Friday run departing at the seventh bell," he said. "I'll send a note to the Arms booking office."

"I'll send it," she said. "I know where it is."

He nodded.

She closed her notebook and set it on the cracked table beside his. Through the window above the grate, the light had gone flat with the angle of early afternoon. Somewhere on the street below, the argument about the cart had either resolved or moved. The Arms kitchen went on with its steady work.

"Whatever Joren was going to do in Sannholt," Theron said, "he'd been planning it for two years. It was important enough to plan around."

"And someone didn't want him to arrive."

He looked at the folded letter in his notebook.

"We don't know that yet," he said.

Sable said nothing.

He picked up his pencil. "I'll have the cargo numbers with the magistrate's clerk this afternoon. When those trace, we'll know whether the freight connection runs directly back to Toll's regular accounts or whether there's another layer." He made a note at the bottom of the open page. "And you'll be in Sannholt Friday night, and we'll know what Pessel knows."

"And we'll know what Joren was going to do," she said.

"And we'll know that." He looked at the note. "Or at least more of it."

She stood, picked up her notebook, and tucked it under her arm. He followed her out of the cold sitting room and into the corridor, where the Arms afternoon was going about its business, carrying trays and correspondence and none of their concerns.
