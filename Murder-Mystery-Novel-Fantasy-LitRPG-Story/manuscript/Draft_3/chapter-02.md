# Chapter 2 — In the Air

The drakes' rhythm came through the hull while Theron was still deciding whether to be awake — three-part pull, then held, then release — the slight irregularity consistent with what the handler had said about Mottled, who rushed the downbeat. He'd been cataloguing it for some minutes before he noticed he was doing it. His class had been active for less than a day and was apparently revising its own opinion of its usefulness.

He got up.

The cabin was small and well-considered: two portholes, a writing desk with drawer handles worn smooth by previous occupants, a wardrobe that someone with optimistic luggage would have found tight. The lamp bracket was angled exactly right for reading in bed, and the desk sat positioned for natural morning light, and someone had thought about all of this and gotten it right. He found himself approving, which was not a reaction he'd expected to have about a wardrobe.

His case slate sat on the desk where he'd left it. The System had updated while he slept.

*[Passenger manifest — 12 first class, 16 second, 44 third (booked manifest). Note: one first-class booking amended post-departure.]*

He read it once, set the slate in his coat pocket, and went to find coffee, because there was no body and no crime and he had promised Warden Desh, specifically and out loud at the graduation ceremony, that he would manage a holiday without treating routine administrative notes as leads. The promise had seemed more achievable on the ground.

In the corridor, Calden was mid-round, moving with the particular efficiency of gondola staff who had done this route many times — the kind of competence that looked like nothing until you tried to replicate it. He produced a folded towel from somewhere in the same motion as his nod.

"First-class breakfast is served through the forward arch, sir. Calm air this morning."

"Mottled's rushing the downbeat," Theron said.

A beat. "Wisp's been adjusting the harness rigging since first watch." The tone of a man who had answered the same question twice already this morning.

"She holds?"

"She always holds, sir." Said with the absolute certainty of someone who had made that particular promise on every previous flight and been right each time. Theron found it genuinely reassuring, which surprised him. He asked directions to the observation deck.

---

The exterior promenade ran along the gondola's starboard side — a narrow, railed walkway with a long view down to a geography that had resolved from specific to abstract sometime in the night. A river ran below. Hills rose in a general direction. The whole of it had the particular distance of altitude, where cities became suggestions of themselves and roads disappeared into the colour of the land.

The drakes were off the starboard bow, pulling in their spread triangle, the harness lines running back to the gondola's nose in long tapered curves. Mottled was on the left — grey-brown, slightly larger — and there it was: the downbeat coming in a fraction early, the wing folding before the full extension. Gravel and Petal adjusted to match her. The correction rippled backward through the lines and emerged at the gondola's nose as a faint, periodic creak.

The equipment hatch at the far end of the promenade opened. Wisp appeared with a tension gauge and looked at the formation, then at Theron.

"She's doing it again," Theron said.

"She's been doing it since Halverstow run last spring." Wisp moved to the railing and made a small adjustment to the gauge's reading wire. "She thinks Gravel corners too wide on the upswing. Takes it personally."

"Does he?"

"He's technically correct." Wisp didn't look away from the formation. "She's also right that his technique is lazy. I've been saying the same thing for two years. She acts like she's the first one to notice." She made a second small notation. "Petal doesn't comment. Petal is a professional."

"Do they know you talk about them like this?"

"Petal knows." She said it with complete seriousness. "Mottled has a theory that I side with Gravel. That's the opposite of the truth, but she's decided it and I've given up correcting it." She capped the gauge, and for a moment they both watched Petal hold her line — steady, unhurried, the wing extension clean through its full arc. Wisp tucked the gauge under her arm. "She's been flying this route since before Mottled was hatched. The temperament comes with the miles." She said it without affection, or with the kind of affection that had been so thoroughly absorbed into observation it no longer showed as a separate thing. "Forward arch, sir, if you want the good coffee. She keeps it at the dining-room end in the mornings."

Theron went.

---

The dining room occupied the gondola's full forward width and smelled of coffee and something warm from the galley that turned out to be pastries in a small covered basket. Three tables set for four, one for two by the window. He stood in the arch long enough to sort the room — the habit had been automatic since third year — and made his provisional assignments.

Madam Esherel at the far table, working through a merchant's folio with the concentrated displeasure of someone who'd located one error and was hunting for a second. No companion. The seal on the folio's cover was a family firm, not a registered house — the device too small to read from here. The ring on her right hand was formal silver; the traveling clothes were not. She'd packed the ring specifically.

"Vannic" in the corner, chair turned to face the wall. Nothing in front of him but his plate. Not reading, not watching the room — just the porthole and whatever was beyond it. The window light fell on his breakfast at a useful angle. Practicality, then, unless the rudeness was incidental. Possibly both. He filed it under *requires more data* and moved on.

Window table: a woman he hadn't placed from the boarding. Mid-forties, academic bearing — the set of the shoulders that came from long hours in reading-room chairs. Sensible shoes. One piece of interesting jewelry, a puzzle ring with three interlocking bands. She was reading a book with no title on the spine and had let her coffee go cold.

She looked up when he crossed the threshold. Looked at his coat. Looked at his pin.

"Investigator." Said exactly, not unkindly. "Fresh posting. Vellaine. Juridical theory, the Larent Institute."

He sat down across from her — four tables, three occupied, the alternatives being Esherel's displeasure or Vannic's back. "Theron Mavik. The pin gives me away?"

"You keep it straight." She returned to the book. "Give it a year."

He looked down. The pin was exactly level. He did not touch it.

The coffee arrived in a small copper pot and was, in fact, excellent. He poured and looked at the room properly — trained observation, left to right, floor to ceiling, everyone given a working category.

Esherel's folio was a recent printing; the binding was still tight. The error she kept returning to was near the front — page two or three, based on how often she flipped back. Business dispute, something specific, and not minor or she wouldn't have the folio out before the breakfast dishes were cleared. The formal ring and the informal clothes said she'd dressed for the journey and packed for the meeting at the other end.

Vellaine across from him: the book's lack of title meant proof copy or personal loan. Her coffee had been cold for some minutes — she hadn't reached for it once in the time he'd been watching. A researcher's focus. The puzzle ring had an academic convention knot on the outer band, probably Larent. She'd looked at his pin before his face, which was a classifier's approach — category before individual. He liked classifiers. They were easier to talk to.

"Juridical theory," he said. "Anything I'd have come across?"

She turned a page. "Probably not at twenty-one."

"What's your symposium?"

"The Halveth Compact. Three weeks of arguments about jurisdiction in inter-regional contracts, all of which could be resolved with a careful reading of the founding charter, none of which will be." She closed the book on her thumb. "Yours?"

"Holiday."

The look at that — measuring, briefly — he couldn't read precisely. Then she went back to the book.

The System noted something quiet.

*[Investigator — Insight +1. She's read your reconstruction paper. The look was recognition, not assessment.]*

He kept his face neutral. *Of course she has,* he thought, and then: *she didn't mention it.* Which was either professional courtesy or a kind of examination he hadn't been warned about, and he genuinely couldn't tell which. He found he didn't particularly mind not knowing.

He poured more coffee and let the room settle into background.

Esherel's dispute with the wine steward materialized at second watch, while Theron was on his second pastry and reviewing his own intake notes — not on any case, there wasn't a case, just the habit of having something to do with his hands. The steward had appeared with a substitute bottle; Esherel was looking at it with the careful attention of someone comparing two documents that should match and did not.

"The Tenner Twelve was specified," she said. Not raised. Just exact. "Four weeks ago. It's in the gondola manifest."

"The substitute is of comparable vintage, Madam Esherel—"

"Comparable isn't equivalent. I'd like it noted."

The substitute was a northern blend — Theron could read the label from here. Cleaner palate, sharper finish. For someone accustomed to the southern houses, the sharpness would register as wrongness even at equal quality. She wasn't being unreasonable. He said so, aloud, before he'd decided to.

Esherel looked at him for the first time. The look was thorough.

"Investigator class," he said. "First posting. I do that."

"At least you know." She turned back to the steward. "He's correct about the finish. Note it, bring the substitute. I'll manage."

The steward withdrew. Vellaine, across from him, had not looked up from her book. The corner of her mouth had moved.

"The reconstruction paper," he said.

"I have thoughts." She turned a page. "Some of them favorable."

"I'm on holiday."

"So am I. Have another pastry."

---

He went back to his cabin after breakfast and sat at the writing desk with the case slate open.

The first-class amendment was still there. He looked at it for a moment, then pulled his intake-notes page up in front of it and wrote *day one: no active queries* in the margin, which was not a thing a person on holiday needed to write but was better than the alternative.

The Wyrm shifted slightly as Mottled corrected her stroke — he felt it through the deck as a brief lateral ease, the asymmetry resolving for a few seconds before it returned — and somewhere below the galley something metal was being prepared for the midday service. The pastries had been very good. He was on the first holiday of his adult life and there was nothing wrong with being thorough.

He closed the slate on the amendment note and left the cabin.

---

The passenger lounge occupied the gondola's midpoint — lower-ceilinged than the dining room, warmer, with a row of padded settees along the port-side portholes and a scatter of corner tables. Late morning light came in at a shallow angle and made the brass trim on the porthole frames look intentional.

The woman in the brown coat had taken the corner table nearest the window. She had papers spread in front of her — the property kind, the kind that arrived in thick envelopes and needed to be read in a specific order and still managed to tell you what you hadn't wanted to know. She was reading one sheet with the expression of someone who had expected it to say exactly this and had hoped to be wrong. A pencil in her left hand. Something underlined, underlined again, then left alone.

The coat, folded on the chair beside her, had chalk on the left sleeve and what looked like paint at the cuff — not office work; something made by hand. He didn't have enough data to go further. Her own errand. No visible interest in the other passengers.

She glanced up when he crossed through — the brief automatic survey of anyone making note of arrivals — and he nodded. She registered him without particular attention and looked back at the letter.

Then she set the property sheet down and picked up something else from the stack: a folded page, narrower, the kind of list printed in small columns. The second-class passenger manifest, or close enough that the format was unmistakable. She ran her finger down it with a steadiness that had nothing to do with idle curiosity — not the half-attention of someone killing time, but the specific focus of a person locating a particular name. She found whatever she was looking for, or didn't find it, and the expression didn't change either way. The pencil didn't move. She folded the page and set it back under the property papers with the care of something being put away rather than discarded. A first-class passenger had no particular reason to be reading the second-class list at all, and she was reading it like someone who'd already been through it once.

He passed through to the reading alcove at the stern, took the window bench, and stretched his legs out. The landscape below was doing its slow thing: a city resolving from a general impression of rooftops into something specific, then falling behind and dissolving back into the colour of the land. A river was still occurring, possibly the same one.

*[No active queries. 49 hours to Halverstow Port.]*

He'd looked at the amendment note. He'd decided not to investigate it. That felt like something Warden Desh would have called progress. He put the slate face-down beside him and watched the view.

Mottled's harness creak found him even here — the Wyrm carrying it bow to stern in the walls — and the coffee in the cup he'd brought from breakfast was still warm, and for a while nothing required anything of him at all.
