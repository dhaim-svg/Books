# Chapter 4 — The Locked Cabin

Theron was on his second coffee of the morning, standing at the writing desk in his shirtsleeves, when he heard Calden knock the third time.

The first two knocks he had registered without registering. They were the small percussion of the corridor coming awake, doors opening to take towels and refuse hot water, doors closing on people who had decided to sleep an hour past the dragon-shift. Calden moved through that work in a known rhythm. Theron had been on the Wyrm two days and his class had already filed the rhythm without consulting him.

The third knock was different.

It came at the same volume as the first two. That was what was wrong with it. The first two knocks had been the unhurried *good-morning-sir* of a man who expected an answer in eight seconds and would, if no answer came, knock once more before assuming a sleeping passenger. The third was that second knock. There hadn't been a fourth. Calden was standing at the door.

Theron put his cup down. He held it, with a small private wince: he had identified the meaning of the knock before he had thought about whether he wanted to.

He pulled his coat off the hook and went into the corridor.

Calden was four doors down, on the port side, his hand still raised. Across from him Vellaine was in her own doorway in a grey travelling robe, her book closed on her thumb. Two doors past Calden, on the same side as Theron, the woman in the brown coat had her cabin door open and her hand on the latch and was not crossing the threshold. Sable. He had her name now, from the dining-room list.

"Mr. Mavik," Calden said.

"How long?"

"Six minutes since the first round. I came back. He asked to be woken for second-watch breakfast specifically — he was firm about it last night."

"Window port?"

"Sealed from inside; they all are at first watch."

"Deck hatch above the cabin?"

"Sealed. Wisp closes them all at second bell."

Theron looked at the door. The first-class cabin doors were good carpentry. Solid panel, brass plate, an interior bolt that engaged a hooked catch in the frame. The steward's master key would turn the lock proper. It would not draw the bolt.

"Service slot," he said.

"Yes, sir."

Calden produced the master from a ring on his belt with the deliberate slowness of a man who had used it perhaps three times in fifteen years of service. The lock turned. The door moved a quarter of an inch and stopped against the bolt.

Calden's hand went to his other key. It was a thin, flat thing, dull steel, the sort of object that did not appear in any guidebook to gondola travel. He slid it through the narrow brass slot at the height of the bolt and worked his wrist twice. There was the small specific sound of metal lifting metal. There was one per gondola, held by the head steward, and it had not left his person since boarding.

The door swung inward.

Vellaine had not moved from her doorway. Sable had not crossed her threshold. Neither of them was looking into the cabin, exactly; they were looking at the doorway itself, the way you look at a window when something has changed on the other side of it without your being able to say what.

Theron went in first.

---

The cabin was Lord Vannic's cabin in every particular he had so far been able to confirm from the corridor. Same dimensions as his own, same desk-and-wardrobe arrangement, the porthole bench under the window, the lamp bracket angled for reading in bed. The lamp was out. The porthole was sealed. The deck hatch above the wardrobe was sealed, the brass ring on the inside flush against the deck plate.

Lord Vannic was on the bed.

He was lying on his back, fully dressed for the day in a dark blue dressing coat over yesterday's shirt, the collar pinned with the small enamel knot he had worn at dinner. His shoes were off and on the floor beside the bed, square to one another, the way a man set them down when he was going to sit for a moment and put them back on. The right hand was open on the bedclothes, palm half up. His eyes were closed, his mouth very slightly open. There was nothing in the room that did not belong to a man getting dressed for a day he had expected to have.

He was the wrong colour.

Theron had seen the colour twice before, in the licensing rotations. Not pale; the word was wrong. The colour of someone whose blood had decided, some hours ago, that it was finished with its work in the surface vessels and had withdrawn somewhere quieter. The face had the stillness of a face that was not being used.

"Calden," he said. "Step out, please, and keep the corridor as it is. Nobody in, nobody past."

"Sir."

The steward went. The door closed without latching.

Theron stood for one count of four with his hand flat on the doorframe and waited for his own pulse to come back to where it ought to be. He noted that this was the first death of his professional life and that the man on the bed was yesterday a man who had thanked the steward by name. He marked that the noting was a kind of housekeeping his class did for him whether he asked for it or not.

Then he went to work.

The bedside table first, because it was nearest and he wanted to know whether the room was going to argue with him. A water carafe, half full. A glass beside it, empty, dry to the touch. Not used. The brass pocket-watch case open and tipped on its hinge; the watch inside reading the right time for the morning, which meant it had been wound at the usual hour the night before. A small ledger-pad with a list in Lord Vannic's hand, *boots, letter, V., breakfast 2nd watch*, and a graphite pencil with the point worn down on the right side, which was the wear of a right-handed writer. There was no bottle. There were no powders. There was no glass that had held anything but air.

The wardrobe next. Three coats, two shirts, a folded cravat, a pair of dress gloves in a paper sleeve. The luggage at the bottom: the boarding cart's three cases, all of them still latched. The latches had not been opened since they were set in place. The dust seal on the brass had not been broken. Whatever had happened in this cabin had not been a search.

The desk. A travel ink in its stoppered well. A short stack of correspondence, one letter half-written in the same hand as the ledger-pad, broken off at *I have given the matter the consideration you ask for and find that the answer must be the one I gave you last—*. The sentence had not been finished. On the blotter the pen lay dry, the nib cleaned. The chair was tucked in, the desk lamp unlit.

The porthole. Sealed at first watch, the brass ring engaged, the seal undisturbed.

The deck hatch above the wardrobe. The same.

The chain trunk in the rear wall, the narrow cased channel that carried the harness chain past the cabins and to the boom. Sealed plate, four screws, screws bedded with the small ring of dust around the heads that came from screws that had not been moved in months.

The interior bolt of the door, drawn. He went over and looked at it from inside. The bolt was a heavy brass slide that engaged a hooked catch in the frame. From the inside, you slid it across with your hand. From the outside, you could not reach it. The service slot was the only access, and the service slot was three feet to the left of the bolt and required a steward's lift-key.

He stood at the door for a moment and looked at the room from the inside the way a man inside it would have. Then he looked at the bed.

He went to the bed.

He sat on his heels beside it and did not, for now, touch the body. The dressing coat was good wool, dark blue, cut for a man with broader shoulders than most. The collar was pinned with the enamel knot, pinned correctly, evenly, by a man who had practised pinning his own collar for years. Neither hand was clenched; the right lay open on the bedclothes, the fingers where he had already placed them in his notes from the gangway. He did not stop there. He had already filed the hand.

The face had relaxed. He looked at it long enough to be sure of what he was looking at, which was longer than he wanted to look. Then he took the small notebook from his inner pocket and began to write, because the writing was the discipline and the discipline was what he had.

*Subject — Lord Petric Vannic. Cabin twelve, deck four. Discovered second watch by C. Calden, steward. Bolt engaged from interior. Service slot used to draw. Window port, deck hatch, chain trunk: all sealed, undisturbed. No bottle, no glass used, no powder, no visible wound.*

He let his hand pause and then wrote:

*Colour — withdrawn. Stiffness — not yet. Cool to touch — to be confirmed.*

He put two fingers, very lightly, to the wrist.

Cool. Not cold. Not the cold of a body that had been on a cabin bed for eight hours in an unheated room. Closer to the cool of a body that had stopped being warm three or four hours ago. Which placed the death somewhere between the small hours of the morning and an hour before Calden's first round.

He wrote: *Three to five hours. Confirm against galley fire and Wisp's last harness walk.*

The pockets. He worked the dressing coat first, then the inner pocket where a man like Lord Vannic kept the things he did not put in his case.

Inner breast: a steel pen-case, monogrammed *P.V.* in a small, expensive script. A folded handkerchief, white, fresh, the kind a man took out of a pile and forgot he had taken. A boarding card, his own, the corner worn from being slid in and out of his pocket. The small enamel signet he wore on his little finger had been removed and was on the bedside table, set down with care, the kind of removal a man did before sleep.

Outer pocket, right: a folded letter in the same hand as the desk's half-finished one, dated the previous evening, addressed to no-one yet, an opening line that read *V — I find I have to write this twice—*. He read it. He set it back.

Outer pocket, left: a folded handkerchief, used. A small steel pen-nib in a paper sleeve. And, between the two, a small wooden fish.

It was no bigger than a knuckle. Carved from a pale wood, possibly birch, the kind a man working with a cheap pocket-knife could shape in an evening. The cuts uneven where the knife had pulled with the grain, a small notch at the tail where a younger or smaller hand had slipped. The eyes were two pin-pricks, off-centre. It had been carried in a pocket for some time; the surface had the soft sheen of a thing that lived against cloth.

He turned it over once.

*Odd personal effect*, he filed. *Note for return to estate.* It belonged with the cheap pen-nib and the used handkerchief and not with the monogrammed pen-case or the enamel knot. A noble man's pocket-corner where a noble man kept the things he could not be seen using. He set it back where he had found it, between the two pieces of folded cloth, and closed the pocket.

The trouser pockets last. A short comb. Three coins, low denomination. A blue-headed pin.

He stood up.

The cabin was the cabin he had walked. The bed was the bed it had been. He let his eyes do one more pass. Wardrobe, desk, porthole, hatch, chain trunk, bolt. The room agreed with itself. Nothing had been forced. Nothing had been opened. Nobody had come through.

He stood with his hand on the brass plate of the door and waited for his pulse to come back to where it ought to be a second time. It took longer this time.

Then the System spoke.

> *[Investigator — quest assigned.]*
>
> *Solve the Death Aboard the Skybound Wyrm.*
>
> *Locked-cabin context noted. Estimated time on station: nineteen hours to Halverstow Port.*

He read it twice and was very, very mildly pleased that his class had spoken in the tone he would have spoken in himself, which was the tone of someone who did not need to be told that there was something to be done. Then he set the pleasure aside because Lord Vannic was on the bed behind him and the pleasure could keep.

He opened the door.

---

Calden was in the corridor exactly where he had been left, his back to the cabin, his hands folded in front of him with the deliberate looseness of a man who had decided not to let his hands give him away. Vellaine had not moved from her doorway. Sable was no longer in hers; the door was open behind her, but she was now standing at the rail of the corridor with her arms folded, looking at nothing in particular.

"Calden," Theron said.

"Sir."

"He has been dead three to five hours. The cabin is sealed from the inside. The interior bolt was drawn, the porthole is sealed, the deck hatch is sealed, the chain trunk has not been opened. Nothing in the room suggests a forced entry. There is no bottle, no glass used, no visible wound. The body is on the bed."

He was speaking in the corridor because in nineteen hours every passenger on this gondola would know that Lord Vannic was dead and he would rather they knew it from a man who had been in the room than from the version of the story that would otherwise be in circulation by midday.

"I'd like the corridor closed at both ends," he went on. "I'd like the cabin locked from the outside with your master and the master held by you personally, not on the ring. I'd like the captain notified that a death has occurred and that I will speak with him within the hour. I'd like the passenger lounge opened for the first-class corridor to use, with you stationed at the saloon door for the day. I'd like the manifest brought to my cabin." A breath. "And I would be grateful for another pot of coffee, when there's a moment for it."

Calden inclined his head a fraction. "I'll see to all of it, sir."

"Thank you."

The steward went. Vellaine watched him go and then looked at Theron, and her face had the expression of a woman who had taught younger versions of him in faculty rooms and was now revising her estimate of this one upward, somewhat against her will.

"Are you all right, Mr. Mavik," she said. It was not a question.

"I'd prefer to be by lunch."

"That's the right answer."

She went back into her cabin and closed the door without making a noise about it.

Sable was still at the corridor rail. She had not looked at Theron since he had come out. She was looking at the carpet at the edge of Lord Vannic's door, in a steady way that did not announce itself as looking.

"Miss Verrin," he said.

She turned her head.

"I'll need to speak to everyone on this corridor at some point today," he said. "It does not have to be now."

"All right," she said.

She held his eye a moment longer than the exchange required. Then she went back into her cabin and closed the door, and the corridor was empty.

He pulled the brass plate of cabin twelve gently to and stood with his hand on it for a count of four.

The hum of the harness chains was coming through the deck the way it had been coming through the deck since the second day, even, three-part, the slight irregularity that was Mottled rushing her downbeat. Petal was somewhere off the bow doing what Petal did. The Wyrm was nineteen hours from Halverstow Port and a man inside this cabin had thanked the steward by name on the gangway and was not going to do anything by name again.

He waited for the pulse to come back a third time.

It came.

He went to find Calden.
