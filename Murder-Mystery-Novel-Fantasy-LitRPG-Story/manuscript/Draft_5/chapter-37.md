# Chapter 37 — Seeing the Unseen

The harbour wall ran along the slip for a hundred yards and then bent inward toward a row of buildings that had been put up over a long time and had been mended with whatever the year could spare. The first of them was a chandlery, closed for the afternoon. The second was an oil-merchant's. The third was an inn with a board hung outside it and one word chalked on the board, which was *stew*, and underneath the word, in a smaller hand, *with bread*.

A cat was on the step.

"This one," Sable said.

"This one."

She did not touch the cat as she went past it; she let it have the step. Theron went past it on the other side, and the cat watched them with the considered unconcern of a working cat that had its own business in the afternoon and had decided they were not part of it.

Inside, the inn was three tables in a front room and a back porch beyond a door at the end of the bar. The man behind the bar had his sleeves rolled and was wiping a glass with a dishcloth that had been white once and was no longer. He looked at Theron and at Sable and at the strap under Theron's arm and at the way Sable was carrying her notebook in her left hand with the pencil through the spine.

"Stew," he said.

"Two," Sable said. "And bread. And whatever you've got that's hot to drink."

"Cider. We've got a cider that's hot."

"That."

The man set the glass down and tipped his head toward the door at the end of the bar. "Back porch?"

"Back porch."

He inclined his head a half-inch, which was the half-inch this part of the country used for all work, and they went through.

The porch was a six-board platform on the inn's harbour side with a low rail and three small tables. None of the tables was taken. The light on the porch was the southern afternoon's working light, long and clear, doing nothing in particular to the wood of the tables. The harbour beyond the rail was the harbour they had walked along. The gondola sat at its mooring at the visitor slip; the slip-cranes still worked at the eastern arm; the customs boat was now tied off, the oarsman gone to whatever an oarsman went to when his boat was tied. A gull was on a piling. It was possibly the same gull. Theron did not test it.

They took the table by the rail.

He set the strap down beside his chair. Sable laid her notebook on the table and the pencil beside the notebook with the point inward. They sat. He put his hands on the table and then took them off and put them on his knees and then forgot about them.

"A week," she said.

"A week."

"Counting today."

"Counting today."

That was the whole of what was said before the cider came.

The cider came in a heavy mug that had a chip on the rim on Theron's side; he turned the mug the half-turn that put the chip away from his mouth without making anything of it. Sable's mug had no chip. She wrapped her hands around it and did not drink.

The stew would be a little while. The man behind the bar had said nothing about how long; nobody on a busy afternoon in a working harbour-side inn told you how long a stew would be, because a stew was the time a stew was, and you sat with the cider until it came.

Theron put his hands around the mug.

*[Investigator — level six.]*

> *Talent unlocked: Seeing the Unseen.*
> *The suspect your training filtered out has the shape you now know how to see.*
> *Filed at the close of: Solve the Death of Joren Aldis.*

He read the line.

He read it again, slower, the way he had read the title line on the slip an hour ago. The way a man read a sheet that had a thing on it he had thought was on a different sheet. The flavour line was three sentences. It was not the flavour-line shape his Conservatory teachers had warned the class about, which was decorative, congratulatory, ornamental. It was the flavour-line shape of a System that had been watching him work the case and had decided what to put on the page now that the case was closed.

The first sentence was the name of the talent and was a name.

The second sentence was about the shape of the suspect he had been trained to see, and the shape of the suspect he now knew how to see, and the gap between them. The gap was Halsa. The gap was every working illusionist on the Mirren coast he had been trained to file in the verify-not-pursue column because the rivalries his class had been taught to weigh were rivalries between people with houses and seals and entries in the registry. The gap was a woman named on a forged paper, sitting eight years in front of a desk, doing what the man who had not asked her brother's name had not done, which was face the outcome.

The third sentence was the title. It named the case the System had filed the talent under. The case had a name and the name was Joren's name.

He did not nod.

He did not, this time, give the screen the small dry nod he had given the System every morning of the week. He read the three lines a second time, in the same order, and then he set the mug down on the table because the mug had gone a small distance off the line of his palm and he had not noticed it going.

He put his right hand flat on the table.

The screen sat where it had sat. He let it sit. He did not look out at the harbour. He looked at the surface of the table — the grain of the wood, the small dark line where a knife had once been dragged across it, the place where someone's mug had left a ring that had not come out — and he kept his hand where his hand was.

Sable was sitting across from him.

She had taken her hands off her own mug. The mug was steaming a thin steam up between them. She was looking at him.

She did not look away.

Without lifting her hand to her mug, or picking up the pencil, or turning her face toward the harbour, Sable sat with her hands in her lap below the edge of the table and watched him read the screen a second time, and when his eyes moved across the third line for the second time and his hand went flat on the table, she stayed where she was, and she watched.

She watched him for the count it took him to read the lines twice.

She watched him for the count it took him to set the mug down.

She watched him for the count after that, which was longer, and which was the count in which he was reading the third line, the line with Joren's name on it, for whatever number of times he was reading it, and she let him have the count.

Then she said: "What did it say."

Her voice was the voice she had used at the door of cabin six and at the foot of the gangway and at the rail of the pier — small, working, hers. The count had been long enough. She had been the one to break it.

Theron looked up.

The screen was still there in his vision. He did not have to dismiss it. He let it sit and looked through it.

"It levelled the class," he said.

"Yes."

"It put a name on what it was."

"What was the name."

He looked at her.

He looked at her in a way he had not looked at her at the foot of the table at Ottren's, and had not looked at her at the rail of the pier, and had not looked at her at the bend of the corridor on the gondola. He looked at her the way a man looked at a person who had just asked him for the name of the thing a System had set down in front of him on the porch of an inn in a harbour in the south, at the end of a week.

"Seeing the unseen," he said.

"Hm."

"The flavour line was three sentences. It named the talent and then it said the suspect my training filtered out has the shape I now know how to see. And then it filed the talent under the case."

"Under Joren's name."

"Under Joren's name."

She let that sit.

She picked up her mug, then, and wrapped both hands around it again without drinking. She looked at him over the rim and did not say anything else for the length of a breath, and then she said:

"Good."

That was the whole of it.

Theron took his hand off the table. He moved it back to his mug. The cider had gone from too hot to nearly drinkable, and he drank a small swallow and set the mug down and put his hand around it again.

The screen had dismissed itself somewhere in the breath after she spoke, and he had not asked it to.

"All right," he said.

"All right." She set her mug down on the boards with the small care of a person setting a thing exactly where it had been.

The stew came.

It came in two wide bowls and was the colour of a stew that had been on a stove since the morning, and it had carrots in it and what looked like mutton and what was definitely a small amount of barley, and the bread came with it on a wooden board with a knife. The man set the bowls down and inclined his half-inch again and went back to his bar.

Sable tore the bread.

She tore it in halves, not pieces — one half put down on Theron's side of the board, one half on hers, the working courtesy of a person who had eaten with people at tables for a long time and knew the shape of dividing a loaf without making anything of it. Theron took his half and tore a smaller piece off it. The bread was warm in the middle. He had not had warm bread since the morning of the day at Halverstow, which had been six days now.

They ate.

The stew was a stew. It was the right stew. It was the stew the chalkboard outside had promised it would be, and it sat warm in him in the way a hot meal sat warm in a person who had not eaten since the dawn loaf on the gondola. Sable ate without speaking, and he ate without speaking, and the gull on the piling lifted off and went down the harbour about its own business and not his.

After a while she said: "There's a room here."

"There's a room here."

"They had two."

"Then there are two."

"Mm." She turned her mug a half-turn on the boards. "For tonight."

"For tonight."

She did not say anything about tomorrow. He did not ask. He was tired in the specific way a person was tired who had been thinking about a man's death for a week and had now stopped thinking about it, and the tired was sitting on him the way the southern light was sitting on the table, and he let it sit.

The slip-cranes worked at the eastern arm.

The customs boat sat at its tie. The oarsman had not come back. The gondola at the visitor slip had its two drakes on their tethered rest with their heads on their forepaws, and Petal was the smaller of the two, and Mottled was the one whose harness creak was now a memory rather than a present sound, and the third drake, Gravel, was up at the rigging house, probably, with Wisp, being told off for whatever Gravel had decided to take personally on the descent.

Theron took the last torn piece of his bread and used it to clean the side of his bowl.

Sable did the same with hers.

She put her bread down on the empty board. She set the pencil parallel to the spine of the notebook. She put both hands around the mug, which was nearly empty now.

"All right," she said.

"All right."

The cat from the front step had come around the side of the inn while they were eating and was sitting on the porch rail two tables down, watching the harbour with the considered unconcern it had used on the front step. The light on the rail was the long clear light of the southern afternoon, and the cat had its tail wrapped around its forepaws, and it was a working cat on a working porch and it was not theirs and they were not its.

Theron set his hand on the strap beside his chair.

The case book inside the strap was the lighter book it had been since the warehouse. He did not open it. He left his hand on the leather and let the leather be warm under his palm, and Sable across the table set down her mug and pushed it the half-inch back from the rim of the table that meant the mug was done, and she did not look at him in the way she had looked at him while he read the screen, because that look had been the look it was, and she was, now, looking at the harbour.

The bread board sat empty between them.

"All right," she said again, the second time, which was a different *all right* from the first.

"All right," Theron said.

He left his hand on the strap. The cat did not move from the rail. The chip on Theron's mug was still on Theron's mug, and somewhere up at the visitor slip a drake shifted in its tether and resettled and was quiet again, and the porch held the two of them in the small working pace of two people who had eaten a meal together at the end of a week.
