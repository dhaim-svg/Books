# Chapter 8 — Two Ways of Reading a Room

Captain Joriss had given him leave for the twelve minutes in three sentences and a folded note, and Theron came down out of the chartroom with the note in his coat pocket and the small clean feeling of an authorization he had not expected to be granted in the same hour he asked for it. He took the aft stair down to deck three. The afternoon had thinned. The light through the long port-side windows was sliding into the colour it took on after the meal service had passed and before the lamps were lit, which was no colour in particular and was Theron's favourite light on the gondola so far.

He found Sable in the second-class lounge bay window, two pencils, a folded paper, and an empty cup arranged in front of her with the careful spacing of someone who had been thinking and had moved items around to keep thinking.

"He gave it," Theron said, sitting.

"Conditional?"

"On Calden's consent, which he'll give."

"Today?"

"Today."

She nodded once and shifted the cup a quarter turn so its handle pointed away from him. The bay window was warm where the late sun had been on it; outside, the long flat west had the cleared look of a sky that had decided to be a sky for the rest of the day.

He took the folded note out, read it once for the discipline of reading it once, and put it back.

"Tell me what you have," she said.

"The captain's three sentences." He counted them off on the cloth. "First — Calden goes off-station at fourth watch only for an authorized errand on his own initiative if a passenger is in distress. Second — the captain logs the report retroactively if the steward judges the matter resolved. Third — Calden is not required to disclose the passenger's name to me, only to confirm that the errand was within the authorized class of errand."

"Class of errand."

"His phrase."

"So a passenger was in distress."

"On the fourth watch. For at least the twelve minutes Calden was off-station."

She turned this over the way he had seen her turn things over — not visibly, but in the small still moment her eyes did not move while the rest of her did, the cup adjusted, the pencil rolled an eighth of a turn. He had begun to recognize the still moment as the thing it was, which struck him as a recent and useful piece of recognition.

"Cabin," she said.

"He'll say or he won't, and the captain didn't authorize the cabin."

"Mm." She set the pencil flat. "I'd ask him in the order. Was a passenger in distress. Then — was the passenger the one in cabin twelve. Don't open with twelve."

He'd had it the other way around in his head ten seconds ago. He looked at her.

"You'd start with the general, then narrow."

"He'll answer the general because the captain authorized the general." She lifted the pencil and set it down a half-inch over. "He won't answer the specific. But he'll answer the general in a way that tells you the specific, if you let him."

The cost of the move he had been about to make, the elegance of the substitution, the small irritation at having needed the substitution at all.

"Right," he said. "General first."

"Then the question you actually want to ask, which is not the cabin."

"It isn't?"

"It's the time. He has the time to the minute. He has not been asked for the minute."

He sat with that. The captain had given leave on the twelve. He had been thinking, on the way down, *push him on the cabin*. He had not been thinking *push him on the minute the twelve began*. Because — he saw it as he heard her say it — the minute the twelve began was a different number than the minute the twelve ended, and he had two different overlap windows on Esherel and Vellaine depending on which end of the twelve he set the anchor against, and the captain had not authorized the cabin but the captain had authorized the duration, and the duration had a beginning, and the beginning was free.

"I had the geometry," he said. "I didn't have the geometry."

She watched him with the small patient expression of a person waiting for someone to finish doing arithmetic in their head. When he was done she said: "You had it. You were going to ask the wrong question first."

"I was."

"Now you're not."

"Thank you."

She made a small sound that was not quite a laugh. "I'd like the coffee back, please. The galley boy will do it if you smile."

He took her cup and went and asked the galley boy, who refilled it without smiling and without being smiled at, and brought it back. She had moved the pencils together while he was gone. He set the cup down where the handle had been pointing.

---

"Tell me what *you* have," he said, which was the question he had meant to open with and had not. "On him."

"On Vannic."

"On Vannic."

She put her hand around the cup and did not drink. "I'll do it the way I do it. It won't sound like anything for a few sentences. Will you wait?"

"I'll wait."

She nodded and let her eyes go to a point somewhere on the wood of the table, which he had begun to understand was where she put her attention when she was assembling what she had seen rather than what she was about to say.

"He stood for ten seconds at the boarding rail before he walked the gangway. Most people stand four or five. He took the extra five and used them looking forward, not at the gondola — through it, at the boom. He wanted to see the harness in motion before he was inside the harness. He was nervous, or he was respectful, or it was the same thing for him." She moved her thumb on the cup's handle. "He addressed Calden by name the first time Calden told him his name and not after — which is what you do when names are useful to you, not when you are a man who has staff. The lord-voice came on after the boarding deck, not before. It was a coat he put on once he had an audience. I'm not telling you anything about who he was before he put it on. I'm only telling you he put it on."

"Right."

"He ate alone where he could. He chose the chair facing the wall not because he was rude, although he was occasionally rude, but because he found the room expensive to be in. People who find rooms expensive to be in sit facing the wall when they're allowed to. I do it. You probably do it."

"I do it."

"I noticed."

He let that sit and did not enjoy it visibly, which felt like effort. She went on.

"He didn't drink much. He drank what arrived. He didn't ask for things. The Esherel feud was something he was tolerating, not running — he wasn't sharpening it on the second night when she came at him about the invoice, he was letting her take the points. The *forty-one* correction he could have answered more cleanly than he did, and he didn't bother. He took his coffee black and left the lemon biscuits alone the first day, ate them the second. He noticed the cook the second night — not the steward, the cook, who came out to talk to Esherel about the substitute bottle — and he watched the cook walk back to the galley with an expression I'd describe as professional interest, like he was filing how a man moved through a service room. I don't know what to make of that. Possibly he liked watching competent people work. Some men do."

"Some men do."

"He was kind to the airsick child."

He waited for the next item. There was a one-second pause, the kind a list had when an item required a sentence of its own before the next item, and then she continued.

"Second night, two tables over from us, the family with the child in the blue coat. The child had gone green between the soup and the second course. The mother was halfway out of her chair, not yet committed to leaving. Vannic was facing the wall. He could see them in the porthole reflection. He didn't turn his head. He slid one of the ginger sweets off his own tray-saucer onto the steward's tray as the steward was passing and said something I didn't hear that was probably the table number. The steward took it over. The mother didn't see who it came from. The child stopped going green about three minutes after she ate it. Not how that kind of man moves through the world. They make sure the room knows it was them, or they don't do it. He did it and didn't look up."

He registered the observation. She was building a social profile of the victim — what the man had been like in the room before he became a body in a room — and the kindness was a beat in the profile the way the wall-facing was a beat and the cook was a beat. He filed it under *Sable's read on his temperament*, made a small mental adjustment to his working sense of *who Vannic was when no one was watching*, and asked the next question.

"Was the general demeanour a temperament or a performance, do you think? Across the whole evening."

She looked at him. The look was the one she did when he had said something useful and she was choosing not to make a thing of it.

"Performance on the outside. Temperament under it. He performed being a lord, badly, on top of being a man who would do that with a ginger sweet without looking up. I don't know which one was the real one. Both of them were him."

"Both of them were him."

"Yes."

He wrote two words on the slate without looking at them and turned the slate face-down on the cloth. *Wall-facing*, he had written, and *cook*. He had not written *ginger sweet*. He noticed, in the back of his attention, that he had not written it because he had filed it already.

"I'd like to walk through the timing with you," he said. "Before I ask Calden. So I know what I'm pushing for."

"Yes."

He pulled the slate back over, turned it the right way up, and they walked it through. He drew the fourth watch as a line; he marked the cabin twelve door as a tick on the line; he marked the steward's station as a longer tick; he marked Esherel's known position from her own account and Vellaine's by inference from the cabin lamp records, and Sable watched and once — only once — took the pencil out of his hand and moved a tick three minutes earlier, and looked at him to see if he objected, and gave the pencil back. He didn't object. The three minutes were on Vellaine's lamp and not on Vellaine's wakefulness, which was a distinction he had not made because he had not had Sable's habit of asking *who would have noticed the lamp going on*.

By the time they were done the slate had a workable shape and the cup had gone cold a second time. She took it away to the galley boy herself this round.

He thanked it silently and turned the slate face-down again. When she came back he was on his feet.

"I'll go to him now," he said. "While the captain's note is still warm in my pocket."

"Good." She set the refilled cup down for him, not for her. "Drink half of that first."

"Why."

"Because you'll be there in two minutes and the steward will notice if you arrived breathing fast. He notices that sort of thing. So do you, on someone else. Drink half."

He drank half. It was warm and slightly burnt at the edge — the afternoon pot, kept on the warmer too long — and the burn settled the small stir of haste he hadn't quite known he had, and he set the cup down.

"I'll find you after," he said.

"In the corridor. I'll be at the alcove."

"At the alcove."

"With Calden's permission, this time." A quick dry look. "I've decided to start asking."

"Noted."

She smiled at *noted* the way she had smiled at it the day before, briefly and without making a thing of it, and went back to her cup. He left her in the bay window with the pencils realigned and the slate in his coat pocket and the captain's note still in the same coat pocket, two folded papers warm against his ribs.

---

The second-class corridor on deck three was the kind of quiet that had people behind it. Late afternoon. A child laughing two cabins down and being shushed; the smell of something with onion from the galley vent at the corridor's mid-point; the wood of the passage warmer underfoot than the first-class boards because the boilers ran beneath. He walked it at the pace of a man who had been authorized and wanted to arrive at the authorization without using it up before the door.

He found, as he walked, that he was thinking about Calden as Calden rather than as *the steward*. The man had a morning behind him — Theron didn't know what, exactly; the small slate by the bell-cords would have it, the substitutions, the early-bell jobs, the routine work of a route the man had run perhaps eighty times — and the morning had ended in a pantry passage with a question from a woman who had not asked it the way Theron had asked it. The man had a half-second slowing past a door because he had been thanked by name. Twelve minutes off-station on the fourth watch, because someone in his class of authorized errands had been in distress. The man would have a face when Theron arrived. The face would be tired or it would be careful or it would be both.

He was going to ask him a difficult question. He should arrive at the question by the route a person would, not by the route a manifest would.

He had the captain's order, but he did not have to use it as the first sentence.

The pantry door was open. The bell-cords were dim against the lamp-light; the slate sat where it had sat that morning, with the day's substitutions chalked in Calden's hand and one new line added below in different chalk, a smaller hand, the cook's — *no more lemon biscuits, advise the saloon* — which made Theron smile in spite of himself, because Sable had eaten the last two with her bread an hour ago.

Calden was inside, with his back half to the door, writing something on the small slate in the careful left-leaning hand Theron had begun to recognize. He turned as Theron's footfall registered.

Theron raised his hand to knock on the open frame.

He did not knock yet. He looked at Calden — at the chalk in his hand, at his face, which was tired and was careful and was both — and held the gesture a beat. Calden looked back. Calden was waiting to be addressed.

He let the hand come down.

"Calden," he said. "The captain's given me a note. I'd like a moment, when you can."

"Now, sir," Calden said. He set the chalk in the rail of the slate without looking down. "I'll finish this after."

Theron stood at the open door, with the second question already in his mouth and the first one waiting behind it, in the order Sable had given him.
