# Chapter 33 — Passengers Assembled

Davren's office was on the second floor above the wool warehouse, with a stairwell that smelled of lanolin in the cold months and the same smell fainter in the warm ones. Theron came up it at half past first bell on Friday with the case book in a leather strap under his arm and the requirements page set as the third sheet down, where Davren would meet it after the manifest and the abstract. Sable was a flight below him, climbing at her own pace. She had said at the foot of the stair that she would come up and stand at the wall and not say anything, and that the case was his to put on the table.

He had said: all right.

The clerk at the top was a young man in a grey-edged coat who had been writing a date on a docket and stopped when he saw the strap.

"Mr. Mavik."

"He's expecting me." Theron shifted the strap to his other arm.

"He is. Go through."

Theron went through. Davren was at the long table by the side window, not the desk, which was the table he used when he expected to be reading rather than receiving. He had a pot of his own and two cups already out. He did not stand. He set down what he had been reading and looked at the strap.

"Sit."

Theron sat. Sable came in behind him without speaking and went to the chair against the wall and sat in it with her notebook closed on her knee. Davren marked her with a small look that was civility and not invitation, and then turned the look back to the table.

"Show me."

Theron undid the strap. He laid the case book down right-side up for Davren. He turned the pages he had decided last night to turn — the abstract, the scry log, the Mirren transcript, the two Pessel letters laid side by side in the order they had arrived, the manifest with its forty flat lines, and last the requirements page with the six lines at the top and the name at the foot of it.

Davren read the abstract first. He read it once. He drank coffee. He read it a second time and turned the page.

The scry log he read without comment, which Theron took as the answer he had been waiting for on the scry log. The Mirren transcript came next, one finger moving down the column. He read both Pessel letters and laid them back down in the order Theron had set them. The manifest he read at the speed a magistrate read a manifest, which was twice as fast as a layman read a manifest and four times as slow as a magistrate's clerk did, and he stopped twice — once at cabin three, where he made a small sound in the back of his throat that Theron did not interpret, and once at the foot of the third-class block, where the unmarked row stood plain.

He turned the requirements page.

He read the six lines.

The name at the foot of the page he read last.

He sat with the page for the length of a slow breath. Then he turned it ninety degrees so the foot of the page was toward him and set his finger flat below the two words and did not, for the moment, lift it again.

"Walk me through (c)."

Theron walked him through (c). He kept it short. He used the words Pessel had used in the letter where Pessel had had them, and his own where Pessel had not. Davren listened without interrupting. He did not move his finger off the page.

"And (d)."

Theron walked him through (d). The trigger left the house with her. The master did not take it back. The sentence belonged to Pessel; Davren let the borrowed weight sit.

"And (f)."

He walked him through (f). The nine years at the Copper Finch. The cash. The pattern of *picked up the work, never wrote it down*. Davren took his finger off the page at the end of (f) and put it back on, half an inch to the left, on the bare strip of paper between the last requirement line and the name.

"It's a working theory," he said.

"It is."

"It's a working theory of a quality I haven't been handed in nine years. The last one was the Veldris matter, which you would not have heard of, and the one before that was before I was magistrate here."

"I know what the page is."

"You know what the page is not."

"I know what the page is not. It is not an arrest warrant. It would not be sufficient for one. Structural exclusivity is not opportunity proven and not act witnessed. It is the work that has to be done before the work that ends in a warrant. I am not asking for a warrant."

Davren looked at him. He set the page down and did not speak immediately. Theron let the silence sit and did not fill it with the second sentence he had prepared.

"What are you asking for."

"A magistral travel-order. The morning service to Sannholt lifts tomorrow at first bell. She is on it. She booked a return three weeks ago at the Halverstow office, paid in cash, single cabin. The order would attach me to the flight as your deputy for the route, with the authority to take a statement from her *en route*, and a clerk of the Sannholt office to receive her at landing for further questioning under their writ. She is not under arrest. She is not free to disembark before Sannholt. She is told she is under magistral accompaniment for the purpose of a statement, and not why."

"Not why."

"Not why. The name at the foot of the page does not travel with the order. The order names Joren Aldis as the open case and Halsa Renn as a person whose statement the investigation requires. The page stays here, in your case-room, until she has read it."

"Until she has read it."

"Until you put it to her at Sannholt and she reads it. That is the courtesy I want. The page is the page. It was built so that the reading of it is its own kind of answering."

Davren did not say anything for a moment. He drank coffee. He looked at the manifest, and at the requirements page, and then at the wall above the side window where the magistral seal hung framed in a small dark frame with its candle-wax button intact.

At the far edge of the table, where the office's own working pile sat squared off from Theron's pages, was a loose sheaf in another clerk's hand — the Halverstow file on Renn, gathered before Theron had ever brought the name to the table. The topmost slip had a small mark stamped in the upper corner, a rondel of three lines folded inward toward a centre, not quite a knot, not quite a wheel. Theron's eye caught on it the way an eye caught on a thing it did not have a place for. He did not have a place for it. He moved his eye back to the requirements page and left the slip where it was, on the far side of the table, with the rest of the working pile that was Davren's and not his.

"You'll bring the case book on the flight."

"I'll bring it. Not the requirements page. The abstract, the scry log, the transcripts, the manifest. I want the statement to be a statement, not a reading."

"And if she asks why the magistral order."

"I'll say what the order says. I'll say no more. I'll be on the flight as your deputy, not as a man on his own work, and the cabin will know it."

"Sable goes with you."

It was not a question. Sable had not spoken from the wall and did not speak now. Davren had looked at her once on the way in and was looking at Theron and not at her, and the sentence belonged to the table.

"She goes with me."

"Then she's named on the order. I am not putting a private party on a magistral flight in a deputy's cabin without naming her."

"Name her."

Davren turned and called through the half-open door for the clerk. The clerk came in with the docket-book and a blank order form. Davren dictated the order in the short clean sentences of a man who had dictated three thousand of them and had stopped putting feeling into the verbs a decade ago. *In the matter of the open investigation into the death of Joren Aldis*, he said. *Magistral accompaniment for the southbound morning service of the seventeenth*. *Deputy: Mavik, Theron, Investigator, licensed by the conservatory of the second city, sealed in this office on date stated. Attached: Verrin, Sable, Witness, in her own capacity, by the deputy's nomination.* He named Halsa under her booking-name and her cabin number and the route. He did not name the requirements page or the working theory or the name at the foot of either.

The clerk set the order on the table for the seal. Davren reached into his coat pocket for his ring and put the wax to the candle and set the ring to the wax and held it the way a man held a seal-ring to wax — not pressing, just resting, the wax doing its own work under the heat. He lifted the ring and set the order down between Theron and the case book.

"Two copies," he said.

"Two copies."

"The original goes with you. The duplicate sits here." He tapped the docket. "If she asks at the dock to see the writ, you let her see the original and she lets you keep it. If she does not ask, you do not produce it until she does. The flight crew has its own copy from the magistral office through the harbour clerk; she does not see that one. The clerk of the Sannholt office will be on the morning post-coach this afternoon and will be at the landing dock when you arrive."

"Understood."

"She'll be told by your clerk-of-mine this evening. He'll go to her boarding-house at sixth bell, when she'll have come back from the Copper Finch if her week is the week we think it is. He'll hand her a sealed note from this office. The note says what the order says. It does not say anything else."

"Yes."

"You will not be at the door when he goes. You will not be at the dock when she boards. You will be on the flight in your cabin when she is shown to hers, and Sable will be where Sable chooses to be, and the cabin numbers will be such that you do not pass her in the corridor before second bell. I will not have it look like an ambush, on the dock or in the air. The order is not an ambush. The order is a courtesy with a magistrate's hand on the door."

"Yes."

Davren took the original off the table and folded it once and slid it into the leather strap above the case book without being asked. He capped his pen. He set his hand on the requirements page one more time and did not turn it.

"This is good work."

He did not look at Theron when he said it. He looked at the page. Theron did not say anything. There was no English available for the sentence that Davren had just put down, and he had learned this week that the absence of English was often the right answer to a sentence of that shape.

Sable had stood up at some point in the last sentence. He had not heard the chair move. She was at the table now, beside Theron's shoulder, not above the page but near it.

"Tomorrow at first bell," she said.

Davren folded the requirements page back into the case book without answering.

He did not close the case book. That was Theron's to do.

Theron closed it.

---

The southbound morning service lifted from the Halverstow harbour-dock at first bell on the seventeenth of November, in the kind of grey light that had not yet decided whether it would be rain or only the threat of it. A quarter the size of the city dock at the southern terminus, the dock had no rope to drop. The handler was a long-armed older woman whose name badge read *Roe*, and she had two drakes in the harness rather than three, and the gondola was a third the length of the Wyrm and lacquered the colour of wet slate. The hum it made through the boot soles was the same hum the Wyrm had made. The bone was the same bone.

Theron arrived at the dock with the leather strap under his arm and the order in the inner pocket of his coat and the case book tied tight inside the strap. Sable was already there. She had a cup of coffee in her gloved hand that she had bought from the dock-side stall and was finishing without enthusiasm. She held the spare cup out as he came up.

"It's not as good as the host's."

"I didn't think it would be."

"It's warm. That's all it was."

He took the cup and drank half of it standing beside her and watched the dock.

Halsa was not at the dock. He had not expected her to be at the dock, and Davren had instructed against it, and the order had been built so that she would board through the second gangway at the far end, where the dock-clerk in the grey-edged coat was standing with the manifest copy from the office and a small leather case at his feet. Theron watched the second gangway without watching it. He saw the clerk shake hands with the gondola purser and hand him a folded sheet and accept the seal-mark in return. He saw a small grey bag set down beside the clerk's case. He did not see the woman who had set it there. He looked back at his own gangway and finished the cup.

"All right," Sable said.

They boarded. Their cabins were on the same passage, near the forward end. Theron's was number two, Sable's was four. Roe was at the boarding door and checked their names off without flourish and gave them keys with their berths stamped in red wax. Theron put his key in his pocket and held the strap with both hands going up the stair.

In the corridor at the head of the stair he stopped, because his cabin door was three steps in front of him and because the case book was heavier in the strap than it had been at the foot of the stair, and because he had not yet, that morning, thought of the corridor as a corridor. He had thought of it as a manifest. The manifest had said *cabin six, Renn*. He looked at the door of cabin six. It was painted the same wet-slate green as the rest of the doors. There was no light at the threshold.

Sable was at his elbow.

"Theron."

"I'm not going down there."

"I know. I'm telling you that I know."

He let his shoulder go off the strap by half an inch. He went into cabin two. Sable went into cabin four. The doors closed at almost the same beat, and the lift began under his feet before he had put the strap down on the writing desk.

He felt it the way he had felt it on the Wyrm — the deep hum rising, the floor's quality changing, the slow courteous separation from the dock — and he sat on the padded bench beneath the porthole and watched Halverstow tilt and drop below the hull line. The river was a thin grey line. The wool warehouses were small dark blocks at the harbour edge. The two drakes pulled out into the slipstream in a tighter formation than the Wyrm's three; Roe had said one of them was new to the route and would settle by the second hour, in the same tone of voice Wisp had used about Mottled, which Theron had stopped being surprised by some time ago.

> *[Investigator — Manifest of the southbound morning service of the seventeenth: deputy-of-record under magistral accompaniment. Subject named on order: Renn, H., cabin six. Order in force from first bell to second bell on landing. Investigator is travelling under writ.]*

He read it once.

It came in dryer than usual, which he had not, last night, predicted. The System was using *travelling under writ* like a man putting a coat on for the weather it was — the noun first, the verb afterward, no commentary. He let it pass with the barest acknowledgement.

The drakes had found their thermal. The gondola was steady. He could hear, faintly through the wall, Sable moving in the next cabin — a bag set down, a coat hung up, the small specific noises of a person putting herself into a small specific room. Two doors past hers, the corridor turned, and at the head of the turning the door of cabin six was closed and would stay closed until he walked down to it.

He did not walk to it yet.

He set the leather strap on the writing desk, the case book on top of it. He sat with his hands flat on the case book for the length of a slow breath he did not count.

The gondola had found its air and was running south, and he had nothing left to do except sit with the case book until the hour came to stand up with it.
