## Prior-round tic status

The Draft_2 sweep identified three tics: (1) the "of someone who" behavioral-history formula, (2) filing-verb crutches (`filed`/`registered`/`catalogued`/`logged`), and (3) passive landscape constructions ("A river was occurring below").

**Tic 1 — "of someone who" formula:** Partially resolved. Draft_2 count was 37 instances; Draft_3 count is **27 instances**. A reduction of 10, concentrated in the worst-offender chapters. Ch. 1 fell from 7 to 3, Ch. 23 from 4 to 1. However, 27 instances across the manuscript is still a meaningful residual load — the formula remains distributed across 19 chapters, with Ch. 1 (3), Ch. 2 (3), Ch. 15 (3), and Ch. 20 (3) as the remaining density clusters. The reduction is real progress; the formula has not been eliminated.

**Tic 2 — Filing-verb crutches:** Mixed result. `catalogued` has been removed entirely (0 instances vs. 1 in Draft_2). However, `filed` and `registered` remain elevated at 49 and 52 respectively, and a new variant has entered: `logged` appears 12 times (metaphorical), up from 3–4. The Draft_2 recommendation was to replace roughly half the instances with active alternatives; approximately half were replaced, but the cognitive-verb cluster persisted and expanded into the new Tic 1 pattern flagged in this round (see below). See note in Tic 1 section: `filed`, `noted`, and `registered` as metaphorical cognition verbs are now the subject of external-editor concern in Draft_3.

**Tic 3 — Passive landscape constructions:** Fully resolved. "A river was occurring below" and "Hills were present in a general direction" do not appear in Draft_3. The one remaining "was present" instance is situational, non-landscape context: *"she was present at the shared observation window on the morning of the incident"* — not the flagged pattern. Tic 3 is closed.

## Tic 2 — "The kind of…" formula and stacked qualifiers

**"The kind of…" total count:** 65 instances (`the kind of` 46 + `a kind of` 16 + `that kind of` 3)

**Stacked qualifier pattern total count:** Approximately 30–35 sentences with two tonal modifiers in close proximity (within 30 characters); approximately 12 paragraphs with 3+ modifiers of the set {small, quiet, slight, careful}.

**Per-chapter distribution** (chapters with 4+ "kind of" instances):

| Chapter | "The kind of…" count | Stacked qualifier examples |
|---|---|---|
| Ch. 2 — In the Air | 4 | "the kind of competence that looked like nothing until you tried to replicate it" |
| Ch. 5 — Academy Mode | 4 | "the kind of almost-smile that meant a man had heard one of his own working principles repeated back" |
| Ch. 10 — The Cleanest Page | 4 | "the kind of sleep a person had when a conversation had finished without anyone being the one to end it" |
| Ch. 15 — The Third-Class Corridor | 4 | "the kind of composed attention that would have read as polite in a shorter conversation" |
| Ch. 17 — The Magistrate and the Debt | 4 | "the kind of face people made when they had an opinion about a name and had learned that opinion was inconvenient" |
| Ch. 29 — The Exam-Fodder Theory | 4 | "the kind of sentence that did its work fast" / "the kind of soup that did better at the early window" — two in adjacent paragraphs |

**Is this a tic or a voice marker?** Both, with register-dependent validity. The external reviewer who praised the rhythm is responding to something real: this construction is doing skilled work when it builds a behaviorally specific portrait (*"the kind of competence that looked like nothing until you tried to replicate it"*) and less skilled work when it replaces specificity with abstraction (*"the kind of soup that did better at the early window"*). The formula is at its worst when applied to objects or situations rather than people — it was developed to characterize behavior and has drifted into describing soup, lists, sentences, and sleep. In those cases it is not voice, it is deflection.

The more structurally significant concern is that the formula now co-occurs frequently with the "of someone who" construction that Draft_2 identified: both are behavioral-history formulas, and they appear in the same chapters. In Ch. 15, for instance, all four "kind of" instances are applied to character mannerism and three of them sit within a chapter that also contains three "of someone who" instances. When both formulas are active in the same chapter, the cumulative weight is visible even if each individual use is defensible.

**Recommendation:** Light sweep of Chs. 29, 5, and Ch. 15 specifically — the three chapters where either the formula has drifted from character to object/situation, or both behavioral formulas are present at density. Ch. 29 is the clearest target: two consecutive uses applied to a sentence and a bowl of soup are below the formula's best work. Chapters 2, 10, 17 read as intentional style — four uses each, all applied to character or social texture, none stacked against "of someone who." Leave those as-is.

**Context note on Ch. 36 (100%):** The 100% rate is a statistical artifact of chapter structure. Ch. 36 is the delivery/denouement chapter: most of the dialogue is ritual exchange ("Ready." / "Sannholt." / "The order." / "Thank you.") without attribution tags at all, and what attribution exists is correctly plain. The `said` monopoly here is not a flaw — it reflects that the chapter is doing something different from a dialogue-heavy investigation scene. Ch. 36 does not require intervention.

**Is this a systemic problem or isolated?** It is systemic in the second half of the manuscript. Chs. 13–14 (both 80%+) represent the early investigation; Chs. 22–23 and 30–35 are the testimony-heavy mid-to-late act. The common feature across all high-said% chapters is extended question-and-answer structure: Theron asking, witness replying, Theron confirming. In this rhythm, both parties "said" everything because neither is performing an action that could carry the tag instead.

The problem is not the word `said` itself — it is that the beat structure of testimony scenes defaults to the verbal tag instead of using beats (action before or after dialogue) or a richer verb when it would be accurate and specific. In Ch. 22's Vannic accounting (41 `said` in one chapter, 28 close-pairs within 400 characters of each other), the cumulative effect is an undifferentiated flatness over a chapter that runs approximately 4,500 words:

> *"The note said this morning," Vannic said. "It's still morning," Theron said. Sable took the chair by the window.*

The three-`said` sentence (two tags plus one use in quoted speech) is the clearest instance. Seventeen pages of similar cadence follows.

**Recommendation:** Systemic pass targeting Chs. 13, 14, 20, 22, 31, and 33 specifically. These are the chapters where: (a) the raw said% exceeds 82%, (b) the chapter contains 20+ dialogue lines, and (c) the structural context (testimony, extended argument, interview) means beats are available but unused. The fix is not synonym substitution — it is identifying where a gesture, a physical tic, or a reaction beat could carry the attribution instead. Ch. 22 is the highest-priority target by both raw count (41) and density (28 close-pairs). Ch. 36 does not require attention. Chs. 7, 11, 23, 34, 37 are secondary: their said% is high but the chapter dialogue load is lower, and the clean tagging reads as restrained style more than poverty of choice.

**Assessment — density problem or register problem?**

This is primarily a **density problem in Chs. 11 and 12**, with a **secondary register problem** across the middle of the manuscript (Chs. 7–12 especially).

The density problem in Chs. 11 and 12 is the clearest case: the four-word collocation "small clean [noun]" and the three-word "small honest beat" recur so frequently in these two consecutive chapters that they have become micro-formulas. In Ch. 11, "small" appears 26 times in a chapter of approximately 2,500 words — roughly once every 96 words. In Ch. 12, 24 times, and the "small clean X" construction (small clean window, small clean way, small clean sound, small clean breath) appears four times in that chapter alone, three of which are within 400 words of each other.

The register problem is different and broader. Across Chs. 1–12, the combination of these four modifiers produces a sustained tonal band: nothing is large, nothing is loud, nothing is abrupt, nothing is reckless. This is partly intentional — the book's register is deliberately *small and careful* in the literal sense, and the modifiers are earning that effect. But by Ch. 11–12, the reader has been in this tonal band for roughly 25,000 words, and the modifiers have stopped doing work because they are no longer contrast. The external note about "keeps prose in the same emotional register" is accurate: when `small` is the default modifier for sounds, objects, sensations, notations, and emotional beats in the same chapter, the word loses both precision and texture.

Ch. 32's "quiet" × 7 is the most compressed single-modifier problem in the late book: the quietness of the resolution chapters is a correct tonal target, but seven instances of the word in one chapter (across approximately 3,200 words, or one per 457 words) means the effect is stated rather than produced.

**Stacked instances requiring specific attention:**
- Ch. 10: *"in the small careful hand she did not have"* — `small` and `careful` stacked as dual modifiers
- Ch. 12: *"the small clean way the cook did it on a good service day"* — three `small` adjectives in adjacent paragraphs
- Ch. 34: *"He let the cabin be quiet for a slow count. Sable at the wall was quiet. Halsa on the bench was quiet."* — triple `quiet` in a three-sentence run (intentional anaphora, but sits in a chapter already carrying 17 modifier hits total)
- Ch. 11: *"small period of mutual professional fiction"* appears verbatim twice in the same chapter (once in Wisp's dialogue, once in Theron's echoing narration)

**Recommendation:** Two-track approach. Track A: Reduce `small` density in Chs. 11 and 12 — aim to cut from 26/24 to ~14/15, targeting specifically the "small clean X" collocation and the "small honest beat" repetitions. Track B: In Chs. 7–10 and Ch. 32, audit `quiet` and `careful` for uses that are stating a tonal quality the scene has already established through action; remove approximately one-third of those instances. Do not touch Chs. 13–40 as a priority — the modifier density drops to manageable levels outside the 11–12 peak, and the late-book uses are more purposeful.

## Priority ranking across all tics

**1. Tic 3 — "Said" tag monotony** (most damages reading experience)
The flatness in testimony-heavy chapters (Chs. 13, 14, 20, 22, 31) is the most immediately perceptible problem for a first-time reader. A chapter running 4,000+ words with 80–86% said-attribution is aurally monotonous in a way that cognitive verbs and tonal modifiers are not. The problem is also the most readily fixable: beat substitution (action before/after dialogue replaces the tag) does not require rewording the dialogue itself, only adding or reactivating surrounding scene texture.

**2. Tic 4 — Tonal modifier density, Chs. 11–12** (sustained register flatness)
Twenty-six uses of `small` in a 2,500-word chapter is the most concentrated single-word density problem in the manuscript. The "small clean X" micro-formula has reached collocation status: the reader has learned to unsee it. Because Chs. 11 and 12 are structurally central (the page-completion/case-resolution hinge of Act 2), flattening the prose texture here has proportionally more impact than the same density elsewhere.

**3. Tic 1 — Cognitive verbs** (cumulative texture flattening)
The specific-sentence violations (the triple-`filed` in Ch. 6, the triple-`noted` in Ch. 4) are immediately fixable. The surrounding diffuse density (5–9 hits per chapter across the first ten chapters) is a longer pass but not urgent at the sentence level. This tic is flagged as external-editor-visible, which means it will read as professional inexperience on a submission read; the sentence-level fixes in Chs. 4 and 6 should accompany any broader manuscript polish.

**4. Tic 2 — "The kind of…" formula** (lowest urgency)
With 65 instances across 40 chapters, the formula averages 1.6 per chapter — below the visibility threshold for most readers. The external reader who praised its rhythm is reporting an accurate effect from its best uses. The target is three chapters (Chs. 5, 15, 29) where the formula has drifted from characterization to situation-description. A light pass of 6–8 revisions across those three chapters is sufficient.
