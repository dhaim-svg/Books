# Internal Audit — Alias-Chain Breadcrumbs (Ch.3, 13, 14, 17)

*Prepared for internal use. Contains spoiler-level information. Read ending-twist.md before using this document.*

## Alias-chain map

This section is a working reference for the audit. It maps every name to what it refers to in the narrative, so the per-chapter analysis can be precise about legibility vs. deliberate opacity.

**Lord Petric Vannic** — the actual living nobleman, passenger on the Skybound Wyrm. The reader and Theron encounter him first only by reputation (Esherel's grievance) and then as a body. He is not a body. He is hiding in third class under the alias "Petor Vann."

**Petor Vann** — the cover identity Lord Petric Vannic uses to book a third-class berth (cabin 3-T). "Petor Vann" is phonetically close to "Petric Vannic" (first syllable, surname initial). This is not commented on in the text; it is a detail that snaps into focus on reread.

**"Lord Vannic" (unnamed illusionist in disguise)** — this is what the reader sees on the gondola throughout Act 1. He is not Lord Vannic. He is a hired illusionist who has taken Vannic's face and manner for the transit. The reader does not learn this until the Act 2 reveal in Ch. 13.

**M. Tannin** — the name on the first-class booking amendment in Ch. 3. This is NOT Lord Vannic, not the illusionist, and not Petor Vann. The known-facts table canonically establishes M. Tannin as a separate first-class passenger (cabin 3, first class), distinct from Petor Vann (cabin 3-T, third class). The amendment is a separate passenger's business — a legitimate sealed administrative entry. The confusion the editor has identified originates here: a reader may conflate M. Tannin with the Vannic-swap operation because both involve a booking amendment and secrecy.

**Joren (Joren Aldis)** — the dead man in cabin 12. Working illusionist. Hired through factor Berric Toll to impersonate Lord Vannic. The real murder victim. His first name is given in Ch. 13; his surname (Aldis) surfaces in Ch. 16 via the Toll correspondence.

**Summary of the chain as a reader experiences it:**
- Ch. 1–3: Reader knows "Lord Vannic" (unidentified impostor) and an unexplained booking amendment to M. Tannin in first class.
- Ch. 4–12: "Lord Vannic" is the apparent victim; the case proceeds on that premise.
- Ch. 13: Vannic is found alive as "Petor Vann" in third class. The dead man is named Joren.
- Ch. 14+: Joren's surname (Aldis), factor (Berric Toll), and contact thread (Pessel/Sannholt) emerge.
- M. Tannin remains an unexplained name in the first-class manifest throughout — never linked to the swap.

## Per-chapter evidence

### Ch.3 — what the chapter actually says

The M. Tannin entry appears in one passage, presented through the System passenger log as Theron reads it on his slate:

> *[Booking amended post-departure: M. Tannin, first class, cabin three. Original booking name on record under sealed administrative note. Steward's discretion.]*

What the reader knows after Ch. 3: There is a sealed name change in the first-class manifest. The original name is hidden under "steward's discretion." Theron notices it, reads it twice, considers six legitimate explanations and three illegitimate ones, and deliberately puts the slate away without acting on it. The chapter performs his restraint as characterization.

What a first-time reader can infer: Something was changed on the manifest. It might be suspicious. Theron suspects it could be, but decides it is not his concern today. The name "M. Tannin" is the *new* name — the person now in cabin 3 of first class.

What remains opaque: Who was in cabin 3 before the amendment. Whether M. Tannin is connected to the dead man or the Vannic swap at all.

**Legibility assessment:** The passage is correctly calibrated as texture. Theron's deliberate self-restraint ("the exercise was for him, not the slate") frames the amendment as an ambient detail, not an investigative flag. A first-time reader passes it. The problem the editor is identifying is not in how Ch. 3 introduces M. Tannin — it is in what happens later, when the connections to Petor Vann and Lord Petric Vannic are revealed but M. Tannin's place in the chain is never clarified.

### Ch.13 — what the chapter actually says

This is the Act 2 reveal. The alias chain is made explicit here for the first time.

**Petor Vann's introduction:**

> *The fold opened on a passenger name entered in the booking officer's round, hurried hand: Petor Vann, cabin three-T, paid passage Mirren Dock to Halverstow Port, second-class berth declined, third-class confirmed.*

And then:

> *Theron had the manifest page in his head: Petor Vann, cabin three-T, third class. He had the paper Sable had folded in her pocket, and the whole first-class column arranged in his mind like a set of slides in a lecture lantern. None of it had prepared him for the door opening.*

**The recognition beat:**

> *"Lord Vannic," Theron said.*
>
> *The name had come out of him with no drama, no deduction, no moment of working backward through evidence — it had arrived the way recognition always arrives, complete and sudden, the face fitting itself over memory while his lungs were still catching up. Lord Petric Vannic, alive.*

**The dead man named:**

> *"Joren. He said I could call him Joren."*

**The mechanics explained by Vannic:**

> *"He said he'd done impersonations before, that the illusion work was straightforward, that as long as I stayed out of the first-class corridor for the duration of the flight—"*

**The factor named:**

> *"Berric. Berric Toll. He operates out of the Mirren west harbour."*

What the reader knows after Ch. 13: Lord Petric Vannic is alive and has been traveling as "Petor Vann" (third class, cabin 3-T). The dead man is an illusionist named Joren (no surname yet). The operation was arranged through a factor called Berric Toll. The real Vannic boarded separately. The kill was targeted — whoever killed Joren knew Joren was Joren.

**On M. Tannin:** Ch. 13 does not mention M. Tannin at all. The known-facts table records a prior error here — "Ch. 13: Theron's recall conflates M. Tannin's Cabin 3 with Petor Vann's Cabin 3-T" — as a Draft 2 issue with a Draft 3 fix. Reading the Draft 3 text, the conflict does not appear: Ch. 13 makes no reference to M. Tannin or to a first-class amendment. The Petor Vann booking is presented as a separate, third-class register entry Sable obtained from Calden's separate box. The cabin designations are unambiguous: first class is "cabin three" and third class is "cabin three-T." They are distinct.

**On bookkeeping legibility:** The revelation that "Petor Vann" = Lord Petric Vannic is delivered clearly. Theron's instant recognition makes the connection firm. The problem the editor is describing is structural rather than local to this scene: the alias chain runs Vannic → Petor Vann (his cover) / Joren (his impostor), and M. Tannin is floating unconnected in a reader's memory from Ch. 3. A first-time reader at Ch. 13 may reasonably wonder whether M. Tannin is Joren's booking, Vannic's booking, or a third party, because nobody explains what it was.

### Ch.14 — what the chapter actually says

The false-identity layer is now presented as established fact to Esherel:

> *"Lord Petric Vannic is alive. He has been traveling on this gondola for the duration of the flight under a false name, in third class, hiding from a forced betrothal. He hired an illusionist to perform the role of Lord Vannic for the run. The illusionist is the dead man."*

This is precise and legible. Theron names:
- The living lord: Lord Petric Vannic
- His cover: "a false name, in third class"
- The dead man's role: illusionist hired to perform the role

The name "Petor Vann" does not appear in Ch. 14; Theron describes Vannic's cover as "a false name" without specifying it to Esherel.

**The Toll correspondence is opened in Ch. 14.** The alias chain gains a new entry — the factor Berric Toll — and a forward thread:

> *The factor had included, as an afterthought in the closing paragraph, a note that the practitioner would be available for a second engagement of similar scope starting the twenty-eighth, as he had indicated he would be in the Halverstow region for three to four weeks on prior personal business. There was a contact method — a postal address in a town south of Halverstow, a name: Pessel, care of the Copper Finch.*

**M. Tannin:** Not mentioned in Ch. 14. The amendment from Ch. 3 has been dropped from Theron's active case work and from the narrative.

**Legibility assessment:** The false-identity layer is introduced clearly and in plain language. The forced-betrothal motive is named. Esherel's reaction ("the man who wouldn't answer my letters was not the man who took my money") confirms the reader's understanding. The chapter does not muddy the alias chain; it consolidates it. The Toll correspondence opens a new thread (Sannholt / Pessel) that is the correct investigative direction.

### Ch.17 — what the chapter actually says

The alias chain is deployed in its most formal and compressed form in Theron's briefing to the magistrate:

> *"The deceased is Joren Aldis. Working illusionist, no formal credentials on record, contracted by Lord Petric Vannic of estate-of-record under the name of his factor, one Berric Toll of Mirren west harbour. The contract was an identity performance: Aldis was to travel the Halverstow run as Lord Vannic; Vannic traveled separately under a false name in third class, cabin three-T, to evade an estate obligation."*

This is the alias chain stated cleanly in one paragraph:
- Deceased: Joren Aldis (illusionist)
- Client: Lord Petric Vannic
- Intermediary: Berric Toll (factor)
- Vannic's cover: false name, third class, cabin 3-T
- Contract purpose: identity performance (Aldis impersonates Vannic on the gondola)
- Vannic's motive: evade estate obligation (the betrothal)

Later in the scene, the magistrate addresses Vannic directly and elicits a corroborating account. Vannic's own summary of the arrangement ("He hired Aldis for the performance and met him twice") is confirmed by the magistrate's interview.

**Petor Vann specifically:** Ch. 17 says "cabin three-T" but does not name "Petor Vann" as the alias. The alias was established in Ch. 13 and need not be repeated; the cabin designation is precise.

**M. Tannin:** Not mentioned in Ch. 17. The name has been dropped entirely since Ch. 3.

**Legibility assessment:** By Ch. 17, the alias chain has been stated in plain terms by Theron at least twice (to Esherel in Ch. 14, to the magistrate here) and the magistrate accepts it without confusion. The chain is not a bookkeeping problem at this point in the book for any reader who has been following. Ch. 17 is where the chain is *most* legible.

## Verdict on the Editor's complaint

**Partially warranted, but the problem is narrower than stated.**

The editor's complaint — that "connections between these names are too opaque too long" — is not accurate as applied to Petor Vann, Lord Petric Vannic, and Joren Aldis. Those three are handled well. The Petor Vann / Vannic recognition beat in Ch. 13 is dramatized clearly (instant recognition, full name spoken aloud, the face fitting itself over memory). Joren's name is given in the same scene, and the connection between the dead man and the impostor role is established plainly. By Ch. 14, Theron explains the whole structure to Esherel in one paragraph. By Ch. 17, the magistrate briefing delivers the alias chain as crisp exposition. None of these feel like bookkeeping errors — they feel like a mystery whose second half has correctly arrived.

The specific complaint that has merit is about **M. Tannin**. The name appears once, in Ch. 3, as a sealed post-departure amendment to the first-class manifest. It is never resolved, never mentioned again, never connected to the Vannic-swap investigation or to any named character. A first-time reader who has been paying attention to the manifest will carry this name as an open thread from Ch. 3 through Ch. 13 and beyond, and will eventually have to accept either that it is a red herring or that the author forgot it. Neither reading is satisfying.

The known-facts table confirms M. Tannin is a separate first-class passenger unrelated to the Vannic-swap. That is the correct answer. But it is not on the page in any chapter. The result is not that the alias chain reads as a bookkeeping error — it is that M. Tannin reads as an *unresolved* element in a story that otherwise resolves cleanly.

The editor has conflated two issues:
1. The alias chain (Petor Vann / Lord Petric Vannic / Joren / Joren Aldis) — this is calibrated correctly.
2. The M. Tannin amendment — this is a genuine loose thread that creates ambient confusion without being a reveal-risk.

## Legibility fixes (bookkeeping-only, not reveal-threatening)

### Fix 1 — Ch. 3: Anchor M. Tannin as mundane at the moment of introduction

Currently Theron considers "six legitimate reasons" and "three illegitimate ones" and then puts the slate away. The exercise is characterization. The problem is that this framing makes M. Tannin feel narratively live — a potential thread the story is deferring.

**Proposed fix:** Add one sentence to Theron's internal accounting that lands the mundane interpretation as the one he accepts. The sentence should occur *after* he lists the possibilities and *before* he closes the drawer — not as a resolution, but as a dismissal.

Current text:
> *There were probably six legitimate reasons.*
>
> *He could think of three illegitimate ones too, and he watched himself doing it, and he made himself stop.*

Proposed insertion (one sentence, between the current second and third paragraphs of the amendment passage):
> *The amendment was a name change made after the boarding manifest was finalized but before the gondola had cleared the dock. That much was new since the morning. Sealed administrative note meant a clerk on the ground had recorded the original entry and locked it under Calden's office, not the System's. The kind of paperwork that happened when a passenger had a reasonable expectation of privacy. A merchant moving under a trade pseudonym. A widow not wanting to be addressed by her late husband's title. An academic incognito.*
>
> *There were probably six legitimate reasons.*
>
> **[INSERT:] He picked one at random — the merchant — and filed it there.**
>
> *He could think of three illegitimate ones too, and he watched himself doing it, and he made himself stop.*

**Why this is safe:** Theron picking a mundane explanation and filing it signals to the reader that M. Tannin is not a thread to follow. It does not close M. Tannin as a mystery (we never learn who they are) but it removes the sense that the narrative is holding it in reserve. It is also in character: Theron is practicing restraint, and filing a plausible reason is an act of deliberate closure rather than investigation. No information about the Vannic swap, Joren, or the identity-chain is introduced or implied.

---

### Fix 2 — Ch. 13 (or Ch. 14): Briefly surface and dismiss M. Tannin post-reveal

Once the Vannic swap is revealed, the first-class manifest is revisited in Theron's mind as he re-evaluates the case. The M. Tannin amendment was at rank 2 in his working suspect list (known-facts table, Ch. 5: "Tannin (sealed amendment) at 2"). A first-time reader who remembered that ranking will expect the name to come up in the aftermath of the reveal — either as now explained, or as definitively cleared.

**Proposed fix:** In Ch. 13, during the aft stairwell landing passage where Theron and Sable recalibrate the case, add one brief reference that clears M. Tannin as a separate matter. The passage currently reads:

> *"The case is not what I thought it was." He said this without inflection, by way of inventory. The corpse in cabin twelve was an illusionist named Joren who had taken contract work impersonating a lord who was alive and hiding two decks below from a marriage he had not chosen. Esherel's forty-one thousand crowns were still real. Calden's twelve-minute absence was still real. The locked geometry of cabin twelve was still real. The dead man's method of death remained unknown. All of that was still on the page. The page had simply acquired a new first line.*

This passage is the natural home for a brief M. Tannin disposal. Proposed insertion after "All of that was still on the page. The page had simply acquired a new first line.":

> **[INSERT:] The Tannin amendment was still in the first-class manifest and still none of his business — a sealed change made before boarding, tied to nothing he had uncovered. He moved it to the back of the column.**

**Why this is safe:** This sentence does not reveal who M. Tannin is. It tells the reader that Theron has looked at the amendment in light of the new information and found no connection. Since Theron and the reader learn together, this is sufficient: if Theron has checked it and found nothing relevant, the reader accepts it as resolved. No information about the swap is added; no new alias is introduced. The Vannic/Joren reveal is not affected in any way.

An alternative placement is the beginning of Ch. 14, in Theron's preparation before knocking on Esherel's door — he is shown composing his opening sentence, which could briefly pass through a mental accounting that includes M. Tannin's dismissal. However, Ch. 13's stairwell landing is the cleaner structural location because it is the explicit moment of case recalibration.

## What must NOT be changed

**1. The Petor Vann / Lord Petric Vannic recognition beat in Ch. 13.**
The instant, wordless recognition — "it had arrived the way recognition always arrives, complete and sudden, the face fitting itself over memory while his lungs were still catching up" — is precisely calibrated. It tells the reader exactly what is happening without belaboring it. Any expansion risks making the recognition feel engineered rather than organic. The name "Lord Petric Vannic" spoken aloud by Theron is the fulcrum; nothing around it should be adjusted.

**2. The phonetic echo "Petor Vann" / "Petric Vannic."**
This is never commented on in the text, and it should not be. It is a reread detail — the kind that makes a first-time reader think "of course" on the second pass. Any authorial underlining of the similarity would both pre-announce the swap and patronize the reader. Leave it as texture.

**3. The System ping in Ch. 13 post-reveal: "The dead man has a name. Cabin twelve is not a locked room. It is Joren's room."**
This is where the moral pivot of the novel is first registered by the System. It does not explain itself; it does not editorialize. It simply names the shift. The phrasing "Joren's room" — claiming the cabin for the dead man's identity rather than the lord's — is doing crucial work. Do not alter it.

**4. Theron's deliberate self-restraint in Ch. 3.**
The whole chapter is built around Theron practicing restraint as a discipline. The M. Tannin amendment is one test of that restraint. His decision to put the slate away rather than investigate is characterization. The proposed Fix 1 (inserting one sentence of internal filing) is the maximum intervention — it does not disrupt the restraint arc. Any fix that causes Theron to investigate the amendment, even briefly, destroys the chapter's thematic work.

**5. The Berric Toll connection remaining opaque through Ch. 13.**
When Vannic names Berric Toll in Ch. 13, the reader does not yet know who is in the third-class manifest or how the killer found Joren. Toll's name is given before Fell's name is mentioned (Fell appears in Ch. 14's manifest work). The sequence is correct: Toll is introduced as the factor, then the investigation identifies who in the third-class pool may have had access to Toll's network. This order must not be collapsed — it preserves the investigative structure of Act 2.
