# Review: Skybound Wyrm

## The Hook & First Impressions
Right out of the gate, the prose style grabbed me. It has this incredibly dry, methodical, almost clinical cadence that perfectly mirrors Theron’s headspace. I thought I was walking into a standard secondary-world fantasy, but the moment the System chimed in with `[Investigator — Field status: Unscheduled interval.]`, I realized this was something different—a subtle, grounded take on LitRPG/Progression fantasy blended with a locked-room murder mystery. 

The world-building is remarkably efficient. You don't dump lore on the reader; instead, we learn about the world through the logistics of drake-handlers, gondola seating charts, and Theron's hyper-fixation on procedure. By the time Calden's third knock went unanswered in Chapter 4, I was entirely strapped in.

## What Kept Me Turning Pages
The twist in Chapter 13 is spectacular. You built a pristine, airtight case against Esherel—I was completely convinced she was at least involved—and then completely pulled the rug out by revealing the dead man wasn't even Lord Vannic. Shifting the mystery from "who killed the Lord" to "who is this man and why did someone kill *him*" breathed entirely new life into the narrative just as the initial gondola investigation might have started to stall.

But the real engine of the story is the dynamic between Theron and Sable. Their banter is fantastic. Theron's reliance on his "page" and rigid categorization contrasting with Sable’s intuitive, human-centric observation (like noticing *how* people walk or turn their cups) creates a brilliant investigative duo. The slow burn of their mutual respect is just incredibly satisfying to read.

## Where I Drifted a Bit
If there's a place the pacing dips, it's the middle stretch in Halverstow (roughly Chapters 18–22). Once we got off the gondola, the tight, claustrophobic tension of the locked-room mystery expanded into a lot of procedural legwork. The tracing of shipping registries, factor shorthand (TL-9), Vantrel Secured Storage, Kolach, and Marbeth felt a bit dry. While it makes total sense for an Investigator to grind through administrative paperwork to find a lead, reading about harbor registry filings slowed the momentum compared to the gripping character interactions that came before and after.

## Predictions: What I Got Right (and Wrong)
* **Wrong:** I totally bought the Esherel red herring. Theron’s logic was so sound I let myself get dragged right along with his "clean page."
* **Right:** The moment Halsa Renn was written off as a "fourth-tier" background passenger with no connections and a cash ticket, my mystery-reader radar started pinging. The quiet ones who observe too much are always dangerous. 
* **Blindsided:** I absolutely did not see Theron’s own thesis being the murder weapon/method. That was a brilliant stroke of plotting. Setting up the "scryproof glamour exploit" as an academic hypothetical in Chapter 1 and paying it off as the definitive *how* in Chapter 29 was immensely satisfying. It tied Theron’s personal journey directly to the mechanics of the crime.

## The Characters
* **Theron:** He is a wonderfully neurodivergent-coded protagonist. His need to categorize, his discomfort with unearned assumptions, and his literal thumbing of his graduation pin make him deeply endearing. 
* **Sable:** She steals every scene she is in. She’s competent, guarded, and fiercely observant. The moment she slides the wooden fish into her pocket, or cuts the bread to share, speaks volumes without her having to monologue about her feelings.
* **Joren Aldis:** You managed to make me grieve for a man who was a corpse by Chapter 4. The slow reveal of his kindnesses—the ginger sweet, the stepping aside on the gangway, the money for his niece—made his death feel like a profound tragedy rather than just a puzzle to be solved.

## The Ending: Did it Land?
It landed perfectly. I respect the hell out of the choice to not have a massive, explosive magical confrontation. A quiet conversation across a desk in a small cabin was the only ending this book needed. Halsa’s motive—the botched scare-job that killed her seventeen-year-old brother—was devastating. It made the conflict morally grey; she wasn't a mustache-twirling villain, just a grieving sister balancing a ledger.

The System finally dropping the `[Investigator — level six. Talent unlocked: Seeing the Unseen]` prompt on the back porch of the inn was a fantastic payoff to Theron's internal growth. 

## Final Thoughts & Stray Observations
* The motif of the carved wooden fish was beautifully handled. It grounded the abstract magical plotting in something painfully human.
* The writing style relies heavily on repetition and precise, almost rhythmic phrasing ("The page was the page," "He inclined his half-inch"). It works incredibly well to establish tone, though occasionally it borders on noticeable stylization. 
* The epilogue/final chapter hook with the strange device on the slip in Pessel's bundle is a great way to open the door to Book 2 without leaving Book 1's main plot unresolved. 

I would read a sequel to this immediately. It’s a smart, quiet, and deeply empathetic mystery.
