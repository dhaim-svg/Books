# Internal Audit — Supporting-Cast Voice Differentiation

**Source chapters read:** 02, 05 (Vellaine lounge scene), 06, 08, 14, 17, 28, 30, 35  
**Reference docs read:** character-profiles.md, standing-style.md

---

## Is the editor's complaint warranted?

Partially warranted, and the "partially" matters. The register homogeneity is real but unevenly distributed. Calden and Vellaine are the two characters most at risk of sounding alike: both speak in short, precise, competence-coded sentences with minimal emotional register and a preference for withholding over volunteering. The surface texture of their dialogue — clipped, declarative, functionally elegant — is close enough that if their names were redacted from adjacent scenes, a reader could plausibly swap several of their exchanges. Esherel is often placed in the same compressed register by the prose around her, but her actual dialogue has more distinctive structural features (the long prefatory build before the point, the ledger-mind that does accounting aloud) that separate her more than the editor's complaint acknowledges. Davren is lightly on the page and genuinely flat — he speaks in magistrate's shorthand, which is intentional, but it is the same shorthand Vellaine uses for academic precision and Calden uses for service discretion. Sable's register is distinct in its functional precision, but because she and Calden both favor short, strategic utterances, the distinction is more in *what* they say than *how* they say it, which can blur in longer shared scenes. The editor is most right about Calden and Vellaine. The complaint is overstated for Esherel, who already has the most developed speech architecture on the page.

## Per-character voice fingerprint

### Calden
**Chapters present in audit:** 02, 06, 08  
**Current voice on the page:** Short declarative sentences, almost no subordinate clauses. Tends to repeat the questioner's framing before answering ("I went past. The corridor lamp at twelve was at trim, as I had set it. I did not stop."). His rhetorical habit is the *paired negative* — what he did, then what he did not do — which is a service discretion pattern: confirm the routine, disclaim the deviation. Recognizable when the pattern fires; indistinguishable from generic clipped competence when it doesn't.  
**Profile alignment:** The profile says: "Knows everything that happens on the gondola but is bound by service discretion. Opens up to Sable, not Theron." The page delivers the service discretion clearly. The "opens up to Sable" asymmetry is shown in narrative description (ch 14: "She'll be more forthcoming than he was at the beginning. You're better at asking him than I am.") but the reader never *hears* that different register directly — Calden's dialogue with Sable is either absent or reported rather than rendered. This is a gap between profile intent and page execution.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "She always holds, sir." (ch 02)
- "I would prefer, sir, to answer that question with the captain's leave. I can tell you that the matter took me approximately twelve minutes and that I was in the second-class corridor for that period." (ch 06)
- "Now, sir. I'll finish this after." (ch 08)

**Differentiation problem:** Yes — specifically from Vellaine. Both speak in short declarative bursts with embedded precision and minimal affect. Calden's distinctiveness rests on the "sir" cadence and the formulaic paired-negative structure, which holds in ch 06 but collapses when he gives one-word or two-word answers.  
**Proposed distinguishing feature(s):** Give Calden a specific structural tell: he *always names the protocol before the answer*. Not just "I went past" but "First-class lamp check: I went past." The protocol-header is how a professional steward organizes information internally, and having it surface briefly in speech — not always, but when the topic is procedural — distinguishes him from Vellaine's academic precision and from the generic clipped register. Additionally, consider rendering one scene (ideally ch 14 or a later chapter) in which Sable hears a different Calden — less formal, more observational, perhaps finishing a sentence with an inference rather than a fact. This is already mandated by the profile; it just hasn't been executed in rendered dialogue.

### Vellaine
**Chapters present in audit:** 02, 05  
**Current voice on the page:** Dry, elliptical, academic. Uses the incomplete sentence as a rhetorical form — she names a thing and stops before the verdict ("Give it a year." / "Some of them favorable." / "Mm."). The ellipsis signals intellectual containment: she has more and is choosing not to give it. Comfortable with silence. No hedging vocabulary. When she does produce a complete sentence it tends to be long and precisely subordinated, as in the ch 05 corridor-report: "I heard Calden's first round at first watch. I heard his second. I did not hear his third. I noticed the third only because there wasn't a fourth."  
**Profile alignment:** The profile says: "Plausible expert in early acts. Not the killer, but she remembers Theron's thesis and watches him with academic interest." The page delivers the academic watchfulness but underuses the *thesis connection*. In ch 02 she has identified him by pin and told him to "give it a year," and in ch 05 (post-discovery) her corridor testimony is clean and contained. But the System prompt in ch 02 — *She's read your reconstruction paper. The look was recognition, not assessment* — is never followed up in dialogue. Vellaine never actually *says* anything about the thesis on the page, which means the most differentiating trait in her profile (she knows his academic work) exists only as subtext.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "Investigator. Fresh posting. Vellaine. Juridical theory, the Larent Institute." (ch 02)
- "Give it a year." (ch 02)
- "I have thoughts. Some of them favorable." (ch 02)
- "I heard Calden's first round at first watch... I noticed the third only because there wasn't a fourth." (ch 05)

**Differentiation problem:** Yes — specifically in the post-discovery scenes where her compressed testimony reads identically in structure to Calden's compressed testimony. The ch 05 corridor report is precise, short, declarative, emotionally contained. So is Calden's. Without the "sir" address, the two passages are hard to distinguish by rhythm alone.  
**Proposed distinguishing feature(s):** Vellaine should have one structural marker Calden doesn't: the *embedded evaluation*. She is an academic, and academics can't resist assessing the quality of their own observations. Where Calden says "I did not stop," Vellaine says "I heard the corridor as I'd hear it any morning, until I didn't" — which is already closer to her register. Extend this: give her the habit of briefly rating the evidential quality of what she offers ("I can't give you the bolt — I wasn't listening for it. The absence of Calden's third round is a cleaner datum"). This is how a juridical theorist thinks, and it distinguishes her from Calden's procedural statements and from Esherel's ledger-logic. A second opportunity exists in letting her say *something* — one line — about Theron's thesis, even obliquely. This would fulfill the profile's "optional setup for a book-two mentor" signal and make her the only character whose speech contains a direct reference to Theron's academic identity.

### Esherel
**Chapters present in audit:** 02, 06, 14, 17  
**Current voice on the page:** The strongest and most differentiated register in the supporting cast. Esherel builds toward her points through a long prefatory structure: she lays out the ledger (facts in order, dates, amounts, names) and lets the conclusion emerge from the accumulation rather than stating it first. Her sentences are grammatically complex by the standards of the cast — multiple embedded clauses, subordinate conditions — and her word choice is the language of commerce and accounting ("comparable isn't equivalent," "the back third," "the fourth paragraph is the one that wants the work"). Her most distinctive rhetorical move is the *corrected accounting*: she catches her own framing and adjusts it in real time, which gives her dialogue a quality of continuous self-audit.  
**Profile alignment:** The profile says: "Cleanest red herring in Act 1, fully cleared by the reveal. In Act 3 she becomes a surprisingly good source on Joren — she actually noticed him as a person, because that's how she does business." The page delivers both the red-herring register (the folio-driven grievance in ch 06) and the Act 3 pivot (ch 14, the wine-glass grip observation, the exits watch) with strong fidelity. The ch 14 "working mages learn early to map a room" line is exactly the profile's intended voice: she doesn't watch people out of warmth, she watches them because it is commercially useful and she is good at it. One minor divergence: the profile says her humor is dry and survivalist; the ch 17 scene with Vannic's signature is mostly played in deadpan competence rather than wit, which is appropriate, but there's no throwaway line in that scene that has the lightly amused register she shows in ch 06 ("It is not a compliment. It is an observation."). She risks flattening in the later scenes into pure function.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "There was one, in eight separate movements." (ch 06)
- "It is not a compliment. It is an observation. I have been on the receiving end of thorough men before. The thorough is good. It is sometimes the thorough's shape that is the problem." (ch 06)
- "I noticed him do that on the second morning and I was annoyed with him about it, because it was inconvenient to my accounting of the man." (ch 14)

**Differentiation problem:** No — Esherel already has the most developed and distinctive voice among the supporting cast. The editor's complaint groups her with the compressed-register characters; this is the most overstated part of the complaint.  
**Proposed distinguishing feature(s):** Minor adjustment only. In later scenes (ch 17 onward) her speech risks losing the faint dry humor that distinguishes it in ch 06. One or two light touches of self-aware commercial wit in her later appearances would sustain the register without requiring structural changes. The current voice is working.

### Davren
**Chapters present in audit:** 28, 35  
**Current voice on the page:** Extremely compressed. Short directives, one-sentence procedural summaries, almost no subordination. Davren speaks in case-management shorthand: he names the gap, he names the standard, he closes the exchange. His characteristic move is the *restatement that reframes*: he repeats back a piece of Theron's report in different terms that clarify what it is and what it isn't ("You have done a good piece of work on the who. Take it the rest of the way."). In ch 35 he is present only by implication (the writ), where his "courteous" register is reported through Halsa's summary of the document rather than rendered in dialogue.  
**Profile alignment:** Davren has no profile entry. He enters as a functional magistrate in Act 2 and operates as a case-management sounding board and authority figure. The ch 28 voice — available, not patient; sparse directives; the two questions left clean in Theron's head — is appropriate to that function. The risk is that his voice is indistinguishable from the Halverstow magistrate in ch 17, who also speaks in short procedural bursts ("Primary suspect pool. Vannic. The Fell lead."). Two magistrates with identical compression is not exactly the same problem as Calden/Vellaine — they never appear in adjacent scenes — but it does mean Davren arrives in ch 28 without any sonic individuality of his own.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "Walk me through it." (ch 28)
- "No. You don't." (ch 28, on having the method)
- "Drink your coffee, Mavik. The morning's still young." (ch 28)

**Differentiation problem:** Yes — minor but real. He is differentiated from the ch 17 magistrate only by name and by the coffee-drinking detail. His dialogue register is the same compressed procedural as every other authority figure. The "drink your coffee" line is the only moment where something like warmth or dry humor surfaces, and it is not followed up.  
**Proposed distinguishing feature(s):** Davren should have one rhetorical habit the Halverstow magistrate doesn't: the *question held back until the end*. His ch 28 pattern — listen in full, then ask the two structural questions — is already present in narration. Let one instance of this show in dialogue form: Davren asks a question and then, before Theron answers it, answers his own question *almost* correctly but leaves the gap that needs filling. This models his "available, not patient" quality in speech rather than in description, and it distinguishes him from the Halverstow magistrate's "give me the names, the facts, and the outstanding questions" directive mode.

### Halsa
**Chapters present in audit:** 35  
**Current voice on the page:** Highly controlled. Very short sentences, no waste. Her distinctive feature is the *structural repetition with variation* — she returns to a phrase and slightly changes it each time ("All right" / "That is all" / "The book is even now"). This has an almost liturgical quality: she is not improvising her speech, she is executing statements that have been composed in advance of the conversation. The effect is of extreme intentionality — every sentence is weighed and placed. This is the most distinctive voice in the entire supporting cast, and it is achieved with minimal complexity.  
**Profile alignment:** The profile says: "Not cruel. When Theron names her, she doesn't deny it. She asks whether anyone used Joren's real name during the investigation." The page delivers this with precision. The "grieving, methodical" characterization in the profile is exactly what the spare repetitive register accomplishes on the page. The one potential gap: the profile notes she is "not cruel" and "tragic," and ch 35 does this, but we only have one chapter of Halsa in dialogue. She benefits from having no pre-reveal scenes to blur her register.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "Joren Aldis. Not the lord's name. Not the man he was wearing. The name he had when he wasn't working." (ch 35)
- "I had a brother. He was seventeen. His name was Tarrin." (ch 35)
- "The book is even now." (ch 35)

**Differentiation problem:** No. Halsa has the most distinctive voice in the cast, achieved through structural repetition, deliberate pacing, and the near-total absence of rhetorical decoration. She is the one character the editor's complaint clearly does not apply to.  
**Proposed distinguishing feature(s):** No intervention needed. The existing voice is precise and character-specific. Leave it as-is.

### Sable
**Chapters present in audit:** 02, 05, 06, 08, 14, 17, 30, 35  
**Current voice on the page:** Functionally precise. Her characteristic move is the *conditional statement without the condition named*: she says what she knows, stops before the inference, and waits. ("I'll do it the way I do it. It won't sound like anything for a few sentences. Will you wait?" — ch 08). She uses very few adjectives. She gives information in operational units — the smallest pieces that carry the most meaning — and she withholds the connective tissue between them (the explanation, the judgment, the emotional register). This is distinct from Calden's service-formula precision: Calden withholds because of professional obligation; Sable withholds because she knows how to read a room and is choosing the right moment.  

**POV note:** Per standing-style.md, Sable's interiority is inaccessible. This audit reads her only through dialogue and action, as required.

**Profile alignment:** The profile says: "Dry, warm, a little teasing. Lets him be brilliant without flinching, but does not let him be careless." The dryness and the non-flinching are well-executed. The *warmth* is present in action (pushing the refilled cup to him, the "drink half of that" in ch 08, the small adjustments) but is somewhat underserved in dialogue. The profile's "a little teasing" quality surfaces only occasionally — the quick dry look at "Calden's permission, this time. I've decided to start asking" in ch 08 is one of the few places where the register is warm rather than simply precise. The teasing register could be a little more present without exceeding what dialogue alone can achieve.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "I'll do it the way I do it. It won't sound like anything for a few sentences. Will you wait?" (ch 08)
- "You had it. You were going to ask the wrong question first." (ch 08)
- "With Calden's permission, this time. I've decided to start asking." (ch 08, quick dry look)
- "The morning the Wyrm landed at Halverstow. The fifth of October. In the captain's day-cabin..." (ch 35, the direct date-anchored recall)

**Differentiation problem:** Minor. Sable is distinct from Calden in *what* she says (observational, inferential, occasionally warm) but not always in *how* she says it (both favor short units, minimal decoration). The risk is in long working scenes where both characters are present and her speech strips down to monosyllabic confirmation ("Yes." "No." "Mm."), at which point only action-beats carry her register.  
**Proposed distinguishing feature(s):** Dialogue-only interventions only (per POV lock). Two techniques: First, Sable should have a recurring habit of *naming the question before she answers it*, not as exposition but as a tell that she is organizing. She does this once in ch 08 ("I'll do it the way I do it") but it could be a consistent register marker rather than a one-off. Second, her occasional humor — which surfaces very briefly in ch 08 and ch 30 — should fire at least once per major shared scene. The profile says "dry, warm, a little teasing": the warmth is in the actions, the dryness is in the short precision, but the teasing needs more dialogue-level presence. Neither of these proposals requires interiority access; both are achievable through the way she phrases observations.

### Wisp
**Chapters present in audit:** 02  
**Current voice on the page:** The most aurally distinct voice in the book. Wisp speaks exclusively about the drakes and speaks about them as colleagues she is professionally managing. Her sentences are longer than any other supporting character's, her emotional register is present but disguised as assessment, and she uses the precise vocabulary of her trade (harness rigging, working thermal, downbeat, extension arc). Most importantly, she volunteers opinions without being asked.  
**Profile alignment:** "Has names for each of the three drakes pulling the gondola and treats them like difficult colleagues. Carries a lot of the book's texture." The page delivers this completely. The line "Petal knows. Mottled has a theory that I side with Gravel. That's the opposite of the truth, but she's decided it and I've given up correcting it" is the profile realized in miniature.  
**Representative quotes:**
- "She's been doing it since Halverstow run last spring. She thinks Gravel corners too wide on the upswing. Takes it personally." (ch 02)
- "Petal knows." (ch 02, said with complete seriousness)
- "She's been flying this route since before Mottled was hatched. The temperament comes with the miles." (ch 02)

**Differentiation problem:** No. Wisp is the editor's own reference point for what successful differentiation looks like. No intervention needed.

## Highest-priority differentiation targets

**1. Vellaine (highest priority)**  
She is the character most likely to blur into Calden in the post-discovery scenes, because both speak in short declarative bursts with a compressed functional style and no warmth markers. Her profile's most distinguishing trait — that she has read Theron's thesis — has never been voiced in dialogue, only in a System notification. She appears in both Act 1 and Act 2 chapters, so there are multiple intervention points. The fix is low-cost (one line activating the thesis connection; the embedded-evaluation habit in procedural responses) but the failure mode is real: in her ch 05 lounge interview she is functionally indistinguishable from a careful witness, and only the narrative gloss ("the kind of thinking she did at the page when she was deciding whether a line was true") distinguishes her from what any precise character would say.

**2. Calden (second priority)**  
Calden's service-discretion register is consistent but one-note. The profile promises a character who opens differently to Sable than to Theron, and this asymmetry is only described in narration, never shown in dialogue. A single rendered exchange between Sable and Calden — in which his formulas drop and he is briefly more observational — would close the gap between profile intent and page execution. His paired-negative habit is a good structural marker but needs to be supplemented with the protocol-header when he is in procedural territory.

**3. Davren (third priority)**  
Davren is the lowest-risk target because he appears in fewer scenes and his functional compression is appropriate to his role. However, he reads as interchangeable with the Halverstow magistrate, which matters less for any single scene but matters cumulatively when both authority figures appear in the same reader's memory. The "drink your coffee" line in ch 28 is doing real work to distinguish him; one more moment of that register — the slight warmth under the procedural exterior — would be sufficient to anchor his voice.

## Lowest-risk characters to leave as-is

**Esherel** — Already has the strongest and most distinctive voice among the supporting cast. Her ledger-logic structure, prefatory accumulation, and self-correcting accounting register are unique on the page. The editor's complaint is least warranted here. The only refinement worth considering is a small dry-wit touch in her later appearances to sustain the ch 06 register, but this is polish, not repair.

**Halsa** — Has the most distinctive voice in the book. The structural repetition with variation ("All right" / "The book is even now" / "That is all") is both character-specific and thematically appropriate. The liturgical quality of her speech reflects a woman who has been composing her final conversation for eight years. Do not touch it.

**Wisp** — The editor's own example of successful differentiation. Functions perfectly. The long sentences, the colleague-register, the volunteered opinions — all working.

**Sable** — Her voice is distinct enough in content and operative precision that the blurring risk is minor and manageable through the two low-cost dialogue techniques described above (naming the question before answering it; one dry or teasing register marker per major scene). She does not require structural work.

## Chapter-specific fix targets

**Vellaine — Chapter 05 (post-discovery lounge interview)**  
Current passage: "I heard Calden's first round at first watch. I heard his second. I did not hear his third. I noticed the third only because there wasn't a fourth." This is clean but reads in the same cadence as Calden's corridor report in ch 06. Proposed change: after "I did not hear his third," add one line in which Vellaine briefly rates the evidential quality of the observation, e.g., "That's the cleaner datum — absence registered against expectation rather than against direct observation. The fourth isn't even an absence; it's the absence of a default." This is one additional sentence, costs nothing structurally, and distinguishes her from every other witness in the chapter.

**Vellaine — Chapter 02 (dining room, System notification)**  
The System tells Theron she has read his reconstruction paper but Vellaine says nothing about it. Proposed change: After "I'm on holiday" / "So am I. Have another pastry," add one brief exchange in which she says something oblique about the paper — not praise, but a qualifier that acknowledges she knows what it argues. For example: "It oversimplifies the signature-compatibility question in section three." This costs one line, fulfills the profile's "academic interest" note, and distinguishes her from every other character in the book.

**Calden — Chapter 14 (or any chapter with Sable-Calden exchange)**  
The Sable-Calden asymmetry exists only in narration; the moment should be rendered once in dialogue. The best available leverage is ch 14, where Sable uses Calden's records at the alcove. Proposed change: rather than rendering Calden's contribution purely through Theron's narration ("Calden's confident about them"), give Sable and Calden one short exchange in which Calden volunteers an observation — about one of the third-class passengers — that he would not have given Theron. The observation should be human rather than procedural: a character note, not a fact. This executes the profile's "opens up to Sable, not Theron" signal in rendered dialogue for the first time.

**Davren — Chapter 28 (post-theory-layout, courthouse office)**  
Current passage ends with "Drink your coffee, Mavik. The morning's still young." This is the best line Davren has and it does real work. Proposed change: no structural intervention needed in ch 28, but when Davren next appears (or when his voice is referenced), lean on this warmth-under-procedure register rather than letting it sit as a one-off. If there is a brief ch 33–35 reference to Davren's writ or instructions, that framing can carry a note of his characteristic "courteous" quality (which Halsa names in ch 35 but which is rendered through her reading of the document, not through his voice). Consider letting one attributed sentence from the writ be quoted directly, in Davren's voice, which would give him presence in a chapter where he never appears.
