# Chapter 27 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes, I'd keep reading past page one. The opening image — a corner table that has silently been claimed as "his," the pot arriving without asking — establishes character and place in a single beat. The voice is distinct and controlled. The one friction point is the phrase "the column he had written the column on" (para 2–3): the prose almost doubles back on itself there, and a first-time reader may re-read the sentence to confirm it said what it said. That small snag aside, the cadence is unhurried without being slow, and the occupational introspection that follows ("He had been writing profiles since his second-year practicum") is curious enough to pull the reader forward.

---

### 2. Planted details

No detail reads as a hard plant in this chapter. Two items stood out:

- The **carved fish** ("The fish stays where it is"): felt natural — a brief closing line in a letter, passed without pause.
- The **Pessel front room description** ("two chairs, tea-set already laid, very still in the shoulders"): slightly composed, but reads as character texture rather than a narrative signal. It did not stop me.

Nothing was underlined or pointed at. Both items moved on smoothly.

---

### 3. POV character

Theron lands as sympathetic-adjacent: competent, self-critical without self-pity, dry but not cold. The "I had not understood, before this morning, how clean an exclusion that was" moment is the most humanising in the chapter and it earns its placement without over-explaining. The reader spends most of the chapter inside his professional mind rather than his emotional one, but the register shift in how he handles Sable's letter (the long sitting, the caraway bread, the careful refolding) suggests depth. A hundred thousand words with him feels like a reasonable proposition.

---

### 4. Tone calibration

Firmly cozy. The Sunday-morning dining room — pot arriving without asking, caraway bread from the kitchen, two factors arguing about slate prices — does the atmospheric work well. The tone never dips toward grim. The one borderline moment is Pessel's question ("Tell me whether he died well") which is quietly heavy; it's earned rather than dark, but a reader looking for strict beach-read buoyancy might feel its weight. That said, the weight is appropriate and the chapter carries it without lingering.

---

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "That was not a small misreading. That was the entire training of his first three years." The two-sentence structure oversells the point; the profiling failure has already been shown on the page and doesn't need the underlining.

**Keep:** "The bread came out of the oven somewhere behind the kitchen door, and the smell moved through the dining room the way good smells do on Sundays, in no particular hurry. He let it."
