# Chapter 02 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes, I kept reading past page one — and past the first section break. The opening hook earns its keep: a protagonist who is cataloguing the drakes' rhythm before he notices he's doing it is a clean, economical way to show class and personality at once. The line "His class had been active for less than a day and was apparently revising its own opinion of its usefulness" is the sentence that fully bought my attention. The one hesitation: the sentence "The amendment could mean anything — a name correction, a cabin swap, a passenger who'd boarded under a temporary record. It could mean nothing" runs through a list of possibilities and then deflates itself. That micro-beat of tension built and released without payoff made me briefly wonder if the book was going to do that repeatedly. It doesn't — the rest of the chapter earns confidence — but it is a small wobble near the top.

### 2. Planted details

Nothing stopped me or felt pointed at. A few details had texture worth noting: "Vannic" eating with his back to the room — noticed, but reads as antisocial characterisation rather than a flag. Vellaine's puzzle ring with the academic convention knot — interesting detail, passed it without lingering. The first-class manifest amendment got one beat of attention and was then deliberately set aside by Theron, which felt earned and not suspicious. The woman in the brown coat with chalk and paint on her sleeve landed as atmosphere. None of these details broke the scene-level flow or recruited my suspicion.

### 3. POV character

Sympathetic, and I would spend 100,000 words with him. The pin that stays exactly level, the promise to Warden Desh already fraying at the edges, the moment he comments aloud on Esherel's wine before deciding to — these are all small, precise, and non-irritating ways to show his training running ahead of his self-awareness. He's slightly insufferable in the way the spec intends: right about things, a little too pleased about it, but not smug. The line "He found it genuinely reassuring, which surprised him" (about Calden's certainty) is the note that makes him likeable rather than just competent. Vellaine's dry "Give it a year" about the pin is exactly the kind of friction that tests a POV character and he passes it well by not touching the pin.

### 4. Tone calibration

Reads as a cozy beach-read throughout. The drake dispute as workplace drama is the best tonal beat in the chapter — "Petal doesn't comment. Petal is a professional" is the kind of line that signals to the reader this book knows what it is. The Esherel wine scene is correctly weighted: a small interpersonal friction with some modest comedy, not a harbinger. The chapter ends on warmth rather than tension. No moments felt too dark. The pace is slow relative to plot-driven genre fiction, but this chapter is in the right register — the prose earns the drift.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "He read it twice. The amendment could mean anything — a name correction, a cabin swap, a passenger who'd boarded under a temporary record. It could mean nothing." The list dissipates what the detail just built. One sentence showing him clocking and setting aside the amendment would do more work.

**Keep exactly as written:** "Petal doesn't comment. Petal is a professional."
