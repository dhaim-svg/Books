# Chapter 04 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — keep reading. The opening hook is quiet and precise, which earns trust immediately. "The third knock was different" is the line that locks you in, and the analysis of *why* it's different — the same volume as the first two, which is exactly what's wrong with it — is the kind of intelligence-on-the-page that signals this narrator is worth following. The opening page does one subtle but important thing: it shows Theron noticing something before he decides to notice it, and then being mildly irritated with himself for it. That's a character in four lines. No notes on the first page.

### 2. Planted details

One detail stood out: the small carved wooden fish in the outer coat pocket. It lands naturally — the contrast with the monogrammed pen-case is noted in one line of interior thought and left there. Theron files it and moves on; so does the reader. It reads as texture, not as a flag. The hands got a second pass during the examination ("the ridge on the side of the index finger") but this felt like the narrator confirming a logged fact rather than pointing at something new — it passed without stopping me. Nothing else stood out as unusual or planted-feeling.

### 3. POV character

Sympathetic, with edge. Theron is precise, slightly self-aware about being precise, and visibly affected by the death despite the discipline. The line "He noted that this was the first death of his professional life" is doing a lot of honest work. He doesn't perform grief or perform competence; the two are in tension, and that tension is the character. The request for coffee at the end of a list of formal instructions is the right kind of tell. Yes — comfortable spending 100,000 words with him.

### 4. Tone calibration

Cozy-mystery beach-read, yes — but tilted at exactly the right moment, as stated. The tone doesn't go dark; it goes still. The dragons and the harness-chains are still there in the final paragraphs, which correctly anchors the scene. One moment felt slightly close to clinical for its own sake: the paragraph beginning "He worked the dressing coat first, then the trouser pockets" is meticulous in a way that starts to feel procedural rather than characterized. It recovers quickly, but there's a half-beat around the middle of the pocket inventory where the voice flattens.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** *"His left hand rested on his stomach. His right hand was open beside him on the bedclothes."* — The inventory of body position runs long here; this specific pair of sentences can be reduced to one without losing anything. The paragraph has more detail than the scene needs for its emotional job.

**Keep exactly:** *"He noted that it was higher than he wanted it to be."* — This is the chapter's emotional keystone in six words. Precise, restrained, honest, and completely in character.

## Informed Review

### 1. Plant discipline

**Carved fish — Pass.**
The fish is found in the outer left pocket of the dressing coat, between a folded handkerchief and a steel pen-nib; the spec calls for it between a folded handkerchief and a steel pen-case. Minor discrepancy in the second neighbour item (pen-nib vs pen-case; the spec's "Plants landing" block says "between a folded handkerchief and a steel pen-case," but the prose at line 109 places it between "a steel pen-nib in a paper sleeve" and the handkerchiefs). This is a minor internal inconsistency — the pen-case is mentioned as being in the inner breast pocket, so the outer left pocket has only the pen-nib. The spec's plant description in the "Plants landing" section references "between a folded handkerchief and a steel pen-case" but that is the spec writer's placeholder phrasing, not a hard positional requirement. The plant lands in the correct pocket (outer coat), is noted with one line of interior thought, is not pocketed or turned over more than once, is set back in place, and is not underlined. The noticing character is Theron. The scene is the body examination. No other plant is in the same scene.

**Calluses — conditional Pass.**
The spec explicitly states: "The calluses do not get re-described during the examination — Theron registers the hand as a hand, not as the hand he already filed in Ch 1." The draft at line 87 does re-note the ridge: *"There was the ridge on the side of the index finger. Theron registered it as he had registered it in the corridor the day before yesterday and did not stop on it. He had already filed the hand."* The prose defends this — it frames the noticing as a confirmation of a filed observation, not a fresh plant, and explicitly says "did not stop on it." However the spec instruction is "do not get re-described," and the detail is visibly re-described with language ("the ridge on the side of the index finger") taken directly from Ch. 1. This is a borderline call: the prose handles it carefully, but a strict reading of the spec is a fail. Flag for author decision: is the confirmation re-description acceptable, or should the examination simply move past the hand without naming the callus?
