# Chapter 24 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — past page one and through to the end without friction. The opening line drops you into mid-investigation without preamble, which is exactly right: "fourteen passengers, minus Vannic" tells you the stakes and the genre in four words. What earns continued reading is the precision of Theron's thinking — the way he catches his own methodological error ("apparent... which was honest. He had also filed it as functional closure, which was not") is the voice of a detective worth spending time with. No hesitation at any line.

### 2. Planted details

The Calden lamp note — "third night, late lamp, nine possibly, not certain" — stood out as a detail with weight. It felt natural rather than pointed: the uncertainty was carefully handled (Calden doesn't know which cabin), and Theron's still face while he keeps the reaction internal reads as disciplined, not artificial. The Sannholt thread / Renn re-examination didn't register as a planted detail so much as an investigative correction — it moved through naturally. Nothing here stopped the read or felt underlined.

### 3. POV character

Sympathetic and engaging. Theron's self-correction — catching that he filed suspects against the wrong map — is the most attractive version of a detective: rigorous about his own errors, no self-pity, just the pencil back down and the next flag written. The "eat something that isn't coffee and case notes" exchange lands the warmth without straining for it. 100,000 words with him: yes, readily. The only small note is that the Renn material is dense case-review in the first scene; a reader coming in cold at this point in the book might need a beat of orientation, but it doesn't distancing.

### 4. Tone calibration

Calibrated correctly throughout. This is cozy-procedural: the dining room, the dray horse at the corner, the tidiness of a practiced traveler's bag, the half-piece of bread — all doing the work of keeping the tone warm while the investigation moves. No tonal wrong notes. The closest it comes to flatness is the mid-afternoon solo work passage (the clean copy, the case book at the desk's far edge), but that scene earns its quiet as a resting breath before the close.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "He thought about adding something about the Roven thread, about Fell's silence and what it covered, about the wall that Vantrel represented. He didn't." — The second sentence does the work. The first sentence tells us what Theron decided not to say, which is a form of saying it. Cut the first, keep "He didn't" attached to what precedes it or let the action carry the ellipsis.

**Keep:** "She looked at him — not the way she looked at the case, but the way she looked at things she was about to leave and wouldn't see for a while." — Precise, earned, says everything about Sable without entering her interiority, and the no-interiority restraint is exactly right.
