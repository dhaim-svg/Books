# Chapter 20 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — keep reading. The opening image (sharpening a pencil with a borrowed knife, checking the tip against the lamp, reading a note while doing it) is specific enough to pull forward, and the first mystery the chapter sets up — who is Roven, why does the name matter — arrives quickly. The scene-setting at the Halverstow Arms is economical. Nothing stalls.

One marginal concern: readers who have not been with this story since the beginning are dropped into a fairly dense web of names (Vannic, Toll, Joren, Roven) with no grounding. As a genre chapter of an ongoing novel this is fine; as an entry point it would lose someone. Not a fault of this chapter — just noting it would not stand alone.

### 2. Planted details

No details registered as planted in this chapter — which, for a reader at this stage, is appropriate. The Roven information lands as pure investigation. The moment that stood out most was Vannic revealing Toll is his estate factor: that felt like a genuine surprise that earned its beat, not something underlined for the reader. It read as a discovered fact, not a planted detail. Nothing in the chapter stopped me as pointed at a future reveal.

### 3. POV character

Theron lands well — precise without being cold, methodical without being robotic. The small behavioral notes carry him: going back to the pencil while he reads the note, sitting in the window chair at the wrong angle without correcting it, the moment he stops at the door with something still unsettled and turns back to ask one more question. He reads as someone whose intelligence is a genuine pleasure to be near, even when he's slightly annoying about it. Spending 100,000 words with him is a yes.

Sable also works well through action and dialogue alone — her "aware, not managing" immediately after Theron's report is the best line in the chapter.

### 4. Tone calibration

Cozy register holds throughout. The comedy of Vannic's position (stuck in a harbor-view room he can't afford to leave, with his estate factor at the center of the murder investigation) lands correctly — the chapter is funny about his situation without cruelty. The investigative content is substantive but never grim. The harbor sounds closing the chapter are the right register: unhurried, ambient, a working day finishing. One moment risked tipping toward too much investigative proceduralism — the long Roven-naming exchange at the Arms — but the human beats (Vannic's jaw tightening, the worn carpet stripe) kept it anchored.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "He had borrowed a knife from the harbourmaster's writing table — good steel, kept for wax not wood, but serviceable — and was working around the cedar barrel in slow even passes when the boy appeared at the door with a folded note and the flat look of someone who had been told not to wait for an answer." The parenthetical detail about wax versus wood reads like earned specificity but costs the sentence its rhythm; cutting from "good steel" to "serviceable" and tightening the rest would move faster.

**Keep:** "Sable looked at the folded notes in her hand. 'Aware. Not managing.'" Exact, quick, and demonstrates what the Witness class does better than any System notation could.
