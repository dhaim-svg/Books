# Chapter 28 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — I kept reading. The opening three paragraphs earn the turn of the page. The setting is exact without being labored (desk that had been the same desk for thirty years), the dynamic is set before either character speaks more than two words, and the procedural economy of "He kept the order he had practised" telegraphs that this is a man who has done this before and knows it isn't enough yet. The first line I considered stopping at was the phrase "He did not say *the suspect*" — it risks feeling mannered — but the next beat ("He said *the column*, and *the working line*, and once, *the person we are looking at*") justifies it. No false start.

### 2. Planted details

None flagged as planted in the traditional sense — this is deep Act 3 and the chapter is doing inference work, not texture-planting. One detail did stop me briefly: the description of Theron *not* writing the inference he most wants to write (the causal arc about the original placement). For a first-time reader, the extended interiority about what the page "wanted from him" is the first moment where the prose self-references its own restraint, which is slightly pointed. It reads less like a character disciplining himself and more like the author demonstrating discipline. It does not break immersion, but it is the one passage that reads as craft-visible rather than scene-transparent.

### 3. POV character

Strongly sympathetic — and notably more so here than a purely cold procedural detective would be. The moment he avoids ringing for coffee because "his face was doing whatever it was doing about Joren" is the chapter's best piece of characterization: we see that he is moved, that he knows he is moved, and that he will not perform it for witnesses. That restraint is the character in miniature. The chapter earns 100,000 words of company. The one caveat: the extended reflection on the placement-as-causal-arc (three paragraphs of Theron telling himself what he does not yet know) slightly slows the middle section, and a reader looking for plot momentum may skim it.

### 4. Tone calibration

Solidly cozy-beach-read. The magistrate's office is warm without being quaint. The harbour detail (rope-master, skiffs, the working Monday pace) grounds the setting without accumulating into atmospheric weight. The pie scene at the end is exactly right — the host recommending the pie over the stew on a Monday because the stew has been on since lunch is precisely the kind of small ordinary competence that carries cozy register. The chapter never dips into grimness. One minor tonal wobble: the paragraph beginning "He had not been ready to think about method since the Vannic frame had broken in Act 2" briefly references prior story structure in a way that reads slightly internal-notes-ish. Not a serious problem, but "the noble-rivalry frame" feels more like a chapter-spec term than a character thought.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** *"He had not, until this single sheet of green-sealed paper, had the placement on a page in plain ink. The two things were not the same."* — The distinction being drawn (confirmed via Sable vs. confirmed on official paper) is real, but the prose states it twice in different forms within the same paragraph, and the second version adds little.

**Keep exactly as written:** *"The host said the stew, but the pie was the better bet on a Monday, because the stew had been simmering since lunch and the pie was an hour out of the oven."* — Does everything at once: characterizes the host, grounds the scene, and is quietly funny in the right register.
