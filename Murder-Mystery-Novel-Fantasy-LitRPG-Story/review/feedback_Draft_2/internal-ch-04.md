# Internal Audit — Chapter 4 (Locked-Room Mechanism)

---

## Lock Setup Passages

**Chapter 4, discovery sequence (early in chapter):**

> "The first-class cabin doors were good carpentry. Solid panel, brass plate, an interior bolt that engaged a hooked catch in the frame. The steward's master key would turn the lock proper. It would not draw the bolt."

**Chapter 4, Theron's interior survey:**

> "The interior bolt of the door, drawn. He went over and looked at it from inside. The bolt was a heavy brass slide that engaged a hooked catch in the frame. From the inside, you slid it across with your hand. From the outside, you could not reach it. The service slot was the only access, and the service slot was three feet to the left of the bolt and required a steward's lift-key."

**Chapter 4, Theron's written notes:**

> "*Bolt engaged from interior. Service slot used to draw. Window port, deck hatch, chain trunk: all sealed, undisturbed.*"

**Chapter 5, Theron repeats the summary to Captain Joriss:**

> "Cabin twelve, deck four. Interior bolt drawn. Porthole, deck hatch, chain trunk all sealed. No bottle, no glass used, no powder, no visible wound."

---

## Opening Method Passage

**Chapter 4, the door is opened (steward using lift-key):**

> "Calden's hand went to his other key. It was a thin, flat thing, dull steel, the sort of object that did not appear in any guidebook to gondola travel. He slid it through the narrow brass slot at the height of the bolt and worked his wrist twice. There was the small specific sound of metal lifting metal."

---

## Mitigating Context

**One explicit constraint is present in Chapter 4:**

> "The service slot was the only access, and the service slot was three feet to the left of the bolt and **required a steward's lift-key**."

This is the sole in-text constraint: the service slot requires a specific tool ("a steward's lift-key," which the narrative describes as "a thin, flat thing, dull steel, the sort of object that did not appear in any guidebook to gondola travel"). The phrasing "did not appear in any guidebook" gestures at obscurity — it implies the tool is not common knowledge — but the text does not state that:

- only credentialed stewards carry or have access to such a key
- the lift-key is kept in a secured location
- the existence of the service slot itself is confidential
- the method requires specialized training or skill beyond inserting the key and "working his wrist twice"

The constraint on *knowledge and tool access* is implied but not stated. The "did not appear in any guidebook" line is the closest the text comes to explaining why a passenger could not replicate this method. It does not say *only Calden has a key*, *only one such key exists*, or *the slot's location is not visible without knowing where to look*.

**No constraint on physical ability is present.** The operation is described as trivially simple ("slid it through ... worked his wrist twice"). Any person with the right tool could perform it.

---

## Chapters Spanning the Premise

The locked-room premise is active for **2 chapters** before the mechanism is explained in the same chapter it is introduced.

- **Chapter 4** — lock is explained, service slot and lift-key are introduced, and the opening method is demonstrated, all within the same chapter. The discovery scene and mechanism revelation occur in chapter 4 itself; there is no preceding chapter in which the bolt-drawn door is established as a puzzle before the solution is shown.
- **Chapter 5** — Theron summarizes the locked-cabin facts to Captain Joriss, treating them as already established.

The locked-room is not presented as a mysterious premise held open across chapters and then resolved. The mechanism is described at the moment of entry. The *interpretation* of the sealed room as evidence of suicide vs. murder is what spans forward from chapter 4 onward, not the question of how the door was opened.

---

## Verdict

**Editor criticism: partially supported.**

The editor's core argument — that the method is available to anyone who possesses the tool — is supported by the text. The lift-key is the only barrier, and no constraint establishes that only Calden possesses one, that such keys are controlled, or that their existence is known only to crew. The "did not appear in any guidebook" line is too weak to constitute a meaningful constraint: it addresses publicity of the *gondola system*, not access to the *specific instrument*.

However, the criticism slightly misframes what the locked-room achieves in the narrative. The puzzle the sealed cabin creates is not "who could have opened the door" (the answer is: anyone with a lift-key, i.e., Calden). The puzzle is "why was the bolt drawn at all." Theron's working interpretation is that the bolt-drawn cabin is evidence the occupant drew it himself — and therefore that no one entered, i.e., Vannic locked himself in and died without external agency. The locked-room is deployed primarily as evidence of *no intrusion*, not as evidence of an impossibly exclusive entry method. The editor is right that the service slot does not make the room impregnable; the text is right that the bolt itself is the story's actual evidentiary object.

The weakness is that this distinction is not made explicit. A reader who expects a classic locked-room mystery — where the locked door means no one could get in — will register the editor's objection. The text treats the locked cabin differently (as evidence against intrusion rather than as an impossible puzzle), but it does not say so plainly enough to redirect that expectation on first read.

---

## Fix Options

**Option (i): Redesign the mechanism**
Replace the service-slot + lift-key approach with something that genuinely restricts access. For example: the bolt can only be reached through the service slot if the slot is unlocked from the outside with a second key held separately from the master (two-key access control); or the slot is sealed with a wax pellet that would show tampering if disturbed; or the service slot is a maintenance access known only to the head steward and disclosed to no passenger. This would make the editor's objection structurally impossible by establishing that access genuinely requires Calden specifically, not merely someone with the same category of tool. Cost: moderate — requires a few lines of setup, possibly rewriting part of the discovery scene.

**Option (ii): Leave it (reader-level readers don't notice)**
The service slot description is dense enough that most readers will not stop to ask "but couldn't a passenger have one of those keys." The tool is described in a way that reads as unusual and proprietary. The locked-room is not the genre-promise of this novel — the question is who and why, not how-did-they-enter — and readers following the investigation will be tracking motive and opportunity, not re-evaluating the entry mechanism. The editor's objection is technically correct but may be below the threshold of disruption for the target readership.

**Option (iii): Add one line that explains why the method wasn't available to others**
Add a single sentence, either in the discovery scene or in Theron's internal survey, establishing that lift-keys are controlled items: e.g., *The lift-key was Calden's alone — there was one per gondola, held by the head steward, and it had not left his person since boarding.* Alternatively, a line of Theron's analysis: *The slot's position was not marked on any passenger-facing plan; a person would have had to know it existed.* This closes the editor's objection without redesigning the mechanism, at the cost of one sentence in chapter 4. This is the lowest-effort fix with the most targeted effect.
