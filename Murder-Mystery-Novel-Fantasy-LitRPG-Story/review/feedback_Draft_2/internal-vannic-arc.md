# Internal Audit — Vannic Arc

---

## First Major Appearance

**Ch. 1, paragraphs ~10–17 (dock arrival and corridor handshake)**

Dock entrance:

> The man coming through was somewhere in his mid-twenties, with the jaw that came from a hundred years of portraits and a coat that communicated its price by not discussing it. Behind him, a personal steward pushed a luggage cart stacked three high. He walked at the front of the convoy with the settled certainty of someone who had never been required to hurry and had developed opinions about people who were.
>
> His gaze moved across the dock once and efficiently. The scholars — dismissed. The family — beneath notation. Theron — assessed as young and not immediately consequential, set aside. Madam Esherel — and here his expression shifted very slightly, the way a man's does when he's encountered something he didn't expect and doesn't want to give ground on — acknowledged, not greeted.

Corridor handshake:

> The grip was firm, brief, bare palm. And the side of his index finger — the raised ridge, built up by years of sustained casting hold, where prolonged contact with a spellwork frame wears the skin to something harder and more specific than ordinary callus. Theron's thesis advisor had one. Both of his practical illusion professors had them. You didn't get that from an occasional hobby. You got it from ten hours a day, reliably, across years.
>
> *Noble who dabbles*, he filed. Then, amending: *practices more seriously than that implies.*
>
> "You look young," Vannic said. It came out as observation rather than insult, which was something.

## Confrontation Scene

**Ch. 13, paragraphs ~5–16 (cabin three-T, third class)**

The scene where Vannic is unmasked does not show slapstick stuttering in the full register the editor describes, but it does show a clear collapse into helpless, agonized passivity:

> The man who opened it was thirty years old at the outside, with the wrinkled look of someone who hadn't slept well in three days and was still wearing yesterday's waistcoat.

> The man who was not Petor Vann made a sound that was not quite a word and stepped backward. He did not shut the door.

> "I don't know what—" he started.

Then, in the extended interview and the magistrate scene (Ch. 17):

> He told her about Berric Toll. He did it in the agonized, digressive fashion of a man who had been rehearsing a story and kept catching himself adding disclaimers, and the magistrate let him go until she had what she needed and then said "stop" and he stopped.

And:

> "I — this is entirely my —"
> "Sit down," the magistrate said.

And the debt scene with Esherel (Ch. 17):

> "To be fair, I didn't — I was avoiding —"
> "Duke Esseven's daughter. Yes, I know."

And:

> Vannic opened his mouth, looked at the folio, and shut it again.

> Vannic made a sound that was not quite a word. He was still holding the pen.

By Ch. 19–20, Vannic is still passive and apologetic but slightly more functional — he provides information under gentle pressure, picks at cold food, and makes "small, uncomfortable" gestures. By Ch. 22 he is deliberately cooperative, though still coded as bumbling and faintly ridiculous ("poached eggs and the evidence of poached eggs," standing up then sitting down, holding a fork for no reason).

The "stuttering" register is present throughout Chs. 13, 17, 19, 20, and 22. It is strongest in Ch. 13 and the Ch. 17 magistrate/debt scenes.

---

## Bridge (if any)

No explicit psychological bridge appears in the text between the two registers.

The closest the text comes is the Ch. 13 cabin description:

> The man who opened it was thirty years old at the outside, with the wrinkled look of someone who hadn't slept well in three days and was still wearing yesterday's waistcoat. He was medium height, medium brown hair, and he had the face of a person who had been hoping this particular knock would not come.

This establishes that Vannic has been in a state of anxious concealment for three days — but it is a *physical* notation (wrinkled, unslept) rather than a psychological account of why a man who has been scanning rooms with predator-level efficiency collapses into "a sound that was not quite a word" the moment he is found. The fear of discovery is implicit in the premise, but the text offers no internal thought, no narrator observation, and no contextual framing to explain the behavioral magnitude of the shift.

**No bridge found** — the gap between registers is unmarked in the text.

## Dock Loitering Scene

**Ch. 1, paragraphs ~6–9 (dock arrival)**

Vannic does not "loiter" on the public dock. He arrives as a late entrant to the boarding queue with his personal steward and luggage cart, crosses the dock directly to the gangway, pauses briefly to let an elderly passenger finish a step, thanks Calden by name, and boards. The sequence occupies perhaps five minutes of elapsed time.

However, the editor's underlying concern has structural validity: Vannic is supposedly a paranoid man evading identification, yet he enters the public dock with *a personal steward and a luggage cart stacked three high*, causes a ripple of social attention ("Lord Vannic," murmured neighbor to neighbor through the crowd), and walks with "the settled certainty of someone who had never been required to hurry." His arrival is conspicuous by design — conspicuous enough that Sable, standing in the boarding area, goes still when she notices him thanking Calden. This contradicts the premise that Vannic is operating a covert identity swap; a paranoid principal would delegate the conspicuous entrance to his double and arrive quietly as Petor Vann, not walk the dock as himself.

Relevant passage:

> The man coming through was somewhere in his mid-twenties, with the jaw that came from a hundred years of portraits and a coat that communicated its price by not discussing it. Behind him, a personal steward pushed a luggage cart stacked three high. He walked at the front of the convoy with the settled certainty of someone who had never been required to hurry...
>
> "Lord Vannic," someone murmured, nearby. The name moved through the remaining queue in undertones, neighbor to neighbor.

There is no subsequent explanation of *when* Vannic transitioned to the Petor Vann cover. The manifest note (Ch. 3) says the M. Tannin amendment was "paid on the dock after boarding but before lift," and the known-facts ledger (Ch. 6) confirms "M. Tannin amendment was paid on the dock after Vannic boarded but before the gondola lifted." The implication is that Vannic boarded first-class as himself (loudly, conspicuously), then somehow transitioned to third class as Petor Vann. No scene depicts this transition, and no explanation is given for why a paranoid man exposed himself on the public dock when the point of the arrangement was to be invisible.

## Manifest Booking Note

**Ch. 3, paragraph ~3 (Theron reading the manifest)**

> *[Booking amended post-departure: M. Tannin, first class, cabin three. Original booking name on record under sealed administrative note. Steward's discretion.]*

And Theron's interpretation:

> *Sealed administrative note* meant a clerk on the ground had recorded the original entry and locked it under Calden's office, not the System's. The kind of paperwork that happened when a passenger had a reasonable expectation of privacy. A merchant moving under a trade pseudonym. A widow not wanting to be addressed by her late husband's title. An academic incognito.

**Assessment:** The booking-change note is not plain text — it is described as a "sealed administrative note" locked under Calden's discretion. The original name is explicitly not visible to manifest readers: the System line only shows the amended name (M. Tannin) and the note's existence, not the original name.

The editor's specific claim that "anyone reading the manifest can see his name change" is not textually supported. The protection mechanism exists: the original name is sealed under steward's discretion. However, there is a narrower security logic problem: Theron can see that *an amendment exists* and that it is sealed. A trained investigator with sufficient leverage (which Theron acquires by Ch. 5 after a murder is discovered) can request the sealed note via the captain's authority — and does exactly that, learning in Ch. 6 that the amendment was "paid on the dock after boarding but before lift." The seal is professional courtesy, not strong protection. Whether this constitutes a "security logic inconsistency" depends on how paranoid Vannic is supposed to be — a more security-conscious principal would have used a name change made before boarding, not a post-departure amendment that flags itself as an amendment.

Relevant passage from Ch. 6 (Calden confirms the amendment's financial timing):

> The M. Tannin amendment was paid on the dock after Vannic boarded but before the gondola lifted.

## Verdict — Problem 1 (Voice Drift)

**Supported.**

The two registers are both present and the gap between them is significant. The Ch. 1 Vannic is a composed, room-scanning, hyper-observant aristocrat with a working-mage's ten-hours-a-day discipline in his hands. The Ch. 13–22 Vannic is wrinkled, sleep-deprived, inarticulate under questioning, digressive, apologetic, and passive throughout his remaining appearances. The gap could be made coherent — three days of claustrophobic hiding in third class, believing his double may have exposed him, waiting for a knock he dreads — but none of this context is supplied on the page. There is no internal thought, no narrator bridge, no explicit scene showing the deterioration. The discovery moment (Ch. 13) and the magistrate scene (Ch. 17) both treat Vannic's collapse as a given rather than as a consequence. This is a real structural gap, not an artifact of close reading.

---

## Verdict — Problem 2 (Security Logic)

**Partially supported.**

The editor's specific complaint — that the manifest booking-change entry is "plain text" readable by anyone — is not accurate. The text describes it as a "sealed administrative note at the steward's discretion," with the original name not visible. This is a legitimate protection mechanism appropriate to the gondola's administrative context.

However, the editor's underlying concern about security logic has two valid sub-points that are textually supportable:

1. **The visible anomaly:** The amendment's *existence* is visible to anyone reading the manifest, including Theron in Ch. 2–3. Vannic's cover relies on investigators not noticing the anomaly, not on the anomaly being undetectable. A truly paranoid principal would not want a flagged amendment at all.

2. **The dock exposure:** Vannic boards conspicuously as himself — named aloud in the crowd, accompanied by a personal steward and stacked luggage cart, his arrival noted and commented on — with no explanation of how or when he transitioned to the Petor Vann cover. This is the larger security logic problem: not the manifest entry but the public dock behavior, which is precisely what the editor flagged as "loitering openly on the public dock."
