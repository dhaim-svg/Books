## Tic 3: Passive landscape constructions

**Pattern:** Inanimate geographic feature as grammatical subject + weak existential/intransitive verb in a flat or awkward construction.
**Search terms used:** `occurred`, `was present`, `were present`, `existed`, plus contextual sweeps.

Total count: **2 instances** — both in the same sentence, Ch. 2

### All instances found:

1. "A river was occurring below." — Ch. 2 (line 235)
2. "Hills were present in a general direction." — Ch. 2 (line 235)

**Full sentence context:** "A river was occurring below. Hills were present in a general direction. The whole of it had the particular distance of altitude, where cities became suggestions of themselves and roads disappeared into the colour of the land."

**Note:** The third sentence in this passage redeems the paragraph — it is alive where the first two are flat. The first two are clearly intentional (the narrator is describing the landscape as processed through altitude-induced abstraction), but an editor unfamiliar with the intent reads them as inert. No further instances of this construction were found elsewhere in the manuscript.

## Summary

Tic 2 (filing-verb crutches) is by far the most pervasive problem: roughly **95 metaphorical uses** across the manuscript, compared to ~37 for Tic 1 and 2 for Tic 3. At a frequency of nearly 2.4 uses per chapter on average, `filed` and `registered` have become invisible to the author and will be invisible to a sympathetic reader — but they will irritate any careful editor or re-reader who notices the groove. The concentration peaks in Chs. 7, 10, 11, and 24 (five or more per chapter), where Theron's cognitive machinery is under the most narrative pressure; this is understandable as an origin but not as a finished state. The triple-`registered` in Ch. 29 and the `filed` x3 sentence in Ch. 7 are the clearest candidates for immediate revision.

Tic 1 (the "someone who" formula) is the most visible to a first-time reader because it clusters in Ch. 1 (seven instances in ~200 lines), which is the reader's primary point of calibration for the author's prose style. Seven uses in a single chapter establishes the formula as a crutch before the reader has finished orienting. One or two per chapter is defensible; five or more in a chapter is excessive. Ch. 23 (four instances) is the secondary concern.

Tic 3 (passive landscape constructions) is present only twice, in a single sentence in Ch. 2. The construction is probably intentional — it enacts the narrator's altitude-induced perceptual numbness — but it reads as inadvertent clumsiness to a cold reader. A one-word fix ("A river ran below. Hills rose in a general direction.") removes the problem without losing the tonal effect of the following sentence.

**Top-priority tic to address in revision:** Tic 2. The filing-verb density is high enough across the manuscript that a systematic pass replacing roughly half the instances with active cognitive verbs ("noted," "held," "marked," "stored away," "let sit") would immediately elevate the prose texture of every chapter Theron narrates.
