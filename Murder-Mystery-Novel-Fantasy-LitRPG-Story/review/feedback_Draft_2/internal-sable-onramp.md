# Internal Audit — Sable On-Ramp

---

## Sable Appearances by Chapter

**Ch. 1 — [Scene 1, boarding area]** — She nearly collides with Theron on the gangway; he notices her coat, her bag, her expression. She catches Calden's eye and signals with one finger. She moves on without speaking to Theron. — **Role: background**

**Ch. 1 — [Scene 2, boarding observation from upper deck]** — After Vannic thanks Calden by name, Theron notices her from the upper rail: she has stopped at the far edge of the boarding area and is watching the specific patch of air where Vannic's gesture landed. She is still for "a moment, in a way that had a quality to it." She then shoulders her bag and goes upstairs. — **Role: background / transitional** (first moment that individuates her as someone who notices things)

**Ch. 2 — [Passenger lounge, mid-morning]** — She has property papers spread on the corner table, pencil in her left hand, reading a document with the expression of someone who "had expected it to say exactly this and had hoped to be wrong." She glances up when Theron passes through, registers him without attention, looks back down. — **Role: background**

**Ch. 3 — [After dinner, corridor]** — Referenced in passing: "the lounge was empty now, the brown-coat woman gone, her papers gone with her." Not a scene, a note of absence. — **Role: background (noted by absence)**

**Ch. 4 — [Discovery corridor]** — Named for the first time: "Sable. He had her name now, from the dining-room list." She is standing at her open cabin door, not crossing the threshold, not looking into Vannic's cabin — "looking at the doorway itself, the way you look at a window when something has changed on the other side of it." After Theron's corridor announcement of the facts, she moves to the corridor rail, watching the carpet at the edge of Vannic's door. She holds Theron's eye "a moment longer than the exchange required" when he tells her he will speak with her. — **Role: transitional**

**Ch. 5 — [Theron's cabin, mid-morning]** — She appears at his threshold holding the second copy of the manifest, which she has brought herself past three cabin doors and a steward's station. Her thumb was on the second-class single-men bracket page — the page Theron had spent longest on. She offers a conditional promise ("if anything occurs to me I'll bring it"). — **Role: transitional / early active** (first moment of unsolicited useful action)

**Ch. 5 — [First-class lounge, noon]** — She is at her corner table with papers. Theron sees that she was looking at the porthole at "the angle of the light along the brass" — not the harness chain position, but where it had been. He files her and turns away. She says "now is when I would have rather you came" when he offers to defer the formal interview. He files the answer and moves on. — **Role: transitional**

**Ch. 7 — [Steward's pantry]** — She is inside the pantry passage, mid-conversation with Calden, getting the answer to the same fourth-watch question Theron had asked the previous day. Calden answered her. She apologizes to Theron; he says she didn't ask anything he didn't ask. She says "He answered me, though." This is the inflection point. Theron proposes: "I'd like to compare, if you've an hour." She agrees. — **Role: active partner begins here**

**Ch. 7 — [Dining room, working lunch]** — She shares what she has on Vannic's behavioral profile (coastal-flat vowel, tiredness, eating habits, biscuit detail). She proposes walking the corridor. She identifies Calden's body-positioning and working geometry from observation. She explains why Calden answered her and not Theron ("because you're going to write down what he says and I'm not"). She establishes first-name basis. — **Role: active partner**

**Ch. 7 — [First-class corridor walk]** — She walks Theron through Calden's sightline geometry, alcove position, and the significance of the half-second slow-past. She tells him she'll listen to what Calden says next without asking. — **Role: active partner**

**Ch. 8 — [Second-class lounge bay window]** — She is already there when Theron returns with the captain's leave. She tells him to ask the general question before the specific (don't open with cabin twelve). She repositions his interrogation strategy in two sentences. She provides the timing insight: ask for the minute the twelve began, not just that it was twelve minutes. She walks the fourth-watch geometry with him, adjusting one tick three minutes earlier. — **Role: active partner**

**Ch. 8 — [Bay window, continuing]** — She builds the social/behavioral profile of Vannic (wall-facing rationale, ginger sweet, cook-watching). She offers to manage Calden's ambient disclosures while Theron does the formal interview. — **Role: active partner**

**Ch. 9 — [Reading alcove]** — She challenges Theron's reading of the Esherel letter: "That is a letter someone is writing to herself." Full analytical engagement. Formal debate structure. She names the distinction between motive and "what she does instead of doing the thing." — **Role: active partner**

**Ch. 10 — [Stern alcove]** — She has heard about the four-crate dining-room scene via the galley boy (has built an intelligence network via the cook). She confirms Vellaine's caution. She manages Theron's pacing on the underline. She closes with a small generous observation on Vannic: "He wasn't as bad as I expected." — **Role: active partner**

## First Active-Partner Passage

**Chapter 5 — the manifest delivery** is the first unsolicited action, but it is ambiguous: it could read as a witness wanting to stay useful, not yet a partner. The clearest textual inauguration of the active-partner role is:

**Chapter 7 (pantry scene and first joint session):**

> "Sable was inside the doorway. Calden was facing her, holding a folded cloth that had something heavy in it, mid-sentence. Theron caught the end of the sentence. '—Second-class corridor, yes, ma'am, but only the run from cabin nine to the steward's station and back. I'd not have crossed past nine.' 'And the door to nine?' 'Closed when I went, closed when I came back.' She nodded, once, with the small finished nod of a person who had been given a fact and was setting it down where she meant to put it."

Followed by the first direct agreement to work together:

> "She waited. Theron registered that he was meant to say the next thing, and that he did not yet have it. ... 'I'd like to compare,' he said. 'If you've an hour.' 'I've an hour.'"

And the partnership's terms are set explicitly at the working lunch:

> "'Why you, and not me.' She looked at him. The look was kind without being soft. 'Because you're going to write down what he says, sir, and I'm not.'"

---

## Is the Shift Earned?

**No — the pivot at Chapter 7 is present, but the ground is not fully prepared.**

What is present:
- A sequence of background observations (Ch. 1–3) that mark Sable as someone who notices things (the Vannic boarding observation, the property papers, the underlined-again line)
- The manifest delivery (Ch. 5) which is an action, though its motivation is ambiguous ("I wanted you to know I was a person you could ask," stated retroactively in Ch. 7)
- The "now is when I would have rather you came" line (Ch. 5) which signals she has something and is not comfortable waiting

What is missing:
- A scene in which Theron learns what she actually is — who she is before she is a partner. We do not know her occupation, her expertise, why she reads social dynamics with professional fluency, or why her knowledge of property law (the papers she carries) co-exists with skills that read as investigative. The narrative withholds this information as part of the twist architecture, but the result is that when she starts providing interrogation strategy in Ch. 8, the reader has no frame for *why she can do this.*
- A scene in which Theron consciously decides she is worth trusting or worth listening to. He does not decide this. She simply proves herself in the lunch scene and he accepts the proof. The acceptance is believable on the page — she earns it in the moment — but the editor's complaint stands: the scene that *earns the shift* (Ch. 7, the lunch) comes at the same moment the shift happens. There is no beat in Ch. 1–6 that tells us Theron has been building a provisional trust.
- The reveal of *why* she is watching. In Ch. 1, her stillness at the boarding area when Vannic thanks Calden is the most individuating detail she gets in the early chapters. It is not followed up. By Ch. 4, she is on the corridor when the body is found, and this is presented without comment. The reader is given no reason for why she was awake, why she was at her door, why she was watching.

## Recommended Seed-Scene Insertion Points

**Option A: End of Chapter 2 or opening of Chapter 3, passenger lounge**
The lounge scene in Ch. 2 establishes her at the corner table with property papers. This scene ends with Theron filing her and moving on. A 1–2 paragraph seed beat here — before he moves to the alcove — in which she does or says something that makes him look twice, would cost nothing in terms of plot pacing. For example: as Theron passes through, she asks him a single question about the gondola's second-class manifest process (she is a passenger in first class asking about second-class records, which is odd), or she makes a remark that shows she has already clocked who else is in the lounge with a precision that surprises him. He files it without understanding it. The beat accomplishes two things: it seeds the idea that she is paying attention beyond her own papers, and it gives Theron a prior observation to recall in Ch. 7 when the partnership crystallizes.

**Option B: Chapter 3, after the dinner scene / before Theron goes to bed**
Chapter 3 ends with Theron going to his cabin via the lounge (noted empty) and corridor (Calden at his book). There is a natural pause at the corridor exchange with Calden. A seed beat in which Theron passes Sable's cabin door on the way back — hears something, or briefly glimpses through an open door something that is not the property papers (a notation system, a map, an unexpected object) — and files it without knowing what to make of it. One paragraph. The beat accomplishes: establishing that she has a life behind the closed door that is different from what her surface presents, giving Theron a filed but unresolved detail to draw on, and preventing the impression that she is simply "the brown-coat woman" until Ch. 7 illuminates her. This placement is quieter than Option A and preserves the mystery of who she is, while establishing that Theron has been clocking her in a way he cannot explain.
