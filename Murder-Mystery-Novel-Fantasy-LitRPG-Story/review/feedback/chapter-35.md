# Chapter 35 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — keep reading. The opening is clean and controlled: "Not for the count of it — for the shape" earns attention on the first line. The short, declarative sentences build immediate tension without signalling what the tension is about. The unnamed stakes (what is this question? who is Joren Aldis?) generate just enough pull. One mild hesitation: readers cold to the gondola setting may spend a beat processing "porthole bench" and "sealing the case book" before they have the geography. But the voice is strong enough that they stay. No stopping point on the first page.

### 2. Planted details

Two details stopped me, but both felt natural rather than planted:

- **The inside coat pocket.** Sable resting two fingers against the cloth there, never taking anything out, feels like genuine restraint — a person who is carrying something and choosing not to use it. Natural.
- **The carved fish.** When Halsa says "That was the wooden fish" with immediate recognition, it lands as the only moment where a reader without prior context might pause — *how does she know exactly what it is?* Slightly pointed, but the chapter earns it quickly by having Sable name it as something already placed in context. Mostly natural.

Nothing else flagged. The noticing-character and the chapter's own rhythm absorb the details properly.

### 3. POV character

Theron lands well. He is present without dominating — this chapter belongs to Halsa and Sable, and his restraint (the repeated "All right"; not writing the name down; not picking up the paper) conveys a precise character: someone who knows when the room is not his. The one sentence that might grate for some readers is the final interior — "He did not open the book." It's good, but after 2,900 words of disciplined understatement, an ending on *didn't do something* risks feeling like an anti-climax. Still: yes, 100,000 words with this man. His three "All right" beats in the Halsa speech are a controlled performance.

### 4. Tone calibration

Calibrated well. This chapter earns a heavier tone than "cozy beach-read" for about 400 words — the Tarrin passage — but the surrounding fabric (the precise, procedural dialogue; the gondola hum; the small physical actions) holds it within the book's register. No moment felt too dark or too grim. One flag: the clause "which was the sound the door of a cabin made on a small southbound gondola at the hour past first bell on the seventeenth of November when the hum through the deck was the hum of two drakes finding their working thermal" is the only place the prose stretches near self-conscious. It works on re-read; cold, it might read as effortful.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "Theron watched the cabin be quiet for a slow count he did not count." The double-count construction ("count he did not count") tips into clever rather than precise — the chapter earns better than a fillip here.

**Keep:** "The book is even now." — Halsa's three-word accounting. Does more work than any paragraph could.
