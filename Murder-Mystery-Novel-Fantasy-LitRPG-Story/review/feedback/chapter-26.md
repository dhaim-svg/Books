# Chapter 26 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — keep reading. The chapter earns its first page. The clerk's knock registered as a specific institutional sound before Theron names it; that single detail (a magistrate's office knock) signals a narrator who notices how the world works, which is exactly the promise of a detective-POV beach read. The opening withholding — "sooner than the ten days Theron had budgeted and approximately four days sooner than he had hoped to be ready for" — is light enough to be banter rather than dread. The pacing through the wire summary is controlled: paragraph-by-paragraph reveals that feel earned. No stopping point on page one.

### 2. Planted details

One detail stood out: *The fish is in my coat. I have not taken it out.* In Sable's letter, this appears as a private reference with clear weight — she is holding something that matters, and she and Theron evidently know what it is. As a first-time reader, this reads as slightly planted: a callback the chapter is careful about, which makes the care itself visible. It felt meaningful without being intrusive, but the formality of the phrase — "I have not taken it out. I will not bring it to the meeting" — reads with an intentionality that a first-time reader registers as significant even without context. It did not stop me; it flagged.

### 3. POV character

Sympathetic and readable. Theron's professional restraint — not writing what he hasn't earned, underlining once and lightly — comes through without explaining itself, which is the right way to characterize a detective. The line "He read it. He read it again. He set the sheet down flat against the table" is economical and lands. The slight wryness about the System ("the System occasionally did this") prevents him from reading as cold. 100,000 words with him: yes, if every chapter reads like this one. He does not oversell the moment.

### 4. Tone calibration

Cozy and well-calibrated throughout. The chapter is procedural in the best sense — forward-moving, not flat. The Saturday harbor texture (factor arguing about Mirren, traveling cleric, tar smell from the slips) grounds the scene without mood-loading it. The Sable letter section is the warmest passage; the epistolary form gives her dry voice room to work. No moments that tipped too dark, too jokey, or too slow. One risk: the chapter is nearly all procedure and very little sensory — readers who need texture to stay engaged may feel the dining room as a blank space.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "He had been wrong about the frame. He had written that down in the Ch. 24 column." The second sentence is author-facing notation that lands as a self-reference artifact — "Ch. 24" breaks the fiction's internal grammar and should not be on the page in a published chapter.

**Keep:** "The case had a name in it that had been there all along. That was something. The rest was work." Earns its closing position, lands the tone, does not oversell.
