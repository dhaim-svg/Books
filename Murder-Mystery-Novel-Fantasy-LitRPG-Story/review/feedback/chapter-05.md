# Chapter 05 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — keep reading past page one. The opening hook is quiet but effective: the brass plate detail, the voice through the door already placing Theron by name, the physical description of Joriss that earns its length. The line "the smell of pipe smoke that had not been smoked in this room but was carried in on the captain's coat" is the kind of specific that signals a writer in control. Nothing stops the read on page one. The only hesitation point is the chapter's structural nature — this is inventory and procedure, and a reader who wants immediate plot momentum may feel the first section is more setup than payoff. That's a chapter-shape choice, not a line-level failure, and the prose is clean enough to carry it.

### 2. Planted details

The scene where Sable brings the manifest herself stood out — she walked past a steward's station to hand it to someone she barely knows. The chapter explicitly registers this as odd ("the part of his head that filed corridor sounds without consulting him"), then immediately refuses to interpret it. That felt natural: the noticing lands without underlining, and moving on is exactly what a cagey investigator would do. Her thumb holding the manifest open to the second-class single-men page also registered as deliberate — but again, the chapter steps away before it becomes pointed. Neither detail stopped the read. They simply accumulated mild curiosity, which feels like the right texture for this genre.

### 3. POV character

Theron lands as sympathetic and spendable. The slight insufferableness the chapter promises comes through in the method confidence — the categorizing that "happened without him," the brisk cadence of the captain scene, the Conservatory citation at the banister. What keeps him likeable is the precision without cruelty: he's thorough but not arrogant in the lounge scenes, genuinely respectful of Esherel's directness, and the final beat with the cold coffee has good self-aware quietness. The line "He was, he was conscious, filing more than he was reading" is the right kind of small doubt — visible enough to be honest, not so loud as to be performed. 100,000 words with him: yes.

### 4. Tone calibration

Solidly cozy. The gondola continues to hum, the coffee is in every scene, the captain's almost-smile lands as texture not portent. The manifest-sorting sequence sustains interest through accumulation rather than menace. The only moment that drifted slightly was the passage where Theron's hand rests on the brass plate of cabin twelve: "the body inside had drawn it once and would not draw it again" is the closest the chapter gets to darkness, and it earns that weight without spilling into grim. Esherel and Vellaine both carry the cozy register well in the lounge — Esherel's "that is a fine answer, I'd like it now" is a small tonal success. No passage felt too dark, too jokey, or too flat.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "He was, he was conscious, filing more than he was reading." The repetition of "he was" strains to be a stylistic choice and reads instead as slightly clunky. The observation is real but this delivery flattens it.

**Keep exactly:** "That's a different sentence." / "Yes, sir." — The exchange with Joriss over the closed-case vs. buildable-case distinction is the sharpest dialogue beat in the chapter. Two words of concession do more character work than two paragraphs of internal reflection could.
