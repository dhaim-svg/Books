# Chapter 10 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes — keep reading. The opening line lands immediately: the character woke already mid-thought, with an opinion about a word he used yesterday, and you want to know what the word was and why he's still pleased with it. The cabin-morning setup is quick and textured enough (coffee under a cloth, the chalk tick on the board) that the world establishes itself without explaining itself. The first potential friction is the corridor passage — *"he went down the corridor at the pace he walked when he was thinking around a problem rather than at it"* — which is the kind of sentence that asks the reader to slow down with it. It works if you're already hooked. It might lose a browser who got there before the Esherel scene. Page one holds; the italicized memory flash earns its interruption.

### 2. Planted details

Two details stood out:

**The corridor step-aside** (Vannic pausing for an elderly passenger with no audience, no gesture). Landed as *natural*. The memory-surface framing — unbidden, not bid for — does the job of making it feel like a wandering mind rather than a pointing finger. Filed and moved on; the reader moves on with it.

**"Vannic wasn't as bad as I expected."** This one is *natural but noticeable* — not in a way that stopped me, but in the way a line does when it's precisely placed. The conversation earns it organically and keeps moving; Theron files it and turns the slate over. I noticed but I didn't stop. That's the right calibration.

### 3. POV character

Sympathetic and slightly amusing in a dry way. Theron's habit of not writing the underline, his cataloguing of Sable's cup angle, his satisfaction at the System ping — these are all lightly endearing rather than grating. The "he almost smiled; he did not, but the not-smiling was the kind of not-smiling that the room got the use of" moment lands as the best character beat in the chapter. I would spend 100,000 words with him. The only friction is that his method-speak can feel self-enclosed: when he narrates his own thinking in the chartroom scene, the prose is doing a lot of lifting and occasionally edges toward telling us he's rigorous rather than showing it. Not a dealbreaker at this stage.

### 4. Tone calibration

Squarely cozy. The Esherel dining-room scene is the chapter's biggest tonal test, and it stays on the right side of surgical-but-not-grim: she's right, she's angry, the room reads her, and the chapter does not moralize about either the rightness or the anger. Vellaine's "be careful with the cleanest column" is the kind of sideways-delivered wisdom that the genre does well. The one moment that flattened slightly: the chartroom case-build section (opening of Scene 3). It's correct and necessary, but the rhythm of *motive on the page, opportunity on the page, demeanor on the page* is expository in a way the surrounding scenes aren't. It's doing load-bearing structural work; it just reads a little like a recitation. Not enough to break tone — this is a cozy mystery, the case-build is expected — but the weakest beat in the chapter.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** *"He drew the corridor on the slate from memory. He drew her likely line of approach. He drew the door of cabin twelve and the half-step pass-off that Sable had walked with him at second-watch end of Ch 7, which had given the corridor's geometry its small clean shape."* The middle clause ("He drew her likely line of approach") is functional but inert, and the parenthetical reference to "Ch 7" is a manuscript artifact — not for the reader. The sentence before and after carry the substance without it.

**Keep:** *"He almost smiled. He did not, but the not-smiling was the kind of not-smiling that the room got the use of."*
