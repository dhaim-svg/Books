# Chapter 11 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes, keep reading. The opening sentence — the page in his pocket "wanting air" — is a small strange thing done with confidence, and it earns its keep before the paragraph is out. The Wisp banter is immediately warm and characterful. The "three fictions" exchange lands as wit without trying. The harness-chain hum note is handled deftly: registered, filed, not labored. Nothing on page one stops the reader. The only small friction: the paragraph beginning "He listened the way the second-year acoustics class had taught him to listen" asks slightly more of the reader than the surrounding prose does — the terminology briefly outpaces the voice. But it doesn't break the first-page test; it's a single bump, not a wall.

### 2. Planted details

One detail stood out: Vannic's sentence about his niece and the carved object. It landed as **natural** — buried in a meal conversation, recalled without pause, filed without emphasis. The prose does not linger on it, and neither did I. The wooden fish mentioned immediately after connected cleanly without underlining. The carry-frame harness note read as flavor/investigation color, not as a plant — it felt like Theron building his case competently. Nothing else pinged as pointed. The Esherel confrontation drew attention (deliberately), but as plot escalation rather than planted detail.

### 3. POV character

Sympathetic and, crucially, interesting. Theron reads as a young man who is smart enough to be a little dangerous to himself — the showing-off to the page, the small satisfied ticks, the pulse-count after Esherel's confrontation. That last detail is the best thing in the chapter: measuring his own recovery time is precise characterization. He does not tip into insufferable. The relationship with Sable works as a structural engine — her cup-turn moment, the not-smiling that the table gets the use of — without becoming precious. A hundred thousand words is not too many; he's good company.

### 4. Tone calibration

Solidly cozy. The lemon biscuit correction from the cook (four instead of six, then a second plate with two more) is the chapter's best cozy set piece — warm, small-human, carrying character without straining. The Esherel scene threatens to tip toward tension, but the chair-that-didn't-move and the not-raised voice hold it exactly in the register of "small public unpleasantness with good manners on both sides," which is exactly right for the genre. No moments felt too dark or too jokey. One flat risk: the gallery case-build scene runs slightly procedural; it reads as necessary setup but carries the least warmth. Not a problem at chapter length, but watch it in sequences.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** *"He listened the way the second-year acoustics class had taught him to listen to a sustained field — the small offset between the carrying note and the carried one, when the field had a second component running parallel to it."* The pedagogical scaffolding slows the rhythm here; the carry-frame concept pays off without the classroom framing.

**Keep exactly as written:** *"He almost smiled. He did not, but the not-smiling was the kind of not-smiling that the table got the use of."*
