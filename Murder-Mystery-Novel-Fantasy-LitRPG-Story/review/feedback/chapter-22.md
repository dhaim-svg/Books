# Chapter 22 — Review

## Blind Review

### 1. First-page test

Yes, I would keep reading. The opening two sentences earn their place immediately: the mundane detail of re-stacking notes followed by the dry parenthetical about Vannic crediting himself for candor establishes voice, world, and character economy at once. The chapter never loses that momentum. The only moment that slows is the transition into the sitting-room debrief (after the second section break) — the shift from interrogation to analysis takes a beat to re-orient, but it recovers quickly. Nothing stopped me.

### 2. Planted details

No single detail stopped me as obviously planted. The mention of Roven as a "talent-sourcer" and Toll's deliberate route selection are both flagged by the prose itself as investigatively significant (Theron's notebook-writing makes this explicit), so they read as plot-delivery rather than planted texture. Pessel's letter lands as a clean narrative reveal — nothing in it felt pointed beyond its stated function. The only detail worth noting: "Joren wrote in spring that 'the time was nearly right'" feels slightly elliptical in a way that invites speculation, but it reads as withholding information (the character Pessel won't explain), not as authorial underlining.

### 3. POV character

Theron works. He is precise without being cold, and the chapter's best beat — his silent confirmation that Sable is right about going to Sannholt — shows him in a good light without making him soft. The methodical notebook-writing grounds his voice without slipping into academic tedium. His one-line responses in the Vannic interview ("He chose the passage," written down without comment) read as earned rather than theatrical. I would spend 100,000 words with him.

### 4. Tone calibration

Cozy throughout, and appropriately pitched. Vannic's comic texture is present in the fork-for-no-reason detail and the "distinction that mattered to him and no one else" line — both land without stopping the interrogation's forward motion. The Pessel letter shifts the register briefly toward something more muted, but the chapter holds. No moment was too dark or too jokey. The sitting-room cold-grate description carries gentle atmosphere without overcrowding. The only minor note: the second and third section breaks feel slightly mechanical — the scene-break after "He closed the notebook" before the summary paragraph could have been folded into the interrogation scene without loss.

### 5. Line-level

**Cut:** "She had that way sometimes, of not disagreeing with a position she had no particular interest in defending." The sentiment is clear from context without stating it; the sentence explains what the dialogue and silence already performed.

**Keep:** "He proposed it," Vannic said, with what was clearly a distinction that mattered to him and no one else." Carries character, comedy, and Theron's authority in one compact movement. Exactly right.
