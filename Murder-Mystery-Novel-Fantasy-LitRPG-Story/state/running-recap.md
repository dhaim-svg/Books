# Running Recap

Three to five sentences per completed chapter. Facts and tone notes only — no analysis.
The tail entry feeds directly into the `### Continuity` block of the next chapter spec.

**Format:**
> Ch. N ends with [event]. [Character] knows [fact]. [Tone note if relevant].

**Ch. 1 — Boarding the Wyrm**
> Ch. 1 ends with the Wyrm aloft and Theron settled in his first-class cabin, pleased with himself and the pin. Calden is the gondola steward; Wisp is the drake handler; the three drakes are Mottled, Gravel, and Petal. Lord Vannic boarded loudly, generating friction with the courier and a mutual wariness with Madam Esherel. In the first-class corridor, Vannic shook Theron's hand — Theron registered a working-mage callus on the index finger and filed it as "noble who practices seriously." In a separate beat, Vannic thanked Calden by name from further down the corridor; the woman in the brown coat (unnamed, three doors from Theron) went still in her doorway before entering her cabin without comment. Tone: holiday arrival, cozy, no murder yet.

**Ch. 2 — In the Air**
> Ch. 2 ends with Theron settled into the first morning of flight, having catalogued the dining room and decided not to investigate anything. He meets Dr. Vellaine (juridical theorist, Larent Institute, traveling to the Halveth Compact symposium); the System informs him she's read his reconstruction paper — she doesn't mention it. Esherel's wine was substituted; Theron comments aloud before he decides to, apologizes, she acknowledges he's correct. "Vannic" eats alone with his back to the room. The first-class manifest contains one post-departure booking amendment; Theron notices it and writes "no active queries" in his notes. The woman in the brown coat is doing property paperwork she'd rather not be doing; Theron files her as: own errand, probably dull. Wisp manages the drakes like difficult colleagues; Mottled is in an ongoing dispute with Gravel about technique. Tone: cozy holiday, Theron's self-discipline about not investigating is already fraying at the edges.

**Ch. 3 — The Manifest**
> Ch. 3 ends with Theron asleep in his cabin on the first overnight passage, the harness chains settled into their night-rhythm. He spent the afternoon pulling the full first-class manifest from the slate and building working categories on each passenger before catching himself doing it; he then put the slate in the desk drawer as a deliberate exercise in restraint. The amended twelfth booking (now under the name M. Tannin, cabin three, original name under sealed administrative note at Calden's discretion) is on record but he didn't pursue it. He found Wisp in the rigging house at second watch; she explained that Mottled does her best work when she believes she's getting away with something and that Gravel only performs when watched. Dinner was quieter than breakfast; "Vannic" came in late, ate with his back to the room, and left before dessert; Vellaine told Theron nobody holds their pin straight in the third week. Tone: cozy holiday consolidating, Theron's investigator-instinct now treated as an active discipline rather than a frustration.

**Ch. 4 — The Locked Cabin**
> Calden's third knock on cabin twelve goes unanswered at second watch; Theron comes out and walks the steward through the protocol. The interior bolt is engaged, the porthole and deck hatch sealed, the chain trunk untouched, and Lord Vannic is on the bed in his dressing coat, three to five hours dead, with no wound, no bottle, and the dust seals on his luggage unbroken. Theron works the room and the body with discipline, logs an odd small wooden fish in an outer pocket between a used handkerchief and a steel pen-nib, and registers the working-mage ridge on the index finger only to confirm he's already filed it. The System assigns the quest *Solve the Death Aboard the Skybound Wyrm* and notes nineteen hours to Halverstow Port. Theron seals the cabin behind him, asks Calden for the corridor closed, the master held off the ring, the captain notified, the manifest brought, and a second pot of coffee; Vellaine asks if he's all right; Sable is at the corridor rail and won't quite look at him. Tone: cozy holiday at the moment of fracture — the dragons still hum through the deck, the coffee is still good, and a man who thanked the steward by name yesterday is not going to do anything by name again.

**Ch. 5 — Academy Mode**
> Theron formally briefs Captain Joriss in the chartroom — locked-cabin facts, wireless silence held to the last hour, captain's noon address to the passengers agreed on. Back in his cabin he works the first- and second-class manifests into a categorized suspect list with the method his Conservatory training taught him: Esherel ranks first to interview, the sealed post-departure amendment under "M. Tannin" in cabin three second, Vellaine and the second-class field at the verify tier; Sable he files at the verify-not-pursue category alongside the courier and moves on. Sable brings the second-class copy herself (Calden gave it to her), exchanges six words about the corridor, leaves; he registers without yet interpreting that she walked past a steward's station to deliver it. By midday the news has settled across the first-class lounge: Esherel asks plainly whether she's a suspect and is told it's on the list, Vellaine confirms she heard Calden's first two rounds but not the third, and Sable says now is when she'd rather he came back, which he files under come back. Tone: cozy holiday cracked but not broken — the gondola flies on, the coffee is still good, Theron believes his method, and the method is the right shape for the wrong case.

**Ch. 6 — Esherel, in Full**
> Theron interviews Madam Esherel in her cabin and gets the full shape of her grievance: forty-one and a half thousand crowns of unpaid back third on a wine contract, two years of unanswered letters, and a closing-of-business letter she'd intended to slip under "Vannic's" door on the last evening, third paragraph rewritten four times. Her alibi covers a four-minute window with Calden at the third bell; the rest of the night is her word. She lampshades Theron's thoroughness — *the thorough is good; it is sometimes the thorough's shape that is the problem* — and he files her honesty as confirmation rather than warning. Calden walks him through the four rounds: cabin twelve was quiet through every pass; the steward's fourth-watch absence was twelve minutes in the second-class corridor on an administrative matter Calden will not discuss "without the captain's leave," and the M. Tannin amendment was paid on the dock after boarding but before lift. Theron passes through the lounge on the way to find the captain, files Sable's still-looking-at-the-harness-chain in the verify column without crossing to her, and notices a half-beat too late that he filed her without looking. Tone: the gondola flies on, the lemon biscuits arrive, the method continues to feel like the right shape and is not.

**Ch. 7 — Working a Scene Together**
> Theron comes down to the steward's station to ask the captain's leave for pressing Calden on the twelve-minute absence and finds Sable already there — Calden has answered her a fragment he stopped answering Theron yesterday (he'd not have crossed past cabin nine in the second-class corridor), and steps back to *sir* the moment Theron registers. Over leftover soup at the small window table while the lounge is at the captain's address, Theron and Sable compare what each has: he has the geometry and the manifest, she has the corridor and the man. In passing she names that "Vannic" reached for the word *harbor* at the first night's dinner, vowel flat and coastal, said it again right two words later — she names it as the kind of thing one notices and does not mention because correcting a lord at dinner is rude; Theron files it under *small talk* and asks his next question. She tells him Calden goes a half-second slower past the cabin twelve door on every round because Vannic had thanked him by name, and that the steward has been waiting all morning for someone he could give the part-answer to; the System notes, dryly, that she is not Theron's assistant. They walk the corridor together — she shows him the alcove sightline at the bell-board and the half-step out to read cabin twelve, and he sees Calden's path as a man's body in a space rather than a notation in a manifest; she tells him she walked past the steward's station yesterday because he had filed her in his verify column and she wanted him to know she was a person he could ask. Tone: the gondola flies on, the lemon biscuits migrate, and the method begins, slightly, to widen.

**Ch. 8 — Two Ways of Reading a Room**
> Theron comes back from Captain Joriss with leave for the twelve-minute matter (conditional on Calden's consent) and finds Sable at the second-class bay window; he runs the structure of what the captain authorized and Sable edits his approach before he uses it, telling him to open with the general (was a passenger in distress) rather than the cabin, and pointing out that the minute the twelve began is also free, which gives Theron two distinct overlap windows on Esherel and Vellaine he had been collapsing into one. She gives him her read of "Vannic" as a person: the wall-facing as expensiveness of the room rather than rudeness, the using of Calden's name from first telling, the lord-voice as a coat put on at the boarding deck — and, in passing, the second night when "Vannic" slid a ginger sweet to the steward's tray without looking up so it could move to an airsick child two tables over (her line: *not how that kind of man moves through the world*); Theron files the kindness under Sable's read on his temperament and asks the next question. She moves one tick on his timing slate three minutes earlier (Vellaine's lamp record, not her wakefulness) and gives the pencil back. They split for the work; Theron goes down to the pantry with Sable's order of questions already arranged in his mouth. Tone: cozy with forward motion — two people who read differently, reading together; the method's shape widening.

**Ch. 9 — A Letter That Did Not Want Sending**
> Theron comes out of the Calden interview with both ends of the twelve-minute window confirmed, cabin three confirmed by inference, and a new free three minutes on the early side of the fourth watch; he and Sable take it to the stern reading alcove and put the corridor onto the slate together. When he turns to Esherel — whom he still has at rank 1 — Sable raises a specific objection: the letter Esherel has been writing for six days, with four rewrites in the third paragraph and a half-page stopped mid-fourth, is not a letter someone is preparing to send. It is a person composing the not-replying; the trade is the alibi for keeping the page open. Theron hears it, walks his case against Esherel out loud (motive, opportunity, candor), and concludes — politely, without softening — that the shape of the letter is a feeling he would have to weigh against three things on the page that point the other way, and that he is keeping Esherel where she is until the page moves her. Sable lets him; she is teasing him a little, not unkindly, and goes off to have a small kind conversation with the cook about Calden eating standing up. Tone: warm and forward-moving — two methods sitting beside each other without merging, the disagreement carried as banter, the page intact.

**Ch. 10 — The Cleanest Page**
> Theron wakes with the underline on *candid* still pleasing him, walks the first-class corridor, and lets the boarding-morning memory of Vannic stepping aside for an old man surface and refile under *idle noble courtesy*; in the dining room Esherel raises her body over a four-crate manifest discrepancy that is real and unflinchingly escalated in front of the room, and Vellaine warns him at six tables' distance to be careful with the cleanest column. He spends the late morning in the captain's gallery building the page that would carry the conclusion against Esherel — motive, two-window opportunity in series, demeanor under stress — and deliberately does not write the underline. In the stern alcove with Sable he walks the page through for her and weighs her reading honestly against the other three; Sable, in the drift of the conversation, says half-shrug that Vannic wasn't as bad as she expected, and Theron files the line under small talk between two people who have been thinking about a man and moves on. He takes the slate to his cabin at the bronzed hour, lays the pencil parallel to the slate's top edge, and decides the morning is the right shape; he is comfortable. Tone: cozy at peak, peak confidence in the wrong direction — the page is the cleanest it has been, and the chapter does not flag it.

**Ch. 11 — Three Drakes in Formation**
> Theron walks the page out onto the starboard promenade, talks with Wisp about Mottled holding the count and Petal handing Gravel a two-breath-wider slot on the return, and standing at the brass rail registers a thin secondary note in the harness-chain hum — a *carry-frame* from his second-year textbook, which he files in the margin under cabin twelve without yet connecting it to anything. At lunch Esherel closes the folio, turns a quarter on her chair, and asks Theron in front of Vellaine and the Henners whether he has a question for her in the open; he answers that he is working the page and will speak to her when it is finished. In the gallery he builds the column with a finer hand, underlines *demeanor* once, and reads a dry System line about a page looking like a face from far enough away without flinching. Over an early supper in the saloon with Sable, in the drift of talk about the cook and the bread schedule, he remembers a sentence "Vannic" had said at the third dinner of the run — that his niece had carved a small object for him at six and would be unimpressed he hadn't lost it yet — and files the line under small social texture of a man whose body is in cabin twelve, while Sable, with the bread halfway to her plate, glances at the porthole and brings the bread down. He takes the slate to his cabin at the close of the bronzed hour, does not write the underline on opportunity, and is comfortable. Tone: cozy at peak, peak confidence in the wrong direction.

**Ch. 12 — The Page Finished**
> At the second-watch coffee in the captain's gallery Theron sees that he has built the case on the corridor sightline from Esherel's dining-room table but not from her cabin door, walks the deck-three corridor at the open of lunch with her cabin empty, and confirms the same half-step kink from her threshold to cabin twelve; at lunch Vellaine, without looking up, says she will be at the long porthole in the saloon this evening as a witness if he wants one, and he accepts. In the afternoon he writes *opportunity (late)* under Esherel's column and underlines it once, sitting the page beside the underline on *demeanor* — the column has closed — and a dry System line lands that he reads twice and does not flinch at. Over the early supper in the saloon with Sable he tells her plainly that he intends to speak to Esherel after supper at the long porthole; she answers only that she will be at her usual corner table, that she had not waited to be asked. He takes the slate up to his cabin at the close of the bronzed hour, sets it parallel to the desk's far edge without opening it, changes into his good shirt with his mother's uneven stitches at the cuff, and goes down to the supper. Tone: cozy at absolute peak, Theron unhurried and comfortable, the page finished, the gondola on its long approach to Halverstow Port through a bronzed sky.

**Ch. 13 — Petor Vann**
> Sable turns up a third-class register entry for "Petor Vann" that Calden had been holding since the amended M. Tannin booking; she and Theron descend to cabin three-T together and find Lord Petric Vannic alive, hiding from a forced betrothal to Duke Esseven's daughter. Vannic confesses he hired an illusionist named Joren through a factor (Berric Toll, Mirren west harbour) to perform the role of Lord Vannic for the duration of the flight while Vannic travelled separately as Petor Vann; the arrangement was invisible to every other passenger. Theron takes the correspondence packet and reorients the case: the dead man is Joren, not Vannic, and whoever killed him knew it — the question is now Joren's working history, not Esherel's motive. Theron instructs Vannic to stay in cabin three-T until Halverstow; Sable tells Theron he owes Esherel an apology, and he disagrees with the word "apology" while conceding the point. Tone: cozy register intact — Vannic is ridiculous and caught, Theron is reoriented and moving to the next problem, Sable is pragmatic.

**Ch. 14 — The Apology That Wasn't**
> Theron interviews Esherel, delivers the news that the dead man was an illusionist performing Vannic, and she is cleared from rank one without drama; she gives him three useful observations — the wine-glass grip, active room-orientation behavior, and visible strain on the third-morning observation deck. Theron and Sable work the third-class manifest in the steward's alcove and mark Maret Fell (cabin seven-T, Mirren east harbour) as the name of most immediate interest; four passengers remain unaccounted for by profession or reference. Theron opens the Berric Toll correspondence and reads that Joren had a destination after Halverstow: three to four weeks of personal business in Sannholt, south coast Halveth, with a contact address under the name "Pessel" at the Copper Finch. The question the correspondence opens is where Joren was going and whether whoever killed him knew about it. Tone: cozy and forward-moving — Esherel is nearly pleased to be right, Theron concedes the column was wrong, Sable is efficient, the gondola is closing on Halverstow.

**Ch. 15 — The Third-Class Corridor**
> Calden's briefing gives soft alibis to two of the four blank third-class passengers; Maret Fell (cabin seven-T, Mirren east harbour) held up under interview but gave clean, correct answers at arm's length, and the textile buyer describes her as looking like someone doing a job. Halsa Renn (cabin nine-T, traveling through to Sannholt on family business) answered in the doorway, gave a simple and complete account, and Theron filed her as fourth-tier with no connection to Mirren west harbour or Toll. Sable notes afterward that Renn notices how people move; Theron folds the sheet and moves on. The active thread is Maret Fell, with Joren's Sannholt destination still in the letter in Theron's coat, and Sable will ask Calden to arrange a shore contact with the captain before landing. Tone: cozy and forward-moving — the corridor close-quartered and specific, the Halverstow harbor lights coming up at the edge of the sky as the gondola begins its descent.

**Ch. 16 — He Had a Name**
> The gondola docks at Halverstow Port; Theron and Sable take the captain's day-cabin while the magistrate is summoned. Sable opens the recall passage by naming Joren Aldis — full name, from the Toll correspondence — and walks through the five planted details in order: the three kindnesses, the coastal accent, the working-mage callus, and the niece-softness whose tone belonged only to him. In the stillness between the fourth and fifth details, Sable's "he wasn't as bad as I expected" from Ch. 10 returns to Theron as memory — the recognition of his own failure to ask — and he sits with it while a coal barge clears the harbor slip through the porthole glass. The System updates the quest to Solve the Death of Joren Aldis; the chapter closes on the carved fish in Theron's hand — birch or ash, the fin at its imprecise angle, worn smooth from being carried. Tone: quiet, precise, non-melodramatic — the structural midpoint, held with the care it deserves.

**Ch. 17 — The Magistrate and the Debt**
> The magistrate interviews Theron and Vannic formally; Joren Aldis is on the front page, Fell thread and Sannholt contact logged; Vannic is cleared of suspicion and held for questions. Esherel then conducts the promised debt conversation with Vannic in Theron's presence: forty-one thousand five hundred crowns acknowledged in a signed letter, Esherel running the room entirely while Theron is a witness-shaped piece of furniture. On the quayside, Sable has arranged an introduction with dock-pilot Master Olen, who confirms Maret Fell's name in the east Mirren yard logs and notes that crossing to Berric Toll's west harbour operation would require a deliberate reason. The next steps are the Gray Quay factors' board this afternoon and the Sannholt contact thread; Theron goes up the gangway to write the written report. Tone: brisk and administrative for the magistrate scene, uncomfortably comic for the debt scene, forward-moving on the harbor step.

**Ch. 18 — The Factors' Board**
> Theron finds Maret Fell's name in the Gray Quay factors' board with a non-standard margin notation — TL-9, self-submitted, on two spring customs-assist jobs, the second ending six days before the gondola run — and has the clerk flag it for the magistrate's office. Sable locates the Halverstow-Sannholt post-coach clearing house through dock loader Pell; a letter to Pessel at the Copper Finch goes in Tuesday's run. Walking back along the harbor, they discuss TL-9 (possibly Toll, not confirmed), the six-day gap, and the two-day wait for the magistrate's flag. At the gangway foot at day's end, Sable says: "Someone has to tell his family properly. In person. Before any letter from the port office gets there." Theron hears it and does not argue. Tone: forward-moving and specific, the Fell thread advanced but not resolved, the Sannholt thread formally opened, the promise made.

**Ch. 19 — The Reply from Berric Toll**
> The magistrate's office returns the TL-9 flag result: confirmed as Berric Toll's private network shorthand, informally registered this spring, meaning Fell was self-identifying as a Toll subcontractor on the two customs-assist jobs. Theron returns to Vannic at the Arms and draws out two new details: Joren had a file on Vannic's specific mannerisms from researched records, and warned Vannic at the dock that if anyone asked about Lord Vannic, to say he was difficult to find — suggesting active prior awareness of being tracked through Toll's network. Sable spots that Theron didn't ask Vannic whether Toll named any subcontractors to him; the question goes as a written note. The chapter closes with Theron writing the clean summary for the magistrate, Joren Aldis in the header, the Fell/Toll triangle confirmed in outline, and both threads (Fell location; Sannholt reply) moving forward in parallel. Tone: brisk and methodical, with a clean "you're better at knowing what the question is / I'm better at knowing when to ask it" beat between Theron and Sable.

**Ch. 20 — The Peacock in the Rain**
> Vannic's reply to the written note confirms Toll named one person in the network: Roven, described as the one who found and vouched for Joren, who had worked with Joren before, and would be "aware of the passage" — framed as reassurance but implying active oversight from inside the gondola run. Theron presses Vannic in person, drawing out that Roven was senior to Joren in the network; the moment Vannic reveals that Toll is also his estate factor — has been for three years — is the chapter's Peacock-in-the-Rain beat. Sable identifies Roven as a fourth structural point in the triangle; the magistrate's constable has located Maret Fell at the Compass and Beam inn, still in Halverstow with a new cargo-verification job booked. Theron requests a formal interview with Fell at the magistrate's office for the following morning, with the TL-9 notation as the opening move. Tone: comic in the right register (Vannic ridiculously stuck with his own estate factor at the center of the murder investigation), investigatively substantive, forward-moving toward Ch. 21.

**Ch. 21 — The Fell Interview**
> Fell arrives at the magistrate's office professional and guarded; she acknowledges TL-9 as Toll's customs reference system, characterizes herself as a one-off contractor, and knows Joren Aldis's name "by reputation through the yard circuit" without placing him aboard the Wyrm. When Theron names Roven, Fell goes still — the first departure from her controlled register — then gives a calibrated admission: Roven is senior in the network, serious-commission work, she has never dealt with him directly and does not know his location. She gives the cargo job reference numbers connecting her spring work to the Wyrm's Mirren freight manifest without hesitation, which Sable identifies as misdirection. A clearing-house confirmation arrives: Pessel at the Copper Finch, Sannholt, is a confirmed active box-holder; their letter was accepted Friday run; reply expected within four to five days. Tone: methodical and controlled — Fell's thread advanced but not resolved, Roven still a ghost.

**Ch. 22 — Vannic's Full Accounting**
> Theron presses Vannic through the full contract accounting: Toll brokered the commission (3,000 crowns, half at signing), Roven was Toll's talent-sourcer and had placed Joren specifically; Toll selected the Wyrm passage for cargo-side reasons and named Fell to Vannic as a reliable regular account on that passage; Dosseren, a clearing broker, handled the fee payment. The Sannholt reply arrives from Pessel: Joren was expected, is eleven days overdue, and Pessel has been waiting for two years — Joren wrote in spring that "the time was nearly right" — but will not explain further in writing. Sable decides plainly to take Friday's post-coach to Sannholt to speak to Pessel in person; Theron stays to follow the cargo job numbers. Vannic has given everything useful and is recruited; Step 7 closes. Tone: methodical and forward-moving, Vannic finally useful, the Sannholt thread newly alive with two years of unspoken purpose.

**Ch. 23 — Everyone Walks Out**
> Theron traces Fell's cargo job numbers to Vantrel Secured Storage (registered agent H. Marbeth, west harbour Mirren) — a partial result that points toward Act 3; he has written to the magistrate's office for a search warrant. In reviewing his early suspect columns, he recognizes that his method was correct for the case he thought he had — he asked who had reason to kill Lord Vannic when the question should have been who had reason to stop Joren Aldis. Esherel sends her niece Norra to report one more observation: on the second evening of the passage, Esherel saw Joren alone in the first-class corridor reaching into his coat pocket for something small, looking at it privately, putting it away; her line (delivered through Norra): "He didn't look like a man afraid of being caught. He just looked like himself." Esherel has asked to be notified when a proper address for Joren's family is available; she intends to write. Tone: methodical and quiet — the investigator's first-class categories dropped, the Esherel thread closing with weight.

**Ch. 24 — Everyone Walks Back In**
> Theron re-examines the fourth-tier dismissals against the correct question and writes a revisit flag on Halsa Renn, noting her Sannholt destination, her observation of Calden's changed walk, and the wrong frame at the time of interview. Calden has given Sable his steward's working notes on the third-class corridor, including an uncertain recollection of a lamp burning past third watch on the third night in possibly cabin nine-T. Theron and Sable divide their threads at breakfast — she carries the manifest notes and the two-year Sannholt context to Pessel in person; he stays in Halverstow to push the Vantrel/Marbeth warrant. Sable reminds Theron he ate half a piece of bread; the chapter closes with her coach confirmed for seventh bell Friday. Tone: warm and professional — the threads divided, the departure imminent.

**Ch. 25 — The Coach Departs**
> The Vantrel/Marbeth warrant result arrives: registered agent H. Marbeth was vouched for irregularly by Vantrel's own operator Fenn Kolach; Theron's next step is a letter of introduction to the Mirren magistrate's office to interview Kolach. Sable departs on the seventh-bell coach. Before she boards, Theron gives her the carved wooden fish to carry to Joren's family; she takes it and puts it in her inside coat pocket with the same motion she uses for her notebook. Calden is present at the yard edge but does not come forward. The chapter closes on Theron in the clearing-house yard writing the two open threads — Marbeth/Kolach and Sable/Pessel — into his notebook and walking back to the Arms. Act 2 ends. Tone: dry and forward — "The case had the right names in it now. That was something. The rest was work."

**Ch. 26 — A Name on the Wrong Register**
> The Kolach result arrives from Mirren faster than budgeted: Kolach broke at the introduction letter and confessed to running a discreet credential-placement service through Vantrel for eleven years; the wire-transcribed channel log for the current year contains twenty-three entries, and the seventeenth is Halsa Renn, personal-referral booking credential placed for the Wyrm's twenty-third departure. Theron cross-references the third-class manifest and recognises that Renn's "no professional reference" was substitution, not absence — she was inside Joren's network, not Vannic's. Sable's first letter arrives by return post-coach: she has not yet met Pessel, but the Whitepost Inn's landlady mentioned that Pessel used to host a coastal young woman three or four times a year, formerly apprenticed to a man in the same magical trades. Theron writes back naming the Kolach finding, declines to call Renn a suspect, and closes the case book with the new column open and the line *Status: in front of us.* Tone: forward, professional, dry. Act 3 has begun, by clerical correspondence.

**Ch. 27 — Unpicking a Profile**
> Sunday morning at the Halverstow Arms, Theron drafts the working profile he should have written in week one: Halsa Renn's cash booking and stated reasons were a cover designed to pass a Wyrm registry, not an investigator; her motive lives in a contract-side, private-currency life his noble-rivalry training had no slot for. He writes the structural finding about his own pattern recognition on a separate page so he will not forget what it cost. Sable's second letter arrives by return coach: Pessel opened the Sannholt meeting by asking whether Joren had died well, then told Sable that Joren had been putting money aside for an unnamed child for eleven years and had intended the Wyrm route to be his last large job, with Pessel herself holding the money; Pessel also confirmed Joren had trained a young coastal woman who used to visit Sannholt three or four times a year and stopped coming about two years ago, without naming her. Theron logs the apprenticeship confirmation in the working column, files the child-and-money information in a separate back-of-book page that is "not the column's business yet," and opens a fourth question — what changed two years ago — which he leaves unanswered. Tone: forward, professional, dry; the Sunday harbour and a caraway loaf carry the warmth.

**Ch. 40 — The Tuesday Coach**
> Monday and into Tuesday, Theron writes Davren's closing report in the stipulated form, uses both H. Renn and J. Aldis on the page, and records Sable's Monday-coach departure as a single procedural line. Tuesday at the quarter past first bell, Pessel comes with the rest of Joren's papers — a book, two coach receipts, the held letter for the family, and one folded slip that does not belong: a clerk's slip with an unsigned delivery clause and a small stamped device at the corner, a rondel of three lines folded inward toward a centre. Theron recognises the device as the same one he registered on a sheet in Halsa's papers at Davren's table the previous Friday and did not pursue. He asks Pessel one question; Pessel cannot help. Theron slides the slip into the back-fold pocket of his personal notebook, books the half-of-one inland coach to Mirren Vale on the outside bench, and the book closes on the painted board over the chandlery going past at the slow working pace of a coach taking the first bend of an inland run, the strap warm across his knees, his thumb moved off the leather and left there. *End.*

**Ch. 39 — The Address on the Page**
> Sunday at first bell in the harbour-side inn; Theron waits while Sable is with Pessel. She returns with two papers — Pessel's address slip for Joren's family and her own great-aunt's deed, which Pessel had held for a second-opinion read. She lays the two on her notebook and says, twice, that the address is in the same coastal stretch as her cottage: Tellan's Cross is the next parish over from her aunt's, the inner coast road forks at the white stone above the chandler's stile. She names the plan — two days at the cottage, then up the inner road to the third house. She slides both papers into the same fold of her notebook, rests two fingers on the inside coat lining where the carved fish has been since Halverstow, and goes upstairs to ready her coach. Tone: cozy denouement, southern morning, the recognition placed not announced, the case as sediment and the next part still hers.

**Ch. 38 — The Case Settles**
> Evening at the harbour-side inn in Sannholt. **Ch. 32 — The Name at the Foot of the Page**

**Ch. 31 — The Person-Shaped Hole**
> Wednesday morning at the Halverstow Arms, Theron raises the gap last night's theory surfaced: an apprentice would learn the seating phase as a method of laying glamour, but only a particular apprentice would learn it as a *trigger architecture*, taught by the master, not in any book. Sable reads two sentences from her own Pessel notebook that hadn't landed at the time — *she could do the work after about three years, but Joren kept her seven more* and *he sent her south alone for the work that needed her hand more than his* — and they sit on the page beside the abstract as the seating phase Pessel was describing without knowing she was describing it. Theron writes a new case-book page, *Person who could have performed the method*, in six lines: sustained-contact apprenticeship of seating-phase duration; signature compatibility produced by it; instruction in the trigger architecture, not standard apprenticeship content; retention of the trigger out of the apprenticeship the master did not demand back; voluntary, trust-shaped late-stage proximity from the victim; a post-apprenticeship working life that did not register on standing-detection rigs. He does not write Halsa Renn at the foot of the page. He turns to the back-of-book and adds, under last night's *exclusivity to Renn — not yet established*, the morning's narrow line: *Requirements list converges to one person on the manifest. Exclusivity not proven. Structural fit is.* He tells Sable tomorrow's work is subtraction across the manifest, line (a) clearing most cabins; she says the list is good, goes upstairs to write to Pessel about the seven years, and he stays at the table with the page closed under his thumb. Tone: quiet, working, no triumph; the prodigy-backstory has done its work without anyone naming it.

**Ch. 30 — The Theory Holds**
> Tuesday at last bell Sable arrives at the Halverstow Arms tired from the coach, eats the bean soup the host saved, and refuses to open the case book until she has finished it; then Theron walks her through the four working-theory lines and the thesis abstract and she asks the two questions that make the technical shape sit on the page: how long is sustained contact, and how far does the trigger reach. They test the theory against the cabin twelve sketch and against the gondola's third-night standing-detection log; the door bolted from inside and the porthole latched stop being an impossibility, and the scry log's *N.R.* in the cabin twelve cell stops being a negative finding and becomes a positive consistency, because the exploit's defining property is that it does not register as cast magic. Sable writes the chapter's line in her own notebook — *only an illusionist, or someone trained alongside one, would know to look for a method that does not show up on a scry* — and Theron copies it into the case book as the fifth method-page line. He sits with the five lines and lets himself name what the theory has done: it holds against the cabin, against the scry, against the apprentice line and the Sannholt back room and the corridor measurement. He then writes the missing piece on the back-of-book page in plain ink: *exclusivity to Renn — not yet established.* Tone: quiet, working, end-of-day, no triumph; the theory holds, and Joren is still dead, and the Arms goes down for the night around them.

**Ch. 29 — The Exam-Fodder Theory**
> Tuesday morning at the Halverstow Arms, Sable's road-letter arrives ahead of her on the early coach: she walked the Copper Finch in Sannholt and got three answers from the proprietor Hesh — a coastal young woman stayed in the back room three or four times a year for nine years, stopped two years ago without quarrel, always travelled south afterward — plus one unprompted line that Sable wrote down in Hesh's words, *did work that needed quiet rooms.* The phrase sits in the column next to a blank method page and shifts something; Theron goes upstairs and brings down the slate-bound licensing notebook from his graduation packet, which he has not opened since boarding the Wyrm, and rereads the abstract of his own final-year thesis on long-contact illusion-chain exploits — sustained glamour, seated within a compatible signature, persisting as ambient field rather than as cast magic, defining requirement sustained contact between caster and target during the seating phase. He writes four working-theory lines on the method page in the unindented hand he reserves for not-yet-earned: seating phase satisfied by an eleven-year apprenticeship, signature compatibility satisfied between two illusionists, once seated the exploit does not require the caster near the target at activation, trigger known to caster and not to target — and adds *to be tested against cabin twelve geometry and against the bolt and porthole on Sable's return.* He reads his oral-defence script for the first time since the pin and registers the corollary he had not stated at twenty-one: a class of exploit that does not register under current scry-detection will arrive looking like something else, which is what a sealed cabin and an unwounded body look like; in the back-of-book he writes *The thesis was exam fodder. It was also correct.* Tone: uncomfortable-but-moving, clerical, no triumph; the method page is no longer blank and Sable's coach is due at last bell.

**Ch. 28 — The Wrong Kind of Certainty**
> Monday morning at the Halverstow courthouse, Theron walks the working column to Magistrate Davren, who listens, asks the two professional questions that close the matter from his desk — did Renn enter cabin twelve, did anyone — and confirms what Theron already knew: he has a column the magistrate can keep in front of him but not yet a case to bring forward, because he has no method. A partial Mirren transcript arrives at the Arms before noon: Kolach's testimony, paragraph nine, names Joren Aldis himself as the personal-referral source for Halsa Renn's original placement eleven years prior; the current-year credential is the renewal of that vouching. Theron writes the placement into the column without editorialising and files the harder reading — Joren almost certainly put her into the trade without knowing she would one day form a grievance against him — on the back-of-book page beside Sunday's note about the money for the child. A courier note from Sable confirms she is leaving Sannholt on the evening coach, with a letter to follow about one further afternoon errand; she will be in Halverstow by Wednesday's last bell at the latest. By Monday evening, sitting with a pie that the kitchen is rightly proud of, Theron is certain about the *who* and explicitly clueless about the *how* — a passenger in cabin nine-T did not enter cabin twelve, and a man died inside without a wound — and he writes *Method — unknown* on a fresh page and closes the case book on the question. Tone: clerical, settled, professional; the private certainty held without speaking.
